			MOTHERS' WORK.
			EMERY STAKE.
"Woman rocks the cradle of the world.—ELLA
WILLIAMS.
First Talk.— Woman.
	“She openeth her mouth with wisdom; and in
her tongue is the law of kindness.
	“Favor is deceitful, and beauty is vain; but a

woman that feareth the Lord, she shall be praised.
“Give her of the fruit of her hands and let her
own works praise her in the gates.”—Proverbs,
Chapter 31.
I.—Her Sphere and Mission.
	(a) In ancient times how was she
appreciated?
	(b) The modern changes.
	(c) As wage earner.
	(d) To be a mother to her children
and companion to her husband.
	(f) God’s commandment to Adam
and Eve. (Gen., 1st Chap., 28th
verse; also Gen., 3rd chapter, 16th
verse.)
Preparation for Her Life's Work.
	(a) How to keep well.
	(b) Need of sympathy.
	(c) Dreams of youth.
	(d) Decision and independence of
character.
	(e) Pure hearts and a virtuous life.
Advantages of Proper character Butlding.
	(a) Confidence in parents.
	(b) Direct the girls’ purpose.
	(c) Self reliance.
	(d) Choice of companions.
	(e) Sunny temper.
	(f) What reading will do.
	(g) Perils of skepticism.
	(h) Religious views essential to a
life’s companion.
	(i) Leaving home.
	(j) How to be beautiful.
	(k) Manners and dress.

Second Talk.—The Wife. 
	(a) Man’s helpmate.
	(b) Religiously.
	(c) Intellectually.
	(d) Economically.
	(e) Her rights, duties and influence-
	(f) Influence of wives in former
times. (Ref. “Women of the Bible,”)
Third Talk.—Parenthood.
	(a) Effects of parental influences.
	(b) Example.
	(c) How to control children and
how to become acquainted with them.
	(d) Parents should train, but should
not change the child’s individuality.
By RUTH MCKEE.
Fourth Talk.—Motherhood.
	(a) Motherhood provides the earthly
pleasure and eternal happiness. (Ref.
page 421, Vol. 6, No. 6, Impt. Era.
	(b) Life is made up of pleasures,
tasks, cares and duties, which, when
added together make a grand total of
human happiness.
	(c) Parental culture is a very im-
portant subject for mothers to study,
gentleness, cheerfulness, and content-
ment.
	(d) Lead by love; study their dispo-
sitions; guard against partiality.
Recite to them in simple language
stories of the Savior. Sing to them.
	(e) Self control. Once able to con-
trol our tongues, and our tempers, we
will be prepared to lead our children.
	(f) Kindness should be taught by
example to be thoroughly instilled into
the child’s nature.
By MRS. B. O. MCINTIRE.
Fifth Talk.—Infancy.
	Infants and how to care for them.
	A child is a precious gift of God.
	(a) The Bath.
	(b) The virtue of olive oil in the
case of children.

[Column 3]
	(c) Food. I. Natural. 2. Arti-
ficial. 3. Given regularly, and why?
	(d) The wardrobe. Clothing should
be light, warm, loose, and not too
long.
	(e) Harm done by giving medicine.
	(f) Essential things for babies; con-
stant care, good milk mixed with
water for food, pure air. and plenty of
sleep.
By MRS. U. E. CURTIS.
Sixth Talk.—Childhood. 
	Let a child’s home be the happiest place on
earth.
I.—Religious Training.
	(a) Prayer.
	(b) Life of Christ.
	(c) Mission of the Prophet Joseph
Smith.
	(d) Honor and respect for the Priest-
hood.
2.—Moral Training.
	(a) Respect for old age a nd for par-
ents.
	(b) Self reliance and self control.
	(c) Truthfulness, be kind and
gentle.
	(d) Teach the children to remain at
home at night, unless circumstances
compel them to be away.
3—.Physical Training.
	(a) Plenty of outdoor exercise and
work directed by parents.
4—.Proper Clothing.
(a) The effects of tight clothing.
	(b) Childrens’ feet should be kept
warm and dry in winter.
	(c) Mention some of the benefits of
going barefoot in summer.
5.—Cleanliness.
	(a) Bathing.
	(b) Care of teeth, nails, hands, and
hair.
Seventh Talk.—Girlhood.
	(a) On what does beauty depend?
	(b) Girls should be taught to read
good books and choose good compan-
ions.
	(c) I. Take good care of them-
selves. 2. Keep their feet dry and
warm.
	(d) Keep both mind and body clean
and pure. I. Confide in mother.
2. Be kind and gentle.
	(e) Teach the girls to be home-
makers in the true sense of the word.
	(f) I. Dress should be suitable to
the employment. 2. Short skirts for
girls until they become women. 3. 
Guard against love of display. 4. To
be neat and tidy is always in good
taste.
By MRS. U. E. CURTIS.
Eighth Talk.—Boyhood.
	(a) Teach them to have faith in our
Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. That
He will hear and answer prayers.
Love for our religion and other
instructions.
	(b) Teach them personal purity.
Energy to be encouraged and pro-
perly directed. To form good habits.
Good habits are worth more than a
fortune.
	(c) To have an object or purpose in
life and to work with zeal to accom-


plish that purpose. (Ref., page 287,
Vol. 5, No. 4, Impt. Era.
	(d) Honor, refer to the fifth com-
mandment. That talents are gifts
from God, given to us undeveloped,
for us to improve and develop.
Prudent, cautious, self control is wisdom’s root.
---ROBERT BURNS.
(e) That marriage is ordained of
God unto man. (Ref., page 713, Vol.
5, No. 9, Impt. Era.)
Mine honor is my life, both grow in one,
Take honor from me and my life is done.
By MRS. B. O. MCINTIRE.
Ninth Talk.—Reverence.
	(a) True reverence, acceptable to
 God, is a filial fear; a reverence of
divine nature proceeding from a just
esteem of His perfections. Producing
in us an inclination to do His will and
serve Him.
	(b) We reverence superiors and age;
we honor authority; we love virtue.
	(c) We should revere our parents
with a submissive, humble spirit,
respecting and honoring them for the
many sacrifices they make lor us.
	(d) A wife should reverence her hus-
band. In chiding children for faults,
we should do it with love. Love
should govern the human heart.
	(e) Religion produces reverential
esteem for all sacred things. We
should revere the law of God and be
submissive to all the requirements.
(Ref. to all the above subjects: Im-
provement Era, Juvenile Instructor,
Young Woman's Journal, and The
Childrens' Faiend.

By MRS. B. O. MCINTIRE.

Tenth Talk.—Home Influences.
(a) Power for directing and modify-
ing.
	(b) A good home influence is made
by a congenial spirit, order, method,
kindly correction, good books, etc.
	(c) Entertainments:
(d) Happy effect of such influence.

By MRS. RUTH MCKEE.
Elevonth Talk.—Recreation for Children.
	(a) Childrens’ natural amusements.
Children are always finding out new
truths, and they are continually look-
ing for things that excite their curi-
osity.
	(b) Observe from nature what nat-
urally amuses the children.
	(c) Guide them in their amuse-
ments so that they are wholesome for
character building. In furnishing
recreation we should make an effort to
make home the most attractive place
on earth.
	(d) Children take delight in playing
what their parents are occupied in
doing for a livelihood.
