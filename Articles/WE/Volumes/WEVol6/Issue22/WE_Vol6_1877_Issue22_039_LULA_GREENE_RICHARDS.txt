	LULA GREENE RICHARDS.


How a dyspeptic was cured.—"I was turned off
by the doctors as incurable, but one was kind
enough to give me some good advice, and it, to-
gether with my own judgment made me a well
man. That was fifteen years ago, and I am well
yet. The rule I followed was to avoid entirely
pork and lard, and anything cooked or fried with
it; also hard-boiled eggs, doughnuts, mince pies,
or any kind of pie, tarts, or pastry; also white
wheat flour, no matter how cooked tobacco, liquor,
molasses of all kinds, corn bread, pickles, and all
kinds of nuts. I lived on the following: Sound,
unbolted wheat flour, stirred into boiling milk,
and thickened to suit the taste, and eat with fresh
butter and white sugar, or the same made into
light bread, twelve hours old at least; cracked
wheat, boiled or roasted potatoes, warm, never
cold, fruit of all kinds, raw or cooked, soft-boiled
eggs, broiled and boiled beef and mutton, rice, rye,
honnny, with moderate use of vegetables, beets
and cabbage very moderate. Eat no cucumbers,
and if you need acid, use lemon juice, or unsweet-
ened lemonade. Have your meals regularly, and
eat nothing between meals. Stick to this twelve
or eighteen months, and you will be a well man.
—New York Ledger.
