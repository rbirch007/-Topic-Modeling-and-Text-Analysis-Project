THE SPRING OF 1896.
WHEN it is evening, ye say: It will be fair weather
	For the sky is red,
And in the morning, it will be foul weather
	Today, for the sky is red and lowering.

March did not come in like a gentle lamb;
	And it left like a roaring lion;
But we expected storms for ’tis well known
	March is not a month to rely on,

But we thought perchance that April,
	The month for shine and showers,
Would gladden earth with warmth enough
	To bring forth waiting flowers.

But we never thought that May time
	Would bring us frost and snow;
For surely if we harvest
	We must have time to sow.

The sun has gone down in splendor,
	The morning brought forth rain,
The clouds have hung low in the morning,
	Ere night we had sunshine again.

The bow of the moon would hold the horn, 
	Yet sunshine has come with the rain;
So what e’er our faith in by-gone years
	We cannot trust the moon again.

The Snipe’s sad cry more rain, more rain
	Has been answered with Meadowlarks thrill;
And in spite of frost, hail and snow,
	The grass and wee blossoms still

Have been growing; and green is showing
	All through the furrowed field:
And yester the bow of promise
	Gave hope for a harvest yield.

We had better be up and doing;
	“Improve the shining hours”
If God wills that we must have rain
	We should work between the showers.
						M. E. T. Griffeth. 
Fairview, Idaho.
