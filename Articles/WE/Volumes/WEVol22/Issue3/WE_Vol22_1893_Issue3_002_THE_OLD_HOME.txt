[WomansExponent | Vol22 | 1893 | August | Issue: 3 | Title: THE OLD HOME. | Author: E. L. G]

THE OLD HOME.

THE gray storm-beaten farm house,
	At the top of the wonderful hill;
And the great barns on beyond it;
	Are standing there cold and still.

The house is quaint and rambling,
	The window panes many and small;
Its color is faded to ashen, 
	From the once pure white of its wall.

The sitting room floor so uneven,
	Is the same as ever today;
It came from a sunken rafter,
	And sloped from the centre each way.

But the low rocking chair of mother’s. 
	Just fitted the slope of the floor;
And father’s arm chair in the centre,
	Stood happily facing the door.

The dear little white-walled chamber,
	Just off from the family room,
Is hallowed by sweeter mem’ries,
	Than any about the home.

For there so gentle and patient,
	My mother in sickness hath lain,
While we watched and waited beside her,
	Through hours of anguish and pain.

And there those dread days in winter,
	Came grim and unbidden death,
And from father, mother and brother,
	Took the feeble, fluttering breath.

The cool, old airy kitchen,
	With its white-sand polished floor,
The little old-fashioned parlor,
	With the obstinate creaking door.

The steep and walled-in stair-way,
	The tiny room beyond,
With its bed and home-made covers,
	By angel hands made fond. 

The well with its oaken bucket, 
	The spring at the foot of the hill,
With the buttercup strewn meadow,
	Delight my vision still.

And the sun that shines upon them,
	Is a brighter fairer sun,
Than shines on other meadows, 
	Though I viewed them one by one.
 
The walls have a subtle perfume,
	And a smile of welcome home, 
They seem to lean and beckon,
	To one who used to roam.


The cellar, the shed and the pantry,
	Have none of the dingy gloom,
Which lingers in other houses,
	And in many a rich-walled room. 

Oh hallowed home of childhood!
	I cannot see thee now!
But the home of father and mother,
	Is a shrine at which I bow.
							E. L. G.
