THREE CHIPPIES.

I walked in a shady, cool garden,
	One lovely blue day in June,
When all the wild birds were a-warble, 
	Each piping his favorite tune. 
The robin, sang love to his lady,
	The bobolink, “Beautiful Day;”
But I listened, smiling sedately.
	To a wee one who only could say—
“Three chippies! three chippies! three chippies!”

Now what can be meant by "Three chippies"
	In birdland's melodious tongue?
I have it! He means his three children
	Soft cradled the green leaves among.
And these must be wonderful, truly,
	He sings so triumphant a strain;
Dame Nature’s high masterpiece surely,
	Hark! over and over again—
"Three chippies! three chippies! three chippies!"

Dear birdie, I murmured, partaking
	His gushing, exuberant joy,
I wish you delight in your darlings,
	Two lovely wee girls, and a boy.
He paused not to hear the concluding,
	For compliments, cared not a pin;
And, even my glances eluding,
	Again poured his rapturous din—
"Three chippies! three chippies! three chippies!"

Offended, and thinking to pique him
	By feigning to doubt, don’t you see?
I said, no, I’ll never believe it
	Unless you will show them to me;
For often, who boasts all the loudest,
	Has least to establish his claim;
And he who walks highest and proudest—
	With a gush like a laugh he proclaimed
"Three chippies! three chippies! three chippies!"

Oh, wisest philosopher living,
	That never will argue in vain,
Nor can be entrapped into giving
	An enemy one point of gain.
For neither believing nor doubting
	Can alter one fact, high or low.
Let scoffers grow weary with flouting,
	I'll still know, whatever I know,
"Three chippies! three chippies! three chippies!"
							LU DALTON.
