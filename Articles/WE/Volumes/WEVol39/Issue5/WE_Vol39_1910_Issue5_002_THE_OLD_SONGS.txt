THE OLD SONGS.
	[In reply to the question, “Do you sing those
sweet old songs now you sung in days “Lang
Syne?”]
	Nay, I canna’ sing the old songs,
		You loved so well to hear;
	Sung oft in happy days how gone,
		When love and life were dear.
	Those charming days of “Auld Lang Syne.”
		Woven with sunshine round,—
	Adown the corridors of time,
		Now but in fancy found.
	Nay, I canna’ sing the old songs,
		There comes a rush of tears,
	And my voice fails to reach the notes
		Soar’d o’er in bygone years.
	I may not touch a broken lute,
		Nor strike a jangled string,
	Nor strive with tones forever mute,
		Those dear old songs to sing.
	My harp is on the willows hung;
		Its strains of music o’er,
	Those dear old songs remain unsung,
		That oft thrilled hearts of yore.
	“Sweet highland Mary”—“Bonnie doon,”—
		“Ben Bolt”—and Nelly Bly,”—
	“Raking the hay in merrie June,”—
		And “Comin’ thro’ the rye.”
	“O thre’s ne’er made sic’ anither,”—
		“Annie Laurie,”—“Ivy green,”—
	“Sweet Home”—“The last rose of sum-
			mer”—
		“Bonnie blue-eyed Kathleen,”—
	“Kate Mavourneen,”—“Rory o’Moore,”—
		“Jessie flower o’ Dumblane,”—
	“Young Mary across the moor,”—
		“When shall we meet again,”—
	“Old oaken bucket,”—“Lochinvar,”—
		“Bide ye darlent, wi’ me,”—
	“Pledged to union in peace or war,”—
		“Land of the brave and free,”—
	*“Now launch the boat upon the wave,”—
		“Do they miss me at home?”—
	“Here’s health bonnie Scotland to thee,”—
		“Westward our footsteps roam.”
	These are some of the old songs,
		Twined in a wreath for you;
	Souvenir of days “Lang Syne,”
		With life’s bright roseate hue.
	They cheer my heart like summer ram,
		As I their names recall,
	I seem to hear each echoed strain,-
		Now blending with them all.
					—L. M. Hewlings.
	Footnote.—This poem is re-published from
Volume 25 of the Woman’s Exponent, No. 20,
April 15, 1897. The writer is long since dead,
but her writings still live and her name is thus
perpetuated.—E. B. W.

	*Greek Emigrants’ Song.
