[WomansExponent | Vol23 | 1894 | April | Issue: 18 | Title: HUNTING A HAT. | Author: AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON]

HUNTING A HAT.

"WHEN did I last buy me a hat?
	I really don’t remember
Whether ’twas some spring conference
 	Or sometime in December. 
You cannot judge by seeing it,
	What might have been the season,
And as for naming, just the style
	’Twould quite bewilder reason.

You see my dear, your ways and mine
	In special matters vary;
You choose and order something fine,
	While I must be so wary;
The first thought must be just how much,
	No—just how little, may be
Taken from all the endless needs
	Of household down to baby.

And that is just the first of it
	Not yet the worst point either,
Two solemn truths present themselves
	And I can hide from neither.
When taste and judgment disagree,
	The task’s not an ideal
Delightful one for me—as you—
	But most severely real.

With superstitious dread I view
 	A thing all black and sober,
No matter if I was born in
	The late month of October.
Nor do I want my hat to bear
	Art’s tinsel grapes and berries,
Or like Italian vender’s tray
	Piled up with flowers and cherries.

If I could reach my hand and make
	Just my own free selection,
From that felicity would turn
	A mental introspection; 
And I would ask myself: ‘‘well now,
	After all your endeavor
And getting what you liked the best
	Don’t you look worse than ever?”

For weary face and faded hair
	Fair milliner, I pray you,
Evoke such colors, style and grace
	As shall beloved make you.
No—that won’t do—’tis well enough,
	’Tis charming when you wear it,
It makes you fairer, younger still,
	But I—I would not dare it.

Is there not something that won’t show
	The contrast all too strongly,
As mist enwraps and makes more fair
	Some scene, nor doeth wrongly?
O, cheek and eyes and smile of youth,
	I read without your speaking:

[Column 2]
“It is not here, has passed away
	Years back, beyond your seeking.”

For bloom of health and hope of youth
	Graced e’en the simplest dressing;
What now in place of childhood’s wreath
	And parents’ fond carressing?
What do we win for what we lose
	While we are growing older,
Have we soul-jewels for bright cheeks,
	Has life grown bright, or colder?

But I forget—I take your time;
	Why should I still look vainly,
For your fair face beside my own
	In mirror answers plainly;
There is no use to argue with
	Facts all too plain and truthful,
Your dainty goods and all their grace
	Belong to faces youthful.

Just pick me out the plainest hat—
	I think at last we’ve found it;
As kindly moss, hides storm-scarred bough
	Just wreathe a veil around it,
And have it large enough, my dear,
	My face to shield and cover
So I might pass unknown beside
	My truest old-time lover.

Can’t you slip in a little tint
	To light it just the slightest,
Some where just as through clouds at night
	One star shines out the brightest?
Or, as in ashes that seem dead
	There hideth still the embers,
That wake to touch like some past joy,
	The heart oftimes remembers?

Ah, lovely milliner, how well
	You know your task and do it,
They could not tell as swift I pass,
	Who sees so plainly through it.
Bright be thy life as in thy smile
	And blessings rest upon it,
And chase forever far ahead
	As I could wish in sonnet—
The time, when you no more can wear
	The loveliest new bonnet.
			AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON.
Bountiful, April, 1895.
