[WomansExponent | Vol27 | 1898 | April | Issue: 21 | Title: OUR OLD FOLKS. | Author: Unknown]

	OUR OLD FOLKS.
No matter which way I look today,
There are heads of white or of silver gray;
There are shoulders bowed and bent;
There are hardened hands that tremble so;
There are feet that falter, so weak and slow;—
Sweet graces which Time has lent—
There are furrowed brows, and eyes grown
	dim,
As from trouble and toil and tears;
There are hollow cheeks and quivering lips,
That have followed the flight of years.
No matter where I turn today,
There are voices near me that seem to say,
  “Oh, what beauty mine eyes behold
In the good and noble who mingle here
To partake of the love, to feel the cheer—
Those beautiful souls grown old—
Grown old neath their burdens of toil and
	care,
Grown old in the school of love,
Grown old with the griefs that have been their
	share;
Grown dear to their God above.”

Each bending form doth a story tell,
'Tis old but true—you may read it well—
The tale of paternal care:
“To provide a home for my girls and boys,
To relieve their wants, to increase their joys,
And to give to each his share
Of happy days and of pleasant hours,
To make his pathway bright,
I have borne the heavy weight of toil,
From early morn till night.”

Those trembling, hardened, sunbrowned
	hands
Have scattered blessings in many lands,
And in many a cheerless cot;
They have sown the seed, they have held the
	plow,
Have tenderly pressed the fevered brow;
Have gifts to the needy brought;
They have deftly fashioned the beautiful
	robes,
Which their sons and daughters wear;
They have lifted the curse from a barren land.
And have made it good and fair.

Those feet that travel so very slow,
And best when they have not far to go,
Once moved with an easy grace.
And they went on errands of love and cheer,
On missions of mercy far and near,
Scarce seeking a resting place,
And no path was too rough, no road too 
	long,
For those willing feet to try,

[Column 2]
Not e’en when it led o’er trackless plains,
So dreary, parched and dry.

Those eyes now dim were once so bright,
They beamed with joy and love and light;
But the passing tide of years
Has found them perusing the gospel plan, 
Or seeking the good in erring man, 
Has found them dimmed with tears; 
Has found them keeping their nightly watch
By some poor sufferer’s bed—
What wonder if they are dim today,
If the luster and light have fled!

Those quivering lips have made our heaven,
Have chided wrong, have counsel given,
Have soothed life’s storms away;
They have told of faith and of holy love.
Of a death below, of a life above;
They have taught our lips to pray;
They have sung the Great Redeemer’s praise
In accents clear and mild;
Have led the wayward sinner home
From haunts by sin defiled.

The furrowed brow, the silvered hair, 
The cheek grown hollow with toil and care
Are tokens of honor due—
Due from their pretty, red-cheeked girls,
With dainty gowns and glossy curls,
And from budding manhood, too.
Then give them a smile as you pass them by,
And bid them a kind good-day;
And be ready to lend a helping hand
To assist them along their way.

And for our dear fathers and mothers true,
May the way grow brighter the whole day
	through,
And the cross less hard to bear.
We know the path has been often drear,
With doubt and danger lurking near,
With heartfelt griefs to share;
But the light that shines from our Father’s,
	throne
E’en now you can dimly see—
In that beautiful home, oh, how full your joy,
How complete shall your glory be!
								ALOFA.
	Written for the old folks’ entertainment at
Fairview Feb. 22, 1899.
