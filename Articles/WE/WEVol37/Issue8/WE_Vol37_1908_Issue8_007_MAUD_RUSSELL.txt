INTERNATIONAL COUNCIL QUINQUEN-
				NIAL.
	A notable gathering of women from all
over the world to be held in Toronto, Onta-
rio, June 15-29, 1909. The National Council
of Women of the United States one of the
twenty-three councils represented. Her Ex-
cellency, the Countess of Aberdeen, wife of
the Viceroy of Ireland, will be present.
	The women of America are now looking
forward to the coming of the Quinquennial
Session of the International Council of
Women, to be held in Toronto, Ontario,
Canada, June 15-29, of this year.
	Representative women from eyery part of
the world will be present at this session,
which will be one of the greatest gatherings
of women ever held on the Western Conti-
nent. These women are familiar with the
needs of their respective country, and can
give a breadth and outlook, attainable in
no other manner, thus, the immense value
of such a gathering may be readily seen.
	It is specifically understood that the Inter-
national Council is organized in the interest
of no one propaganda, and has no power
over its members beyond that of suggestion
and sympathy. Its purpose is to provide a
means of communication between women’s
organizations in all countries; also to provide
opportunities for women to meet together
from all parts of the world, to confer upon
questions relative to the welfare of the com-
monwealth, the family and the individual.
	Like most great movements, the Inter-
national Council of Women has been a de-
velopment. Its formation is due to Mrs.
Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Miss Susan B.

[Column 2]
Anthony, American women, who, after con-
sultation with friends in England and France,
decided to convene a representative as-
sembly of delegates from as many countries
as possible, this assembly to be held in
Washington, in 1888, to celebrate the for-
tieth anniversary of the “Woman’s Rights”
movement, and to consider the possibility
of organizing International and National
Councils of Women. As a result the Inter-
national Council was organized with Mrs.
Millicent Garrett Fawcett of England as its
first president, Miss Clara Barton, of the Red
Cross Society, vice-president, and Mrs.
Rachel Foster-Avery as corresponding Secre-
tary.
	At the same time, the National Council
of Women of the United States was formed,
with the late Miss Frances Willard as its
president.
	National Councils of Women have been
formed in the United States, Canada, Ger-
many, Sweden, Great Britain and Ireland,
New Zealand, New South Wales, Italy;
France, Holland, Denmark, Austria, Switzer-
land, Netherlands, Tasmania, Argentine,
Victoria, South Australia, Hungary, Nor-
way and Queensland.

	The president and nine delegates from
each Nationai Council federated with the
International Council of Women, together
with the general officers and conveners of
standing committees, have the right to vote
at the Quinquennial Sessions.
	The President from the United States
Council is Mrs. Mary Wood Swift, of Ber-
kely, California; the Corresponding Secre-

[Column 3]
tary is Mrs. Lillian M. Hollister, of Detroit,
Michigan, and the Treasurer, Mrs. H. Josie
Nelson, Union City, Indiana.
	Speakers of National reputation will be
heard at the various meetings, all of whom
will assist in making known to the general
public the magnitude and variety of woman’s
work for humanity.
	The International Council of Women has
for its President, Her Excellency, The Coun-
tess of Aberdeen, wife of the Viceroy of Ire-
land. The Countess of Aberdeen is noted
the world over for her philanthropy and for
her efforts to better the condition of women
less fortunately situated. Believing that all
children, no matter what their station in
life, should be taught some useful occupa-
tion, her son. Lord Haddo, was brought up
a practical farmer, while her daughters are
skilful housekeepers who can dispense with
their servants if need be, or face the rough
life of a colonist without any sense of griev-
ance. Although modest and unassuming,
her royal position is such that she travels in
state second only to that of King Edward
himself. Lady Aberdeen is at present per-
sonally engaged in a crusade against tuber-
culosis in Ireland.
	The Corresponding Secretary of the Inter-
national Council of Women is Mrs. Maria
M. Ogilvie Gordon, D. Sc., Ph. D., F. L. S.,
of Aberdeen, Scotland. Mrs. Gordon, who
is a cousin of Countess Aberdeen, was the
first woman to present an original work on
any natural science subject for the degree of
D. Sc. in London, and was also the first
woman to enter for the degree of Ph. D. at
Munich, which she won with the highest
honors. Mrs. Gordon has written several
books on science, and was among the first
group of scientific women admitted as Fel-
lows of the Linnaean Society of London.
	Mrs. Elizabeth Sanford, a woman of pro-
minence in Canada, is the Treasurer; Mrs.
Sanford has traveled extensively in the in-
terests of the Council of Women.
	Any person, whose name is accepted by
the Executive Committee and approved by
the Council of his own Nation where one
exists, may become a life patron of this
Council upon the payment of $200; or may
become a quinquennial contributor upon the
payment of $25.00 within the quinquennial
period.
	Every member of every society, belonging
to a federated National Council is a member
of the International Council, and can attend
and take part in the discussion at its meet-
ings.
	The influence of the International Council
can at present be best traced through the
results of the work of the National Councils,
which it has been the means of forming.
	These Councils can show proof of having
been instrumental in breaking down preju-
dices, and in promoting mutual understand-
ing and charity between adherents of es-
sentially different faiths, races, parties, and
also providing a pivot round which all who
desire to labor for the good of humanity,
may unite for the common cause; they have
also collected and spread correct information
concerning woman’s work, and created a
center which will prove a rallying point for
women, who, in all countries, desire to live
for others and leave the world better than
they found it.
