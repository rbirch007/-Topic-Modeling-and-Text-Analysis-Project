	A CHRISTMAS QUEST. 
			A Wish.
				I.
Be merry and glad this Christmas tide,
And Christmas cheer, may it be world-wide!
			Greeting.
				I.
Far from the Christmas chimes of lofty church, 
And glow of Christmas candles flick’ring
	bright,
I, shelter’d ’neath my dizzy mountain porch, 
Far up the rock-ribbed, solitary height, 
Greet ye, who light the candles, Christmas
	Night.
				II.
Safe-cabin’d, marshall’d round with pine and 
fir, 
Agreen ’neath fleecy drapes of light 
And bare-limb’d quaking aspens white—
Bare maples—bushes bare,—
Bend ’neath the weight of winter—
But winter’s wither’d leaves no longer wear.
				III.
I, never bold, in truth a coward I, 
Though all alone in this wild home 
Send through my fire-lit pane a signal light, 
Greeting for a prosp’rous New Year with 
Christmas love bedight, 
A star to glimmer ’cross the hills, 
A star to watchers in the hollow. 
A star no lumb’ring foot may follow, 
For snow on snow hath locked in frost 
Each living thing! The Earth itself seems lost.
				IV.
E’en the wild willow fring’d creek,
From where it makes its mountain leap
In vain I seek; 
No track zigzags ’tween oaks athwart the steep 
And odd quaint line of purpling birch, 
And e’en the brave snow birds desert their
	perch.

[Column 2]
				V.
Above yon rythmic hills’ snow-quilted beds 
Proud wearers of the ermine lift their heads; 
Above, below, each way the same—
Picture on picture fills my window pane. 
A picture new to make I need but move, 
And need not move to look forth on a world 
of love. 
			A Quest. 
				I. 
A recluse I, in nature’s hermitage,
For lo these years, 
In quest of life and its divinest fluid 
Youth, hath built a temple in “The Groves.”
	A Druid, 
Lo, in centuries far back in Lethe’s stream,
Worship’d among the stones and trees. 
I wonder if he, too, did dream?
				II.
A wand’rer I, among the wilds 
Of lonely hills, 
Always my quest alone I take 
For her dear sake. 
(Unlike the Spanish cavalier who sought 
Perpetual youth and found it not), 
For lo, in truth I have it found, 
My impulse quicken’d, my slow step renew’d,
I find it in the lark’s first song at morn, 
And where the restless ancient lake is born 
Amid the ferns—the maidenhair and mosses, 
Where dripping water moldeth a cool cup 
For him who climbs and crosses 
The divide, or him who seeks 
To reach the unseal’d peaks
And looks into the heavens,
And back to nature turns for drink.
				III.
Yea, I have found it ’mong the roots of the 
	young cedar, 
The hemlock, fir, mahogany and ash, 
That I for my retreat do gather 
To there transplant, and in the crash 
Of ancient trees that feed my winter fire.
				IV.
It is yonder ’mong the highest sandstone rocks, 
So hath withstood the quaking of ages’ shocks,
For there the vision flieth o’er chain on chain
To where the Inland Sea frets the white salted
	plain.
				V.
I know it lingers where I gather 
Grass and moss and all that’s fleeting,
When the cold comes creeping, stealing 
Berries bright and leaves that wither, 
But retain some tint, some feeling, 
Some suggestion of the Harvest, 
Just a thought, a hope of Summer.
				VI.
I find it in my bearskin coat and rug, 
High shoes and cap from mountain lion’s hide,
Fashioned with wit; 
I find it in the fire beside which now I sit 
And laugh at cold, 
In the pine nuts popping in the ashes, 
While pine burrs from the fire flashes,
A haunch of red-fleshed venison 
Drips, sizzling, o’er the iron pan, 
Beside the smoking mug!
			Alone.
				I.
But banished. They could not share 
My pilgrimage, a danger unto them, 
So must I bear this quest of life alone. 
I wonder do they live, I know they think of me, 
If yet they do abide at the old home. 
But I must tear my thoughts away from there, 
And dry these tears 
All else were madness. 
I must yet abide a few more years, 
Ere I may seek them who now wait my com-
	ing.
[Column 3]
				II.
I wonder if he doth repent the wrong.
I wonder if that sneering ape—ah! hush!
’Tis Christmas night—the hour grows late, 
I must look out: My fate
Is there in the rising wind. I hear 
My fire-lit pane the cold drifts brush. 
Fresh, down the lonely mountain, deer trails 
Show dimly through light scint’ling snow veils.
				III.
Ah, thou art safe; my snow shoes could not
	track
The young doe from her proud mate tonight. 
But on the wind a song floats back. 
Rejoice! lift up thine eyes! 
Look through the veils into the skies, 
In these snow veils, and fresh snow trails 
Shroud ye thy sadness, 
Thy snow shoes give to the Spirit of Gladness, 
And hope let wing to the peaks its flight 
But bury thy sorrow and woe tonight! 
		A Christmas Grave. 
				I. 
Bury them low 
In a cold grave of snow, 
For there’s aye good luck in a Christmas
	white.
Then hail Good Luck this Christmas night!!
				II.
Bury so low 
All thy sorrow and woe, 
Bury thy woe where the wild winds mow
O’er chilly fields of shivering snow, 
Icy, fleecy, shimmering stars
’Neath the palest moonbeams bars, 
Whirling they go,
Swirling aglow, 
Never to linger, 
Forever to wander, 
Never to grow—
E’en in Spring not to grow–
This day Christmas love only may grow
				III.
Bury so low 
All thy trouble and woe; 
Bury them low, 
With the sneers, 
With the jeers, 
And all the dark fears 
Of thy past fleeting years, 
Of the fast fleeting years; 
Ay, with the tears, 
The heartaches,
The heartbreaks, 
The sears, 
Hiding deep wounds; 
Make these snow drifts, 
These cold rifts 
Their biers.
For the North Wind can chill, 
The Ice King can kill 
Thy troubles, thy woe, 
This glad Christmas, I trow.
				IV.
Yes, lay them low, 
All thine anguish and woe; 
Forget thou hadst ever a foe; 
Forget that he ever was faithless; construe 
The coward as one who was weak, not untrue, 
But think on the one who has always loved you 
Aye foster the truth that is new!
				V. 
Bury the woe, 
Thine anguish lay low, 
Lay it low, make a head-cross of snow; 
Icy graves of white strow 
Deck with colors that glow: 
With the roseberries red, 
Christmas holly-beads, spread 
With green ivy 
And wreaths of white mistletoe.
Kneeling,
Sighing
A requiem low,
O’er the new grave of thy sorrow and woe. 
From its ashes, thou, mortal must grow, 
And only from ashes can the soul ever grow.


The Star.
		“Lo, I am with you always.”
				I.
From ages afar,
A mystical star
Lights my house,
Steers my course.
As the star-guided wise men
Laid gifts in the manger
Have I seen a star,
And my gift would bestow.
And lo, hear the choir,
That sang, to the Shepherds
In dark Bethlehem,
Singing, “Glory to God
And Peace to ye men!
				II.
“Lo, the crucified Christ,
Crucified not in vain,
The Redeemer, the Savior
Whose message hath glimmer'd,
Whose radiance hath shimmer’d
Adown the long chain
Of time,
That ye hear in the carol
And in the church chime,
Bringing peace to ye men.
Him receive ye again.”
			The Promise.
				I.
And far o’er the chorus of angels
My Savior
I hear:
“Lo! on the wing
Of love
I healing bring
To thee.
Be thou made whole.
Thy soul
Is known to me;
Thy desires
I understand,
Thou art no stranger,
My younger brother,
Call thou my name,
And I shall answer.
This key
I give to thee
To be thine forevermore.
A cross I bore
Greater than thou doth bear;
Yea, through Death’s portal
My Father sent me,
To sorrow lent me,
That thou mightst die to live
Immortal.”
Then, this glad Christmas time,
Lo! I open my soul
To enshrine
The DIVINE!!!
				—Alice Merrill Horne.
