with  Brother  Williams,  and,  finally,

"Thank  goodness,  Annie,  you've 
all  your  linens  and  a  proper-sized 
hope  chest."  Mrs.  Griffiths  sighed. 
"You're  so  glad  I'm  going  to  be 
using  it  at  last,"  teased  Annie. 
"I  am,"  said  her  mother  stoutly, 
and  then  a  cloud  passed  over  her 
face.  "I  am,  and  then  I'm  not,  if 
you  know  what  I  mean." 
"I  do,"  said  Annie  softly.  "I  feel 
that  way  myself  sometimes."  She 
stroked  her  mother's  worn  cheek 
gently.  "We'll  do  lots  of  visiting 
back  and  forth." 
Her  mother  smiled,  "Your  father 
was  saying  a  wedding  was  such  a 
happy  occasion,  it  is  odd  how  there 
can  be  a  drop  of  sadness;  yet  there 
is." 
But  Annie  was  already  talking 
about  her  wedding  dress.  Marie 
had  sent  word  that  Annie  was  to 
use  the  same  dress  that  she  had  been 
married  in.  They  could  save  it  for 
their  children,  because  she  would 
never  have  the  heart  to  let  it  be 
remodeled  into  a  useful  gown. 
"Sisters  often  are  married  in  the 
same  dress,  and  truly  I  couldn't 
have  a  dearer  sister.  My  only  re- 
gret is  that  my  delicate  condition 
won't  let  me  take  any  kind  of  promi- 
nent part  at  your  wedding  recep- 
tion, so  you  must  let  me  enter  in 
the  festivities  by  your  doing  me  the 
kindness  of  wearing  the  dress  that 
brought  me  more  happiness  than  I 
dreamed  possible  in  this  human  life. 
You  have  no  idea  how  much  better 
my  health  has  been  since  dear  little 
Sissy  was  born." 
Annie  was  overjoyed  with  Marie's 
long  letters.  Everyone  was  far  too 
good  to  her,  she  felt. 
Brother  Griffith  delivered  Annie 
and  her  trunk  to  the  Williams  in 
the  early  part  of  June.  Her  mother 
planned  to  come  to  stay  a  few  days 
before  the  wedding,  but  the  Wil- 
liamses  insisted  Annie  come  sooner, 
so  alterations  could  be  made  on 
Marie's  dress. 
Annie  put  on  the  wedding  dress 
while  Aunt  Agnes  and  Mrs.  Hemp 
kneeled  with  their  mouths  filled 
with  pins.  Sister  Williams  could 
not  keep  from  exclaiming  again  and 
again  at  how  lovely  Annie  looked 
in  the  heavy  satin  gown.  The  ma- 
terial took  on  almost  a  white  trans- 
lucent sheen  when  Annie  moved. 
She  caught  her  breath  at  the  sight 
of  the  gown  in  Sister  Williams'

HTHERE  was  not  only  the  wedding 
dress  to  be  fitted,  but  a  gown 
from  the  Williams  was  presented  to 
Annie  as  an  early  wedding  present. 
That,  too,  must  have  the  stiffening 
placed  exactly.  The  crowning  gift 
came  just  a  little  over  a  week  before 
the  wedding,  from  Aunt  Agnes. 
Annie  cried  when  she  saw  it,  but 
Aunt  Agnes  gruffly  told  her,  if  she 
didn't  have  a  right  to  do  a  little 
work  in  secret  on  a  present  for  their 
own  Annie,  she  didn't  know  what 
the  world  was  coming  to.  Annie 
tried  on  the  long  blue  velvet  coat 
with  the  white  fur  at  the  throat  and 
the  matching  velvet  bonnet.  Never 
had  she  dreamed  she  might  look  so 
elegant. 
"You  can  have  one  or  two  things 
almost  as  lovely  as  you,  I  guess," 
said  Aunt  Agnes  with  satisfaction. 
"Parker's  getting  the  prettiest  bride 
in  the  whole  valley,  and  you  might 
as  well  have  a  few  things  to  show 
off  his  prize  with." 
"But  this  is  far  too  much,"  Annie 
protested,  stroking  the  beautiful  vel- 
vet with  her  hand. 
Annie's  mother  came  down  four 
days  before  the  wedding.  She  added 
gifts  of  petticoats  and  long  warm 
stockings  to  the  many  things  Annie 
already  had.  She  exclaimed  unbe- 
lievingly over  the  beautiful  clothes, 
and  admired  the  gifts  that  were 
already  arriving  at  the  Williams' 
house  in  advance  of  the  reception. 
Parker  and  Annie  took  her  moth- 
er and  father  to  see  the  house  that 
the  Josephsons  and  Parker  had  built 
several  blocks  east  of  his  father's. 
The  wide,  light-colored  two-story 
house  seemed  unreal  to  Annie,  sit- 
ting in  the  buggy  staring  at  its 
gleaming     windows. 
"It's  a  lovely  house,  Annie,"  her 
father  said,  "and  if  there's  half  the 
love  in  there  that  your  mother 
brought  to  our  house,  you'll  always 
have  a  home  you  can  call  blessed." 
Annie  felt  very  humble  as  she 
laid  her  head  on  her  father's  shoul- 
der. The  dresses,  the  house,  per- 
haps they  were  a  dream,  a  temporal 
inconsequentiality.  What  was  im- 
portant was  the  love  she  and  Parker 
had.  She  sat  upright  with  her  back 
stiff  against  the  buggy  seat. 
"I  promise  I'll  remember  what  is 
really  important,  Papa,  all  my  life." 
OARKER  stopped  by  to  help 
Annie  open  more  of  the  gifts 
that  arrived  at  the  Williams.  He 
was  pleased  that  Brother  and  Sister 
Griffith  had  liked  the  house,  he  told 
Annie. 
Annie  looked  at  him  thought- 
fully. "How  could  anyone  help  but 
like  such  a  beautiful  home?" 
Parker  stroked  his  hand  across 
the  dark  wing  of  her  hair.  "Of 
course,  we  don't  have  much  furni- 
ture yet.  I'm  afraid  it  will  seem 
pretty  bare  for  awhile.  After  I  be- 
gin teaching,  we'll  be  able  to  do 
more  about  .  .  .  ." 
"Oh,  Parker,"  Annie  cried,  "don't 
you  realize  that  it  could  be  a  tepee 
or  a  dirt  dugout,  and  it  would  still 
be  the  most  wonderful  place  in  the 
world  as  long  as  you  were  there." 
Parker's  eyes  darkened,  and  he 
moved  to  take  Annie  in  his  arms,

Parker  turned  to  her  in  exaspera-

"Am  I  the  luckiest  man  in  the 
world  or  not,  Aunt  Agnes?"  He 
put  an  arm  around  her  waist  and 
waltzed  her  around. 
Aunt  Agnes  came  down  on  her 
heels  in  a  determined  hurry  as  soon 
as  she  caught  her  balance. 
"You're  both  lucky,  but  you're 
the  kind  who  make  your  own  luck 
with  the  good  Lord's  blessings." 
Parker  looked  at  her  seriously. 
"I'm  thankful  for  my  blessings  and 
humbled  by  them.  You  know  that, 
don't  you,  Aunt  Agnes?" 
"I  do."  The  elderly  lady  pulled 
his  cheek  to  hers  and  kissed  him. 
"I  used  to  do  that  more  often  when 
you  were  a  little  shaver  skinning 
your  knees  climbing  trees.  And  I 
expect  I'll  be  doing  it  to  your  chil- 
dren." 
'TTiE  twenty-first  of  June  was 
warm  and  sunny. 
"We  have  a  beautiful  day,"  Sister 
Griffith  smiled  at  Marie  in  the  hall. 
Sissy  was  dancing  around  her 
grandmother's  room  with  her  curls 
bouncing  with  golden  sunlight. 
"She's  so  like  Marie."  Sister  Wil- 
liams beamed  at  Sissy  as  she  caught 
her  hand  to  take  her  down  to  break- 
fast. 
Annie  stood  looking  out  of  the 
window  of  her  bedroom.  Her  dark 
hair  tumbled  down  her  back.  Her 
white  nightgown  was  caught  in  ruf- 
fles at  the  wrist.  She  must  start 
getting  ready.  Yet  she  knew  if  she 
made  a  sound,  her  room  would  be 
filled  with  women  helping  her  dress, 
urging  her  to  eat.  Surely,  she  had 
just  a  moment  left.  She  could  see 
the  Josephsons'  yard.  Was  Parker 
still  asleep?  What  was  he  think- 
ing about  this  morning?  After  to- 
day no  longer  would  she  be  Annie 
Griffith.  How  much  of  her  iden- 
tity besides  her  name  would  she 
lose?  She  leaned  her  cheek  against 
the  warm  window  pane.  Of  what 
does  a  person  think  on  this  morning 
of  all  mornings?  Was  she  feeling 
a  little  sad?  Dismayed?  She  wait- 
ed for  the  happiness  of  the  past 
months  to  come  tumbling  in  upon 
her.    She  felt  nothing  for  a  moment. 
Then  the  back  door  of  the 
Josephson  house  opened.  Parker 
was  out  walking  in  the  yard.  She 
leaned  her  head  again  to  the  win- 
dow. He  was  fully  dressed  in  his 
good  dark  suit.  She  should  not  be 
looking  at  him  like  this.  She  drew 
back  a  little  from  the  window.  But 
just  then  Ruff,  his  dog,  came  bound- 
ing around  the  house.  He  leaped 
eagerly  against  Parker,  and  there 
was  Parker  going  down  on  one  knee 
to  stroke  Ruff.  She  saw  him  bury 
his  face  against  the  dog's  soft  fur. 
"Oh,  Parker,  Parker."  She  shook 
her  head,  smiling  through  her  tears. 
How  could  she  ever  make  up  for 
all  that  was  so  dear  in  Parker  that 
was  to  be  hers?  She  must  dress 
quickly.  Parker  was  to  be  with  her 
for  all  time  and  eternity,  and  she 
had  no  extra  time  to  be  wasted.
