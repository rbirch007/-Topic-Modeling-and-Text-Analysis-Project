The  littlest  one,  who  never  quite  gives  up  motion, 
After  awhile  squirms  out  of  her  tucked-in  blanket 
Into  the  cold  uncover  of  night's  dark  stream; 
Then,  being  chilled,  whimpers  and  stirs  position 
Till  she  is  enough  aware,  enough  awakened 
To  come  small-mouse-creeping  into  the  parents'  room. 
Softly  she  clambers  over  their  lift-and-lower 
Of  breathing,  over  bone  and  the  yielding  ridges, 
Till  she  finds  the  narrow  valley  they  hold  between; 
There  she  hunches,  tight-curled  as  bud  of  flower — 
Yet,  seeking  still  with  stretched  foot  or  elbow,  reaches 
For  something  more  than  mere  hollow  on  a  plain. 
Now  one  of  the  sleepers,  disturbed,  draws  her  into  the  quilt-cave; 
And  there,  close  against  the  parent  warmth  soft-rising, 
Slowly  the  small  tight  bud  opens  in  drowsing, 
Having  found,  for  all  hours,  comfort  and  certain  love.

MOST  homestead  shanties 
were  about  the  size  and  shape 
of  a  railroad  box  car,  and  just

single  door,  one  or  two  small  win-

not  one  in  a  hundred  had  felt  the

But  our  homestead  home  —  even 
though  it  conformed  closely  to  the 
general  pattern  —  was  a  home  of 
distinction. 
Our  cabin  had  a  porch/ 
We  always  referred  to  it  as  'The 
Front  Porch"  —  a  high  sounding 
phrase  which  seemed  to  imply  that 
somewhere  in  the  immediate  en- 
virons there  might  be  also  a  back 
porch.  But,  of  back  porches  there 
was  none;  and  even  The  Front 
Porch  was  not  exactly  pretentious. 
Its  roof,  tar-papered  like  the  house, 
ran  the  full  length  of  our  fourteen- 
foot  cabin,  and  was  supported  by 
undressed  two-by-four  studdings;  its 
front  was  enclosed  by  woven  wire 
netting,  stretched  from  floor  to 
roof,  and  its  pine  board  floor  sloped 
a  little  to  the  outer  edge  so  water 
might  drain  off  more  quickly  after 
summer  rains,  or  following  the  vig- 
orous weekly  scrubbings  Mother 
gave  it  with  a  broom. 
After  serving  through  the  winter 
as  a  semi-protected  storage  space  for 
stovewood  cut  in  the  cedar  breaks, 
The  Front  Porch  came  into  its  own 
with  the  first  warm  days  of  early 
summer.  Before  the  end  of  May, 
the   wire   netting   front   would   be
