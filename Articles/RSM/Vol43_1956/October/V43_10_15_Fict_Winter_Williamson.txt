"VTOW  is  the  time  to  think  about  mak- 
-L^  ing  winter  arrangements  and  dried 
bouquets  for  your  home.  Try  to  get  out 
before  the  heavy  rains  begin  and  the  snow 
covers  the  dry  summer  weeds  and  grasses. 
Gather  armfulls  of  materials,  tie  them  in 
bundles,  and  hang  them  from  the  ceiling 
of  your  storeroom  for  future  selecting. 
Look  for  unusual  and  interesting  pieces 
of  dead  wood.  If  you  are  near  the  sea, 
pick  up  driftwood.  If  you  are  not  en- 
tirely inspired  by  nature,  drop  by  the  li- 
brary and  look  through  the  innumerable 
and  beautifully  illustrated  books  on  dried 
flower  and  plant  arrangements.  Some  of 
the  recent  Japanese  arrangement  books  are 
helpful. 
Simplicity  is  the  key  word.  Remember 
this,  and  you  will  select  from  your  har- 
vest a  few  choice  specimens  and  arrange 
them  so  that  they  become  part  of  your 
home  and  your  personality.  You  will  find 
the  effort  to  be  worth  the  field  trips. 
Often  in  your  home  among  your  pos- 
sessions you  will  find  choice  pieces  of 
china,  pottery,  or  glass  which  may  be 
used  as  accessories  with  your  arrange- 
ments. A  tiny  blue  horse  with  a  piece  of 
gray  weathered  driftwood  and  stalks  of 
dried  barley  or  wheat  in  an  old  glass  pitch- 
er are  unusually  attractive.  Try  different 
effects.  The  colors  of  dried  plants,  wood, 
and  grasses  are  neutral,  so  they  will  blend 
harmoniously  with  many  color  schemes. 
Remember,  too,  that  a  few  sprays  of 
wheat  or  grass  are  a  lovely  accent  with 
late  blooming  fall  flowers.
