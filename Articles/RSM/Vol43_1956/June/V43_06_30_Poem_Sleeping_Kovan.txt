"Once  I  spaded  up  a  bed  because  I

Sometimes  we  uproot  our  homes 
and  children  to  get  rid  of  a  weed 
or  two  of  wrong,  that  would  wither 
later,  on  their  own.  What  children 
really  need  more  than  spading,  is 
warm,  loving  sunshine  .  .  .  the  sun- 
shine of  a  mother's  loving  smile.  A 
smile  that  is  as  dependable  as  the 
sun's  rays." 
Wanda  realized,  with  a  pang  of 
consternation,  that  her  children  had 
seen  her  face  torn  with  anger.  No 
wonder  they  had  looked  so  startled. 
She  secretly  vowed  to  try  to  keep  it 
from  happening  again. 
Lilac  dusk  was  creeping  upon 
them,  but  Wanda's  mind  had  al- 
ready taken  up  its  routine.  She 
could  make  spaghetti  and  meat  balls 
for  supper.  The  family  enjoyed  the 
dishes  they  were  used  to  more  than 
something  new  and  fancy,  anyway. 
And  the  water  cress  gift  from  Gran- 
ny Crockett  would  make  a  lovely 
cress  and  hard-boiled  egg  salad, 
which  would  be  a  treat  for  Vince 
and  the  girls  on  a  spring  evening. 
And  she  would  pull  out  a  cherry 
pie  from  the  deep  freeze,  as  a  sort 
of  celebration  of  her  burying  the 
hatchet,  so  to  speak. 
"I  must  hurry,"  Wanda  said.  Her 
face  smiled  like  the  big  white  pan- 
sies which  Granny  Crockett  knew 
would  later  bloom  in  her  garden. 
"And  thanks  .  .  .  thanks  for  every- 
thing!" Wanda  added. 
When  she  walked  into  her  home, 
it  seemed  her  own  little  world  had 
righted  itself.  The  school  clothes 
were  put  away,  the  coats  hung  neat- 
ly in  the  hall,  and  Marie  and  Con- 
nie were  doing  the  dishes. 
"We  were  going  to  surprise  you," 
they  chorused  when  caught  in  the 
act. 
"What  a  happy  surprise!"  Wanda 
smiled  a  pretty  violet-eyed  pansy 
smile  .... 
Granny  Crockett  walked  through 
the  garden  and  over  the  smooth 
green  lawn  which  sloped  to  the  lily 
pond.  A  bluebird  came  flashing 
out  of  nowhere,  and  stopped  short 
on  seeing  her  so  closely,  then  flitted 
away  among  the  peonies  beside  the 
garden  fence.  Granny  smiled,  all 
was  calm  and  smooth  again.  The 
pansy  faces  all  were  smiling  faces.

f^LD  Tucson  isn't  nearly  as  old  as 
it  sounds— or  looks.  Its  crum- 
bling adobe  walls  seem  to  have 
weathered  centuries  of  desert  wind 
and  rain.  Its  warped  lumber  seems 
to  have  been  burned  brown  by  the 
desert  sun  and  sucked  dry  of  all 
moisture.  Its  hitchracks  appear  to 
have  tethered  a  thousand  horses. 
But  all  this  is  make-believe.  Old 
Tucson  was  born  in  1940,  created 
by  Hollywood  for  the  movie  "Ari- 
zona." 
Built  in  the  Tucson  Mountains 
a  few  miles  west  of  Tucson,  Ari- 
zona, the  set  was  constructed  to 
represent  the  old  Spanish  settle- 
ment of  Tucson  during  our  Civil 
War  days.  When  the  film  was 
finished,  the  movie  company  moved 
out  and  the  Junior  Chamber  of 
Commerce  moved  in.  They  kept 
the  set  repaired  as  a  show  place  for 
tourists  and  as  a  location  for  their 
annual  Tucson  Daze  celebration 
held  each  autumn. 
Since  Tucson  is  a  square  dancing 
center  with  a  dozen  or  more  clubs 
meeting  regularly,  the  J.C.'s  inaug- 
urated square  dancing  at  Old  Tuc- 
son. These  weekly  get-togethers 
are  well  attended  by  visitors  and 
natives  alike. 
Since  1940,  the  movie  companies 
have  moved  back  to  Old  Tucson  on 
several  occasions.  In  1954  they 
came  back  for  the  Cinemascope 
production  of  Greer  Garson's 
"Strange  Lady  in  Town."  They 
completely  remodeled  Old  Tucson. 
They  covered  some  of  the  adobe

Grace  Barker  Wilson 
The  shades  are  drawn,  the  doors  are  closed, 
And  all  about  an  empty  look 
Proclaims  a  solitary  house 
That  somebody  forsook. 
Around  the  yard  the  grass  grows  tall, 
And  weeds  crowd  every  flower  bed; 
The  stray  dogs  in  the  neighborhood 
Hold  counsel  there,  instead. 
At  night  the  darkened  windows  have 
A  ghastly,  special  glare,  and  some, 
Both  animals  and  flowers,  wish 
The  owners  would  come  home.
