TT  began  as  a  dream,  or  even  before 
the  dream  took  form,  it  was  a 
longing  deep-planted  in  woman's 
heart,  to  have  a  home,  to  have  one 
spot  designated  particularly  and  set 
apart  around  which,  as  a  warming 
fire,  Relief  Society  workers  might 
gather. 
It  was  an  insistent  urge  com- 
panioning Latter-day  Saint  women 
as  they  were  driven  from  their  own 
hearthsides  again  and  again.  And 
now  at  last  on  the  third  day  of 
October,  one  thousand,  nine  hun- 
dred and  fifty-six,  the  seeding  and 
garnering  are  over,  and  the  harvest 
has  fulfilled  the  dream. 
It  was  the  practice  in  early  days, 
for  the  sisters  to  provide  their  own 
meeting  places  in  the  wards.  In 
1897  President  Zina  D.  H.  Young 
said  "she  was  pleased  with  the  ex- 
ertions of  the  sisters  in  getting 
houses  to  meet  in."  Relief  Society 
Halls — so  named — provided  homes 
for  Relief  Societies  in  metropolitan 
centers,  in  verdant  valleys,  and  in 
bleak,  sparsely  settled  communities. 
The  sisters  had  Relief  Society  homes 
and,  in  addition,  through  obedience 
to  the  voice  of  their  prophets,  they 
had  granaries  guarding  life-sustain- 
ing wheat. 
The  practice  changed.  Relief  So- 
ciety Halls  were  abandoned  as  room 
for  Relief  Society  work  was  pro- 
vided in  meetinghouses.  Individual 
granaries,  not  always  satisfactory 
storage  places,  were   emptied,  and 
later  great  Church  elevators  were 
filled  as  the  result  of  thousands  of 
small  loads  of  Relief  Society  wheat. 
But  while  ward  Relief  Societies 
were  provided  with  meeting  places, 
what  of  the  Central  Board  of  Relief 
Society?  They  had  no  place  of  their 
own  in  which  to  meet;  they  had  to 
borrow  a  meeting  room;  and 
there  was  no  place  for  the  pres- 
ervation of  general  Relief  Society 
records.  And  so  the  dream  of  one 
functional  and  beautiful  home  in 
which  the  work  of  Relief  Society 
throughout  the  world  might  be  con- 
ceived and  from  which  it  might  be 
directed  was  born,  and  is  today  a 
reality.  A  home  of  the  heart  of 
Relief  Society  of  which  every  Relief 
Society  member  is  a  part.  A  home 
containing  the  loveliest  offerings  of 
the  world-wide  sisterhood.  United- 
ly, through  sacrifice,  and  with  the 
assistance  of  the  Church,  The  Re- 
lief Society  Building  stands  an  en- 
sign to  women. 
In  the  present  and  future  years 
this  symbol  of  the  devotion  of  Re- 
lief Society  women  and  of  their 
lofty  destiny  in  the  gospel  plan  will 
speak  to  the  sojourners  from  other 
countries  and  remote  civilizations: 
"We,  the  members  of  Relief  Society 
of  the  restored  Church  of  Christ, 
built  this  home  through  sacrifice 
with  the  help  and  under  the  guid- 
ance of  the  Priesthood  of  God.  No 
other  women  in  all  the  world  are 
blessed  as  are  we.     Relief  Society

came  into  being  through  the  inspira- 
tion of  the  Lord  to  his  Prophet 
Joseph  Smith  in  1842.  Another 
prophet  in  1956  dedicated  this  our 
home  to  the  Lord.  We  are  con- 
tinually guided  and  sustained  by  his 
servants.  This  Building  is  mag- 
nificent, but  its  beauty  has  been 
fashioned  as  that  of  the  virtuous 
woman  of  Solomon's  Proverb.  Those 
who  labor  in  Relief  Society  do  so 
in  a  spirit  of  service,  of  deep  humil- 
ity, thankfulness,  and  dedication. 
We  are  nurtured  through  the  com- 
passion we  extend  to  others.  We 
follow  our  grand  key  words,  "Said 
Jesus,  'Ye  shall  do  the  work  which 
ye  see  me  do/  '  Arrogance  in  pos- 
sessions has  no  place  in  our  home. 
The  offering  reflects  the  refine- 
ment, graciousness,  intelligence,  and 
strength  of  faith  possessed  by  the 
early    sisters,    exemplified    in    Lucy 
Mack  Smith,  by  the  staunch  and 
true  pioneer  member,  and  the  mem- 
ber of  today  and  tomorrow,  the 
bedrock  of  whose  character  has 
remained  the  same  —  selfishness, 
compassion,  charity,  and  love. 
Relief  Society  today  through  hu- 
mility and  service  and  with  the  help 
of  the  Lord,  will  earn  the  blessing 
invoked  for  the  Building  by  Presi- 
dent David  O.  McKay  in  his  glori- 
ous Dedicatory  Prayer  when  he 
asked  "May  the  building  and  sur- 
soundings  radiate  a  hallowed  influ- 
ence even  to  passersby,  as  perfume 
is  wafted  from  sweet-scented  flow- 
ers." 
From  this  spirit  will  blossom  a 
rededication  to  service  for  the  poor 
both  in  spirit  and  means;  for  the 
needy,  for  the  afflicted,  and  sorrow- 
ing. Thus  the  Building  may  and 
must  be  hallowed.        — M.  C.  S.

AGAIN,  at  the  One  Hundred 
Twenty  -  Seventh  Semi  -  Annual 
Conference  of  the  Church,  held  in 
the  historic  Tabernacle  on  Temple 
Square  on  October  5,  6,  and  7, 
1956,  the  saints  received  instruc- 
tion, inspiration,  and  a  renewal  of 
their  faith.  Truth  seekers  the  world 
over  were  invited  to  learn  of  the 
restored  gospel  and  its  eternal  mes- 
sage. 
That  Jesus  is  the  Christ,  the 
power  of  The  Book  of  Mormon  as 
a  witness  for  Jesus  the  Christ,  the 
importance  and  effectiveness  of  hu- 
mility and  faith,  ways  of  perfecting 
the  saints,  reverence  in  houses  of 
worship,  the  Word  of  Wisdom, 
tithing,  chastity,  kindness,  patience, 
charity,  honesty,  peace  for  the  indi- 
vidual and  for  the  world,  and 
obedience  to  all  the  principles  and 
ordinances  of  the  gospel,  were 
teachings  given  special  emphasis. 
President  David  O.  McKay  pre- 
sided and  conducted  all  meetings. 
The  general  sessions  were  broadcast 
by  radio  and  television  over  a  wide

Seventy,  who  was  confined  to  his

TN  his  opening  address,  President 
McKay  reviewed  the  material  prog- 
ress of  the  Church,  the  increase  in 
membership  and  activities,  the 
many  new  chapels,  the  great  ex- 
penditure of  means  and  effort,  all 
directed  towards  "perfecting  the 
soul."  President  McKay  then 
asked:  "Have  we  progressed  spirit- 
ually as  well  as  in  these  physical  and 
teaching  organizations?"  He  defined 
spirituality  as: 
Every  noble  impulse,  every  unselfish  ex- 
pression of  love,  every  brave  suffering  for 
the  right;  every  surrender  of  self  to  some- 
thing higher  than  self;  every  loyalty  to  an 
ideal;  every  unselfish  devotion  to  prin- 
ciple; every  helpfulness  to  humanity; 
every  act  of  self:control;  every  fine  cour- 
age of  the  soul,  undefeated  by  pretense  or 
policy,  but  by  being,  doing,  and  living 
the  good  for  the  very  good's  sake  —  that 
is   spirituality. 
President  Stephen  L  Richards 
spoke  of  the  request  from  "a  distant 
foreign  country"  for  a  statement  of 
the  beliefs  and  purposes  of  the 
Church.  A  reply  was  submitted, 
which  included  a  copy  of  the  thir- 
teen Articles  of  Faith,  and  this  state- 
ment of  the  objectives  of  the 
Church: 
The  objectives  of  the  Church  are  to 
bear  witness  to  the  divinity  of  the  Lord 
Jesus  Christ;  to  teach  all  men  the  prin- 
ciples of  his  Gospel;  and  to  convert  and 
persuade  them  to  follow  in  his  ways  and 
keep  the  commandments  of  God,  that 
they  may  thus  advance  the  Kingdom  of 
God  in  the  earth,  to  bring  brotherhood 
and  peace  to  men  and  nations,  and  earn 
exaltation  for  themselves. 
A  warning  against  the  influence 
of  doctrines  and  cults  not  in  har- 
mony with  the  gospel  of  Jesus 
Christ  was  expressed  by  President 
J.  Reuben  Clark,  Jr.  He  spoke  of 
the  "Olivet  Discourse"  of  the  Savior 
and  its  divine  implications.  Presi- 
dent Clark  summarized  his  testi- 
mony of  the  gospel  in  words  of 
strength  and  simplicity: 
Jesus  did  live.  First,  there  was  the 
great  plan  in  heaven;  that  did  actually 
occur  ....  the  earth  was  formed,  Adam 
came,  the  human  family  followed.  We 
came  here  to  prove  ourselves.  Finally, 
Christ  was  born  in  the  meridian  of  time. 
He  lived.  He  taught.  He  gave  instruc- 
tions. He  was  crucified.  Then  on  the 
morning  of  the  third  day,  he  was  resur- 
rected, thus  bringing  to  each  and  every 
one  of  us  the  blessings  of  the  resurrec- 
tion. 
As  members  of  Relief  Society,  we 
feel  especially  grateful  and  uplifted 
by  the  conference  messages  which 
gave  inspiration  and  direction  for 
us  in  our  homemaking  activities,  in 
our  responsibilities  as  wives  and 
mothers,  and  as  workers  in  the  aux- 
iliaries of  the  Church.  May  we 
manifest  these  blessings  in  our  lives 
and  share  them  with  our  friends 
and  neighbors. 
-V.  P.  C.
