Happenings By Annie Wells Cannon
AUGUST.—Nothing like a good book and a shady nook to drive dull care away.
MISS E. M. DELAFIELD'S new novel, "A Good Man's Love" is a delightful satire on the marriage market.
MRS. FRANCES WINWAR GREBANIERS' "Poor Splendid Wings" a biographical narrative of the Rossettis and their contemporaries of Victorian England won The Atlantic prize of $5,000 for the best non-fiction book of the year.
REBECCA WEST'S "St. Augustine" is one of the most popular of recent biographies.
MARY JOHNSTON'S new novel, "Miss Delicia Allen" is another charming story of Civil War days.
MINISTER RUTH BRYAN OWEN has adopted a very mannish attire for office and street wear.
THE favorite gowns at the fashion show at the Chicago exposition were made of sheer linen and filmy Swiss.
PRINCESS INGRID, of Sweden, made a great hit in "A Queen for a day," a play given for charity. She was charming in a gown which belonged to her ancestor, Josephine Beauharnais, granddaughter of the Empress Josephine.
MLLE. SANDRA RATTI, Italian movie star, had the consent of her uncle Pope Pius to go on the stage providing she assumed a stage name.
DR. MARY E. WOOLLEY has been elected chairman of the department of international justice and good will of the Federal Council of Churches in America.
EDWINA BOOTH, heroine of "Trader Horn" who is seriously ill from exposure to an African sun is a Salt Lake girl.
BILLIE BURKE in promoting and presenting the Ziegfeld Follies has entered a new venture for women.
RACHEL CROTHERS' new play, "When Ladies Meet" was a brilliant success when presented in London.
MISS CELESTE JEDEL was an interesting figure at the London economic conference. She is legal advisor to Prof. Raymond Moley.
SECRETARY FRANCES PERKINS has been awarded the degree of doctor of laws of Goucher College, Baltimore.
ALAMEDA DAY McCLELLAN, Utah's oldest mother, died this summer. She was 101 years old and left a posterity of 439.
THE Church of Scotland in general assembly gave qualified approval to the admission of women to church offices, removing sex barriers so far as elderships are concerned.
MRS. EVALYN WALSH McLEAN, former owner of the Washington Post, is offering for sale the famous Hope diamond worth $250,000 in order to start another newspaper in Washington for her sons.
MRS. LOUISE Y. ROBISON presided at one of the important sessions of the Woman's Congress held in Chicago and was otherwise signally honored.
MRS. FLORENCE G. GREEN, artist and editor, has had a portrait selected for exhibition at the World's Fair.
