THE BUILDING OF THE ORGAN

By Linda S. Fletcher

The organ is a wonderful instrument. It has been called the king of instruments, and rightly so, for it can produce more varied effects than any other musical instrument.

The pipe organ is built on the principle of the human voice. The pipes correspond to the vocal cords, and the wind chest to the lungs. The organist controls the instrument by means of keyboards called manuals, and a pedal keyboard played with the feet.

The pipes are made of metal or wood, and vary in size from a few inches to thirty-two feet in length. The largest pipes produce the lowest tones, and the smallest pipes the highest tones.

The wind is supplied by electric blowers, which force air into the wind chest. From the wind chest the air passes through valves to the pipes when the keys are pressed.

The stops control groups of pipes of similar tone quality. By drawing different combinations of stops, the organist can produce many different tone colors.

Modern organs have from two to five manuals, and from a few hundred to several thousand pipes. The largest organ in the world is in the Convention Hall in Atlantic City, New Jersey. It has seven manuals and over 33,000 pipes.

The organ has a long and interesting history. The first organs were built by the ancient Greeks and Romans. During the Middle Ages, organs were built in the great cathedrals of Europe. The art of organ building reached its highest development in Germany during the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries.

Today, organ building is both an art and a science. The modern organ builder must be skilled in acoustics, mechanics, and electricity, as well as having a thorough knowledge of music.
