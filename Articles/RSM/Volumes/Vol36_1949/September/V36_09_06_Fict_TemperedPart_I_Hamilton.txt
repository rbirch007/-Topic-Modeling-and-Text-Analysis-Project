[Tempered—Part I by Gladys I. Hamilton]

Tempered PART I Gladys I. Hamilton ARNET BLENDALL tried to appear natural as she stood on tiptoe to kiss her husband, Lee, goodbye. Then, quite casually, she plucked a minute speck of lint from her father-in-law's rough whipcord coat. Her blue eyes were sad, and within herself was a cold numbness, overlaid with a sincere regret and an aching weariness.
Only she knew that her kiss was to be a final one as Lee's wife. Her marriage had been a mistake from the beginning, and now she was going to remedy it as quickly and as quietly as possible.

She had stayed long enough to help Lee's mother through the crisis, and she had learned things about herself that she hadn't known before. She had found that she could work hard and like it when it was something that counted. She had found that there was a deep well of tenderness in her for people who needed care. But most important of all, she had found that a marriage based on nothing more than a wild infatuation and a desire to get away from an unpleasant situation at home couldn't work, no matter how you tried to patch it up.

The suitcase she had hidden in the closet was packed and ready. Tomorrow, when Lee went to town for supplies, she would walk to the highway and catch the bus. She had written the letter of explanation and it lay sealed on the bureau. There wasn't anything more to do now except say goodbye to the folks who had been kinder to her than her own family had ever been.

Down in the kitchen, Mother Blendall was putting away the supper dishes. She looked up with a smile as Garnet came in.

"Feeling better now, dear? You looked so tired at supper."

"I'm fine." Garnet's voice was steady. "Mrs. Blendall, I want to thank you for everything. You've been wonderful to me."

"Why, child, you talk as if you were going somewhere." The older woman's hands grew still on the dish towel.

Garnet took a deep breath. "I am going somewhere, Mrs. Blendall. I'm going home tomorrow."

For a moment there was silence in the kitchen. Then Mrs. Blendall said quietly, "I see. Does Lee know?"

"Not yet. I've written him a letter."

"Garnet, my dear, don't you think you should talk to him first? Marriage isn't something you can just walk away from when things get difficult."

"It isn't the work, Mrs. Blendall. It isn't anything like that. Lee and I... we just don't belong together. We never did. I know that now."

Mrs. Blendall came over and put her arms around the girl. "Sometimes when we're very tired, things look different than they really are. Why don't you wait a few days before you decide anything final?"

Garnet shook her head. "I've thought about it for weeks. My mind is made up."

The kitchen door opened and Lee came in, stamping the snow from his boots. He looked at the two women, sensing immediately that something was wrong.

"What's the matter? You both look like somebody died."

Garnet lifted her chin. "Lee, I have something to tell you. I'm leaving tomorrow."

He stood perfectly still, his face blank with shock. "Leaving? What do you mean, leaving?"

"I mean I'm going back to Mother's. Our marriage was a mistake, Lee. Surely you can see that."

"A mistake?" His voice was incredulous. "What kind of talk is that? What's gotten into you, Garnet?"

"Nothing's gotten into me. I'm just facing facts, that's all. We don't love each other, Lee. We never did. We just thought we did."

"Speak for yourself!" His voice was harsh with hurt and anger. "I love you, all right. I love you so much it's tearing me apart to watch you moping around here like you'd rather be anywhere else in the world."

"Oh, Lee, don't make this harder than it has to be. You know as well as I do that we're not happy together."

"Happy?" He laughed bitterly. "What's happiness got to do with marriage? You think married people are supposed to float around on clouds all the time? Marriage is work, Garnet. It's sticking together when things get tough, not running away at the first sign of trouble."

Mrs. Blendall slipped quietly from the room, leaving them alone.

"It isn't the first sign of trouble," Garnet said wearily. "We've been in trouble since the day we got married. We're just too different, Lee. I don't fit in here, and I never will."

"You haven't given it much of a try."

"I have tried! But it's no use. I'm going home, and that's final."

Lee stared at her for a long moment. Then he turned and walked out of the kitchen, letting the door slam behind him.

Garnet stood there listening to his footsteps going up the stairs. In a few minutes she heard his bedroom door close with a decisive bang. She had expected him to argue more, maybe even to plead with her to stay. His abrupt acceptance of her decision left her feeling strangely empty.

She went slowly up to her own room and sat down on the edge of the bed. Through the thin wall she could hear Lee moving around, and once she thought she heard him sigh. But there were no sounds of packing, no indication that he was doing anything but getting ready for bed.

Well, what had she expected? That he would go down on his knees and beg her not to leave? Lee wasn't the type for dramatic gestures. He was probably relieved that she was making it easy for him by leaving of her own accord.

She undressed and got into bed, but sleep wouldn't come. Every time she closed her eyes she saw Lee's face when she told him she was leaving. Under the anger and hurt, she had glimpsed something else—a look of such loneliness that it made her heart ache.

But it was too late to change her mind now. Tomorrow she would be gone, and in time they would both forget this unhappy interlude and get on with their separate lives.

When morning came, gray and cold, she dressed carefully in her good blue dress and combed her hair into a smooth roll. The suitcase felt heavier than she remembered as she carried it downstairs.

Mrs. Blendall was in the kitchen, but there was no sign of Lee. "He's already gone to the barn," the older woman said quietly. "Garnet, are you sure about this?"

"I'm sure." But her voice lacked conviction.

"He loves you, you know. More than you realize."

"Love isn't always enough, Mrs. Blendall."

"No, I suppose it isn't. But it's a good place to start." She handed Garnet a paper sack. "There's a sandwich and some cookies. The bus ride is long."

Garnet took the sack, her eyes filling with tears. "I'll write to you."

"See that you do." Mrs. Blendall kissed her cheek. "God bless you, child."

Garnet walked quickly toward the highway, not daring to look back at the house or the barn where Lee was working. The morning was crisp and clear, and her breath made little puffs of steam in the cold air.

She had gone perhaps half a mile when she heard footsteps behind her. She turned to see Lee coming after her, his long legs covering the ground quickly.

"Garnet, wait!"

She stopped, her heart hammering. He came up to her, breathing hard, his face flushed from running.

"I couldn't let you go like this," he said. "Not without trying once more to make you understand."

"Lee, please. Don't make this any harder—"

"Listen to me!" His voice was urgent. "I know I'm not much of a husband. I know I don't say the right things or do the romantic things you probably want. But I love you, Garnet. I love you more than I ever thought it was possible to love anybody."

She stared at him, seeing something in his eyes she had never seen before—a complete vulnerability, a stripping away of all pretense.

"I know you think we're too different," he went on. "Maybe we are. But different people can learn to fit together if they want to badly enough. I want to, Garnet. I want to learn how to make you happy."

"Oh, Lee..." The tears she had been holding back began to flow.

"Don't cry, honey. Please don't cry." He reached for her, and suddenly she was in his arms, her face pressed against his shoulder.

"I thought you didn't care," she sobbed. "I thought you'd be glad to see me go."

"Glad?" His arms tightened around her. "I've been going crazy thinking about losing you. I couldn't eat, couldn't sleep—"

"But you seemed so... so calm about it."

"I was trying to be fair. If you really wanted to leave, I didn't figure I had the right to stop you."

She pulled back to look at his face. "What about now? Are you stopping me now?"

"I'm asking you not to go. I'm asking you to give us another chance."

For a moment she wavered, seeing in his eyes all the love she had thought wasn't there. Then she thought of all the problems that would still be waiting if she stayed—the differences in their backgrounds, their wants, their dreams.

"I don't know, Lee. I just don't know if it would work."

"It'll work if we make it work. Please, Garnet. Don't give up on us."

She looked down the road toward the highway where the bus would be coming soon. Then she looked back at Lee, at the hope and love in his face.

Slowly, she set down her suitcase.

"All right," she said softly. "We'll try again."

His smile was like sunshine breaking through clouds. He picked up her suitcase with one hand and took her arm with the other.

"Come on," he said. "Let's go home."

As they walked back toward the house together, Garnet felt the tight knot of despair in her chest begin to loosen. Maybe love wasn't enough by itself. But love plus determination, plus a willingness to work at understanding each other—maybe that would be enough.

She looked up at Lee and smiled. "I love you, too," she said. "I never stopped loving you."

He squeezed her hand. "I know. I was just afraid you'd forgotten."

They were almost to the house when Mrs. Blendall came out on the porch. She took one look at them walking together, saw the suitcase in Lee's hand and the linked arms, and her face broke into a wide smile.

"I was just putting on the coffee," she called. "Perfect timing."

Garnet laughed, feeling lighter than she had in weeks. Perfect timing, indeed. Sometimes the best things happened when you stopped running away and decided to stay and fight for what you wanted.

She squeezed Lee's arm. They had a lot to learn about each other, a lot of problems to work through. But they had something to build on now—not just love, but understanding, and a commitment to make their marriage work.

It was a good beginning.
