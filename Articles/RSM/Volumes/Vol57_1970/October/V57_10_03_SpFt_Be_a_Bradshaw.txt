Be a PROFESSIONAL Homemaker, My Daughter
Lillian Y. Bradshaw

Taking you back to school the other night, sweet daughter nearly twenty, we talked of school and working and future plans of marriage. You said, "But, Mom, I lke to work. I like the excitement of it." I heard the echo of my own voice just that many years ago as I said to your father, "But honey, I want the challenge working outside the house would give me. I would feel like I'm doing something. I want to be someone, not just lost in diapers and dishes and scrubbing floors." And then I had bitterly added, "Anyone can do that!" I felt again a warm breeze blow a wisp of hair across my face as your father left for work that morning. Diaper and another diaper and another diaper blew in the Hawaiian breeze and tears streamed down my cheek. Thumb tackedthat's just how I feltthumb tacked to my home! Everytime I wanted to go out I had to weigh the cost of baby sitting, if I could find a baby sitter, against the food budget or think of little hands tugging at my skirts and crying to go home while I shopped just for the necessities. Resentment, acrid and hurting, choked all the beauty of my island home from my heart and mind and I longed to be free to go to work again. I had known the important feeling of being a head dietitian at one of the hospitals before I was married. I knew the feel of money of my own jingling in my pocketsto be free like my husband, talking with new vital people everyday. Just the luxury of talking real talk would seem like heaven and having luncheons. Which didn't have to be accounted for out of the family food budget! Like the diapers whipping vigorously, the harder my mind wrestled and twisted and thrashed as thoughts breezed through, the more I felt helplessly pinned. It was the treadmill of everyday blending into the last and so it seemed it would be forever! Emotionally spent and hurting all over with "outside-itis," out of the clear bluetoo audibly to denycame the thought quiet and pleading, '""Have you forgotten? Have you forgotten so soon?" Somersaulting one upon the other came thoughts of the prayers I once asked to find just the right man for me, then the prayer for a temple marriage, and then prayers that followed later that a healthy child, then another and another be born. Then sobbing in humility for all I had forgotten, I determined from that momentif I could not go out to find the business worldI would make our home my business and bring all I had learned into my home. I would find the excitement there. And if I couldn't find it I would create it. The same thought would go into running our home eflficiently as if I were still in the business world. I realized for the first time that my home held all the possibilities of achievement I could hope to find outside in any field.
And with that thought I actually became transfused with new life and began making the change from housewife to professional homemaker. Looking at my kitchen I saw it through different eyes. I couldn't believe the disorder in terms of what would have been done at the hospital. Pots and pans were unbelievably far from the center of preparation. With a sense of being alive again I thought in terms of casseroles being prepared, the making of bread, cakes being baked. Pots Lillian Y. Bradshaw and pans were rearranged and shifted to a different cupboard, closer to the flour and sugar supplies. The mixer was placed on a counter. Minutes were added to my dayВ—five minutes to be exact in baking a cake. Watching the clock, I began to calculate how many minutes it took to prepare certain dishes. Saving time became a game. In most cases I was amazed to find that what seemed to take hours, in reality took only minutes. Menumaking had been important to keep costs down at the hospital, menu-making at home became important. A shopping list completed the task. I used to love to work in disorder, though at the time I didn't know it was disorder. I can well remember my sewing room when I was first married and had the luxury of an extra bedroom as my sewing room. Patterns, needles, threads, scissors, material lay out all over just as I left them. Sometimes while making a dress it would stay that way for days. And I thought I was saving time. Through your father's eyes, mine were opened to things as they were, and then to think professionally how they could be. Seeking to be professional in any work needing to be done in the home I little by little acquired proper tools for myself. Now minutes really began to total up. Before, I had felt time being taken from me! My children, my home, everything was taking my time away from me. With time created, I now could give time. Just aS you save money by pennies to make dollars to be able to do special things, just so you save minutes to make hours to create time to do special things. A housewife becomes a professional the moment she begins to create time by being organized. She does not expect to find time, for time is never found lying around waiting to be picked up. Minutes are saved by being organized. 726 Let me give you a few ideas of how to save time and then you, too, will know by doing someday in your own home. Dressing and making the bed were accomplished before entering the kitchen to prepare breakfast. I have taught you children to do the same. Already you have the first step to being efficiently organized. Breakfast prepared and eaten as a family, beds all made and the house put in order before you left for school has been the second step. Shortly thereafter I do some preliminary preparation for dinner that evening. With the home basically in order, you will then find that the rest of the day can be organized as you desire.
