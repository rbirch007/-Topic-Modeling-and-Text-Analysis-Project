HANDCART CAME They knew how far it was By measured longings for a home. Exulting as they went, their cart wheels spun Into the garnet-studded West. Children of the evening, they? No, of the noon. The scars of the night were washed away, The Zion of their days begun. A thousand miles and pull a cart! They, of avalanche and stone, Of fiords and crags, Of bounties of the sea and shore. Possessions now, were millstones gone. The throated burning in their breasts Enough to feed upon. They knew how far it was, The lingering distance stalked with death. With spider strength, dragged Carcasses of bed and skillet, Flour and salt, through grass of June, July's scorched lips, then to lay their burdens down Upon November's sod. Ground so hallowed, pray Walk unshod. Hazel Loomis Meeting of Desert and Mountains, Southern Utah Transparency by Lucien Bown Lithographed in Full Color by Deseret News Press Handcart in the West Photograph by Dorothy J.
