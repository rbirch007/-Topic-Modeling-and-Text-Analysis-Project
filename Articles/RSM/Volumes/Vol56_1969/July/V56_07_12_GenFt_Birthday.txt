Watson, Member, General Board of Relief Society w Mama left a legacy of happy memories for her family. Our friends and relatives young and old loved to come to her gracious, hospitable home. Some who knew Mama best said it was because she was generous, kind, and witty, with a penchant for making every guest feel special and welcome. To this I wholeheartedly agree, but Mama had something else which drew people to her. Mama was an excellent cook. She liked nothing better than to share her cuisine with those she loved.. Unfortunately for her daughters, Mama never used a recipe. All of her dishes were concocted by feel, consistency, or taste. A pinch of this or a handful of that. Mama didn't even own measuring cups or spoons. One of the happiest memories Mama left her children was our annual trip to "the Lakes." Mama prepared for several days for this special event. I can close my eyes now and it seems as though I can almost taste some of her special goodies. As the tantalizing aroma from Mama's preparatory baking permeated the air, it appeared that it touched off the appetite alarms of all the youngsters in our neighborhood and those children who came to our kitchen door never went away hungry. For the long drive to "the Lakes," our outing site, Mama usually brought a lunch of milk, cinnamon buns, or slices of raisin and nut bread spread with freshly churned butter. She would pack them in the folds of snowy white dish cloths to keep them fresh, since wax paper or plastic wrap had never been heard of in our town.

Our favorite food was Mama's baked beans. Their cooking was an all-day process. How we loved the smell of the molasses and brown sugar, mustard, and the salt pork nestled cozily among the brown beans. Combined, they produced a flavor that was delectable. Homemade root beer was a must. This was an exciting change from milk or the ever-popular lemonade. Mama's root beer had a special tang. We all loved the bottling process and as we put the corks into the bottle tops and tied them securely to the bottle with string, we would beg Mama to put our mark on just one bottle reserved especially for each of us. In our home town, most everyone had at least a small garden. Fresh vegetables were common fare. Mama said they were good for us and so she used them in every possible way. Happily, Mama had the knack for giving even the lowlhest variety a special flavor. Pickled cucumbers were our special favorites so Mama always included them as part of our picnic menu. Wheat was indeed the staff of life in our home. Mama used it in a variety of ways. From a breakfast food which she simmered for hours on the back of our coal range, to hot cakes, baking powder biscuits, buns, scones, to wonderful crusty homemade bread. We loved to spread fat slices of this bread with cream skimmed from the milk pan and flavored with sugar, or covered and dripping with honey or syrup. Long loaves of what we now call French bread were our favorite for an outing. What is so delightful as homemade ice cream? No picnic or outing was complete in our family without it. Since Mama _ never counted calories, her ice cream was rich and delicious. The best and sweetest part was that which some lucky individual would lick from the dasher. Mama made many flavors and each kind we would avow to be our favorite. Try a delicious peach variety for your family. A dripper cake, usually Mama's special fudge cake recipe, completed our lunch. Even though we had eaten our fill we could never resist this special treat. Papa always concluded our picnics by arising to his feet and proclaiming that this picnic was the best ever. His only complaint being that he had eaten too much. Why did she make everything taste so good? Then, one by one, each one of Mama's children in his own way would echo Papa's words. Many of you, like Mama's children, have treasured memories. Many of you, as we do, long for "Mama's" cooking. Why not plan a family outing and try these recipes or those you and your family enjoyed and remember so well? Then make the effort to live up to your resolve. "Picking the brains" of others sounds almost unpardonable, but that is what the mentally alert do every day of their lives. If you meet a ceramic artist, learn about ceramics. If you meet an old-timer, learn more about your community. If you meet a scout-explorer, learn more about mountaineering.
