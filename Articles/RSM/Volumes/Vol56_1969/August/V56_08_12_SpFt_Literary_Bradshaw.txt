Literary  Contest  Announcements  1969 
The  Relief  Society  Poem  Contest  and  the  ReUef  Society  Short  Story 
Contest  are  conducted  annually  by  the  General  Board  of  Relief 
Society  to  stimulate  creative  writing  among  Latter-day  Saint  women 
and  to  encourage  high  standards  of  work.  Latter-day  Saint  women 
who  qualify  under  the  rules  of  the  respective  contests  are  invited  to 
enter  their  work  in  either  or  both  contests. 
The  General  Board  will  be  pleased  to  receive  entries  from  the 
outlying  stakes  and  missions  of  the  Church  as  well  as  from  those 
in  and  near  Utah.  Since  the  two  contests  are  entirely  separate,  re- 
quiring different  writing  skills,  the  winning  of  an  award  in  one  of 
them  in  no  way  precludes  winning  in  the  other. 
The  Relief  Society  Poem  Contest 
The  Relief  Society  Poem  Con- 
test opens  with  this  announce- 
ment and  closes  November  15, 
1969.  Prizes  will  be  awarded  as 
follows: 
First  prize   $40 
Second  prize   $30 
Third  prize   $20 
Prize  poems  will  be  published 
in  the  April  1970  issue  of  The 
Relief  Society  Magazine. 
Prize-winning  poems  become 
the  property  of  the  Relief  Society 
General  Board  and  may  not  be 
published  by  others  except  upon 
written  permission  from  the  Gen- 
eral Board.  The  General  Board 
reserves  the  right  to  publish  any 
of  the  other  poems  submitted, 
paying  for  them  at  the  time  of 
publication  at  the  regular  Maga- 
zine rates. 
Rules  for  the  contest: 
1.  This  contest  is  open  to  all  Latter- 
day  Saint  women,  exclusive  of  members 
of  the  Relief  Society  General  Board  and 
employees  of  the  Relief  Society  General 
Board. 
2.  Only  one  poem  may  be  submitted 
by  each  contestant. 
3.  The  poem  must  not  exceed  fifty 
lines  and  should  be  typewritten,  if  possible. 
Where  this  cannot  be  done,  it  should  be 
legibly  written.  Only  one  side  of  the  paper 
is  to  be  used.  (A  duplicate  copy  of  the 
poem  should  be  retained  by  contestants 
to  insure  against  loss.) 
4.  The  sheet  on  which  the  poem  is 
written  is  to  be  without  signature  or 
other  identifying  marks. 
5.  No  explanatory  material  or  pic- 
ture is  to  accompany  a  poem. 
6.  Each  poem  is  to  be  accompanied 
by  a  stamped  envelope  on  which  is  written 
the  contestant's  name  and  address.  Nom 
de  plumes  are  not  to  be  used. 
7.  A  signed  statement  is  to  accom- 
pany the  poem  submitted  certifying: 
a.  That  the  author  is  a  member  of 
The  Church  of  Jesus  Christ  of 
Latter-day  Saints. 
b.  That  the  poem  (state  title)  is  the 
contestant's  original  work. 
c.  That  it  has  never  been  published. 
d.  That  it  is  not  in  the  hands  of  an 
editor  or  other  person  with  a 
view  to  publication. 
e.  That  it  will  not  be  published  nor 
submitted  elsewhere  for  publica- 
tion until  the  contest  is  decided.

ROADSIDE  MOMENT,  SUMMER 
DiJrer  the  artist  etched  the  growing  world 
As  few  have  paused  to  see  it.  .  .Once, 
Halted  by  a  roadside,  I  looked  out  level  and  deep 
Into  his  summer  world— at  blade,  stem,  blossom,  pod,  leaf, 
Whorled  and  curved,  planed  and  shaped, 
Divided,  yet  intricately  twined  and  curled, 
(Each  one  an  entity  individually  rising) 
Diversity  in  a  random  plat 
So  myriad,  the  eye  seeking  thought  by  thought 
For  reason,  was  dazzled  by  the  shades 
Of  light  given  back  from  so  subtle  a  variance 
Of  delicate  greens. 
—And  these  were  weeds! 
Never,  no  never  before  had  I  seen 
Such  wonders  springing  in  so  small  a  space.  .  . 
Great  Moses  once,  in  a  burning  bush. 
Saw  God.  .  . 
I  too  have  looked,  and  marveled 
In  my  small  way,  at  his  gifts  of  grace! 
—Katheririe  F.  Larsen

Lillian  Y.  Bradshaw 
■  At  the  circus,  the  Uthe  tight- 
rope walker  gracefully  climbed 
the  pole.  With  breathless  excite- 
ment we  watched  him  test  the 
wire.  A  hush  fell  upon  every  lip 
as  with  slippered  feet  he  glided 
along  the  wire — one  cautious  step 
after  another — his  balance  mea- 
sured by  every  thought  and  move- 
ment of  his  body.  A  slip  here,  a 
slip  there,  a  quick  bend  of  the 
body  far  to  the  left  and  then 
quickly  to  the  right — we  gasped 
with  relief  as  he  regained  his 
balance  once  again  to  move 
swiftly  forward.  And  though  we 
sat  taut  on  the  bench  below,  in- 
side we  were  keenly  aware  we 
were  taking  every  step  with  him. 
Now  the  goal  but  a  few  steps 
more — joyfully  and  with  obvious 
relief  we  all  exclaimed  "Hurrah!" 
as  the  platform  on  the  other  side 
was  reached  in  safety. 
And  so  it  is  with  children.  .  .  . 
Some  seem  to  be  thus  blessed 
as  they  enter  in  on  this  tight- 
rope life — well  slippered,  testing 
each  step,  seemingly  gifted  with 
a  balance  all  their  own.  And 
though  they  slip  to  one  side  or 
the  other  and  we  hold  our  breath, 
they  always  seem  to  know  how 
to  right  themselves.  It  is  with 
excited  joy  and  anticipation  we 
see  them  perform.  And  many 
are  the  hurrahs. 
And  then  there  are  those 
children  who  enter  as  though 
wearing  hiking  boots,  and  act 
as  the  clown  who  performs  clum- 
sily— slipping,  sliding,  arms  and 
legs  askew.  We  are  at  times 
caused  to  laugh,  yet  when  we 
see  this  young  one  lose  his  bal- 
ance our  hearts  reach  out  and 
achingly  we  ask  ourselves  "Oh, 
what  can  I  do?"  "What  can  I 
say?"  "How  can  I  help,  don't 
you  know  how  important  you 
are?"  And  then  he  falls  into  the 
net  of  life  and  walks  about  with
