"When you're finished here will you come and help me set out the crockery, please, boys?" Jean asked. "There are two packing cases to empty before we can have any supper."

"Sure thing, Mum," Martin replied, "and can I have some peanut butter sandwiches, please, to keep me going until suppertime? I'm starving."

"That child must have an abyss for a stomach." Steve shook his head. "He ought to be like a plum dumpling, but he never gets any fatter. Off you go, boys, I'll see to this while you help your mother."

Gradually the bright autumn colors deepened to the mellow tints of autumn, and the family settled down in their new home. Trub had been wildly excited when introduced to his new home. He had run around inspecting every tree in sight, hurtling through the house like a miniature tornado, skidding on the polished floors. Finally he had settled in an untidy heap on the hearth rug, his exploration completed. The older boys had quickly made new friends, while Jean's bright disposition came to good use, and she was soon on friendly terms with the occupants of nearly every house on the road.

One person, however, always answered her kindly greeting with a grunt and never smiled. An upright, elderly gentleman, the retired Colonel Watkins, who lived further along the road, and seemed very brusque. Jean often wondered about him, living alone as he did so unfriendly. He didn't even have a smile for young James in his push chair, until one day when Trub took a turn in the events.

"Mum!" Martin came rushing into the kitchen. "Mum, I've been to see Colonel Watkins, and I think he's ill and he was angry."

"You've been to see Colonel Watkins?" Jean was astounded. "Yes. You see Trub escaped," Martin began, "and I went looking for him and I went to the colonel's to ask him if I could look around his garden and he did look ill," Martin ended breathlessly.

"Well, why was he angry?" Jean inquired. "I hope you weren't rude?"

"Oh, no," Martin answered firmly. "He asked me why I wanted to look in his garden, and I answered and said very politely, 'I'm just looking for Trouble,' and he said that I was a naughty impur . . . imp . . . a . . ."

"Impertinent," Jean finished for him.

"Yes. Impertinent little boy, and he told me to go home, Martin went on, "and then he began to cough and he looked real bad."

"Oh, dear," Jean sighed, not knowing whether to laugh or cry. "I will have to go and explain, and if the poor man is ill, I'll see if he needs any help."

"I'll play with James while you go," offered Martin.

"Thank you, dear." Jean hurried out and down the road
