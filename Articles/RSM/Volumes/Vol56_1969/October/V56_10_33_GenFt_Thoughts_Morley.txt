I am also remembering the events of today: leaving home before dawn, and the long drive in the rain. Then, when we had reached our destination, driving around the beautiful campus, with its broad stretches of lawn, and the stately old buildings harmonizing beautifully with the lovely new ones. Then, after we had delivered all of your belongings at the dormitory, which would be your new home for the coming year, we drove you back to the Administration Buildingwhere we would part. I feel that I can now understand to a small degree the feelings of our Father in heaven as he sends each of his children to earth. He dearly loves them and would like them to remain with him always, but for their development and progress, they must be parted for awhile. And so each one, as the time is right, is sent to earth to gain experiences here. Perhaps you were feeling and know to some degree, how very difficult it was for us to leave you walking alone up that beautiful tree-lined path toward the door of your future. As you turned to wave goodbye, you suddenly seemed so very young. This in direct contrast to the thoughts I had been thinking for many monthsthat you had grown tall and were becoming an adult. And now you seemed almost too young to leave on your own. The years have slipped by rapidlyso very rapidly! Simple foods, such as stews and leftovers, take on a new elegance and importance when served as casseroles. One-dish meals require little watching, and with a side serving of salad, bread, and milk, little else is needed for a hearty meal. Fortunately, casserole recipes are usually quite flexible. Proportions and main ingredients can be varied without diminishing the flavor or quality of the dishes. Happily, in these days of high food prices, less meat may be used, more potatoes, rice, spaghetti, and vegetables may be added for bulk. Either fresh, frozen, or canned foods, depending on their availability or price, may be used. Casseroles are well known as time savers. Provident homemakers find it a good practice to prepare two or more casseroles at one time from like ingredients, one to be cooked for tonight's dinner, the other to be popped into the freezer to be brought out for unexpected company, for dinner on Relief Society day, or to be taken to a sick friend. Preparation for the Sabbath day may be minimized by frequent use of casseroles prepared in advance. Casserole recipes cover a wide range of foods. They may be served in a simple or an elegant manner, as the occasion demands. Why not try some of the following delicious recipes? Or you may wish to concoct some of your own. of the gospel or any related subject. Any accountable person can gain a testimony if he will obey the laws upon which the receipt of such knowledge is predicated. But here, as elsewhere, there are certain prerequisites, which may be listed roughly as follows:

1. The seeker must desire to know the truth of the gospel or whatever is involved in his quest.

2. He must study and learn everything possible with relation to the matter involved. In John 5:39 we read: "Search the scriptures; for in them ye think ye have eternal life." And we are directed in the Doctrine and Covenants 1:37 to "Search these commandments."

3. The applicant must practice the principles and truths which he learns and bring his life into harmony with them. The Savior said: "My doctrine is not mine, but his that sent me. If any man will do his will, he shall know of the doctrine, whether it be of God, or whether I speak of myself." (John 7:16, 17.)

4. He must constantly pray to the Father and exercise faith that the truth will be manifested by revelation through the Holy Ghost.

Moroni tells us that by the power of the Holy Ghost ye may know the truth of all things. (Moroni 10:3-5.)

When the Apostle Paul answered Christ's question as to who he, the Savior, was, he had the spirit of revelation, and Jesus said to him: "Blessed art thou, Simon Bar-jona: for flesh and blood hath not revealed it unto thee, but my Father which is in heaven." (Matt. 16:17.)

This bears out the claim that direct and personal revelation is available to men if they will learn the necessary prerequisites and conform their lives thereto.

In speaking or writing to members of the Relief Society one is addressing the builders of the homes of our communities and of the Church. Every home has a spirit and that spirit will depend in large measure upon the testimony which the mother has of the divinity of the work in which we are engaged. This emphasizes the importance of her gaining a testimony and under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit guide and direct the affairs of the home and the lives of her children, having in mind always the relationship which exists between the Lord and his children.

In other words, the mother must have great faith—faith in God, faith in herself and in her companion, and faith in the ultimate triumph of righteousness—a faith which ventures beyond knowledge and by which she may be able to meet the tests of practical life and religious experience. She will be guided by intellectual as well as moral imperatives. A mother who has a testimony will, therefore, live a righteous life—one which the children can emulate with safety; the kind of life which is inspired and directed by the Holy Ghost, whose companionship she will constantly seek.

Of course the knowledge which the mother gains in her search for a testimony will be most helpful in her presiding over her home. But that knowledge must be observed and incorporated into the daily life of those concerned. He who knows the precepts and neglects to obey them is like one who lights a candle in the darkness and then closes his eyes.
