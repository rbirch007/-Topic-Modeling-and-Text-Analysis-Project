691 September 1969

 ART IN THE HOME (Continued from page 678) As we were leaving, he said, "Graycie, keep painting all your life for your own enjoyment, as I have advised my own children; but never take up painting seriously as a vocation; it is too heartbreaking."' He hesitated and, even at that early age, I caught the struggle of an artist's life especially in an isolated community. I did not heed his advice, as the urge to express in paint, plus early exposure to good art, held me forever. My most precious recollections were in Alfred Lambourne's modest studio on North Main Street, where I sat in the bay window on some rainy days and listened breathlessly to his true tales of pioneer days and the lonely beauty of the plains as the pioneers made their tortuous trail to this unknown land. He was a mystic and poet, as was his pioneer father, John Lambourne, and his descriptions of his research into space, the stars and the "music of the spheres" he had actually heard, held me spellbound. He instinctively knew about matters not of this earth, and was host to celebrities and Eastern men of thought in his modest studioall seeking, as man will seek forever, his own destiny; as Paul Gauguin asked in one of his famous last paintings, "Where do we come from? What are we? Where are we going?" I am grateful indeed to my parents and the circumstances surrounding the unique cultural life of this then-isolated community in the mountains, on the edge of the desert and the inland sea.
