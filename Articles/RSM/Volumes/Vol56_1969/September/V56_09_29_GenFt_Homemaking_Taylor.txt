From the mangled, gangrenous broken leg demanding a life, through amputation with hideous pain without anesthetic, to the most refined and delicate painless operation upon the very seat of the mind itselfall these but indicate man's Lesson Department conquest of his inhibitions and limitations.... However stable or unstable, however complete or incomplete these conquests may be, they mark human progresshow great relatively we may not measure for we do not know the ultimate. But man seems to have come great distances. But these conquests of energy and force, which make human progress, add to human comfort and well being, increase knowledge and quicken intelligence, and, above all (and this the end of it all), promote human happiness, for the ancient prophet declared, "... Men are that they might have joy." (Ibid.)  President Clark points out a very important difference. He states: But the things already spoken of [mastery over the physical limitations] only lead out toward perfection but not to it, for perfection may not be reached by mastery over the material things alone. Perfection means also the _ subjugation of the intellectual and spiritual limitations and inhibitions which are incident to the flesh. But there is an essential and fundamental difference between conquest and achievement in the physical and in the spiritual world. One need not do the works of Morse or Edison or Marconi in order fully to enjoy and use the telegraph, the telephone, the gramophone, or the radio; nor need one do the works of the Wrights in order to enjoy and use the airplane. The most ignorant and depraved of men may use all these things for his own purposes, to serve his own ends, good or bad, for the benefit or injury of others, as fully as they who made them. So likewise it is with the works of the great artists, sculptors, musicians, dramatists, and men of letters. Save only the creative joy which those alone who make may know, these masterpieces may be fully understood, appreciated, and enjoyed by any one without doing the works of the creator. But it is not so with the things of the spirit. (Ibid.) THINGS OF THE SPIRIT Class Discussion 1. What do "things of the spirit" mean to you? 2. Enumerate several specific things. President Clark makes this clarification, referring to things of the spirit: One must do the works of Elijah if one would know his joy, have part in his blessings, share his glory, exercise his power and authority, and do the things he did. And surely the power to bring rain to a famine stricken land, to call down fire from heaven, 716 to cause the starving widow's barrel of meal not to waste, her cruse of oil not to fail, to restore to life her dead son, to declare the past and foretell the futuresurely these things are beyond compare; they may not be valued, nor purchased, nor hired; they are beyond price. Here the works and the use are companions that never part. ([bid.) Save for the atoning sacrifice of the Son and the doing of certain initiatory rites for the dead, (and even these must be accepted and adopted by the dead) nothing can be vicariously done, in matters of the spirit, by one for another. FUNDAMENTAL TO CHRISTIANITY Although we may thus assist one another to grow in spirituality, it is fundamental to Christianity that each individual is responsible for his own life, his own upward reach, his own soul. A mother's faith and unwavering testimony belong to her, my neighbor's readiness to forgive does not make of me a forgiving person. Each must earn these blessings for herself. President Clark states: Thus every person shall make his own mind [as to] what his life shall be. None shall make it for him. Whether he will be satisfied with that part of salvation which pertains to the physical world, or whether he shall go on and add to this the salvation and exaltation of the spiritual universe, is for his own choice. For him indeed who would conquer the flesh for the blessings of the spirit, there is a joy awaiting which exceeds all power of description.. (lbid.) OF OUR BETTER SELVES As we travel the upward road toward those higher goals for which we reach there may. Be periods of accelerated growth. Certain individuals, special situations, and occasions may motivate us to a better performance than usual, to wider horizons of thought, where we may understand what we have never known before. These we should not forget. These we should cherish always. Christmas can be one of these occasions. During the kindly yuleLesson Department tide, we reach out beyond ourselves with compassion for others, motivated to a _ generosity of thought and deed. In this season of good will the lonely, the poor, the forgotten become our individual concern. If we will close our ears to the sounding brass and the tinkling cymbal of material things we may hear wise voices reminding us not to seek for the riches of earth. Voices saying such as this: After all, this existence of ours here is temporary. It is smaller than a drop in the ocean, as compared with eternity. We were before we came here; we are here; and we shall be after we go hence. This life is but one tiny line in this vast extension of existence. Do not forget,... The goodness and the mercy and the kindness of the Savior. Do not forget the blessings which the Gospel has for you. Do not overlook the joy of the spirit, the joy which comes from living as the Savior has taught us to live.. Seek after the treasures of the spirit, seek after the riches of goodness and mercy and righteous living. Go always after those things which are good.... Discard the dross, cast it aside, trample it underfoot; cherish always and guard that which is beautiful in your lives. (Jbid., lesson 20.) Such counsel is for the upward reach of man. Listen to it through this season of remembering the birthday of our Lord. For Home Doing Bring as much spirituality into your home this Christmas time as possible.
