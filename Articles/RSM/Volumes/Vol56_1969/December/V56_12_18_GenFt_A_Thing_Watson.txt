Watson, Member, General Board of Relief Society C0 Relief Society women are ingenious. Show them a thing of beauty and lo! With their own magic hands they can create its likeness. Since variety is a necessary requisite of any worthy Relief Society project, they change and add, putting a new detail here and color there, enhancing and beautifying most every object. Such it was with the candlesticks made by the sisters of Valley View Eighth Ward, Valley View Stake, under the direction of Sister Janet G. Ogden. Each one was different in color and design, and each was decorated by the sisters to blend and add charm to the decor of the different homes. The tall, more ornate candlesticks were designed for use in the living room or family room areas. The shorter, smaller ones were planned to grace a dining room or kitchen table. A variety of finishes was used. Some were painted with a flat paint and finished with an antique glaze. The smaller ones were covered with enamel paint; the large ringed candles were stained in walnut finish with an antique glaze added. Flowers, fruits, greens, and ribbons were used to decorate them. Some of the sisters made their own candles where a particular color and size was needed.

Celebrating Christmas Without Going Into Debt CELEBRATING CHRISTMAS WITHOUT GOING INTO DEBT (continued from page 918) Finding the names of people who might not have a pleasant Christmas, and sharing with them, has brought unspeakable joy and insight to our family. Time after time my children have demonstrated unselfishness, and have put other people's happiness before their own desires. Such times make a parent's heart burst with pride. To develop fully a child's imagination, toys should be interchangeable. Therefore, a truck should not look too much like a truck. It should quickly convert to a bus, fire engine, moving van, or any other four-wheeled conveyance. With this in mind, Tom, my husband, has built ride-in box carts that have served every imaginable purpose. He has cut blocks in all sizes and shapes, built work benches, bulletin boards, and other household accessories. My contributions include stuffed animals, rag dolls, bean bag toys, special dresses or costumes, hand puppets, and similar items. Our children's accomplishments show real imagination, and they improve continually. Celebrating Christmas this way means burning the midnight oil every night for weeks. It means tolerating horrible messes from cookie decorating, salt clay, and other projects. Projects are in various stages of completion and must be stored everywhere. Boxes of scraps, spools, detergent and bleach bottles, pop bottle caps, and numerous other things are stacked high in storage, awaiting time for construction. The ironing doesn't always get done, and the house seems to be in utter chaos, and Christmas morning finds us physically exhausted, but spiritually refreshed. We strive to remember the true meaning of Christmas, and to give thanks for the birth of the Savior, together, as a family united. Heslop Delila Burgess Eagar, Eagar Ward, St. Johns Stake, Arizona, has won blue ribbons for her quilts, rugs, crocheted tablecloths, doilies, stuffed toys, pillows, and pillowslips. Her greatest concern is keeping her hands busy. Sister Eagar designs her own quilts, and has donated countless numbers to Relief Society. Her six children, twelve grandchildren, and three great-grandchildren are recipients of many lovely articles; while her relatives who came before her are benefiting from the countless hours she spends on genealogy and temple work. Sister Eagar has served as Relief Society president, on the stake board, as a class leader - for most of the classes, as a secretary, and a visiting teacher, as well as in responsible positions in other auxiliaries of the Church.
