(Recipes continued in the July Magazine) 449 — — Annual Report for 1964 'he Retief Society, established by the Prophet Joseph Smith 123 vears ago, providing for the women of the Church an organization ''according to the natures of women,'' continued to bless and strengthen its increasing number of members during 1964. The 274,703 members who made up its ranks in the past year 'included sisters of many nationalities and races, including more than 6,000 coeds on various college campuses. The mothers ranged in age from their late teens to grandmothers and great-grandmothers crowding the century mark. This represents an increase of 12,701 members. Of this total membership 2,412 were women wha were not baptized members of the Church, but who were edified by the lofty program and activities of the organization. The varied aspects of the Society were carried forward by a devoted corps of 182,966 leaders including 4,568 stake officers, and 1,273 district and mission officers. The work in 5,735 wards and branches throughout the Church was accomplished by 21,596 ward and branch executive officers, 11,567 other officers (including choristers, organists and Magazine representatives), 23,251 class leaders and 120,611 visiting teachers. Increased testimony, cultural development, and unbounded satisfaction came to these leaders as they unselfishly gave of themselves in service. The blessings of the organization were extended to sisters of the Church in 400 stakes and 74 missions located in each of the United States and in 53 other countries throughout the world from the tip of Tasmania, the Union of South Africa and Argentina on the south, to Alaska, northern Canada, the British Isles, Finland, and the Scandinavian countries on the north. Areas in which Relief Societies are located include, among others, Germany, Samoa, Japan, Korea, Libya, Lebanon, Brazil, Spain and Guatemala. Sisterly visits were made by visiting teachers visiting in pairs to 559,795 Latter-day Saint families throughout the Church and to 10,779 families who were not members of the Church, but who welcomed the visits of these sisters. These visits were made monthly to observe instances of need in the respective homes and to carry a spiritual message to the mother of the home, with a total of 4,466,668 visits being made. Inspiration and musical development came into the lives of 43,711 sisters who united with heart and voice in singing the songs of Zion as they participated in 3,113 Singing Mothers choruses. Their messages of faith, testimony, peace, and happiness warmed the hearts of saints and friends assembled in conferences, in special concerts throughout the Church, at The American Mothers Annual Awards Luncheon and at the New York World's Fair, and in social functions and programs conducted by Relief Societies. A total of 231,933 Relief Society Magazines carried into Latter-day Saint and non-Latter-day Saint homes the spirit of this great women's organization. They contained articles of general reading interest. Inspirational messages by Genera! Authorities and the Relief Society General Presidency, suggestions on good homemaking, the courses studied by Relief Societies throughout the world, accounts of important events of historical interest, instructions to Relief Society leaders, and stimulating creative writings in fiction and poetry. Sympathetic, kindly, merciful services to the sick, bereaved, and those in distress, which is the fundamental work of Relief Society, claimed many hours of service by sisters upon assignment of their Relief Society presidents. This included 29,896 eight-hour days care of the sick, 407,621 visits to the sick and homebound, assistance at 10.203 funerals, the dressing of 855 bodies for burial and the giving of 684.805 hours to other compassionate services. Many anxieties were lessened, hearts comforted, and suffering assuaged through this untiring service. Relief Society, in 1964, also aided in the care of the needy through service considered a part of the Church Welfare Program. Under the direction of bishops, 108,948 visits were made by Relief Society presidents to families in need, 73,580 additional contacts were made, and 809,524 hours contributed by females on welfare projects. Through attendance at the weekly Relief Society meetings, the sisters consistently studied and saw the application in their lives of truths contained in The Doctrine and Covenants and in good literature. They came to understand more fully divine law in relation to Church government. They increased their homemaking skills. As a result of their conscientious efforts stimulated by bazaars and the teaching of homemaking skills, the sisters completed a total of 1,164,990 articles under the supervision or at the direction of Relief Society. This included 747,236 quilts, articles of men's, women's and children's clothing and household furnishings, and 417,754 useful and artistic articles which did not require sewing. All of these studies, services, and activities engaged in during the past year helped to equip the members of Relief Society to be better wives, mothers, homemakers, members of the Church, and citizens of their respective communities. Thus Relief Society continues to accomplish the purposes for which it was divinely established. 460 NOTES FROM THE FIELD French Polynesian Mission Relief Society Seminar, Papeete, Tahiti October 1964 Front row, seated, left to right: Esther Bennet, Tuamotu District; Diane S. Stone, Supervisor, French Polynesian Mission Relief Society; Eleonora Mataoa, Tahiti District. Back row, standing, left to right: Tiaki Menemene, Leeward Islands District; Elisa Sam You, Tubuai District; Teriiotemana Tahiata, Tubuai District. Sister Stone reports: "Over 150 Relief Society officers and teachers and branch and district presidency representatives gathered at the French Polynesian Mission headquarters in Papeete for a two-day Relief Society Leadership Seminar. Members from as far as the Noumea Branch in New Caledonia, almost 3,000 miles away, were greeted by the mission Relief Society Supervisor Diane S. Stone, under whose leadership the seminar was organized. "Throughout the seminar, emphasis was placed on the role of Relief Society in the home and the community. With this in mind, members of the mission Relief Society presidency explained the purpose and organization of the Relief Society, underlining the important part women play in the spiritual welfare and progression of the family. To assist the sisters when they return to their branches, the importance of developing leadership and effective planning was stressed. This included demonstrations of effective visiting teaching methods, teaching with visual aids, conducting prayer meetings, discussing the annual mission Relief Society bazaar, and explaining and distributing the lesson books which had been prepared in both the French and Tahitian languages. A slide presentation of Relief Society activities and achievements during the past year and an inspirational testimony meeting were held. Members of the Papeete Relief Societies prepared a luncheon for their guests each day of the conference. Coming from more than twenty different islands, the sisters organized themselves into Singing Mothers choruses, a different district performing for each conference session. Representatives from each district would then give a report of their progress during the past year." JUNE 1965 Uintah Stake (Utah) Relief Society Fashion Show January 28, 1965 Appearing in jumper dresses made by their mother, Mollidean Morton, are: Marsha Kay, Carol Dean, Shelly Ann, and Valerie Jean Morton. Elouise N. Turner, President, Uintah Stake Relief Society, reports: "The theme of the Fashion Show was 'Stylishly and Modestly.' Its two-fold purpose was to encourage women to sew for themselves and their families, and to use styles that would be attractive and acceptable to Church standards of dress. "The general chairman was Helen Millecam of Vernal Third Ward, with Dixie Akelund of Davis Ward and Kathryn McCarrel of Vernal Fourth Ward, as her assistants, and also a ward assistant from each of the seven wards. "Colorful paper flowers were pinned on each guest at the door by stake board and committee members. Huge paper flowers formed the background decorations. These were made by committee members. Prelude music was played on the piano by Diane Abplanalp. Sister Turner welcomed the sisters, numbering about 300. The Singing Mothers of Jensen Ward sang 'My Happiness,' accompanied by Kay Trujillo. The fashions and models were presented in groups. Narrator was Helen Spendlove, who also introduced the numbers of the musical program which was presented between the appearances of the fashion models. Over 100 mothers, daughters, and sons appeared as models. Some of the styles were made from other articles of clothing; some were sister look-alikes; and some were mother-daughter outfits. Many of the fashions were made by the young people themselves." Glendale Stake (California) Annual Visiting Teacher Convention January 29, 1965 Front row, seated, left to right: Edna Beal, President, Glendale Stake Relief Society; Florence Dalton, Work Counselor; Leah Frandsen, Education Counselor., Second row, standing, left to right: Ruth Gough, Secretary-Treasurer; Nancy Scott, work meeting leader; Annie Anderson; Jesse Schade, visiting teacher message leader; Edythe Fairbanks, Magazine representative; Cynthia Clarke; Barbara Moffat, literature leader. Sister Beal reports: " 'Visiting Teaching Is the Key to Relief Society' was the theme of our Annual Visiting Teacher Convention, and was carried out in our decorations, featuring large gold keys on blue-satin-ribbon-festoon<d luncheon tables. Three hundred ten visiting teachers enjoyed a delicious fried chicken luncheon, and each one was presented a key ring of the gold Relief Society seal embedded in blue plastic. Our program featured the Magazine, and slides and narration dramatized the achievements of Relief Society. The Singing Mothers sang two beautiful numbers. Because they had attained 100 per cent visiting teaching, Garvanza and Highland Park Wards were each presented with a Book of Mormon for their libraries. We are proud of our stake's record of 99 per cent visiting teaching, and we hope that this year each of our eleven wards will attain 100 per cent."
