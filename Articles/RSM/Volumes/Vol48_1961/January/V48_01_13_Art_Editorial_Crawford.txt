Even a small offering taken to a home where there is illness or loss, may illuminate a segment of time for EDITORIAL someone else. A small potted plant, a single blossom, a lunch for the homebound at noon, these may brighten time and companion it with shared blessings. One woman, whenever she heard of the illness of a neighbor, would quickly stitch up a gay-colored apron, and would take it to the homebound woman, saying, "Hurry up and get well. 'This apron needs some wear." And there a7 is much a new apron can do by way of encouragement in a gray interval of time. We are of those spirits who once accepted with rejoicing the gift of time, the opportunity of life upon the earth. We are among those spirits who have been given direction by precept and by command to use well the gift of time, which is life, here and hereafter. —V. P. C.
