THE MOTHER AND THE WIFE OF GEORGE WASHINGTON

By FAY OLLERTON

George Washington was blessed with two wonderful women in his life—his mother, Mary Ball Washington, and his wife, Martha Dandridge Custis Washington. Both played important roles in shaping the character of the man who was to become the Father of his Country.

Mary Ball Washington was born in 1708 in Lancaster County, Virginia. She was left an orphan at an early age and was reared by relatives. In 1731 she married Augustine Washington, a planter and land owner. George was born February 22, 1732, the first child of this union.

Mary Washington was a woman of strong character and deep religious convictions. She was strict in the training of her children, but her discipline was tempered with love and understanding. She taught George the principles of honesty, industry, and reverence for God that were to guide him throughout his life.

When Augustine Washington died in 1743, Mary was left with five young children to rear. George was only eleven years old. The responsibility of managing the plantation and caring for her family fell upon her shoulders, and she met it with courage and determination.

Mary Washington lived to see her son become the leader of the American forces in the Revolutionary War and the first President of the United States. She died in 1789, just a few months after George's inauguration.

Martha Dandridge was born June 2, 1731, in New Kent County, Virginia. At the age of eighteen she married Daniel Parke Custis, a wealthy planter. Two children were born to this union—John Parke Custis and Martha Parke Custis. When Daniel Custis died in 1757, Martha was left a wealthy widow at the age of twenty-six.

It was in 1758 that Martha met Colonel George Washington. Their courtship was brief, and they were married January 6, 1759. George was twenty-seven and Martha was twenty-eight.

Martha proved to be an ideal wife for George Washington. She was a gracious hostess, a capable manager of household affairs, and a devoted companion. During the long years of the Revolutionary War, she spent the winter months with her husband at Valley Forge and other military camps, sharing the hardships of army life.

When George Washington became President, Martha assumed the role of First Lady with dignity and grace. She established many of the customs and traditions that are still followed in the White House today.

Martha Washington died May 22, 1802, at Mount Vernon. She had been a faithful companion to George Washington for forty years, and her influence upon his life and character was immeasurable.

Both Mary Ball Washington and Martha Custis Washington were women of exceptional character who contributed greatly to the development of one of America's greatest leaders. Their influence upon George Washington helped to mold him into the man who could lead a nation to independence and guide it through its early years as a republic.
