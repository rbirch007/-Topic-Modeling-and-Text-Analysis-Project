The Japanese Theatre
By Mrs.
Elbert D. Thomas
A word by way of explanation
is due you before I begin.
It
is thirteen years since our return from Japan and things may be
different now.
Also I am not, and never have been, a student of the drama in
any land.
In my youth I indulged in amateur acting, thus forever
disqualifying myself from serious, disinterested study of the stage.
During my
travels
I have always been
interested
in
things
connected with the ancient and modern theater.
It has been my
good fortune to have seen much
; but
it has been my bad fortune
to have been so ill-prepared with proper background in history and
in
literature
that
I
couldn't
fully appreciate many
of
the things
that
I did
see.
For me the playhouse has always been a place of entertain-
ment, and not study.
I enjoy nearly everything I see, from a Mack
Sennett Comedy up or down.
We were in Japan during a period of mighty transition.
Most
things in respect to Japanese culture and
life were in a condition
of flux.
The great wave towards the Europeanization of every-
thing had passed and Japan had
settled back
to an appreciation
of her own
; but at the same time, she could not avoid European
influences.
The Imperial Theater, which was
destroyed by the
earthquake and
fire of 1922 was conceived,
built,
dedicated, and
in constant use for two full years during our stay in that country.
When viewed from
the
standpoint
of
the
stage
itself
this
theater was probably the greatest one ever built.
It had in
it the
best that the West has been able to evolve
; in addition to that,
it
retained such features from the oriental playhouse as the revolving
stage the hanamichi (the runway which extends from the stage to
the audience), that great adjunct to the stage proper, which permits
the action of the play to assume in a sense, an extra direction.
To
make
this
clear
let me
call your
attention
to
the
fact
that
the
European stage permits of a right, a
left, a back, and an up and
down direction.
But the footlights bar a forward direction.
You
will remember when William
Gillette
played
Sherlock
Holmes,
that he used what was then a novel bit of new business, by coming
in a darkened room down to the front of the stage and describing
for you, the imaginary wall and the one window which he tells you
is
there.
That
gives
the
extra
direction,
which
the
Western
stage may suggest but which the Eastern stage has.
I'm not unmindful of the fact that entrances were permitted
from the front in Shakespeare's time and in the pre-Shakespearian
period, also that our modern musical comedies and
girl plays use

I doubt whether we have ever had such an example of the
meeting of two highly developed cultures—cultures, too, that have
developed the striking contrasts that were found and made to har-

East and West met there, with-

The Japanese accomplished this by

each chair had an adjustable bottom, which, when pulled out, made

In numbers of other ways the Eastern and the Western stage
met and mingled in the Imperial Theatre.
In the same perform-
ance one might enjoy a scene from Shakespeare in
translation,
acted very well, or an American or European society drama, or a
comedy of manners, played and staged as we would do in our coun-
try, along with a No
(classic) or Jidai-mono (Japanese historical
play).
When Western plays were produced, the orchestra, playing
Western tunes and wearing full dress clothes, performed in the pit
as
it does in our plays.
When the Japanese plays were produced,
the orchestra was on the
stage, dressed in Japanese
clothes and
performing on Japanese instruments.
There was a splendid co-mingling in the restaurants and the
drinking rooms of the theater, where one could buy beef-steakie
or poru-ka-chopo and eat them with either knives and forks or a
Japanese bento (consisting of cold compressed rice, raw fish, smelly
pickles) with chop sticks.
(Wooden chop
sticks, always accom-
panied by toothpicks, are furnished with each lunch bought.)
I
shall not describe the co-mingling of drinks or drinkers.
It has
been assumed by all writers,
I think, that that has been a common
practice since even before the days that Henrick Hudson
is said
to have traded a barrel of whiskey for half of Manhattan.
Let us turn to the real Japanese plays.
To understand properly anything Japanese, one must realize
the fact that the Japanese civilization in one sense is very primitive
in that it retains many features that are found only among peoples
who have developed on the fringes of great world cultures.
At
the same time Japan has had cultural contacts with the world's
mightiest civilizations—the Chinese, the Hindu, the European.
From
all
these, Japan has taken much, so much that many
thoughtless writers have said that the Japanese are not creators but
imitators, a statement which must be condemned
; for the Japanese
have contributed much to the culture of the world.
When they
adopt something from
other
lands,
they
invariably Japanize
it.
This has been so generally done that we must use the adjective
Japanese when we speak of Buddhism or Confucianism, or Chris-
tianity;
as Japanese Buddhism, Japanese Confucianism, or Jap-
anese
Christianity.
A description of the Japanese drama must therefore include
a description of their primitive mask and pantomime plays, as well
as the highly developed modern drama.
In other words one may
see
in Japan today
practically every type of theatrical perform-
ance
that
the
various
world's
civilizations
have
produced;
for
Japanese culture retains the extremely primitive as
it accepts the
ultra modern.

to

day,
