Horse Racing Discontinued
As we dictate this editorial, it appears that the racing at Lagoon, which has furnished an opportunity for so much betting, which is gambling, is probably brought to an end. This news will ease the minds of countless mothers, some wives, and, what is more regrettable, perhaps some husbands. A good deal has been said about the conduct of young people. The retort is not infrequent from persons addressed that their concern is not so much over the young people as over married


people, who can hardly be classed as youths, but who, as fathers and mothers, indulge frequently in very unseemly conduct. Many people have been shocked beyond expression to discover that in our communities, where we are wont to boast of a Christian civilization, there are wives and mothers betting on horse races. Only last week we heard a rather young married woman declare that she had not missed a single horse race this season. She added, "Of course the fun is betting on the horses." Last evening we heard another woman in the presence of her husband say she was "broke," and she gave as her reason that she had just come from the horse races. Relief from this evil appears to have come late, but the old adage asserts itself once more: "Better late than never."
Correction
At the April Conference of the Relief Society Sister Lulu Y. Smith, counselor to Sister Lizzie B. Owen, made an address in the Theological Department. Sister Owen, the president of Cache Stake, was announced on the program for the address, but owing to a death in the family Sister Smith appeared in her place. The Conference notes followed the published program, consequently Sister Owen's name appeared instead of Sister Smith's name. The address of Sister Smith was greatly appreciated and we wish to acknowledge the error in the notes.
