Lund I watched the fluttering sparrows eat their fill Of pyracantha berries when the chill Of winter held earth in its frozen grip, And felt a joyous thnll I did not clip The berried branches as a center spray For my Thanksgiving table that bleak day. —_- Prayer Preface Dorothy J. Roberts May bared branches utter the naked heart As unashamed as leafless limbs their lineage cry, Lettering in charcoal for the land to read, Their past, in parable, printed on the sky; On every lost and fallen leaf, its scar. As boughs have written their dark roots of prayer, Holding the shapes of harps upon the air To windy plucking and their twigs to star, Let me emerge revealed, as winter's tree, Wittten and drained, to be filled by thee. Jools for Your Cl ild Helen B. Morris 66 HERE'S no use spending a lot of money for toys," one mother complained. "The children never play with them anyway." Actually, from my experience as a mother and as a former teacher, I feel it is just as important to provide your child with proper play equipment as it is to buy his clothing and furnish him with a balanced diet. Play is the means by which the child expresses himself and the way he learns about the adult world into which he is growing. Proper self-expression feeds his soul and nurtures a radiant, well-adjusted personality. A busy child is happy. Play Equipment Should Include Toys Which Encourage Creativity Building blocks are, perhaps, the most important single toy a child can own. 'They provide him with countless hours of enjoyment from the time he begins to toddle about until he is eight or nine years old. Blocks become towers or trains. They may become corrals or highways.
