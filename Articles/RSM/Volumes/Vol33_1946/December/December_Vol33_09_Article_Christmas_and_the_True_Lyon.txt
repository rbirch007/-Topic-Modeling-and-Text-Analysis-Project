Christmas and the True Christian Spirit Elder T. Edgar Lyon HE festive season known as Christmas is more universally celebrated by Christians than any other seasonal _ tradition.
Although it is usually thought of as the birthday of the Savior, both historical evidence and modern revelation indicate that Jesus of Nazareth was born in the springtime. In the early centuries, the birth of the Savior was celebrated in various places in January, March, April, and May. Apparently, the celebration of Christ's birth was established on December 25th because of pagan influence.
Prior to the Christian era, this day and others, a few days before and after it, had been celebrated by pagan peoples as the feast of the winter solstice or the birthday of Mithras, one of the most popular pagan deities. It appears that Christian leaders finally decided, after attempting to suppress these pagan feasts, without success, to designate December 25th as the birthday of Christ.
Many ancient pagan customs, such as the use of holly and mistletoe, the yule log, lighted trees, spiced punches, and fruitcake, were kept as part of the celebration, because they were such powerful social traditions, and the new symbolism gave to them Christian meanings. In America, the celebration of Christmas became further complicated and farther removed from the real spirit of Christianity through the addition of the mystical figure of Saint Nicholas, or Santa Claus, as he has come to be popularly designated.
