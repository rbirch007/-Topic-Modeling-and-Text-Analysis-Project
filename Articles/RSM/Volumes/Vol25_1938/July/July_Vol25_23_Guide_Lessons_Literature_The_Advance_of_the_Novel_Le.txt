In 1702 Defoe published an article which caused him to be placed in the pillory and later in Newgate prison where he associated with criminals and adventurers who had been in all parts of the world. It is thought that from them he gathered much of the material which he later placed in his novels which he wrote with great rapidity even though he was more than forty-five years of age when he began upon them. He died in 1731, "a real or fancied fugitive from the public."
The English novel with Defoe got started upon its glorious trail toward the stars. According to Professor Phelps, if Defoe had never written his masterpiece, Robinson Crusoe, he would still be the first novelist among English people. Pamela, by Richardson, is often referred to as the first novel, but as has been inferred, it was so named by those who make a sharp division between romance and the realistic novel. In this course, we are not doing that; we are following more or less the easy trail marked out by Professor Phelps.
Suggestions
1. Read chapters I and II, "The Advance of the English Novel," by William Lyon Phelps. Read also, if it should happen to be available, Development of the English Novel, by W. L. Cross, (Macmillan), or any other such book that may be at hand.
2. Give some definitions of a novel. It may be that you can think of a better one than any given here.
3. Comment upon the first sentence found in chapter two of the Advance of the English Novel: "Queen Anne brought prose fiction from heaven or hell to earth, and gave us the novel." The men of Queen Anne were Defoe, Richardson, Smollett, etc.
4. Perhaps class members could be assigned the pleasant task of presenting briefly the lives of two of our first novelists:
(1) Defoe. (2) Richardson.
Sketches could be found in any library and in some school texts and encyclopedias.
