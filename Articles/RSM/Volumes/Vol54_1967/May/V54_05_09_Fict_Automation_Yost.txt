As she sat on the stool, Grandma Frankum started traveling on memory trails.... "Winnie girl, come help your old Grandma with the clothes baskets. We'll go down to the river and get the washing done." Winnie Kimport had planned to lie on the grass and braid clover blossoms, and just watch the cloud patterns in the blue, blue sky. But her lazy, lovely plans just never worked out. Children were made to fetch and carry, and today was the day to help Grandma Kimport with the washing at the river. "It's only a jump and a step 336 to the river,' Grandma said encouragingly. Winnie, following closely at her heels, counted the steps. There were one thousand nine hundred and three good-sized steps to the river. Winnie's job was largely to fetch and carry. Grandma Kimport took pains with her wash, and her red, bleeding knuckles were proof of it. That they healed from one wash to another was a sheer miracle. When each article was whiter than white, she wrung it out and handed it to Winnie to spread on the rocks by the river bank. A second memory trail led to the many days Winnie helped her mother on washday. It was summer, and the wash could be done outside, which kept the house cool and free from washday steam. The water had to be hauled from the spring, and heated over an open fire in a large caldron. Mother's homemade soap gave forth a lye odor as it bubbled over and through the clothes. Winnie's job was to stir the clothes as they boiled, and, occasionally, raise them high out of the water to inspect the cleanness of the product. Winnie's mother, also, took pride in her wash, and when the clothes were whiter than white, they were dipped out of the boiling water, rinsed in three different cold waters, and hung on the line to dry. One day the clothesline broke. The clothes then had to be gathered from the dusty, dirty ground and redone. It wouldn't have been so bad, but the water from the wash was all poured out. They had had to start hauling water again, and gathering chips for a fire. Summer washdays were' pleasanter than winter even so. In winter, clothes hung on lines from door to door. To walk from room to room was a duck-and-dive process. Then, too, the steam from the drying clothes formed moisture on the windows, which froze solid. A memory trail led Winnifred Frankum to the glorious day in the Kimport family when Papa brought a gas engine home. Its main purpose was to pump the water from the well. But Papa figured out a belt system in which the gas engine could be used to run the washer, both the agitator and the wringer. This device had changed their lives completely. "Mama, Winnie is a big girl now, and I'll leave Burt to help her and they can do the wash for you. That way you can stay in and care for the little ones and get dinner." Papa tumed directly to Winnie, and asked: "Winnie, you do know how to wash, don't you?" "Yes, Papa." Winnie knew how to wash. As long as she could remember she had been helping with the wash. Second-in-command, you might say. Now she was being promoted, commander-in-chief. The gas engine and the belt were a trial, but even so, the Kimports had it lots nicer than most of their neighbors who had to run the agitator and wringer by hand.... When summer came hot and dry, Grandma Frankum had lawn that was lush and lovely, because she hadn't wasted a mite of water while she was using the automatic washer. She smiled happily as she walked to the grove and saw the fine picnic tables all ready and waiting. The shade was just right, enough to shield a person from the hot rays of the sun, and not dense enough to be chilly. "My family will have a reunion to remember. I can hardly wait for the day." Now the big day Grandma Frankum had looked forward to had arrivedthe family reunion. Grandma Frankum's family came from far and near. Sux counted her posterity. There were seven children, four daughters and three sons, and doubling that with marriage made fourteen. There were thirty-one grandchildren, and all but four were married, which made fifty-eight of the third generation. The fourth generation al- _ready numbered twenty-two little folks, and there were good prospects for more.... God had said to multiply and replenish the earth. Grandma Frankum was proud of her posterity. They were all physically well, spiritually sound, and financially progressive. But her granddaughter Karen and her husband John Wilcox had had some setbacks and were 338 struggling to keep their heads above water.
