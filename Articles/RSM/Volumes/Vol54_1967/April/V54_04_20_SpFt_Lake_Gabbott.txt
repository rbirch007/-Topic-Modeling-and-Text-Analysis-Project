[Vol54 | April 1967 | Page 286 | Special Feature | Lake Country, England | Mabel Jones Gabbott]
Lake District is a circle, with a radius of fifteen miles from Lake Country, England the central point, largely within the county of Cumberland. It is sometimes called the Lake County, the Lakes, or Lakeland, and includes some of Westmorland and Lancashire Counties. These few hundred square miles contain most of the principal lakes of England. Some lakes have picturesque island groups; some have soft wooded banks; some show an open expanse with steep rockbound shores. Within the circle, beside Windermere, the largest lake, is the highest point in England, Scafell Pike. The mountains are no less beautiful than the lakes, with bold sweeping lines, unbroken by vegetation, often ending in sheer cliffs or crags. At the foot of the mountains are green valley floors and, in the lower parts, lovely woods. Not only in England, but far outside the confines of Great Britain, this Lake Country is known for its remarkable beauty. And here in the midst of the beauty of nature, William Wordsworth founded the Lake School of Poets, and laid down his theories of poetry, which he said had grown out of the soil and substance of the lakes and mountains, and out of the homely lives of the people of Cumberland and Westmorland. When Robert Browning whote, "O to be in England/Now that April's there," I wonder if he was remembering the beautiful Lake District in England. THE MIGHTY OAK AND I Laura M. Gowing Lowestoft, Suffolk, England You are tall and great and strong; You have been here ages, Long before this town began.! Shelter beneath your branches, Lean against your gnarled old frame: And though 1 am nearly three score years and ten, I feel but a child again As I measure Your years, against mine. You, too, must feel the same, When measured against his eternity: For he was, before the world began.
