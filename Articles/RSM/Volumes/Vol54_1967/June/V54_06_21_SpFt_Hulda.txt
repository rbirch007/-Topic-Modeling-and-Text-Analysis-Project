Strengthened testimonies, increased understanding of principles of the gospel as they have application in the lives of women, greater skill and effectiveness in performing household duties and in home management have been a part of the benefits that have come tc Relief Society members regardless of whether they lived in Norway, Austria, Guatemala, Korea, Australia, the Islands of the South Seas, Argentina, United States or in any of the other countries throughout the world where Relief Societies are located 57 in total. The skills learned in the homemaking program have been applied to the cultures of the respective localities in making a total of 1,361,894 articles of clothing, quilts, household furnishings, and non-sewed handicraft items. Through the social relations lessons which were correlated with those studied by the Melchizedek Priesthood, Latter-day Saint women have been able to see more clearly their responsibility to human relationships that originate in the home and extend into areas of Church and community living. Increasing numbers of sisters have responded enthusiastically to their study of the new cultural refinement course dealing with literature and fine arts which reflect and motivate the development of desirable attributes and characteristics to be emulated in the life of every Latter-day Saint woman. The greater percentage of sisters attending regular Relief Society meetings is indicative of their appreciation and acceptance of the enrichment available to them through the varied courses of study which are carefully coordinated and guided under the direction of the Church Correlation Committee. Bounteous rewards of enlarged abilities, talents, and testimonies have also come into the lives of 196,259 Relief Society members who have served as leaders in carrying on the program of the organization, in 837 stake, district, and mission organizations and 5,972 ward and branch units throughout the Church. 1966 Marked progress has been made in the quantity and particularly the quality of visiting done by 130,301 visiting teachers, who, as emissaries of Relief Society assigned in pairs, have made 4,704,379 nearly five millionvisits to families carrying with them a message of spiritual uplift and with a charge to observe any instance of need in the homes where they visited. Services given by Relief Society to the distressed and the aged have been far-reaching. A total of 753,371 hours of sympathetic, loveinspired service was given at the direction of Relief Society presidents, in addition to 395,181 visits to the sick and homebound, and also other services such as bedside nursing care and assistance to families at the time of death. At the direction of the Priesthood, 56,212 devoted women contributed 765,086 hours of service on Church Welfare projects, such as assisting at the canneries in processing vegetables, fruits, and meat products, and also in making clothing and household articles for the needy, As an aid to the bishops, Relief Society presidents made 99,512 visits and 86,812 other contacts to families needing assistance. Music continues as a vital aspect of the program of Relief Society. In addition to the development gained by its members through congregational singing in the regular meetings, 50,561 sisters have participated in 3,601 Singing Mothers choruses whose refining influence has, in turn, been carried over into their homes. The Relief Society Magazine, the Voice of Relief Society, which since June 1966 has been published in Spanish, as well as in English, is providing a direct channel of communication to thousands of LatinAmerican sisters. This marks a historic step forward in making available to this second largest language group in the Church the Magazine in their own tongue. This divinely established organization thus continues in its great mission of guiding and developing the women of Zion and aiding in building the kingdom of our Heavenly Father. She knew the townspeople were enjoying the situation immensely. Jed made a good impression. She needn't have worried about him. And while she toyed with the idea of purposely missing a word, her idea came too late. Jed missed one first. Tue bishop had long since given up the spelling book in favor of the dictionary. He was working through the "'o's" when Nora was startled to hear Jed say "obfuskate." He was reading from his paper, too. He, as several of the others, was aided by seeing the word, and it was ruled that this was fair, everyone being given the same right, to see the word in writing before spelling it orally, if he chose so to do. Immediately, as if. Correcting one of her pupils, Nora found herself saying, "obfuscate." She looked at Jed. No, she had not embarrassed him. His face was a big grin as she began receiving the first of many congratulations. And it was not until the next morning that what had seemed to her to have been almost a deliberate error proved to be just that. She found Jed's paper, crumpled and dropped on the floor where he had been. There was no mistaking the handwriting. She had seen too much of it for that. But there was the word, as plain as could be, obfuscate, yet he had spelled it orally with a "k." Nora's heart skipped a _ beat. Jed had deliberately misspelled the word. But why? Why had he been concerned for her, as she 460 had been for him, that there be no embarrassment? Perhaps he had felt that her embarrassment would be doubled before both parents and her students? Nora could not know. But she taught that day with a light step and in an especially happy frame of mind. Nora had found that within the small community of Banner there was a surprising number of people who could enrich the experiences of her students by sharing their own. As missionaries for their Church, the men of the town had traveled the world over. Nora had heard the children speaking of the wood carving done by Freedom Lang, and the idea had been growing in her mind to invite the old fellow to school to show his work and to talk about it. She had a double purpose, actually, desiring to do something that might help to draw this man, who must be very lonely, into the group life of the little village. She knew herself to be somewhat of an outsider, but not as he was, and she could not say '"'those among whom he lived," for he did not live among them. He came into town only when he absolutely had to have supplies. She mentioned her plan to Ben. "I'll have to see him right away or the school year will be ending," she said. "I wonder if you would drive me out there after school one night this week." "Old Free? Miss Blake! You're not really going to invite him to come to school!" "And why not? I've heard from several people about the beautiful things he carves. I think it would OO be very worthwhile to have him show them to the class and perhaps tell a bit about how he does it. Who knows? We may have someone in class who might be interested in learning to carve." "I know you, Miss Blake. You have someone in mind already. It's Joe, isn't it? Joe Pine?" Nora laughed. "You're too sharp, Ben. Yes, it is Joe. He has quite a talent for modeling with that clay we brought in from the ditch bank. I'd like to see what he could do with wood." Still Ben appeared reluctant to become a party to the plan. "I don't know," he said. "'I'd have to tell Jed where I was going, and I know he won't like it. Me going out there, or you The Golden Chain either." Nora caught her breath. There were times when she had been tempted to question Ben. She would have liked to ask, Does he ever talk about me, Ben? Does he' care at all? But always she rejected the impulse as not auite honorable. She kept her voice low as she said, "Ask him, Ben. Tell him we plan to go tomorrow after school." But the following day Ben was absent. Not to be dissuaded, Nora determined to walk. It was probably not more than three miles out to the Lang cabin. Then, as the last student left, Nora looked up to see Jed standing in the doorway.
