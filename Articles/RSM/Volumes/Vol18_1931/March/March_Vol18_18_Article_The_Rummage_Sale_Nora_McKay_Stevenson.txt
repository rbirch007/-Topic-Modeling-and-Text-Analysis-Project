The Rummage Sale
By Nora McKay Stevenson

THE Relief Society rummage sale! What visions the words conjure up! What memories they bring back! What experiences they recall!

To some women the rummage sale is a bugbear, a necessary evil to be endured for the sake of the cause. To others it is a delightful adventure, a treasure hunt, a social event of the first magnitude.

The rummage sale has its humorous side. There is the woman who brings a contribution and then spends the entire day of the sale buying back her own things at double the price she could have bought them for at the store. There is the sister who brings a dress that she "simply can't wear any more" and then discovers that her neighbor has bought it and looks perfectly stunning in it. There is the woman who contributes a hat that she has worn for three seasons and then sees it on the head of the bishop's wife, who declares it is "just the thing" she has been looking for.

Then there are the customers. The early bird who arrives before the doors are opened and who knows exactly what she wants and where to find it. The bargain hunter who goes through every article with the thoroughness of a detective, looking for hidden values. The browser who "just came to look" and who goes away with her arms full of purchases. The woman who comes to buy a dish towel and goes home with a complete outfit.

The workers at the rummage sale are a study in themselves. There is the efficient chairman who has everything organized to the last detail. There is the willing worker who doesn't know much about values but who is eager to help. There is the experienced hand who can spot a bargain at a glance and who knows just how to display the goods to the best advantage.

But after all, the rummage sale serves a real purpose. It provides a means whereby the Relief Society can raise funds for its charitable work. It gives the sisters an opportunity to clean out their closets and attics and to pass on to others things that they no longer need. It provides clothing and household articles at reasonable prices for those who cannot afford to buy new things.

And so, despite its humorous aspects, despite the work and worry it entails, the rummage sale continues to be one of the most popular and successful methods of raising funds for Relief Society work. Long may it flourish!
