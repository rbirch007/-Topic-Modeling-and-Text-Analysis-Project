These dishes were my particular trial because, no matter how far I put them back in the cupboard, they managed to escape occasionally and would show up on the table at the most inopportune times. Yet, regardless of my resolution to send chipped plates and cracked cups to the ash can, as soon as the damage occurred, I kept them around. Do not ask me why. Then I was explaining to Bob L., as he wiped the dishes for me, about my inability to throw away damaged dishes. "I would much rather," I told him, "have had that cracked cup that you drank from at dinner (how you happened to get it is beyond me) smashed in a dozen pieces than to have it just slightly cracked." "Would you really like to have the cup broken?" he asked. I assured him that I would.
Without further ado, he picked it up and solemnly dropped it emphatically to the floor, and then asked, "Got any more?" I had, and before I had time to waver he had sent two nicked plates, a handleless cream pitcher, and a soup dish, minus an ear, crashing after the tea cup. It takes high resolve and strong nerve to do such a deed, but you have a grand and glorious sense of achievement once the wreckage is cleared away. Need I tell you that Bob has a standing invitation to dinner!
