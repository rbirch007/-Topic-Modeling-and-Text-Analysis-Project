V. P. C. The Russells Did Not Go to Church CHAPTER 3 Edith Russell ENGLAND is so rarely prodigal of sunshine, I remember that beautiful morning as though it belonged only to yesterday. The sun spilled gold on the dusty windows of the ugly office building to which I daily turned my steps, and a great armada of white clouds sailed before a stiff breeze blowing in from the North Sea. I had no premonition of what the day might hold, beyond the usual galaxy of accounts, ledgers, the bevy of tired, middle-aged women making their contribution to the war effort, and another evening at home, reading before the fire while my mother knitted and waited expectantly for Antony's step on the path. At the office I took off my coat and prepared for the day. The sunshine lighted up the faces of my "school" of women; there were some eager and animated, others there were who were anxious and old. I knew how it felt when letters from the front were delayed. As I smiled and said "Good morning," the door of the classroom opened to admit the elderly person of the office manager followed by a younger and vigorous man in his late twenties. "Good morning, Miss Russell," the manager said. "Please to meet Mr. Gregory Miner, late of the King's Own Royal Rifles. I wish you would be good enough to give him some insight as to the function of your school. He is to learn the business and will need to know." Mr. Miner bowed. He was possessed of a fine healthy color, a somewhat stocky physique, and he exuded an atmosphere of fastidious cleanliness. I liked him at once, and as soon as the manager had retired, I gave him into the charge of a nice homely matron, at whose desk I could conveniently observe and instruct him. I was near enough to see that on his left hand he wore a most peculiar ringa great silver Indian heada rather vulgar trinket, I thought, on the person of an Englishman. It flashed in the sunlight and I wondered why he wore it and from where it came. It seemed a trifle incongruous compared to the quiet conservative quality of the rest of his appearance.. A little before lunch I approached his desk to inquire after his progress. 'The ring flashed silver in the morning sun and, smiling, he covered it with his hand. I smiled, too. "What an unusual ring!" I re marked, and he lifted it for my examination. "But here is one even more unusual," he volunteered, and from his pocket he withdrew a dull gold object, shaped like a shield and covered with peculiar hieroglyphics I could not understand. I had a momentary and entirely incomprehensible vision of the vicarage at St. Matthews and a woman called Hannah Shuttleworth with her illegitimate child, then a peculiar sensation of warmth suffused my whole being, the same one feels on beholding an old and intimate friend with a familiar greeting on his lips. I was six years removed, watching Michael, my brother, waiting for him to pick up a book my mother had forbidden; a book in which were lurid tales about a strange people called Mormons. That evening I was home late for dinner. The hungry Antony whooped for joy when I entered the cottage, but my mother was concerned lest my delay had been occasioned by anything disastrous. "Of course not, darling," I reassured her, as we sat down. "As a matter of fact" I was magnificently offhand"As a matter of fact, I have been talking to a Mormon in town." "You have beenwhat?" Horror expressed itself in every line of my mother's features. Antony helped himself to more vegetables and inquired: "You mean he was in a circus, or something?" I could feel my mother's eyes fixed upon me, but I avoided them and waived Antony's question. "He is a very charming fellow, Mother," I said anxiously. "I feel sure he is perfectly respectable. He is being taught the business at the office. And hehe has only one wife." I was concentrating upon the food on my plate. I heard my mother say in a cold, shocked voice: "How do you know?" I took a deep breath. "Because I asked him," I said, and my face burned at the memory. He had no conception of the extent of my disloyalty. 674 Luncheon, at home, that day, was an awkward session. Excitement had taken away my appetite, but I strove to eat so as not to irritate my mother further. Antony, intrigued by thoughts of someone having the temerity to "duck" his sister, asked embarrassing questions throughout the meal. But at last it was over. I escaped to my room and put the few things I should need into my bag. I could hear my mother dressing in her room. "Tm going, Mother!" I called. "Goodbye!" Her voice, a little strained and unfamiliar, came back to me. "Wait for me! I'm going into the city, too. We may as well go together." She appeared a moment later, and without further conversation, we went out into the street where the bus was waiting. The vehicle was very full and, though we sat together, we did not talk. My thoughts were filled with the wonder of what was going to happen to me. A part of me ached for the sweet woman beside me, but withal, I was gloriously happy.
