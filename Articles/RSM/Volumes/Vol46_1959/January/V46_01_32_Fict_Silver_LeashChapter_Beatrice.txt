 | Author: Beatrice Rordame Parsons]The Silver Leash—Chapter 1

By Beatrice Rordame Parsons

CHAPTER ONE

The train pulled into the station with a grinding of brakes and a hissing of steam. Passengers began to alight, and among them was a young woman who stepped down carefully, looking about her with curious, apprehensive eyes.

She was perhaps twenty-two or three, with dark hair drawn back smoothly under a small hat, and she wore a simple traveling dress of dark blue. Her face was pleasant rather than beautiful, with clear gray eyes and a mouth that looked as though it smiled easily, though just now it was grave with uncertainty.

She stood for a moment on the platform, a small suitcase in her hand, watching the other passengers hurry past her. Then she walked slowly toward the station building.

"Miss Carla Brent?" The voice was masculine and pleasant.

She turned quickly. A tall young man was approaching her, hat in hand. He had brown hair and eyes, and his face was bronzed as though he spent much time outdoors.

"Yes, I'm Carla Brent."

"I'm Jim Hadley. Mrs. Morrison sent me to meet you." He reached for her suitcase. "The car's just outside."

"Thank you." She fell into step beside him, stealing glances at his profile as they walked. So this was one of the Morrison ranch hands. He didn't look like her idea of a cowboy, somehow. There was something about him—a quiet assurance, an air of education—that didn't fit the picture she had formed.

The car was a rather battered sedan, but it was clean and ran smoothly as they drove through the small town and out onto a road that stretched ahead between rolling hills dotted with cattle.

"First time in this part of the country?" Jim asked.

"Yes. I've never been west of Chicago before."

"It's different from the East."

"Very different." She looked out at the vast expanse of range land, the distant mountains purple against the sky. "It's—overwhelming."

He glanced at her quickly. "You'll get used to it. Most people do."

"I hope so." But her voice was doubtful.

They drove in silence for a while. Then Jim said, "Mrs. Morrison is looking forward to having you. She's been pretty lonely since her daughter married and moved away."

"I hope I can be of help to her. The agency said she needed someone to help with correspondence and household accounts."

"That's right. The ranch keeps her pretty busy, and since her husband died two years ago, she's had to take over a lot of the business end of things."

Carla nodded, then asked hesitantly, "What—what is she like?"

"Mrs. Morrison? She's fine. You'll like her. She's got a lot of spunk for a woman her age, and she's fair in her dealings. The men all respect her."

The road began to climb now, winding between hills that were greener than the range they had left behind. Presently they topped a rise, and Jim pointed ahead.

"There's the ranch."

Carla looked and caught her breath. Below them lay a valley, green with pasture land and crossed by a stream that caught the afternoon sunlight. The ranch buildings were scattered along the stream—a large, rambling house, barns, corrals, and various outbuildings. It looked peaceful and prosperous, and somehow permanent, as though it had grown out of the earth itself.

"It's beautiful," she said softly.

"Yes, it is. The Morrisons have been here for thirty years. Built it up from nothing."

As they drove down into the valley, Carla could see men working around the corrals, and horses in the pastures. Everything looked busy and purposeful.

The house was larger than it had appeared from the hilltop—a long, low building of logs and stone, with a wide veranda across the front. Flower beds bordered the walk, and there were trees that must have been planted years ago, for they were large and gave welcome shade.

Jim stopped the car in front of the house, and as he did so, the front door opened and a woman came out onto the veranda.

She was perhaps fifty-five, with gray hair and a face that was still handsome despite the lines that years of sun and wind had etched around her eyes. She was tall and carried herself erectly, and there was something about her that spoke of strength and determination.

"You must be Carla," she said, coming down the steps with her hand extended. "I'm Helen Morrison. Welcome to the Silver Leash."

"Thank you, Mrs. Morrison. I'm very glad to be here."

Helen Morrison's handshake was firm, and her blue eyes were kind as they studied the girl's face. "You look tired. It's a long trip from New York. Jim, bring Miss Brent's things in, will you? I'll show her to her room."

The interior of the house was as pleasant as the outside had promised. The rooms were large and comfortable, furnished with pieces that looked as though they had been chosen for use rather than show, but which achieved a kind of beauty through their very simplicity and fitness.

"I hope you'll be comfortable here," Mrs. Morrison said as she led the way down a hall. "This will be your room."

It was a corner room with windows on two sides, furnished with a bed, dresser, and comfortable chair. The windows looked out over the valley, and Carla could see the stream and the pastures beyond.

"It's lovely," she said. "Thank you."

"Supper will be ready in about an hour. That will give you time to rest and freshen up. The bathroom is just across the hall."

When Mrs. Morrison had gone, Carla sat down on the bed and looked around her. The room was simple but pleasant, and through the open windows came the sound of running water and the lowing of cattle. It was very different from her small apartment in New York, with its view of brick walls and fire escapes.

She unpacked her few belongings and hung them in the closet, then sat down in the chair by the window. The sun was getting low now, and the mountains in the distance were turning purple and gold. It was beautiful, but it was also strange and a little frightening.

What was she doing here, so far from everything she had known? The job had seemed like a godsend when the agency had offered it to her—a chance to get away from New York and the memories that haunted her there, a chance to start over in a new place. But now that she was actually here, she wondered if she had made a mistake.

A knock at the door interrupted her thoughts. "Come in," she called.

It was Mrs. Morrison. "I thought you might like some coffee," she said, carrying in a tray with a pot and cups. "And I wanted to talk to you a little before supper."

"That's very kind of you."

Mrs. Morrison poured the coffee and handed Carla a cup, then sat down in the other chair. "I want you to feel at home here," she said. "I know it's different from what you're used to, but I hope you'll give it a chance."

"I will, Mrs. Morrison. I'm grateful for the opportunity."

"The agency told me a little about your background, but I'd like to hear more about you, if you don't mind."

Carla hesitated, then said quietly, "There isn't much to tell. I worked for a law firm in New York for three years, doing secretarial work and bookkeeping. When—when I decided I wanted a change, the agency suggested this position."

Mrs. Morrison studied her face. "Running away from something?"

The direct question startled Carla, and she felt color rise in her cheeks. "I—yes, I suppose I am."

"Want to talk about it?"

"Not—not just yet, if you don't mind."

"Of course not. When you're ready." Mrs. Morrison's voice was gentle. "We all have things we'd rather forget. The important thing is to go on from here."

"Yes," Carla said gratefully. "That's what I want to do."

"Good. Now, about your work here. As Jim probably told you, I need help with correspondence and accounts. The ranch business has grown quite a bit in the last few years, and I find I can't keep up with all the paperwork. Can you handle that sort of thing?"

"Yes, I think so. I did similar work in New York."

"Fine. We'll start tomorrow, and you can see what's involved. Don't worry if it seems confusing at first—ranch business is different from city business, but you'll catch on."

They talked for a few more minutes about the work and the routine of the ranch, and then Mrs. Morrison rose to go. "Supper will be ready in a few minutes," she said. "Just come to the dining room when you hear the bell."

When she had gone, Carla finished her coffee and then went to the mirror to tidy her hair. She looked pale and tired, but there was something in her eyes that hadn't been there for months—a spark of hope, perhaps, or at least of interest in what lay ahead.

The supper bell rang, and she made her way to the dining room. It was a large, pleasant room with a long table and chairs that looked as though they could seat a dozen people comfortably. Tonight, however, there were only three places set.

Mrs. Morrison was already there, and with her was Jim Hadley. He had changed from his work clothes into clean slacks and a white shirt, and Carla noticed again that air of quiet assurance about him.

"I asked Jim to join us tonight," Mrs. Morrison explained. "He can tell you something about the ranch and the country around here."

The meal was simple but well-prepared—roast beef, vegetables from the ranch garden, and hot biscuits. As they ate, Jim told her about the ranch operations, the cattle, the seasonal work that kept the men busy.

"We run about two thousand head," he said. "Most of them are Herefords, though we're experimenting with some crossbreeding. The ranch covers about fifteen thousand acres, so there's plenty of range."

"It sounds very complicated," Carla said.

"It is, in some ways. But it's a good life, if you like the outdoors and don't mind hard work."

"Jim is my foreman," Mrs. Morrison explained. "He's been here for five years now, and I don't know what I'd do without him."

Jim smiled. "Mrs. Morrison is prejudiced. She thinks anyone who can stay on a horse and count cattle is indispensable."

"Don't let him fool you," Mrs. Morrison said to Carla. "He has a degree in animal husbandry from the state university, and he knows more about cattle than most men twice his age."

Carla looked at Jim with new interest. So her first impression had been right—there was more to him than appeared on the surface.

After supper, they sat on the veranda for a while, watching the stars come out in the clear mountain air. The night sounds were different from those Carla was used to—the distant lowing of cattle, the call of a night bird, the whisper of wind in the trees.

"It's so quiet," she said.

"You'll get used to it," Mrs. Morrison assured her. "After a while, you'll find the city seems noisy and confusing."

"I hope so." But again there was that note of doubt in her voice.

Jim looked at her curiously. "You don't sound very sure about staying."

"I—I want to stay. It's just that everything is so different."

"Give it time," he said gently. "Change is never easy, but sometimes it's necessary."

There was something in his voice that made her look at him more closely, but his face was in shadow and she couldn't read his expression.

Presently Mrs. Morrison rose. "I think I'll turn in," she said. "Tomorrow will be a busy day. Carla, you don't need to get up early—say, eight o'clock for breakfast. That will give you time to get oriented."

"Thank you. Good night, Mrs. Morrison."

"Good night, my dear."

When she had gone into the house, Jim and Carla sat in silence for a few minutes. Then he said, "She's a fine woman. She's had a hard time since her husband died, but she's never complained."

"She seems very capable."

"She is. But she's lonely, too. Having you here will be good for her."

"I hope I can be of help."

"I'm sure you can." He paused, then added, "If you need anything, or if you have any problems, don't hesitate to ask. Mrs. Morrison or myself—we'll be glad to help."

"Thank you. You've both been very kind."

He rose to go. "Well, I'd better turn in too. Morning comes early on a ranch. Good night, Carla."

"Good night."

She sat alone on the veranda for a while longer, listening to the night sounds and looking up at the stars. They seemed brighter here than they ever had in the city, and there were so many more of them.

Tomorrow she would begin her new life. She hoped she would be able to make it work, to find here the peace and purpose that had been missing from her life for so long. The past was behind her now—she must look ahead.

But as she finally rose and went into the house, she couldn't quite shake the feeling that the past had a way of catching up, no matter how far one ran.
