The Magazine Honor Roll for 1944
Vera W. Pohlman, Former General Secretary-Treasurer. THE momentum of the 1943
Magazine subscription campaign, when 13,000 new subscriptions were added, carried over into 1944, resulting in a further increase of more than 4,300 additional subscriptions. By the end of the year the subscription list had reached a new high figure of 71,200, an increase over the 66,790 subscriptions reported at the end of 1943 of 4,210 or six per cent.
More Than 1000 Names On Honor Roll
This year we are not reproducing the pictograph which for the two previous years were published (August 1943 and June 1944) to portray for each stake and mission the rate of Magazine subscriptions in relation to Relief Society members. As usual, however, we are recognizing in the accompanying honor roll all stakes, missions, wards, and branches, whose subscription rates in relation to number of Relief Society members equal or exceed 75 per cent. The 1944 honor roll contains more names than ever before?1020, an increase of 22 per cent over the 847 in 1943 and of 159 per cent over the 293 in 1942. The honor roll is arranged in alphabetical order and the name of the Magazine representative appears opposite the name and record of each Relief Society listed. In those instances where no one is appointed as Magazine representative, the name of the president appears. Great credit is due the supervisory stake, mission district, and mission Magazine representatives, and all of the ward and branch Magazine representatives who have done the the ward and branch Magazine work of procuring subscriptions with the assistance and support of Relief Society officers and members. As an expression of its appreciation, the General Board is again awarding a free, one-year Magazine subscription to all local representatives who procured subscriptions equal to 75 per cent of the ward or branch Relief Society enrollment as of the end of 1944, and to all stake, mission district, and mission Magazine representatives appearing on the honor roll. The Magazine honor roll is based on subscription reports submitted to the General Board. However, Lethbridge and Oahu Stakes, and the British, East Central (except North Carolina District which submitted a report), Hawaiian, South African, and Texas Missions (of the English-speaking missions) failed to submit subscription reports for 1944. Also, a few districts of some of the reporting missions failed to respond?3 in the California Mission, 3 in the Northern States, 1 in Northwestern States, and 1 in the Southern States. Reference to the annual Relief Society reports compiled by the secretaries indicated that some of these unreported stakes and missions had local Relief Societies with rates entitling them to a position on the honor roll and they were, therefore, included. However, none is included from the British Mission from which neither report had been received by press time. 100

Many of these stakes nearly made the honor roll last year but in striving for honor roll position this year, several recorded considerable gains during 1944; for example, Uintah Stake which boosted its 1943 rate of 69 per cent to 101 per cent; Park Stake which rose from 74 to 103 per cent; Salt Lake from 65 to 88 per cent; Oakland from 66 to 86 per cent; Emery from 61 to 80 per cent; Sugar House from 56 to 75 per cent; Southern Arizona and San Bernardino both of which advanced from 71 to 89 per cent. Cottonwood Stake, also in this list, is mentioned in the "Notes From the Field" in this issue as having risen from a 1942 rate of 26 per cent?one of the lowest in the Church?to 90 per cent in 1944, a noteworthy accomplishment in a short period of time. Achievements of Missions All three of the missions which appeared on the 1943 honor roll-Australia, Western Canadian, and Western States?maintained their positions in 1944. Southern States, 88 per cent; Northern States, 82 per cent. The number of mission districts (comparable in function to stakes) appearing on the honor roll increased from 11 in 1943 to 24 in 1944. Four of these 11 districts (Bakersfield in the California Mission, North Indiana and North Michigan in the Northern States Mission, and Great Falls in the Northwestern States Mission) failed to maintain their honor-roll rating. The other 7 districts which maintained their honor-roll status are: South Coast in California Mission; Central Ohio, North and South Illinois, South Ohio, and Wisconsin, in Northern States Missions; and Central Florida and South Georgia in the Southern States Mission. The 17 mission districts appearing on the honor roll for the first time this year are: Santa Barbara, San Gorganio, and Mojave of the California Mission; Central Indiana, East Michigan and West Michigan of the Northern States Mission; Northern Montana of Northwestern States Mission; Alabama, Florida, Georgia, Mississippi, South Carolina, South Florida, South Georgia, West Florida, and West South Carolina of the Southern States Mission, and South Louisiana of the Texas Mission. Subscriptions From Non-ranking Societies Also Appreciated In addition to the stakes, missions, and mission districts whose local Relief Societies attained rates sufficient to place the entire area on the honor roll, 63 stakes and 10 missions are represented not by a rating for the stake or mission as a whole, but by one or more of their local Relief Societies which attained honor-roll status. Accordingly, a total of 134 stakes (all but 14) and of 16
English-speaking missions (all but 2) are represented on the 1944 honor roll by one or more local Societies. It is regretted that a few (Beaver, Panguitch, South Ogden, and Moon Lake Stakes, and the Hawaiian Mission) which were so represented in 1943 by high-ranking local Societies are not represented by even one ward or branch this year. Nevertheless, we are mindful and appreciative of the thousands of subscriptions which have helped to swell the total subscription list but which were obtained by local Relief Societies whose individual rates did not entitle them to a place on the honor roll. It is hoped that the accomplishments of the year 1944 will encourage these Relief Societies to renew their efforts during 1945 and match the achievements of their sister Societies.
