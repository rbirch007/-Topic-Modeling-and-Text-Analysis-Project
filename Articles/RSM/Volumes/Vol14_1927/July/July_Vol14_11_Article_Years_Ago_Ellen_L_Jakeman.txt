THE 24TH OF JULY, 56 YEARS AGO
By Ellen L. Jakeman

I was born in Salt Lake City, July 24th, 1871, the twenty-fourth anniversary of the entrance of the pioneers into the valley. I have often been told that I was the first baby born on that anniversary, and that I was taken to the celebration in the afternoon. I have always felt that this was quite an honor.

I remember the celebration of 1876, when I was five years old. The celebration was held in the old Tabernacle. I remember the beautiful decorations, the flags and bunting, and the great crowd of people. I was dressed in a white dress with blue ribbons, and I felt very important.

In those days the celebration was a real community affair. Everyone took part in it. The children all had new clothes for the occasion, and the mothers spent weeks preparing for it. The celebration lasted all day, beginning with a sunrise salute and ending with a grand ball in the evening.

I remember the parades of those early days. They were wonderful affairs. All the different wards took part, and each tried to outdo the others in the beauty of their floats. The children all marched, carrying flags and singing patriotic songs.

One thing that impressed me very much was the fact that in those days it was quite unusual to find a girl of sixteen years of age who does not smoke cigarettes or drink liquor, or as yet as not bobbed her hair.
