Romaine's  hands  were  busy 
smoothing  sheets  and  bed  clothes, 
but  her  mind  was  prematurely  busy, 
carving  a  soap  figurine. 
"Momie,  come  and  see."  Kim 
and  Kent  rushed  into  the  bedroom, 
and  were  swishing  her  skirts.  "We 
have  a  surprise!  Come  see  the  sur- 
prise! "  They  hurried  her  to  the 
utility  room. 
"Look,  Momie,  we  helped  you. 
We  cut  the  soap  in  bars,"  they 
chorused.  "It's  all  ready  for  wash- 
day." 
"Yes,  darlings,  the  soap  is  all 
ready  for  washday."  Romaine  man- 
aged to  keep  her  voice  calm  while 
her  heart  thudded.  She  hurried 
from  the  house,  lest  the  children 
see  the  tears  she  could  not  control. 
Later,  she  would  gather  up  the 
crudely  cut  bars  of  homemade  soap, 
and  store  them  away  for  washing. 
Outside,  the  birds  sang  in  the 
hedges  and  trees,  or  darted  over 
fields  on  either  side  of  her.  Neither 
her  children  nor  the  birds  knew 
that  her  hopes  and  dreams  had  been 
cut  in  pieces  by  several  gashes  of  a 
butcher  knife. 
T17ASHDAY  and  ironing  day  came 
and  went,  but  Romaine  could 
think  of  nothing  but  having  some- 
thing lovely  to  enter  in  the  sculp- 
ture division  at  the  county  fair. 
There  wasn't  any  more  grease  to 
make  soap  and,  anyway,  it  was  too 
dangerous  to  repeat  with  the  chil- 
dren always  underfoot. 
Then  one  morning  Sam  came  in 
from  milking  and  said,  "Romaine, 
the  hay  is  all  up,  and  there's  a  lit- 
tle time  now,  I  think  I'll  borrow  a 
mixer  and  get  some  cement  and 
sand  mixed,  and  pour  the  forms 
and  floor  for  the  chicken  coop." 
Romaine  looked  at  Sam  as  he 
stood  in  the  doorway.  Sam  wouldn't 
be  classed  as  handsome,  but  he 
certainly  was  attractive,  even  in 
work  clothes,  with  his  shock  of 
thick  dark  hair  and  his  features  lean 
and  sharply  defined.  What  had  he 
said?  Pour  cement  for  a  coop. 
Why!  Why!  Romaine's  mind  was 
revolving  like  a  second  hand.  She 
remembered  that  Miss  Rembrant, 
her  art  teacher,  had  told  of  a  man 
in  Florida  who  did  all  of  his  carv- 
ings in  cement.  Not  hardened 
completely,  just  set-up  cement.  If 
Sam  poured  the  cement  for  the 
coop  now,  she  could  use  some  of 
it  and  carve  her  figurine  for  the 
fair! 
"What  do  you  think  about  going 
ahead  with  the  cement  now?"  Sam 
asked  again. 
"Sam,  I  think  that's  a  wonderful 
idea,  pouring  the  cement  now,  I 
mean."  Inwardly  Romaine's  heart 
was  pounding  with  excitement,  but 
she  thought  it  best  not  to  bring  up 
her  plans  about  the  leftover  cement. 
She  and  Sam  had  many  things  in 
common,  but  one  of  them  wasn't 
a  love  of  art. 
Sam,  seeing  the  sparkle  and  ex- 
citement in  her  eyes,  put  his  arms 
tenderly  around  her.  "Gee,  honey, 
I  knew  you  wanted  a  chicken  coop, 
but  I  didn't  know  it  meant  this 
much  to  you.  I'll  get  things  around 
right  away."
