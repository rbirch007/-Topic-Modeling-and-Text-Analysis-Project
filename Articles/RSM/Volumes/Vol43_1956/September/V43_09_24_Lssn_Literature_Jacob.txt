Objective:     To  feel  more  profoundly  the  peculiar  power  and  mystery  of  Shake- 
speare's poetry  that  we  might  read  his  works  with  enlarged  understandings  and  hearts. 
\  new  approach  to  Shakespeare 
has  been  the  major  contribution 
of  20th  century  scholarship.  It  holds 
that  plot  and  character  are  impor- 
tant but  secondary:  the  dramatic 
intensity  of  the  play  itself  is  best 
sensed  in  the  dense  verbal  texture 
of  the  words  as  spoken  by  Shake- 
speare's characters.  The  emotion 
lying  deep  within  the  situation  is 
evoked  by  the  words,  not  by  plot 
or  character,  which  themselves  come 
to  the  reader  through  Shakespeare's 
poetic  concentration  and  imagery. 
We  cannot  remind  ourselves  too 
often  or  earnestly,  then,  that  Shake- 
speare the  poet,  is  the  very  heart  of 
Shakespeare  the  dramatist.  To  un- 
derstand Shakespeare  we  must  con- 
stantly work  downward  from  the 
surface  of  his  poetry,  phrase,  image, 
and  symbol.  In  his  words  lies  the 
key. 
The  Nature  of  Poetry 
Macaulay  says,  "By  poetry  we 
mean  the  art  of  employing  words  in 
such  a  manner  as  to  produce  an 
illusion  on  the  imagination,  the  art 
of  doing  by  means  of  words  what 
the  painter  does  by  means  of  color/'

Hallmarks  of  poetry  are  brilliance, 
freshness,  power,  condensation.  It 
has  a  high  singing  all  its  own  which 
can  no  more  be  defined  than  de- 
nied, as  in: 
When  the  sweet  wind  did  gently  kiss  the 
trees 
And  they  did  make  no  noise. 
Merchant  of  Venice,  V.  2.3 
These  beautifully  simple  lines 
catch  the  tranquil  silence  as  only 
lovers  of  nature  can  hear  it.  Like- 
wise it  is  the  words  which  reveal 
Macbeth's  fears  that  Duncan, 
whom  he  is  to  murder,  had  lived 
so  meek  and  clear  a  life  as  king 
that:

Here  Shakespeare  blends  together 
into  a  perfect  oneness— concise  state- 
ment    (first    sentence);     play    on 
words  (love-love,  alters-alteration, 
remover,  remove);  simile  Love  is 
mark,  or  star;  not  a  fool);  personi- 
fication (Father  Time  with  his 
sickle);  intellectual  wit-turn  (last 
couplet).  So  skilled  is  Shake- 
speare's craft  that  we  are  scarcely 
aware  of  what  blends  the  whole  to- 
gether; namely,  rhythm  (it  is  the 
star  to  every  wandering  bark);  and 
rhyme  (at  the  ends  of  alternating 
lines:  minds-finds,  love-remove, 
mark-bark,  shaken-taken,  etc).  Yet 
each  blender,  each  part,  though  de- 
finable on  the  surface,  speaks  to 
something  deep  within  us,  some- 
thing which  can  be  expressed  in 
no  other  way.
