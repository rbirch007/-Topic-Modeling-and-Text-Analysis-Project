CHOOSING A DESSERT
By Lucy Rose Middleton

The dessert course is the crowning glory of the meal. It should be chosen with care to harmonize with the rest of the menu and to satisfy the appetite without being too heavy or too light.

In planning desserts, consider first the character of the meal that has preceded. If the meal has been substantial, with meat, vegetables, and other hearty foods, a light dessert is preferable. Fresh fruit, fruit salads, sherbets, or simple puddings are appropriate. If the meal has been light, a more substantial dessert may be served—cake, pie, or rich pudding.

The season of the year should also influence the choice. In summer, cool, refreshing desserts are most welcome—ice cream, sherbets, cold fruit desserts, and gelatin preparations. In winter, hot puddings, steamed desserts, and warm fruit dishes are more satisfying.

Consider also the age and preferences of those who are to be served. Children usually prefer simple, sweet desserts, while adults may enjoy more elaborate preparations or those with more subtle flavors.

The time available for preparation is another factor. If time is limited, simple desserts that can be prepared quickly or made in advance should be chosen. Elaborate desserts requiring much time and skill should be reserved for special occasions when adequate time is available.

Cost is always a consideration in menu planning. Expensive desserts should be balanced by economy in other courses, or simple, inexpensive desserts may be made attractive and satisfying through careful preparation and attractive service.

Finally, consider the nutritive value of the dessert in relation to the entire meal. If the meal has been lacking in certain food elements, the dessert may be chosen to supply these needs. Fruit desserts add vitamins and minerals; milk desserts contribute protein and calcium; egg desserts provide protein and iron.

With these factors in mind, the wise homemaker can choose desserts that will add both pleasure and nutritive value to her meals.
