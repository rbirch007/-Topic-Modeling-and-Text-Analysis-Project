I'M
sure, my brethren and sisters,
especially my sisters today, that
I was never more grateful than
I am today, for the realization that
I have that
I continue to live under
and to enjoy the
inspiration of my
mother.
When we add to that the
inspiration
of
a good
wife, we

most
fortunate
of
all
men,

I have been conscious,

afternoon,
that
you
sisters represent the mothers of the
Church.
They,
in
turn,
influence
the homes of the Church in which
there are 1,600,000 members of the
Church.
The scope and sphere of
your influence are continually grow-
ing and
increasing, not only so
far
as numbers
are
concerned,
but,
I
am sure, that your influence is being
felt
progressively
to
a
greater and
greater
degree.
This must be the
case because
of the
increase,
influ-
ence, and efficiency of the work of
these fine sisters — you all — all the
Relief
Society
workers
of
the
Church.
So

little wonder that

of the
Relief
So-
ciety,

ice, we

essen-
tial
to put
aside
all
else
to render
that service. Now these few thoughts
make me even humbler than
I was
when
I came in here today to speak
to you, and
I pray with
the other

great

I start out with a statement found

''Whate'er

well
thy
part."
These

a

some

him
to do

Had he not

reared

he was not doing
his
duty

dent McKay

in

ion was
Peter G. Johnston.
They

They had been

in
tracting.
They

They

walking
around

ancient
castle, and,
in
the words of President McKay, they
were not doing their duty.
That
is
hard
to
imagine
isn't
it?
''As we

ing half-finished," President McKay
writes,
''and from

sidewalk
to
my
surprise
I
saw
an
inscription
carved in stone on the lintel of the
front door.
I said to Brother John-
ston,
'I want
to go over
there and
see what that inscription
is.' "
He
hadn't gone more than halfway un-
til he
read
these
words
carved
in
stone:
"Whate'er thou art, act well
thy
part"
(David O. McKay, The
Impiovement
Era,
October
1959,
page 727).
