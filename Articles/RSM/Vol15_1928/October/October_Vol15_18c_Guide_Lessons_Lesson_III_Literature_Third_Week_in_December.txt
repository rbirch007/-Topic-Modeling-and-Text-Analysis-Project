LESSON III Literature (Third Week in December)
GLADYS ANN WAGSTAFF
Gladys Ann Wagstaff, a poet who has brought to herself considerable honor and to the people who read her poems joy and enrichment of life, was born in Salt Lake City August 9, 1900. She obtained her education in the public schools of Salt Lake, the Salt Lake High School, and later in the University of Utah. She left the University of Utah to take a business course and for a number of years was serving the business world. At odd moments in her life she found time to write poetry. When just a tiny girl she took, she says, "immeasurable delight" in competing in poetry contests and the like. This competitive activity won for her buttons, badges, and small monetary prizes, all of which sent her "winging to the Seventh Heaven." Sensitive to joys and sorrows, she spent "many childhood hours plunged into darkest depths over some imagined hurt which a 'thoroughly normal' child would have utterly overlooked." Painful as these hours were to her, she realizes, as many other artists have realized, that of her sensitive emotions her poetry is born. To use her own words, she says, "I attribute that ability to feel as wholly responsible for my first gropings for expression. Not for just a passing whim, surely, did God imbue me with highly sensitized emotions. Consequently, on those rare occasions when 'inspiration' really comes, expressing the feeling in verse comes as easily as the unfolding of a flower. Writing, for me, when inspired, requires no striving."

Since leaving school our author has taken several courses in the construction of poetry?endeavoring to master technique and the forms and beauty of poems written by the old masters. In addition to all of the local papers, her verse has appeared in The New York Times, The San Francisco Examiner, The Long Beach Telegram, The Chicago Daily News. In standard periodicals such as: Good Housekeeping, The Delineator, College Humor, Love Story Magazine, Motion Picture Magazine, Vogue, St. Nicholas, and in many poetry journals, The Lariat, Lyric West, Palms, etc. Gladys Ann Wagstaff was married to Earl William Pinney August 18, 1926. She has a daughter born April 10, 1928. The Lilac appealed to Miss Wagstaff. As a result we have "Lilac-Time," which we include.

RUTH HARWOOD
Ruth Harwood is the daughter of J. T. Harwood, the well-known Utah painter and Harriet Richards Harwood. She tells us that it is not surprising that she should have a taste for sketching and drawing because of her father's recognized talent and the fact that her mother, too, was gifted in this line. That her mother was gifted in sketching should not be a surprise to our readers for Ruth Harwood's mother is of the same family as Lee Greene Richards, the well known portrait painter of Utah. In the December, 1927, issue of The School Arts Magazine Miss Harwood has given us a unique statement of her life which we include.
"I believe that fortune has smiled on me doubly in making it possible for me to earn the necessary bread and meat of life at one art while the other can be kept sacred and undisturbed by any grind of existence.
"As my father and mother were artistic, the desire to draw came both hereditarily and environmentally but that more precious spark of poetry came unexpectedly and has cast a secret glamor over the world ever since.
"Lyrics do not flow as readily to the happy heart as to the wistful and so in more prosaic hours one branches into long narrative poems, and short stories and even embryo novels.
"Then there are drawings, decorative designs with beautiful dancing figures illustrating poetry themes, to be made. There is a whole world of these fancies to be born yet, but some day, perhaps, the workmanship will come.
"The days go on, working, living, and once in a while there is a red-letter day. It was such a day when the word first came that the annual poetry prize of the University of Utah had been given to me?just for the happy songs I had woven together for myself. That was the first time my songs had ever reached out and brought me happiness.
"There was another such day when there came word that a student in the art department of the University of California had won the yearly poetry prize. So many coveted friends came to me on the wake of that success, and then, my mother was alive to be happy too.
"Then there was a turbulent year filled with romance and my first taste of death. I dressed one day in a black shroud, marched around with many others in a sweltering heat and came home with M. A. In art from the University of California. That was the first time my mother was not there to rejoice too, but my father was there, kind and patient, trying to take the place of both.
"A wonderful red-letter day when a librarian friend showed me a book from London of a hundred best poems of the year from English and American writers and there was my name and one of my poems. Lesser red-letter days scattered along with poems and drawings and articles appearing here and there in magazines! Always working eagerly, impulsively and dreaming of bigger things!
"This summer another shining day with word that the prize for the best sonnet on Browning from the Poetry Society of America was for me. I immediately burst into a dozen more sonnets.
"You ask my hobby. My profession is art, my ambition writing, so perhaps my little house is the hobby. I love it that much. If it looked the way I would want, it would have arches and a swooping roof and a host of modern conveniences inside as I do not crave domestic pursuits.
"But the supreme view! Fifteen feet of window toward the bay and at night Berkeley, and Oakland and Richmond twinkling below as far as the eye can see * * * then the black water and the soft glory of San Francisco in the distance and over all a dome of stars, except when the fog creeps in and wraps its fingers around the hilltop.
"My father, one of Utah's finest artists and art director of its state University, has been such a blessing in reclaiming the small garden spot from the wild brush and rabbits that skip about ready for any chance green that may be struggling for existence.
"Two years now I have been away teaching art, one as supervisor of art in the New Mexico State Teacher's College and one as design instructor in the University of Utah. That is a long time to be away from California and what joy it will be to be back on the hilltop with my friend and my work and my poet self."
This summer Miss Harwood has been abroad with her father, an experience which will be enriching to one gifted as she is gifted. She tells us she makes her living through her sketching and that writing poetry is a diversion that feeds her soul. As she feeds her own soul so does she feed the souls of others. A poem known as "The Shoe Factory" (Song of the Knot-Tyer) has proved to be the most popular of Miss Harwood's poems. It is a poem that dignifies manual labor. Consequently we hail it with pleasure. It was published first in "Poetry," and was selected by an English anthologist for publication in his volume, "The Best Poems of 1922." It was conceived after a visit to a shoe factory by a psychology class for the purpose of investigating some phases of mental reaction. It may be found in Stevenson's Home Book of Verse?and Cooper's Poems of Youth.
THE SHOE FACTORY (Song of the Knot-Tyer)
They told me When I came That this would be drudgery, Always the same Things over and over Day after day? The swift movement In the same small way.
Pick up, Place, Push, And it's tied, Take off, Cut, And put It aside.
Over and over, In rhythmical beat? Some say it is drudgery, But to me it is sweet.
Pick up, Place, Push, And it's tied, Outdoors The sky Is so blue And so wide!
It's a joyous song Going steadily on, Marching in measures Till the day is gone.
Pick up, Place, Push, And it's tied, Soon end Of day Will bring him To my side.
Oh, I love the measures Singing on so fast, Speeding happy hours Till he comes at last!

Among Miss Harwood's numerous poems is one entitled "Silver Morning," which we feel sure will appeal to our readers.
SILVER MORNING
God has dipped this morning In a silver pool, And left it iridescent, Glistening and cool.
All the sky is silver, And the hills between Veil their verdant beauty With a silver sheen.
Even buds are silver On the waking trees, And the larks discover Silver melodies.
And the rippling river Croons a silver song To the sentient willows As it purls along.
And the shining morning That must pass so soon Leaves a silver echo In my lilting tune.
The Acacia seems to have made very special appeal to Miss Harwood as she writes a poem entitled "Acacia Rapture."
ACACIA RAPTURE
It matters not how many years Of waking springs I see, Each fresh time there appears Acacia with its wealth of golden filigree I stand before it, rapture mad, As to some deity.
I fancy that the fervent earth Has climbed all year to this, And that acacias in their worth Are to her as the utmost kiss.
It matters not how many years The gold acacias break, They make me mad and glad to tears, And turn me robber for their sake.

She refers to California in a poem called "Yearning" which we include.
YEARNING
O to be in California in the spring! While torpid earth is held in winter shrouds, To see her wreathe herself in pearly clouds And break into a song of burgeoning!
And O, to watch the barren hills unfold; First comes the mossy carpeting of green, Fog-misted, sometimes, to a silver sheen, And then to watch them flare to mauve and gold!
And O, to see acacias burst in bloom, Like clustered sunshine billowing the trees, To hear the droning music of the bees, And scent the air weighed heavy with perfume!
To think she's showing now her pageantry In all the glory of spring's verdant dress And flinging, open-armed, her loveliness! To think of spring! * * * And I not there to see!

PASTEL
To keep this evening's loveliness I seek, This sunset hour in my garden spent, Among my iris where each purple cheek Of velvet softness lulls me with content.

Her poem entitled "Vagabond Song" carries in its lines the abandoned joy of one who really lives close to nature.
VAGABOND SONG
Because I have so few of goodly things That money brings, perchance the world is bold To scoff at me, because in lacking gold, I could not hope to know the joy life brings.
But O! I have a heart that gaily sings, And feet that swiftly dance, and wealth untold, For I have eyes to catch and soul to hold The gold of sunsets and the jewels of springs.
And I find gems in all the stars that shine; And dazzling diamonds strung on spider's lace; And glistening silver on the water's face; And full red rubies sparkling on the vine.

I doubt if you, in any gilded place, Feel richer in your wealth than I in mine!
A poem entitled "Poetry Magic" is interesting because it really reveals something of the way in which the poet works.
POETRY MAGIC
My poetry Is like a magic wizardry. For with deft hands I pluck a few Glad-chosen words, And build for me A rainbow tinted bridge; And leave the sentient solemn earth, And dance down my fairy bridge, And find some secret glowing place Of dream-lit wonders; And wander there, And thrill and sing, And twine dream flowers in my hair, And dance and fling My carefree arms into the air.
And so?when suddenly You speak to me, You must be patient Until I can return from wanderings A thousand worlds away.
