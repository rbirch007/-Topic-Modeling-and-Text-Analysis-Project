RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE
Personality By Elsie C. Carroll
She is beautiful. She is brilliant. She is efficient. She is clever. She has a wonderful personality.
Perhaps any of the above tributes would be music to a woman's ears. But to a great many, the last would be most highly prized. At a convention of the American Association of Psychologists, considerable attention was given to the subject of personality.
Such questions were considered as: What is personality? Of what characteristics is it composed?
What relation does it bear to mental hygiene, to social adjustments? How is it related to crime, to success, to happiness, and how can it be cultivated? A number of interesting papers were given reporting various studies in this complex field. One study of special significance aimed to determine what characteristics, or traits, make up a pleasing personality.
Over a period of several years this study has been in progress at the Horace Mann school in Teachers' College at Columbia University. The professor who gave the report took the high school girls of this institution as a group for study. He chose them, because they were girls from well-to-do families, who had more than average opportunities for cultural contacts and training. In other words, they were girls who should have a lot of personality according to popular understanding.
