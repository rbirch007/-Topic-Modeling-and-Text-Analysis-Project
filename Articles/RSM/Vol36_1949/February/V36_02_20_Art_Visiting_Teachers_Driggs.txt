Objective: To follow the Savior in his final preparation for the supreme sacrifice and show how he prepared his apostles for their great missions. Note: All quotations which are not followed by references are taken from the text Jesus the Christ. HATEVER transpired during the transfiguration, it is evident that Jesus knew when he came down from the holy mountain that he must prepare for the consummation of his earthly ministry. He had received the transcendent assurance that his Father was well pleased. He had felt again the glory of heaven and had conversed with two of his own "divinely-appointed ministers." To his pure soul it must have been disturbing to know that he must go back for a time to unbelieving and wicked people and finally descend into the "valley of humiliation and death." With sublime courage he faced the ordeal. Healing of a Youthful Maniac At the foot of the mountain he found his other apostles and a multitude of people. One of the multitude had a sorely afflicted son. This man knelt at the feet of the Savior and said: Master, I have brought unto thee my son, which hath a dumb spirit; And wheresoever he taketh him, he teareth him: and he foameth, and gnasheth with his teeth, and pineth away: and I spake to thy disciples that they should cast him out; and they could not (Mark g:17-18). The failure of the disciples to heal the afflicted youth had brought upon them criticism. 'The unbelieving scribes had ridiculed the disciples and, indeed, had cast doubt on the | authority of Jesus himself. However, when Jesus appeared the same scribes remained silent. Of course, this lack of faith and consequent lack of power among his chosen and ordained servants pained the Lord and he chided them:... O faithless and perverse generation, how long shall I be with you? How long shall I suffer you? Bring him hither to me. And Jesus rebuked the devil; and he departed out of him: and the child was cured from that very hour (Matt. 17:17- 18).  A Perry Picture From a painting by Hofmann

Py LESSON DEPARTMENT — In chapter 9 of Mark a more extended account of this miracle is given. 'The boy was evidently not in a condition to exercise faith in his own behalf. In reply to the man's earest request, "If thou canst do anything," which really implied doubt, Jesus stated, "If thou canst believe; all things are possible to him that believeth." The man cried out: "Lord, I believe." In the miracle just considered, the afflicted youth was permanently cured.
Jesus had charged the demon to "enter no more into him." The apostles, while on their missions, had cast out devils but were unable to do so in this case.
They were distarbed and asked the Lord, "Why could not we cast him out?"
Jesus answered, "Because of your unbelief." However, he added, "Howbeit this kind goeth not out but by prayer and fasting." 'This lesson given by Jesus teaches so well that faith is a power, but that faith must be firm, genuine, and pure. The Lord's Death and Resurrection Again Predicted From the place where the last described miracle was wrought, Jesus and the apostles started toward Capernaum.

They had received a testimony that he was the Christ, but he wanted them to know of his approaching death and resurtection. 'This, they were slow to comprehend. On one occasion he said, "Let these sayings sink down into your ears " (Luke 9:44).
Then he proceeded to explain that he would be killed and would "rise the third day."
Luke says, "But they understood not this saying, and 125 it was hid from them, that they perceived it not: and they feared to ask him of that saying" (Luke 9:45).
They seemed not to-want to believe because the very thought was so terrifying to them. The Tribute Money Supplied by a Miracle Soon after Jesus and his followers reached Capernaum, a tax collector approached Peter and asked, "Doth not your Master pay tribute?" The inquiry related to the temple tax which had been required of the Jewish male adults since the days of Moses. The question was not put to Jesus, probably because of respect or awe which was felt for him.
Priests and rabbis were usually excused from paying this tax. It was a small tax, amounting to half a shekel or thirty-three cents in United States money. It was a Jewish contribution and not a tax levied by the Roman government. As Peter was about to submit the matter to the Master, the latter forestalled him by asking of Peter, "What thinkest thou, Simon? Of whom do the kings of the earth take custom or tribute? Of their own children, or of strangers?
Peter saith unto him, Of strangers.
Jesus saith unto him, Then are the children free." The meaning was apparent—Jesus was the Son of God. The temple was the House of God. It was inconsistent to ask the Son to contribute to his own Father's house.
Peter had already pledged payment without consulting Jesus.
