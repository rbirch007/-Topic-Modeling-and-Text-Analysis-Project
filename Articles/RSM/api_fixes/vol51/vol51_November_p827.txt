Gratitude

and that this life is to prove the individual. As one looks back over a time of dire distress, often the good which emerged can be traced, although at the time it seemed impossible to find it. It may be long afterwards before gratitude fills the soul.

The Lord decreed, "In the sweat of thy face shalt thou eat bread." This may be viewed as a curse — forcing men to work hard, or it may be viewed with gratitude as an opportunity to work. The understanding and attitude of the individual make the difference. A mother may work long hours in homemaking, disciplining children, encouraging them, serving them, cleaning, sewing, cooking, nursing, gardening — at the end of the day, however, her gratitude and satisfaction well up to the Lord for work well done, and the ability to perform the duties which devolve upon her, a sign of a noble soul, according to Aesop.

Almost everyone needs practice in cultivating and expressing gratitude, in searching out and acknowledging the good both in tribulations and blessings. As one approaches an attitude of gratitude for all things, one reaches nearer the avowal of the Savior "Not my will, but thine, be done."
                                                                                —M.C.S.
