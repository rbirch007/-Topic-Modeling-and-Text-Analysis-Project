"Gramps seems so deliberate. . . ."

"He's concerned and I think

Early the next morning Na-
omi heard the three of them tip-
toe past the door and go quietly
downstairs. She nudged Myra.
"We'd better get up, too."

"Isn't it terribly early?" she
asked sleepily.

"Gramps never sleeps late."

When they went downstairs a
few minutes later, they were sur-
prised to see Brother Stewart
and David out on the patio alone.
Brother Stewart was bent over
laughing and David was writing.

"They certainly seem cheer-
ful," Naomi commented. "At
least Brother Stewart."

Myra looked puzzled. "He is
always serious at school."

"Something has struck him
funny."

Myra watched them for several
more minutes, then she turned to
her. "Maybe they're laughing at
Gramps."

"Of course they're not."

"Then why isn't Gramps with
them? If I thought they were
ridiculing his stories—like Darrel
Murch did—I'd—I'd—"

"I'm sure they aren't."

"Poor Gramps." She flung the
apron she had just started to put
on across the room and banged

out the door. "Where's Gramps?"
she asked coldly as she swung
past the two startled men.

Brother Stewart had stopped
laughing as he pointed to the
backyard. "Out there," he said.
David stared after her.

[ILLUSTRATION]

Naomi followed, then stopped
abruptly when she saw Gramps
sitting forlornly in an old chair
at the edge of the yard whittling
on a piece of wood. "Oh, no,"
she cried. Myra was right;
Gramps had been hurt again—
and it was her fault; she should
have insisted. . . . She watched,
unable to move, as Myra ran
to his side and knelt beside him,
resting her head on his knees.
"Gramps," she whispered. "Dear
Gramps, I'm sorry."
