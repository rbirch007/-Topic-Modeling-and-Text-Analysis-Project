Naomi took her daughter's hand in hers. "Are you sure he's the right one?"

She kissed her daughter. "Then I hope he loves you, too. Now we'd better go downstairs and finish dinner."

"What's the menu?"

Naomi smiled. "Things I thought your guests would like—fried chicken, mashed potatoes, hot biscuits, cake, and all the rest."

"Sounds great. Good food, plus pleasant conversation, I trust," Myra said hopefully.

"The evening will go well."

She was right, too. Myra and David kept a steady conversation going about school, mutual friends and Church activities. Gramps sat straight in his chair listening to every comment made by David, ready to pounce on any provocative statement, while Brother Stewart, a little more subtle, was just as concerned about the welfare of his only grandson. How she wished she could read their minds, or did she? The two young people didn't seem the least concerned.

It was after dinner when they were in the living room that Gramps ran his fingers through his hair, as he often did when he had a point to make, and looked directly at David. "This modern generation, I don't think they have much purpose in life. Why, when I was a boy, we knew...."

"Well, Sir, I guess each generation thinks it's the best. I don't agree with you that your generation had more purpose.... I think the young people of today...."

Mr. Stewart broke in. "You're probably both right; each generation has its problems, and its aspirations, too."

"Folks are soft today. Believe me in my day you didn't live it easy."

"We try to meet the challenge of our day, Sir. True, we aren't called to walk across the plains like many of our ancestors, but I'll wager if we were called upon to do that today, we'd manage."

Gramps raised his eyebrows. "You don't agree with me on much of anything, do you, Son?"

"If you mean about our generation, I'm afraid I don't. Oh, I know there are slackers today, but there were in your time, too."

"Let's change the subject," Naomi said.

Myra looked worried. "Yes, let's do," she said.

"I find the subject stimulating," Brother Stewart remarked. "You know, Brother Lawes, I have some interesting memories of our day, too."

"I remember when I was a boy," Gramps began, and the conversation continued until bedtime—probably way beyond. Naomi and Myra could hear them talking long after they had retired.

"Oh, Mom," Myra buried her head in the pillow. "David will never, never accept another invitation. He must be terribly bored."
