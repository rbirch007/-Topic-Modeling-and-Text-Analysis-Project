THE WOMAN TAKEN IN SIN

WILLARD DONE

And the scribes and Pharisees brought unto him a woman taken in adultery; and when they had set her in the midst,

They say unto him, Master, this woman was taken in adultery, in the very act.

Now Moses in the law commanded us, that such should be stoned: but what sayest thou?

This they said, tempting him, that they might have to accuse him. But Jesus stooped down, and with his finger wrote on the ground, as though he heard them not.

So when they continued asking him, he lifted up himself, and said unto them, He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her.

And again he stooped down, and wrote on the ground.

And they which heard it, being convicted by their own conscience, went out one by one, beginning at the eldest, even unto the last: and Jesus was left alone, and the woman standing in the midst.

When Jesus had lifted up himself, and saw none but the woman, he said unto her, Woman, where are those thine accusers? hath no man condemned thee?

She said, No man, Lord. And Jesus said unto her, Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more.—John 8:3-11.

This beautiful story from the New Testament has always appealed to me as one of the most touching incidents in the life of the Savior. Here we see divine mercy and justice perfectly blended.

The woman had sinned. There was no question about that. The law of Moses was explicit—"such should be stoned." But Jesus, while not condoning the sin, showed infinite compassion for the sinner.

The scribes and Pharisees thought they had trapped the Master. If he said, "Stone her," they could accuse him of being unmerciful. If he said, "Let her go," they could charge him with setting aside the law of Moses.

But Jesus, with divine wisdom, turned the tables on them. "He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her."

What a searching test! One by one, "being convicted by their own conscience," they slipped away, "beginning at the eldest, even unto the last."

Then came those wonderful words of the Savior to the trembling woman: "Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more."

Here we see the perfect balance between justice and mercy. The sin was not excused, but the sinner was forgiven and given another chance.

This incident teaches us several important lessons:

First, that we should not be too quick to judge others. "Judge not, that ye be not judged" is a divine commandment we too often forget.

Second, that mercy should always be tempered with justice, and justice with mercy.

Third, that no matter how far we may have fallen, there is always hope for redemption if we truly repent.

Fourth, that the Savior's love and compassion extend to all mankind, regardless of their past mistakes.

The woman went away with a new lease on life. She had been forgiven, but she had also been warned: "Go, and sin no more."

This is the gospel message—forgiveness for the penitent, but also the responsibility to live a better life.

May we all learn from this beautiful story to be more charitable in our judgments of others, more forgiving of their faults, and more determined to overcome our own weaknesses.
