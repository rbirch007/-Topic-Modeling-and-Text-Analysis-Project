Grandmother's Quilts
By Alice M. Snock

Grandmother's quilts! What memories they bring back to us! The very mention of them carries us back to childhood days when we visited at grandmother's house and slept under those warm, beautiful coverlets that were the pride of her heart and the admiration of all who saw them.

In those days quilting was not only a necessity but a fine art as well. Every well-regulated household had its quilting frame, and the women folks spent many pleasant hours piecing and quilting the bed coverings for the family. It was a social occasion too, for the neighbors would come in to help with the quilting, and while their fingers flew over the intricate patterns, their tongues kept pace with news and gossip of the community.

The quilts of our grandmothers were things of beauty. The patterns had names that were as quaint and interesting as the quilts themselves. There was the "Wedding Ring," the "Log Cabin," the "Flying Geese," the "Dresden Plate," the "Grandmother's Flower Garden," and scores of others, each with its own particular charm and each requiring skill and patience in its making.

The materials used were largely scraps left over from the family sewing, and it was wonderful how these bits of cloth were transformed into objects of beauty and utility. Nothing was wasted. Every scrap, no matter how small, found its place in some quilt pattern. The women of those days had to be economical, and they made a virtue of necessity.

But grandmother's quilts were more than just bed coverings. They were works of art, expressions of the maker's personality and skill. Each quilt told a story. The colors chosen, the pattern selected, the fineness of the stitching—all these things revealed something of the character of the woman who made it.

Today, when we see one of these old quilts, we are filled with admiration for the patience and skill that went into its making. We marvel at the tiny, even stitches, the perfect matching of colors, the intricate patterns worked out with mathematical precision. We realize that these quilts represent hours and hours of loving labor, and we treasure them not only for their beauty and utility but for the memories they hold of the dear hands that fashioned them.

In our modern homes, with their steam heat and electric blankets, we may not need grandmother's quilts for warmth, but we prize them for their beauty and for the link they provide with the past. They remind us of a time when life was simpler, when women found joy and satisfaction in creating beautiful things with their own hands, when the home was truly the center of all activity.

Let us cherish these heirlooms from the past. Let us preserve them carefully and pass them on to our children and our children's children, that they too may know something of the skill and devotion of the women who have gone before us.
