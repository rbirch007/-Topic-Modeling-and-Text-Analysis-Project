The Magic Word Efficiency Lucille C. Richards MOMIE, will you read me a story?" "Yes, Jimmie, just as soon as I get this work finished. 1 have And so it goes on and on, Jimmie waiting for a story, and Momie busy with the house. How can we make this house a home?
Just what does the hoe need to provide? 1 he problem given to mothers and homemakcrs is to organize their households in a manner that will re-lease a larger portion of time and strength for devotion to the higher welfare of the family.
This higher welfare is concerned with the development of happy, healthy, and clean-minded human beings proper-ly equipped for the fulfilling of their responsibilities to one another, to the community, and to the race. In mastering this task the home-maker's satisfaction will be deeper and more keen if she performs her work swiftly and perfectly, with the greatest possible economy of time and effort.
This can be accomplished only through the magic word efficiency, That person is efficient who finds and follows the easiest and most effective way of doing anything. Ef-ficiency will enable the housewife to work more swiftly, and with less fatigue. It will give her increased lei-sure for things more important than mere mechanical routine. It will cure the habit of hurry and worry, and it will promote happiness and harmony in the home. The keynote of efficiency is interest. If we could adopt the child's point of view and approach our problem in the spirit of play, mountains would be leveled, difficulties vanish before the concentrated pow-er of interest. We should learn to sec in the management of a home, not a routine governed by many formulas, but a fascinating field for investigation and experiment, whose boundless possibilities furnish scope for the exercise of all the initiative, ingenuity, creative ability, and knowledge we possess. T1ie bulwark upon which efficiency rests is planning.
Plans are necessary to success under all conditions of life and in every trade and profession practiced by man, but nowhere are they more essential than in the profession of homemaking. To work without a plan is to work aimlessly and to accomplish little. The housekeeper finds her-self at nightfall overwhelmed with the consciousness of tasks unfinished or wholly neglected. It is well to plan the entire day's program and, in most cases, the best results will be obtained by planning the entire week in advance, taking care not to overcrowd any one day. In planning the general routine do not feel obliged to follow the traditional order of washing on Mon-day, ironing on Tuesday, and so on.
Sunday should be a day of cessation from household cares, so far as can be arranged, and it is a mistake to devote any part of it to sorting clothes, mending, and soaking clothes, or in any way preparing them for the wash on Monday. Page 102 By postponing this work of preparation until Monday, and doing the washing Tuesday, additional leisure will be gained on the day of rest, and there will be time on Monday for the extra cleaning which is always needed after Sunday. PLAN daily the doing of some extra piece of work, not a part of the regular routine, such as special cleaning of a bedroom, scouring the bathroom, doing the kitchen, ironing, or polishing silver. If the rooms are gone over thoroughly once each week, doing one or more each day, and merely "tidying" up on the other days, the housekeeper need not worry about her house being clean. In planning for the ironing, a great deal of time and effort can be saved by handling articles a minimum of times. The ironing can be done the day it is washed, except, perhaps, for some of the starched things.

The simple and plain articles can be taken from the line and ironed immediately if they have been hung up straight and free from wrinkles. They can be folded and put away nearly as quickly as they can be crumpled into a clothes basket and kept to be ironed a day or so later. Keep the washing and ironing "done up," and do not let them "stack up." Not only do we need to have a plan as a working basis for this efficiency, but also a schedule, a definite hour for the assigned task. Begin with the big things and schedule them for the periods which are most fruitful of accomplishment. Do not fritter away your time on trifles, leaving the important task untouched. Prompt dispatching of the task leads to a household run smoothly and efficiently. Planning, scheduling, and dispatching will cure procrastination, strengthen will power, increase leisure, and promote happiness and harmony in the home.
