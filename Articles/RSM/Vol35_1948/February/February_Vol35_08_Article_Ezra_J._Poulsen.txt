Anne went to the chicken coop to gather the eggs. Happy, childish voices floated up from the creek bottom below the garden. The sounds were like music to her ears. She called, and in a few moments Dick, her six-year-old son, and Betsy, her three-year-old daughter, answered. } "Supper's ready."

There was no doubt about their loving the farm; the complete freedom of their environment affected them as it might a pair of young fawns made them blithe and beautiful. But Anne admitted to herself, rather sadly, that lonesomeness was almost overwhelming her. She heard Joe yelling at the cattle up in the hollow. Again, the wires snarled and sang, and the posts creaked. The cattle would be over the hill in a few minutes, and Joe would be mending the fence. The sun was almost down to the ridge above the dry farm, and long fingers of shadow were creeping into the gullies and ravines above the edges of the valley. NNE was more in a mood for reflection than discussion; consequently, she made no reply, and when Joe left the house, she busied herself washing the dishes and getting the children off to bed. She could feel a rift growing between them, which seemed to flourish on many little quirks of circumstances, altogether simple, yet quite 97 uncontrollable; and, with a feeling of growing helplessness, she realized they both possessed fixed attitudes from which there seemed to be no retreat.
Anne had met and married Joe in the city, where she had always lived, and where she had a promising position with a utility company.
Early in their acquaintance, he had told her he owned a homestead on the outskirts of the valley in which he was born, and that he had increased it somewhat by purchase.
This had been wonderful news. It showed Joe's sense of practical understanding; and when he had asked her, half playfully, if she would like to live on it, she had answered affirmatively. 'The whole thing seemed like a youthful adventure, then. But the hard work, the lonesomencss, and the crop failures had been bitter disillusionment. Her folks had objected from the first, especially her sister Della, who declared she would expect her home within a week.
This had started the antagonism between Joe and Della, and now that Della was coming, there would be some new flare-ups. There was a tartness in his voice she didn't like, but she firmly held to her point. "It's just a matter of good business, Joe, you know it is, and while I realize how you hate to leave, I'm certain it doesn't do any good to put off what has to be done!" "Honey," he spoke impatiently, "will it be all right if I leave im- mediately after dinner, or shall I go before?" He strode toward the little shed where they kept the car, his long, energetic steps leaving no doubt that his mind was at last made up.
Quickly repentant for her sharp words, Anne tried to call him back, but she seemed incapable. A moment later she heard the motor start, and she hurried into the house to fix dinner while he tinkered about the car, getting it ready for the journey. It seemed an amazingly short time before she stood in the yard watching him drive away. He had denied that he was angry, as he had kissed her and the children. "Do be careful, Honey, around the cows and horses. I'll be back as soon as possible." "And you be careful, Joe," she cried, suddenly shaken by an uncontrollable emotion. "Drive ever so carefully." As she watched the car disappear, a vague dread began to clutch her heart. Had she been wrong in demanding to be taken away from the homestead? Had she been still more wrong in her impatience? For the first time, she admitted a tinge of regret at the thought of leaving the farm.
After all, they had lived there most of the time for more than three years; and now, she felt as if it clung to her, and dared her to leave. She thought of the garden, the chickens, the cows.
Loneliness overwhelmed her as she called in the children and began to busy herself with the chores.
Even then, there was no relief. The loneliness grew into a dread, and she became frightened. If she could have called Joe back, she would have done so, She wasn't half so sure of her ability to take care of the cows as she had made him believe. 'Oh, dear me," she said anxiously to Dickie, "I do hope Daddy doesn't stay away long." But Dickie could offer no solace. He was about ready to cry himself. And that was but the beginning.
