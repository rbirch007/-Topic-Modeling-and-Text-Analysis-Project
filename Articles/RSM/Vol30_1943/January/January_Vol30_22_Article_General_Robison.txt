Because of her sympathetic nature and her exquisite needlework, Sister Robison was made chairman of the important burial-clothes department of the General Board.
Able to express condolence in a delicate, tactful maner, she was a genuine comfort to the bereaved. Sister Robison took a special interest in home beautification, and through her enthusiasm and under the Society's direction trees and shrubs were planted and homes were improved. In view of Sister Robison's interest in furthering human welfare and happiness, she was chosen to represent the Relief Society at two conferences of the Travelers Aid Society and at three National Conventions of Social Workers, appearing on the program at one of them. Her reports of her attendance at 'the White House Conferences for Children breathe love and sympathy and a longing for improved conditions for the nation's young. In 1928, Sister Robison was appointed general president of Relief Society. She has traveled widely in the stakes and missions of America and in the missions of Europe, carrying messages of gladness, stirring people's hearts, arousing love and enthusiasm for the Gospel and its work. Her grace of manner, natural eloquence, kindliness, her remarkable memory for names and faces, her personal interest in Relief Society workersall have made her a most welcome visitor. At a World Congress of Women in Chicago, while the World's Fair was open, the president of the National Council of Women, Lena M. Phillips, appointed Mrs.
Robison to preside at one session, which was attended by hundreds of women from all over the world.
When a delegate to the International Council of Women in Paris, France, Mrs.
Robison by request represented Lena M.
Phillips at two meetings of the international presidents.
Only the properly accredited women were admitted.
While in Europe, Sister Robison visited Relief Societies in some of the branches in England, Belgium, Holland, Switzerland, and Germany.
Many of the Saints still hold her visit in precious remembrance. Sister Robison sponsored institutes in social welfare to teach Relief Society women better methods of caring for the poor. During Sister Robison's presidency a notable event occurred. A monument was erected by the Relief Society at Nauvoo, Illinois, to mark the site where Relief Society was organized March 27, 1842.
This monument is of stone, four by eight feet, surmounted by two bronze tablets giving historical data of Relief Society. At the dedication ceremony, July 26, 1933, hundreds of people sat under spreading locusts, or stood nearby. Mr. W. D.
Chenery of Illinois wrote in a newspaper there that "Mrs. Louise Y. Robison, presided over the ceremonial with a graciousness and tact that might be the emulation

Another writer commented that her pleasing manner and ready wit caught the crowd, Another important project encouraged by Sister Robison, and one which became a very popular phase of Relief Society work in America and in the foreign missions, was the development of Singing Mothers' choruses. Sister Robison has done much temple work during her lifea work that has been for years one of her major satisfactions. 'The spiritual side of the-Gospel appeals to her nature. Late in 1939, when the General Boards of Relief Society and Primary 715 were reorganized, Sister Robison and her Board were honorably released. She was then asked to be an ordinance worker in the Salt Lake Temple, and took much pleasure in fulfilling that call. She is at present actively engaged in Relief Society work in San: Francisco Stake. She accepts many invitations to speak in wards and stakes. Sister Robison and her Board inaugurated the fouryear Relief Society membership campaign and carried it forward for two years. Her interest in this project continued throughout the campaign. The true spirit of the Gospel emerges in her voice and shines forth in her countenance.
