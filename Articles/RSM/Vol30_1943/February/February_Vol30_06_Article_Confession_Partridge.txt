CONFESSION Ruth Louise Partridge
When I was very young and small I wanted to grow old and tall, And now the years have brought to pass The thing I yearned for, but alas, I find with wormwood and with gall It isn't what I want at all!
TO MARRY OR NOT TO MARRY IN TIME OF WAR 99
Gardening for Victory Hazel D. Moyle Garden Editor, The Deseret News
To my garden let us raise Daughters of Jove, a song of praise! Orchard of unfailing fruit, Grape and apple, herb and root, Of all the wealth that gardens give Whereby man may eat and live. Here, in fellowship, agree Pleasure and utility.
THUS did the ancient poet Asinius, celebrate in verse the praises of home gardeningand today, almost two thousand years later, gardens again have become important contributors toward the pleasure, safety, and comfort of family and national life. The 1943 home garden will play an important role in our country's mighty program of war, for gardeners too have been called to arms, but our weapons will be the digging fork and the hoe. We must polish off these trusty weapons and prepare to plant as never before; we must make every small bit of earth in our possession produce "all the wealth that gardens give whereby man may eat and live." We must not only raise flowers, but fruits and vegetables as well, for food, we are told, will play a leading part in winning victory. Every row of carrots or tomatoes we can raise and use at our family table will mean just that much more food for our boys on the fighting fronts throughout the world. Our country must produce tremendous quantities of extra food this year to send to our Allies as well as the starving millions of the war ridden earth.
