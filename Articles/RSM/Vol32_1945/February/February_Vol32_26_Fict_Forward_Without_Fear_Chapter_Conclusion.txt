I was terribly ashamed of myself and I made up my mind to get the bureau at once. Our youngest daughter, Emily, was going to get married. Her mother kept sewing and sewing, and there were patterns and goods and thread scattered all about and stacks of new things around. One afternoon I came in early from the field, and there sat Bertha at the sewing machine, treadling fast. She stopped suddenly and looked up at me. In a minute tears were streaming down her face. She turned away and treadled again. Emily went over and touched Bertha's gray hair and asked, "What's the matter, Mother?"
I knew what it was. She wanted that bureau so that Emily's wedding things could be put away and kept in good shape until she was ready for them.
This time I was surely going to get the bureau. I told Emily my plans. I was going to surprise her mother by bringing home a bureau. On the way to town I met Bart Sanders. He asked me if I wanted to buy his mowing machine.

I needed it the worst kind for my own mower was broken down, and was always at the blacksmith shop just when I needed it most. A good machine would be an investment. I told Bart I would take it and pay cash down. I hated to do it, but I thought that sometime later I could buy a better bureau for Bertha and she would be that much more pleased. She could afford to wait a while longer since she wasn't expecting the bureau anyway. I went home with the mowing machine and showed it to Bertha and Emily, but I didn't say anything about having intended to spend the money in another way. SIX months later our Emily got married.
Bertha and I were alone in the house. Alone. I tell you I never heard so much silence.
Then one night after supper when I was sitting at one side of the table and Bertha across from me, she said quietly,  You didn't get it, did you?" I shifted around in the chair. "Get what. Bertha?" Her voice was level. The bureau.
Emily told me you were going to get a bureau to keep her wedding things in. I believed you really would. But it doesn't matter any more. Now the children have come and gone there isn't anything left to use a bureau for." Her face was gray and she didn't say anything more.
After a while she went into the bedroom and closed the door quietly. I don't remember how long I sat at the table. She wouldn't need a bureau now, she had said, but a woman can always use a bureau.
Five years after Emily was married the lucern seed crop was good. I had some extra money in my pocket. I hitched up and drove the wagon fifteen miles over to Mayfield and tied up at the Emporium.
They had lots of bureaus, big high solid-oak ones, and wider ones made of white maple, and polished walnut bureaus with six drawers.
Then all at once I saw the one 1 wanted. It was a beauty.
Painted green, and over that, for trimming, big pink roses. It had golden looking handles and carved legs with shining casters. I bought it.
Fifty dollars, but it was worth twice that. I loaded it on the wagon, covered it with an old quilt, and drove home. I remember, yet, how anxious I was to get there.
Bertha had gone out somewhere. I set the bureau by the bed in our room and then I went into the kitchen to wait.
What would she say?
What would she think of the green paint and the pink roses? It was fall, and when Bertha came in she was wearing her coat, a gray coat buttoned up tight. "Well, John," she said, "when did you get home?" "Just now." "Where have you been?" "Nowhere." She took off her coat and started for the bedroom. I held my breath. She stood in the doorway. She kept standing there.  "Bertha," I said, "don't you like it?"
