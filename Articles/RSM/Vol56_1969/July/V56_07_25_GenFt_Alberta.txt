"THE HIND AND THE PANTHER" John Dryden's lines from his long poem entitled "The Hind and the Panther" emphasize the danger of trying to coerce others into a belief which matches our own: The Hind and the Panther Of all the tyrannies of human kind The worst is that which persecutes the mind. Let us but weigh at what offence we strike, "Tis but because we cannot think alike. In punishing of this we overthrow The laws of nations and of nature too. Beasts are the subjects of tyrannic sway, When still the stronger on the weaker prey; Man only of a softer mould is made, Not for his fellows' ruin but their aid; Created kind, beneficent and free, The noble image of the Deity. The final line of this excerpt with its echo of Genesis 1:27 is intended to make explicit what has already been hinted at throughout the poem: only that man who remembers in whose image he is made can be expected to understand that Lesson Department coerced freedom of conscience is neither necessary nor beneficial. John Dryden (1631-1700) was one of the most versatile of English poets. He seemed to be at home in dramatic, satiric, narrative, and didactic poetry. He was also one of the greatest of English translators and one of the most accomplished writers of English prose. In 1686 Dryden was converted to Roman Catholicism and "The Hind and the Panther," his most famous religious poem, is a defense of his new faith. The Hind apparently represents the Roman Catholic Church, while the Panther stands for the Church of England. Discussion Question If most of us know that man "convinced against his will is of the same opinion still," why do we still try to force conformity? "THE INDISPENSABLE OPPOSITION" Walter Lippmann (1889) was a member of the famous Class of 1910 at Harvard University. Since his graduation, he has had a distinguished career as a journalist, editor, and author. For members of the Church, Mr. Lippmann's insistence on the necessity of opposition strikes an immediately responsive chord. In the second chapter of 2 Nephi we find the following lines: For it must needs be, that there is an opposition in all things. If not so righteousness could not be brought to pass, neither wickedness, neither holiness nor misery, neither good nor bad. (2 Ne. 2:11.) In reading Lippman's "The Indispensable Opposition" we

 must distinguish at once between that opposition which stems from mere contentiousness and that opposition which is creative. The Book of Mormon warns us against contention in the eleventh chapter of 3 Nephi, verse 29: For verily, verily I say unto you, he that hath the spirit of contention is not of me, but is of the devil, who is the father of contention, and he stirreth up the hearts of men to contend with anger, one with another. One of the cues as to what is essentially wrong with contention is given in the phrase "with anger." Too often our opposition is expressed in emotional, exaggerated charges and counter-charges. In such a statewe are not receptive to the whisperings of the Spirit. The Holy Ghost is not able to penetrate the wall of strong feeling which we have erected. We haven't ears to hear the truth. A dispute is not even a detour toward truth; it becomes an end in itself, a dead end. We quickly become less concerned with finding answers than we are in winning arguments. How rarely anyone is listening in an argument. We wait impatiently while our opponent speaks so we can stun him with our side as soon as he finishes. The opposition Mr. Lippmann - finds "indispensable" has little to do with mere disputation. Rather, it is the presentation and consideration of alternatives that test and refine our own beliefs. Truth has nothing to fear from error and _ since the very word "definition" suggests pointing out that something is not as well as what it is, we can focus and sharpen 556 our perceptions by contrast as well as comparison. It must be remembered, however, that Mr. Lippmann is talking primarily about secular truth. That to which the Spirit has testified to us is not illuminated by error. Woodrow' Wilson, twentyseventh President of the United States, in his essay "What is Liberty" presents an attractive picture of a harmonious society in which each part is adjusted to and balanced against every other one. It is important to remember, in reacting positively to such a concept, that such an adjustment might be temporarily attained where the basis for the social group is almost wholly evil, that' is, men are mere cogs in a machine. OTHER POEMS Poems by Henry David Thoreau and James Oppenheim stress the importance of peace of mind and personal integrity in the meaning we can individually give to liberty. Of course we must understand that the Latter-day Saint definition of liberty is in accord with the Lord's definition of liberty. Thoreau's "Independence" is as follows: My life more civil is and free Than any civil polity. Ye Princes, keep your realms and circumscribed power, Not wide as are my dreams, Nor rich as is this hour. What can ye give which I have not? What can ye take which I have got? Can ye defend the dangerless? Can ye inherit nakedness? To all true wants Time's ear is deaf, Penurious States lend no relief Out of their pelf: But a_ free soulthank God Can help itself.  No trade upon the street Wears its emblazonry. Along with his friend and mentor Ralph Waldo Emerson, Thoreau believed strongly in a self-reliance which was, _ ultimately, a reliance upon God. The fervor of Thoreau in this poem seems out of place if we do not remember this. It is a little unfair to think of Thoreau as the great rebel in American literature and it is even more unfair to believe that the group of which Thoreau and Emerson were a part  the Transcendentalists  were characterized by personal eccentricity and harsh independence. What all the Transcendentalists insisted upon was the necessity for each person to find out for himself what he believed in. They were sure that men of faith and good will would finally come to essential agreement, since God is the author of all truth but they felt strongly that each person must come to that unity in his own way. In this poem Thoreau tries to reject all the crutches which men have tried to use to avoid facing the truth and establishing a personal liberty based on that truth. James Oppenheim's "The Slave" is equally committed to the necessity for each man to free himself before he can conceive the meaning of liberty. Crane himself felt it was a "daisy" of a story. It is surely all of these but, in addition, few narratives equal its sensitive probing of the nature of freedom. In Walter Lippmann's article we see the part played by opposition in clarifying liberty. In "The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky" we are helped to understand the necessary connection between freedom and responsibility. There is no talk of freedom in this tale. The characters we meet are not given to philosophizing, but their instinct is sure. Even Scratchy Wilson, blurred by whiskey and unfaltered rage, is sobered and calmed by Jack Potter's announcement that he is now married.
