[Social Relations Restraint The Road to Freedom, Part]
Goal,  Understanding,  and 
Technique 
Jean  Larsen, 
Instructor  in  Child  Development 
and  Family  Relationships  at 
Brigham  Young  University 
■  The  answer  to  the  question, 
"Should  there  be  discipUne?" 
comes  through  loud  and  clear, 
"Yes,  there  must  be  discipline!" 
But  the  what,  the  why,  and  the 
how  of  discipline  and  guidance 
are  not  so  clearly  spelled  out. 
Not  knowing  what  is  expected  on 
either  end  of  the  guidance  situa- 
tion provides  some  frustrating 
experiences  for  all  concerned. 
We  recall  the  story:  Bobby 
had  been  playing  by  the  duck 
pond  for  several  minutes  when 
Father  called  for  him  to  come 
in  the  house.  Bobby  approached 
his  father  slowly,  looking  down 
at  muddy  feet  and  wet  pant  legs. 
When  Bobby's  father  saw  the 
mud  and  the  wet,  he  said  in  a 
stern  voice,  "Well,  Bobby,  how 
many  times  must  you  be  told 
to  stay  out  of  the  mud  and 
water?  Go  get  me  a  willow,  maybe 
a  'willowing'  will  help  you  re- 
member." Bobby  was  gone  for 
several  minutes  before  he  slowly 
approached  his  father  again — 
this  time  with  his  hands  behind 
him.  His  father's  voice  was  again 
stern  as  he  said,  "Well,  I'm  wait- 
ing, give  me  the  willow."  Bobby 
drew  both  hands  around  and 
produced  not  a  willow  but  sev- 
eral rocks  in  each  hand.  Then 
he  said  in  a  meek  little  voice, 
"I  couldn't  find  a  willow,  but 
here  are  some  rocks  to  throw 
at  me." 
GOAL 
As  adults  being  responsible 
for  guiding  the  child  we  are 
concerned  about  his  behavior 
and  ways  of  altering,  controlling, 
or  instigating  certain  behaviors. 
Guidance  can  become  a  battle 
of  wills  between  adults  and  chil- 
dren. But,  as  in  all  battles,  some- 
one must  be  the  victor.  Should 
it  be  the  adult  at  the  expense 
of  the  child's  curiosity,  spontane- 
ous expression,  and  self-esteem, 
or  should  the  child  be  the  winner 
because  of  his  ability  to  display 
compulsive,  negative,  and  vio- 
lent behavior?  Power  struggles 
are  a  risk  to  any  relationship  and 
should  be  avoided.
