The  True  Concept  of  God 
Among  many  other  funda- 
mental doctrines,  the  missionary 
teaches  the  investigator  the 
true  concept  of  God.  Of  all  the 
doctrines  of  the  gospel  this  one 
stands  out  as  one  of  the  most 
important  for  people  to  know 
since  it  is  life  eternal  to  know 
God.  (John  17:3.)  One  of  the 
difficulties  a  nonmember  may 
have  in  accepting  the  concept 
that  God  is  personal,  having  a 
body  of  flesh  and  bones,  is  God's 
attribute  of  omnipresence.  The 
apostate  "Christian"  world  be- 
lieves that  God  is  a  spirit  with- 
out form;  whereas  the  Lord  has 
revealed  to  his  prophets,  ancient 
and  modern,  that  he  has  a  body. 
He  may  be  said,  however,  to 
be  a  spirit  the  same  as  "man 
is  spirit;"  that  is,  he,  as  well  as 
man,  has  always  been  a  spirit, 
from  eternity  to  eternity  though 
in  earth-life  and  the  resurrected 
state  the  personal  spirit  is  as- 
sociated with  a  body.  (D&C  93: 
33;  130:22.)  If  God  has  a  tangible 
body,  and  he  is  not  a  spirit  only, 
then  how  can  he  be  omnipresent, 
or  everywhere?  The  "Light  of 
Christ"  or  spirit  of  the  Lord 
emanates  from  God  to  fill  all 
space.  (Acts  17:27-28;  D&C 88:12.) 
Because  of  this  omnipresent 
power,  God  has  communication 
with  all  of  his  creations,  and 
thus  he  is  everywhere,  as  the 
scriptures  aver. 
Salient  Principles  of  Doctrine 
and  Covenants 
The  Doctrine  and  Covenant 
is  different  from  the  other  three 
standard  works  of  the  Church 
because  it  is  a  little  more  diflfi- 
cult  to  maintain  interest  in  sus- 
tained reading  unless  one  has  a 
definite  objective  in  his  read- 
ing, such  as  theological.  There 
is  relatively  no  narrative  in  the 
Doctrine  and  Covenants.  The 
exception  to  this  is  Section  135, 
the  historical  account  of  the 
martyrdom  of  the  Prophet  Joseph 
Smith  and  his  brother  Hyrum 
at  Carthage  Jail  125  years  ago. 
I  presume  that  it  would  not 
be  amiss  to  suggest  an  exagger- 
ated comparison  of  the  Doctrine 
and  Covenants  and  reading  the 
dictionary — in  both,  there  is 
statement  after  statement  of 
truths  not  always  connected 
with  the  preceding  information. 
This  fact,  however,  has  its  ad- 
vantages. One  does  not  have 
to  look  very  far  to  find  rich, 
inspiring  thoughts.  Also,  one 
must  concentrate  on  his  read- 
ing in  order  not  to  miss  salient 
principles.

A  Witness  for  the  Savior 
The  Lord  declared,  however, 
that  the  revelations  were  given 
after  the  manner  of  the  Prophet's 
language.  (D&C  1:24.) 
The  Doctrine  and  Covenants 
stands  as  a  witness  for  Jesus  that 
he  is  the  Savior  of  those  who  want 
to  be  saved.  The  doctrine  that 
salvation  comes  only  to  those 
who  accept  him  and  thus  he 
saves  only  them,  is  taught  plain- 
ly. (D&C  42:1;  29:17.)  In  truth, 
the  Doctrine  and  Covenants  is 
an  answer  to  the  present  thought 
among  some  theologians  that 
God  is  dead,  a  notion  that  has 
received  wide  publicity.  The 
modern  scriptures  declare  plain- 
ly that  God  lives!  Two  impor- 
tant accounts  of  the  appearance 
of  the  Savior  in  this  generation 
are  given  in  the  Doctrine  and 
Covenants:  (1)  the  stirring  de- 
scription by  the  Prophet  Joseph 
Smith  and  Sidney  Rigdon  is  a 
testimony  to  this  fact. 
And  now  after  the  many  testimonies 
which  have  been  given  of  him,  this 
is  the  testimony,  last  of  all,   which  we

To  the  Latter-day  Saint  the 
Lord  has  revealed  the  why  and 
wherefore  of  human  suffering, 
and  in  doing  so  he  has  given  us 
the  strength  and  power  to  en- 
dure. The  inspired  words  given 
to  the  Prophet  Joseph  Smith 
in  Liberty  Jail,  Missouri,  pro-

shall  be  for  thy  good.  The  Son 
of  Man  hath  descended  below 
them  all.  Art  thou  greater  than 
he?"  (D&C  122:7-8.)  Truly,  when 
man  knows  the  purpose  of  suffer- 
ing, he  is  better  able  to  endure 
it.  Ultimately,  sorrow,  suffering, 
and  like  adversities  are  for  our 
development,  growth  and  puri- 
fication. (Lesson  92  "Adver- 
sity," Relief  Society  Magazine, 
October  1968.)
