Black Walnut Gathering

Black walnuts had been used in every possible way: in the apple salad with its homemade sweet dressing; in the plain and chocolate cakes and cookies. Broken nutmeats spilled from the white and dark icings and topped the pumpkin pies.

After all were satiated with rich food, the men assembled walnut harvesting gear—pails and gunny sacks. The older women stayed behind to do the dishes and straighten up the kitchen and dining room, and get caught up on news and recipes.

The children dashed ahead, dogs barking and darting between them. When they reached the grove, they vied with each other in finding the biggest nuts, and filling pails.

In no time, it seemed, sacks were full and stood in neat rows, waiting to be tied and loaded into the waiting wagon. This done, the children clambered aboard, and with much shouting, urged the horses homeward.

The older members of the party walked slowly toward the house savoring the beauty of the day, the fresh air and exercise.

Back in the farmyard, the children drank long draughts of cold well water, and washed the grime, but not the stain, from their hands. This would remain for weeks. It would be the identifying mark that led one schoolmate to extend his hands to another, and say, "You've been walnut hunting, too."

While the men reloaded the pungent nuts in the various cars, the women scurried about, putting the leftovers from dinner on the table, where each could help himself.

As the signs of evening approached, everyone made ready to leave in order to get home by dark. In the corral, cows lowed, horses neighed, and calves bawled for food. Although the red sunset of the "sailor's delight" emblazoned the sky, one could faintly see the outline of the moon. The scarlet leaf of a maple drifted by.

Goodbyes were called, the cars started, and people settled back against the hard cushions. Truly, this black walnut gathering had been one to remember!

AUTUMN

[ILLUSTRATION]

Autumn has not come for me,
Until I see a chestnut tree
In all its golden majesty,
On some October day.

As sunshine's penetrating beams
Reach into branches, nature seems
Triumphant in the color schemes
That glitter, dance, and play.

Brilliant hues of gold are fired,
And tones of bronze and brown acquired,
As amber, saffron, leaves are pyred
In breathtaking array.

Of all the season's rare delights,
This scene most pleases and excites,
And lifts my soul to unknown heights,
On some October day.

—Ruth G. Rothe

Make Your Own

Baby's Book

Grace Diane Jessen

Every child treasures the stories, the pictures and mementos that tell of him alone. When these bits are bound together under one cover that can expand with the years as he grows and learns, they become doubly precious. You can make a book for your baby (or a grandchild or a friend) that will be more cherished than any book purchased in a store, because a bit of your love, your personality and creativity will remain stamped onto the pages forever. This book will be less expensive in terms of dollars and cents, but more precious to the one who receives it because you make it just for him.

The following directions tell how you can make a basic baby book. You may wish to make many changes or additions to fit your own ideas, and, of course, many pages can be added as the child grows.

MATERIALS NEEDED

First, purchase these materials from genealogical supplies:

One inexpensive "Book of Remembrance" cover. (These cost about 50¢ and are available in different colors and designs.)
One personal record page
One personal health record page
One pedigree chart
One portrait pedigree chart
One dozen sheets of plain heavy bond paper. (This has prepunched holes as do the other genealogy pages.)

Assemble your old greeting cards, appropriate pictures from magazines, a few packages of baby and flower seals, or a package of baby gift wrapping paper. These items can be used as you desire to add decorative touches to the pages or you may prefer to draw your own if you have artistic talent. You will need a fine felt-tip pen in a bright color for writing page headings and also a bottle of glue or rubber cement.

PROCEDURE

Begin by using one of the heavy plain sheets for the inside cover page. Use a stencil or write as neatly as possible "This is the story of" followed by a blank line if the book is for an unnamed baby. Write in the name in large, attractive letters if you know it. Trim the page with flowers, birds, pictures, or whatever appeals to you, but make the name the focal point of the page.

Page two is the personal record sheet, trimmed, if desired, with tiny flowers in the upper margin. Under the heading "Important Events," mother may wish to write such details of baby's birth as his measurements, attending physician, and time of arrival.

Page three is the personal health record which may be decorated similar to page two.
