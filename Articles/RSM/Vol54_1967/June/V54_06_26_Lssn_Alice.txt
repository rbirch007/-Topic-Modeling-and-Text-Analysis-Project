Alice Colton Smith =В» The Church in the spring of 1833 reflected the season's hope. Intellectual and spiritual ferment was in the air. True, the mob spirit was abroad in Missouri, but, in Kirtland, the brethren were engaged in raising money for a schoolhouse in which they could study together. As a group they would learn to magnify their callings. They were beginning to learn more about the Lord whom they worshipped. He was a God of love, permissively allowing all men to act in free agency, but for those who chose to be in his kingdom, as joint heirs with him in eternity, there were rules, laws, and expectations. As in any great kingdom, one's free agency must be exercised within the bounds of responsibility. The Lord (the King) whom the saints worshipped and wished to follow, called them to the realm of excellence. One's faith and one's love were not to be blind. Love was to be fortified by faith, and also by learning. Out of the combination of love, faith, devoted service, and learning would come wisdom. On May 4, a conference of High Priests initiated plans for raising funds to erect a schoolhouse in which structure the elders might be instructed. They were carrying out instructions previously given by the Lord. Two days later, the Lord by revelation taught the Church that "The glory of God is intelligence, or, in other words, light and _ truth. Light and truth forsake that evil one" (D&C 93:36-37), and that "He that keepeth his command-

We are to learn how to be responsible for one another. Our greatest concern should be for those whom God has entrusted to our care: our families, our friends, our neighbors, the saints of GodВ—in time, all men. We should be prepared to risk all in their behalf, at the Lord's request. We are to take time to love. There is to be no sitting on the sidelines. The Lord calls us to action, tempered by study, learning, wisdom, faith, intelligence, and the promptings of the Holy Ghost. Taylor = Our leaders from the Prophet Joseph Smith down to President David O. McKay, have been devoted exponents of the influence of the home upon the families who dwell thereinВ—both parents and children. We are all familiar with the emphasis which our beloved President McKay has given and is giving to the importance of the home in the rearing of our children. President Brigham Young said in one of his discourses, ""The only heaven for you is that which you make for yourselves," and again, ""Make your homes lovely, and adorn your hearts with the grace of God." The homemaking discussions for 1967-68 continue to be based on the general topic, Development Through Homemaking Education, and are designed to encourage Latter-day Saint mothers to make of their homes places of beauty, where happiness and mutual contentment are natural characteristics. To achieve this end, the discussions make a practical approach to the various contributing factors.
