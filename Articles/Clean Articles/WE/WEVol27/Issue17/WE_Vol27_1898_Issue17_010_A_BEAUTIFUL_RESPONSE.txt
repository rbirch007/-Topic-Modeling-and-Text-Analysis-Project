A BEAUTIFUL RESPONSE. 

A very bright little army woman rose to
the occasion well a few days ago when she
was one of the guests at a small luncheon
given at one of the large hotels for some
distinguished New England people. It was
a large affair and in an impromptu manner
a number of toasts were given. In the
midst of it the little army woman with hor-
hor heard some one say:
	“We have among us the wife of an
officer in the regular army, and I think it
is only fitting that she should respond to
the toast ‘The Flag.’ ” The men applaud-
ed and the women smiled encouragement,
and trembling a little as she held up the
glass of wine, the officer’s wife spoke:
	“I belong to a class of women to whom
the flag is especially near and dear. All
day it waves over the roofs of our little
homes in the far frontier, and at sundown
the rustle of its folds, mingled with the
notes of ‘The Star Spangled Banner,’ is
the lullaby of our babies. We know that
it will be the winding sheet of those dear-
est to us. Since the word Santiago has been
written in our hearts I see yet more in this
flag—in its red, the blood of our martyrs;
in the white, the paleness of their ashen
faces: in the blue, the lips set with the
word ‘Freedom,’ and in the stars the soul
that will shine on forever.’’

WOMAN’S EXPONENT. 

Salt Lake City, Utah,
			Sunday, Jan. 15, 1899.
Dear Friend:
	In my left hand I hold the last bright
little leaf that has fallen from your balmy
tree, while with my right hand I essay to
trace a few slight veins with the hope that
they may appear, with no unsalutary effect,
on your next leaf when it drops.
	Upon entering your beautiful city last
evening, knowing I had but twenty-four
hours to remain, and that all the daylight I
should spend here would be while the
sacred Sabbath was lulling her children in-
to quiet inactivity, a feeling of disappoint-
ment crept over me. But I had not long to
droop over my misfortunes, for I had scarce-
ly made myself comfortable at my hotel
when dinner was announced. Being hun-
gry I was glad to be seated at table with
the other guests, and not very anxious to
enter into conversation with my entirely
new neighbors until my appetite was some-
what abated.
	The first I remember of speaking was
when a young woman at my right offered
me pickles and I said I would prefer catch-
up. That broke the ice.
	“Are you from the North or the South?”
she asked.
	“From the East,” I answered. “And
yourself?”
	“I came up from the South to attend the
funeral. Suppose you are here for the
same purpose, are you not?”
	“Didn’t know there was to be a funeral,”
I answered. “Whose is it, and when does
it take place?”
	“You did not know that Brother George
Goddard’s funeral is to take place tomorrow
at 2 o’clock, in the Tabernacle! Ah, but
you are a stranger here then! Pardon me,
I had no thought but you were one of our
people.”
	We got on finely after that. My newly
found friend proved to be, I must believe,
a newly appointed guardian angel to con-

[Column 2]
duct me where I might find the door that
will be opened to all humble seekers after
light and truth, if they will but knock.
	She it was, that friend, that angel of
mine, who put into my hand this
First of January “Woman’s Exponent”
with its most wonderful tracings of
your magnificent organization, the Relief
Society, some of the marvelous works of
which are shown in the brief reports con-
tained in that paper.
	But she explained much to me regarding
your people in a way and manner that I
shall hardly find in books or papers, and
she took me with her to the Great Taber-
nacle to attend that most splendid funeral.
	I never can forget what I saw and heard
there, and I never want to. Henceforth I
would like to work for and with those same
Sunday School children, among whom that
grand chieftain of yours, General George
Goddard, has spent so much of his life.
	Your teachers of righteousness are the
most thrilling, soul-inspiring speakers I
have ever listened to, and I have heard the
most eminent and eloquent divines in dif-
ferent parts of the world.
	In making my adieux, I have now but to
say of your wonderful city and people, “The
half has never been told.” And I feel like
adding, “Your people shall be my people,
and your God my God.”
