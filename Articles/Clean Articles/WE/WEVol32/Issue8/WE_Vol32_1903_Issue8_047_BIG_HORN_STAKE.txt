Lesson VI.—Household Economy:
‘Tis not how much we have, but how we use it.’

	1. How may mothers be economical in 
		the home?
	2. What kind of material is always 
		cheapest? Why?
	3. How shall we care for our clothing?
	4. Teach children to have a place for 
		their clothes.
	5. Show how economy may be used 
		in cooking.
	6. Need we be stingy to be economi-
		cal?
	7. Will economical habits produce 
		happiness?
	8. How may we be economical in 
		making presents. 
		(Ref. p 146, Young Woman’s
		Journal vol. xiv, No. 5.)
				Annie Bischoff.

Lesson XIII.— What the Home can do for
	Intellectual Training:
1. Give opportunity to apply know-
	ledge gained at school.
2. Furnish regular attendance for the
	school.
3. The home should keep up interest
	in school work (a) keep school
	work before child, (b) give any
	needed assisstance.
4. Support the teacher-wlth good will
	and good word.
5. Furnish opportunity to use knowl-
	edge so that child sees its prac-
	tical value.
6. Provide all necessary books and
	material for school work.
7. Relieve child of home duties suffic-
	iently to guarantee time for prep-
	aration and home study.
8. The home above all should inspire
	a love for culture and emulation
	of worthy lives, by study of
	biography and good literature in
	the home reading that every
	family circle should provide.
		LUCY F. GRANT, Pres.
