Disarm 

By Minnie Johnson Hardy 

Spring has come to the desert, and yucca blossoms so white, Waxen and fair and fragrant, bloom in the morning light; A wood-dove coos in the thicket; but what is this Voice I hear? As the soft wind stirs the chaparral, it comes to me sweet and clear. It comes from the graves of dear ones, who wore the blue and the gray, Where flowers and flags are drooping, in loving memory today. It comes from the restless ocean, that cradle so blue and deep, Where many a brave young sailor has gone to his last, long sleep. From the graves of our splendid heroes, who, by some sad mischance, Have folded their hands forever, "Somewhere on the fields of France." The red poppies bloom in Flanders, but what do the white crosses spell? Let your heart speak, dear mother! You know full well—too well. In honor today of our heroes, our flags now float at half-mast; We have closed a chapter in history, on a sad mistaken past. "O nations of Earth, awaken, be not slaves to war's wild alarm," 'Tis the voice of our cherished dear ones—''Peace—Disarm— Disarm." "Peace, peace on earth, O my brothers; it is our dear Father's will; Hear ye not his voice commanding," "My children, ye shall not kill?" "Cease making men into soldiers, cease making guns to destroy, Cease building monsters-of-the-deep to slay your neighbor's boy." A mockingbird sings in the chaparral, but still on the wind that Voice, "Let Brotherhood rule in the future, Live, Love; In peace rejoice." It echoes from city to village, from palace to cottage and farm; "Let not our sacrifice be in vain, Peace—Disarm—Disarm.
