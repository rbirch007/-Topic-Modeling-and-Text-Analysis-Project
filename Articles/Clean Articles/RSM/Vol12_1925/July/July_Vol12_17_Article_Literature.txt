LITERATURE

THE BOOK OF MORMON AS LITERATURE

By ORSON F. WHITNEY

(Continued from June Number)

The Book of Mormon is not only a religious work of the highest order, but it is also a literary production of great merit. Its style is dignified and impressive, its language pure and forceful, and its narrative portions are marked by dramatic power and vivid description.

The book contains many passages of rare beauty and sublimity. Take, for instance, the vision of the tree of life, as recorded in the first book of Nephi. Here is imagery of the most exalted character, presented in language that is both simple and sublime. The description of the tree, "whose fruit was desirable to make one happy," the "rod of iron" leading to it, the "great and spacious building" filled with people "in the attitude of mocking," the "river of water" and the "mist of darkness"—all these are portrayed with a vividness and power that place this vision among the great allegories of literature.

Another passage of exceptional beauty is found in Alma's discourse on faith, where faith is compared to a seed that, when planted in the heart and nourished with care, grows into a tree bearing precious fruit. The analogy is sustained throughout with remarkable skill and effectiveness.

The farewell address of King Benjamin is another literary gem. Its tender pathos, its lofty moral tone, its practical wisdom, and its spiritual fervor combine to make it one of the most moving orations in all literature.

The book abounds in passages of poetic beauty. Many of the prophecies, particularly those of Isaiah as quoted by Nephi, rise to heights of sublime poetry. The Psalm of Nephi, in the fourth chapter of Second Nephi, is a beautiful expression of religious feeling, cast in poetic form.

In dramatic power, the Book of Mormon is especially rich. The story of the conversion of Alma the younger, the account of the Anti-Nephi-Lehies and their covenant of peace, the thrilling narrative of Captain Moroni and the title of liberty, the tragic tale of the destruction of the Nephites—these and many other episodes are told with a dramatic force that holds the reader spellbound.

The book's literary excellence is further evidenced by its influence upon the style of many writers who have come under its spell. Its rhythmic prose, its balanced sentences, its apt figures of speech, and its general dignity of expression have left their mark upon American literature.
