Pioneer Stories and Incidents ON THE RIVER PLATTE President Amy Brown Lyman [This is the fifth in a series of true Pioneer incidents to be Published by The in honor of the 1947 Utah Centennial Celebration) THE Platte River, which stretches across the continent from the Rocky Mountains to the Missouri River, has been the scene of countless thrilling incidents in the history of the Latter-day Saints. Their travels, for approximately seven hundred miles, were scarcely ever more than a mile from its shores. It was a river of hope for the thousands who traveled by its side and who crossed and recrossed it by ford, by ferry, or by boat. Its shores teemed with Indians and also with buffalo, elk, birds, and other wild life seeking water and feed.
If the old Platte could speak, it could tell innumerable stories�stories both romantic and pathetic, but all of the most thrilling interest. It could tell of the unique experiences of pioneers and pathfinders, of stampedes, prairie fires, thunderstorms. It could tell, also, of the joys and pleasures of the pioneers, and of their difficulties and sorrows. It could supply data for a great epic�the epic of the plains. Even today the Old Mormon Trail along the Platte is marked, because a great portion of it is covered by the roadbed of the Union Pacific Railroad.
One of the interesting stories of the Platte River is that of a little New England girl of nineteen, and a tall, brave young teamster of twenty-seven, whose coming together has left an entire period of their journey along the shores of the Platte from the Missouri River to Deer Creek, Wyoming, where their marriage took place just before their entry into Salt Lake Valley. It was in the year 1852, when the migration across the plains was at such a height that there was an almost unbroken stream of covered wagons winding its way to the West. This interesting young couple were Mary Ann Stearns and Oscar Winters, parents of Sister Augusta Winters Grant.
Mary Ann was the daughter, by a former marriage, of Mary Ann Pratt, wife of Parley P. Pratt. In the absence of Elder Pratt, who was doing missionary work for the Church at the time, Sister Pratt and her three children, Mary Ann, nineteen; Olivia, twelve; and Moroni, eight; made their own way across the plains. Mary Ann was of great help to her mother, and willingly assumed a good share of the family responsibility.
When they reached the first camp five miles on their way, and it was found necessary to discard their heavy new cooking stove to lighten their load, Mary Ann was sent back to Kanesville, in a buggy going that way, to order a thin, sheet-iron stove. Three trips in all were made before the stove was ready, and, on all these trips, Mary Ann went with Oscar Winters, who drove her back to be on hand whenever Sister Pratt Page 747 needed help of any kind. He often assisted her teamster, who was inexperienced in handling oxen, over difficult situations, and saw that her equipment was kept in good repair. These various contacts gave him added opportunity to pay court to his sweetheart. As a matter of fact, Oscar Winters was helpful to everybody. An expert in the handling of horses and oxen, he was the wagon master and_ had charge of all the teams and drivers in the company. He was, therefore, a familiar figure to everyone as he rode back and forth on his horse, helping wherever he was needed. Oscar had first met Mary Ann six years before this, in 1846, in Nauvoo, during the exodus from that city. He had been a member of a company of infantry which had been trying to guard the temple and the city from the mob in the battle of Nauvoo. He was no doubt happy, now, to be able to renew his acquaintance with the Pratt family. Oscar's father, Hyrum Winters, who was himself preparing to leave for the West with a later company, had also befriended Sister Pratt by seeing that the wagon which had been provided for her by the Emigration Fund was in good condition. When he had delivered it to her he had informed her that it was one of the best made in his shop. The son Oscar's journey across the plains was a happy and romantic one, but that of his father and mother was sad. During their trip, his mother, Rebecca Winters, passed away and was buried near Scotts Bluff on the Platte River, where her grave was marked at that time by a piece of the tire of a wagon wheel. Many years later, however, a monument was erected by her family as a tribute to her and to all other mothers who lost their lives on the plains. Also a marker was placed at her grave by the Daughters of the American Revolution, as a tribute to a "daughter." It was in August that the marriage of Oscar and Mary Ann took place at Deer Creek, on a Sabbath evening. 'The company had laid over this week end for repairing the wagons and for a rest on the Sabbath. On her wedding day, Mary Ann looked charming and cool in a green gingham dress, which had been carefully pressed by a flatiron borrowed from a neighbor, their own iron having been left at one of the camps where it had been set out to cool, and for8otten. Her hair was done in proper fashion, although she had no mirror to consult. The ceremony was performed by Elder Lorenzo Snow, who had paused at the camp on his return home from a mission to Italy. The wedding meal, which consisted of fresh bread baked in a skillet, fresh butter, a piece of meat, and cold water, was greatly enjoyed by all. Soon after their arrival in Utah, Brother and Sister Winters settled in the beautiful little village of Pleasant Grove, which became their permanent home, and where their eight children were reared. & 748 ?
