With this assurance to buoy them, these men walked past the Indians and into the thicket. Hours later when they had reached the camp of Washakee, the peace-loving Chief of the Shoshonies, they knelt and thanked God for his protection. Washakee, upon hearing of their misadventure, sent some of his own braves to recover the horses and to punish the Indian who had stolen Brother Richards' mare. Days later, when Lieutenant Knowlton and his men arrived back in camp, they learned that Captain Smith had led another party into Idaho to recover some property that 657 the Indians had stolen from the Government. For days this group lived upon dry bread and the roots and berries they found. As they attempted to cross a mountain stream, a horse fell into a deep pool, and its rider was drowned before his comrades could reach him. His was the only death sustained by the entire company. While the Smith group was still in Wyoming, the company commanded by Colonel Burton was mustered into the service of the United States by the Governor of Utah Territory. While on duty, this company escorted several notable Westerners across the plains. Because of the spring thaw, their task was extremely arduous. At one point the snow and slush were so thick that they had to accompany a Congressman through an area ruled by a band of lawless renegades, some red, some white. In this area they found over a dozen deserted and pillaged way stations, from which they gathered many sacks of mail and sent them to Salt Lake City for distribution. After turning over their command to Colonel Connor and his soldiers, these brave and peaceful men returned to their homes, taking with them the thanks of the United States Government. Of them some latter-day chronicler might have written as Helaman wrote of that similar group of Americans whose story is related in the Book of Mormon.
