Mendenhall Former Member, General Board of Relief Society Sur Auckland ISLAND a To you, I extend an invitation Pon liberal amounts of sunshine to relax in your most comfortable chair and dream with me of a paradise anchored deep in the vast South Pacific � beautiful New Zealand. This most picturesque and lush paradise consists of two principal islands sprawling lengthwise from the north to the south, covering an area of approximately 103,736 square miles. These islands rise forth out of the deep, glistening, white-capped waters and truly remind me of two delicately shaded, highly polished pieces of greenstone. A semi-precious, very hard stone located, to a great extent, at Arahura on the west coast of New Zealand's South Island. This great temperate-zoned Dominion, which lies about equal distance between the equator and the South Pole, is nestled between latitudes 34 degrees and 48 degrees south and is blessed with a very enjoyable climate. The beautiful rainstorms, water from the snowcapped peaks, and more 188 help to provide a fertile area� making a land of diversity and magnificent wonderment. To the 180,000 Maori people, New Zealand's native race, this is Aotearoa, the land of the Long White Cloud. This is home, although many of our dear bronzeskinned, dark-haired friends continually speak in lovely, softtoned voices of their genealogies, repeating them from memory, and of their ancestral homeland in far distant Hawaiki. From a Maori legend we read: We came from Hawaiki�the Great From Hawaiki�the Long�from Hawaiki�the Distant Hawaiki is the largest island in the leeward group of the Society Islands at the hub of the Polynesian Triangle. The Polynesian people within this triangle are kinsmen of the Maori, and thus they bear resemblance and speak the same basic language, but as my husband and I have traveled from island to island, such as from Tonga to Samoa or Tahiti to New Zealand or Fiji�and as we have lived among these children of God, whom we love and admire, we have been immediately aware of the fact that there is in each group a little different pronunciation for the same word. A dear old Maori gentleman explained to us that this was'brought about by dropping different letters from the dialect and by adding others. For example the '"k" and "ng" have been omitted by the Polynesians living in the Society Islands, and thus the word Hawatki is pronounced Havait. In New Zealand the '"w'' replaces the '"'v' and the "k" is restored, thus the land of the Maori forebears is pronounced Hawaiki. In the Cook Islands, the "h" is omitted, and thus the word Hawaiki is pronounced Avaiki. So we see a similarity of names and people. Yes, it was from this Hawaiki that, centuries ago, navigators, explorers, and great sea-faring Maori people, many with their wives, families, food, seeds, water plants, and their cultures, dispersed in handmade canoes, with a star and sufficient faith to guide their deliberate venture into the unknown. Uncharted waters of the Pacific. The first European to discover New Zealand was Abel Janszoon Tasman, a Dutchman. It was in 1642 that he attempted to land, but he met strong resistance from the Maori and his landing was foiled. Captain James Cook was successful in his endeavor to go ashore in 1769�127 years later. Captain Cook then became the claimant of New Zealand for New Zealand�A Silhouette in Green Great Britain; thus the twilight of freedom, to the extent it had been known by its settlers, the Maori, had arrived. A_ treaty known as the Treaty of Waitangi was signed, in 1840, by Maori chiefs, submitting the supreme power to British sovereignty, and, in return, their rights pertaining to land were guaranteed by law. By 1840, European settlement was well underway, and today, Europeans number approximately two and a fourth million. Many of the Maori and Pakiha (Maori name for the Europeans) have formed true _ friendships. They are not only neighbors but are neighborly. They sit side by side at Church meetings. Some work closely together in Parliament, and many in community, business, and church activities. The children attend the same schools and are uniformed alike. They are playmates. All are loyal to the British flag and are working together for the prosperity of their young country and the happiness of all within its great boundaries. Wellington, the capital city of New Zealand, covers an area of only twenty-eight square miles, but has a population of approximately 232,000 residents. In 1865, the seat of the government was transferred to Wellington from Auckland. Here the Parliament Building is located near Lambton Harbour. New Zealand is governed by its own parliament of eighty elected members, four of whom are Maori. The Governor General is appointed by and represents Queen Elizabeth II. Wellington City overlooks the mighty Cook Strait which flows between North and South Islands and is practically enclosed by water and by broken hills, heavily populated. It is New Zealand's central seaport and one of its important nerve centers, and is also a very busy metropolis. Trams, cable cars, taxis, buses, bicycles, private cars, and pedestrians practically choke the highways and byways during the morning, noon, and evening rush hours. The principal business and commercial center of New Zealand is Auckland, which has a population of 520,000 people, the largest in the country. On North Island, Auckland and Wellington are the main urban areas and have the finest harbors. On South Island, Dunedin (which has a great religious history) and Christchurch are the principal cities. The country has a wonderful educational system for its people. They have classes for children in kindergarten, primary, and postprimary schools. Every child must be enrolled at age seven in a public or private school. For higher education, New Zealand has a university college at Auckland which specializes in architecture, commerce, and engineering; the Victoria University at Wellington specializes in law, the Canterbury, in engineering and music; and Otago, at Dunedin, in medicine, dentistry, engineering, and mining. They have two agricultural schools, Massey Agricultural College, and the Lincoln Agricultural College. Last, but not least, is the Latterday Saint Church College of New Zealand. It is open to all students, Latter-day Saints and nonLatter-day Saints of approximate 190 ages from thirteen to nineteen years, whose past conduct would render them worthy to be enrolled. It is co-educational and accommodates both boarding and day students. Now for diversity, let us dream of Wairakei and Rotorua on the North Island. Here one stands in awe while viewing and listening to one of the thermal wonders of the world. Here geysers spout skyward, grayish brown mud pools boil rapidly, with uncanny force, through winding cracks and crevasses in the earth, white billows of steam curl in clouds, and _ pressures shake the ground on which one stands. I can readily understand why a friend of ours from America said, "Oh, let's get out of here." The constant trembling of the earth reminds me of a continual earth tremor. Hot pools of water provided the Maori women of earlier days with laundry privileges and a place to cook their food. Today, engineers have drilled through earth's hot crust deep down into the ground, many times over 3,000 feet, and have tapped this underground hot. Water system, harnessing its forces for electric power and for heating purposes. For contrast, my husband and I have enjoyed motoring to New Zealand's largest lake � Lake Taupo. Here in our small boat we drift along out into the bluish green waters with utter peace roundabout. We look toward the south, and there on a bright sunny day we see Mount Tongariro, with its cap of snow, and not too far distant, Mount Ngauruhoe, an ac- tive volcano, with its billowing vapor circling in the blue sky. The lush verdancy which surrounds us is breathtaking. The luxuriant New Zealand fern, bushland, and wild flowers with their coats of many colors are indeed something to behold. On South Island, we find New Zealand's highest mountain, Mount Cook. It towers above the long chain of the Southern Alps, its peaks reaching upward toward the azure sky as high as 12,349 feet. Surrounding them is Mount Cook National Park which covers an area of 173,000 acres. Approximately one-third of this great park is covered with permanent snow and_ glaciers. We have watched these glaciers spilling downward from great heights, with cracks and crevasses opening and closing. They slowly move forward at times as much as two feet a day. Let's wave our magic wand now and view something entirely different in nature. The Hastings area is often referred to as the "Orchard Garden Center' of New Zealand. When we visit in the homes in this city, fresh fruit is served with all meals and at tea time (mid-morning and mid-afternoon). The apples, pears, raspberries, and potatoes are most delicious, and our dear Maori friends supply our needs wherever we are in New Zealand. The fruit yield from this area is approximately two and a quarter million bushels of first grade fruit per season and more is being produced annually. A never-to-be-forgotten sight is to witness the fruit orchards in full bloom. This is indeed a glimpse of quiet beauty and a New Zealand�A Silhouette in Green perfume not yet perfected in bottles. The flower arrangements in hotels and in the homes are beyond description, pleasing to the eye. We were motoring through New Zealand not long ago with some of our American friends interested in re-forestration. We were certainly in the right place, for the largest manmade forest in the world is found in this small country. It covers 284,000 acres. Our friends were also interested in farming, livestock, freezing works, and wool production. We drove through the countryside, along the river edge, low hills and higher slopes, feasting our eyes on the green paddocks enclosed with hedgerows where sheep and cattle were peacefully grazing. We saw wild flowers in full bloom, the pohutukawa (New Zealand's Christmas tree) dressed in its profusion of red flowers, white mountain lilies, pines, and bushlands. We discussed the fact that New Zealand produces over one-tenth of all the wool in the world, that fifty per cent of the mutton and beef produced is exported, as well as ninety-three per cent of the lamb, so one can see that it is plentiful. The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints has been mindful of New Zealand and its people for many, many years, for it was in 1854 that the first missionaries were sent out from Church headquarters to that faraway land. In 1880, or twenty-six years later, the elders were assigned to go out into the villages and there organize and conduct classes in schools that the children might receive an education. This assignment was carried out until the government public schools were available. The first school of consequence to be erected by our Church in New Zealand was the Maori Agriculture College near Hastings in Hawkes Bay. This was dedicated in 19138, and classes were in session in those buildings for eighteen years until they were destroyed by the Napier earthquake. In 1952, twenty-one years later, construction began on the new Church College of New Zealand on Tuhikaramea Road, near Frankton. It is now known as Templeview. This school was erected under the Church building labor missionary program. Our own Elder Matthew Cowley at the very young age of seventeen years was called to fill a mission in New Zealand. In a short time he had won the hearts of the Maori. I have heard him say, "It took me five years to fill a three-year mission."' He did remain for five years in order to complete an assignment given him by the President of the Church to translate the Doctrine and Covenants and the Pearl of Great Price into the Maori language, also to revise and edit the previous translation of The Book of Mormon. In 1938, Elder Cowley responded to a call to serve as the President of the New Zealand Mission. He now was not returning to a strange land, people, and customs, but to familiar places and friends�to a people he loved and who loved him. It was during this period that Brother Cowley saw the need of and felt a desire for a co-educational college in which the hearts and souls of Latter-day Saint 192 youth of New Zealand, as they gained an education, might radiate a greater spiritual development through an_ increased knowledge of the guiding principles of the gospel. On January 24, 1955, President David O. McKay and Sister McKay were to arrive in New Zealand on Church business and to visit the saints. This was the first time a President of the Church had visited their Maoriland, and only a few had ever seen a President before. Hundreds gathered at Whenuapai Airport. Hearts were beating rapidly and eyes were searching the skies for a view of the large airliner. As President and Sister McKay stepped from the plane, hundreds of voices blended in beautiful harmony, and many songs were sung by those with tearstained cheeks, red eyes and noses, but with hearts full of gratitude. A royal welcome was given at the college site, after which President McKay said, ''With all my heart I say, God bless you that you may ever treasure in your hearts that which has brought us together here tonight, without which we never would have met, and that is the gospel of Jesus Christ, the Church to which we belong. May we treasure the ideals of it, remain true to it, and thus cement for time and all eternity the friendship, the love, that dwells in our hearts tonight." He also said, ""This school is already opened if young people are learning not only the trades but to beautify their homes. This is worth the entire trip."
