PATCH QUILT Time brought back Cut up in pieces And then sewed down In tiny stitches To warmly cover Snow-white batting. Scraps of cloth? No, Dancing daughters, Rowdy sons in sturdy breeches. The wool once worn at skating parties And cotton shirts one festive autumn, The cashmere dress you wore one Christmas, The satin when you sang that Easter, And denim when you dug at ditches. Scraps of cloth? No, Friends returning, Thimbles clicking—smiling—telling. Children underneath the frame Appetite for play and pudding. Scraps of cloth? No, Memories brought forth To bless the home For a time like this. —Hazel Loomis 589 The breeze skimming the wisteria below my window brought the sound of laughter into my room. When I turned to snap out my lamp, the magazine I had been reading slipped from my bed to the floor. I could hear her voice above all the others: "You don't know my mom." "Cool it." "Give her the plan." "Bye, kid." "I'll meet you at lunch by the east door." In a moment I could hear the door click and the familiar scuff of her shoes in the tile foyer. Then I waited to recognize the comfortable sounds of her midnight snack: the clap of the refrigerator door, the clatter of dishes. But she did not go into the kitchen. The soft steady beat of her shoes against the carpet continued into the stairwell, up the stairway. Marilyn McMeen Miller When my door cracked I started forward in the darkness.
