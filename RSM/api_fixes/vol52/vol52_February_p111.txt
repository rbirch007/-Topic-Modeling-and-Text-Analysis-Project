Madame Letitia
Dorothy Clapp Robinson

TISH Haworth woke with a sour taste in her mouth; not, she told herself, from anything she had eaten the night before, but because of something she had discovered about herself.

She was an old woman with no mind, will power, or memory. She had suspected it a time or two, but had refused to believe the evidence. There was the time she had lost her legal papers, and had eventually found them right where she had put them so she wouldn't forget them. Around a quilt on Relief Society work day, she had heard younger women tell of doing just such thoughtless acts. That proved it was not age that made her forget, but last night was different. No younger woman would have made such a fool of herself. There was no place in society for one of her age and degree of frayed will power.

Tish reached and moved the window drape so she could see out. Cold weather, cold, rainy, and dreary. She dropped the drape and tried to coax sleep by snuggling under the blanket. No use. The events marched relentlessly through her mind.
