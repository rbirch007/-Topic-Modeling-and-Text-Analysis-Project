	WE CAN.

The dearest of all precious treasures,
	The pet and the pride of our hearts;
What sunshine and sweetness and gladness
	Its heavenly presence imparts!
OUR darling will stay with us, surely;
	For its future we lovingly plan;
WE "couldn't keep house" without Baby—
Yet it goes, and we find that we can!

What wealth of intelligence reigneth
	In manhood's awakening power;
What purity crowneth our maidens,
	Like th' freshness of opening flowers.
Oh! the young folks. our hope, who are taking
	First parts in life's beautiful "play;"
Death comes and selects, oft the brightest—
	We can hear it—there's no other way.

The kindest and truest companion
	The fendest and gentlest friend,
At whost loss every prospect seems blighted,
	Life's interests all suddenly end:
Such are called, and half blind and bewildered,
	We rise from the groveling dust;
We stagger and reel, but we gather
	And stand, for we CAN when we MUST.

The leaders of people and nations,
	The men whom we value and prize,
And look upon as without equals,
	So noble and gifted and wise;
They leave us, and yet we press onward,
	As earnest and seemingly strong;
There is no standing still for the people,
	We must and we can move along.

We can live, though bereft of the blessings,
	Which seem more than half of our lives;
The babies, the youths and the maidens,
	Even parents, and husbands and wives:
Our Prophets, indeed, may be taken,
	And we bow'neath the chastening rod,
'Till the blessed, sweet Comforter cometh—
	We can do without all but our GOD!
		LULA.
L. G. Richards


"STAND STILL."

	A young friend asked me what we should
do "if Congress, or the Supreme Court of
the United States, should take us in hand
as earnestly as it appears they intend to
do?"
	I answered by opening the Bible and
reading from the fourteenth chapter of
Exodus, thirteenth verse: "And Moses
said unto the people, Fear ye not, stand
still, and see the salvation of the Lord,
which He will shew to you to-day."
	This is the way I feel, I said. If the
Lord wants us to make any particular move
in the matter, more than we have already
done, He will let us know. Otherwise, we
will "stand still." We will at least show
our enemies that we have more patience
and politeness than some of their party
possess. We will listen while they are
speaking to us and of us, no matter how
absurd their expressions may seem to us.
And that is more than some of our accusers
in this city are willing to do.
	At the Woman's Mass Meeting held in
the Theatre, two ladies of the opposing
party took seats directly back of where I
was sitting. And when Miss Snow in her
remarks said, "As true and loyal American
citizens we claim the right," &c., one of
these ladies observed, in a tone loud enough
to be distinctly heard, "Not very loyal!"
	Further on in her remarks Miss Snow re-
ferred to the Latter-day work as being es-
tablished "upon the principles of purity,


peace and righteousness," when one of the
ladies at my back echoed, "Purity! and
Righteousness!"
	Thus the speakers remarks were com-
mented upon, until she spoke of the feel-
ings of sympathy instead of revenge which
she felt for those banded together in our
midst to work evil, and who are trying to
measure arms with the Almighty. Then
came whispers from the ladies behind me,
of "Let's go!"
	And they did go. Instead of listening
to the Mormon women's vindications and
explanations of their faith and practices,
and why they held to them, these ladies
arose and left the house before the first
speaker had concluded her remarks.
	This is but one of a great many instances
of a similar kind. And such are the char-
acters who represent us, or misrepresent us,
as disloyal, unrighteous and everything
bad. They do not know us, and they refuse
to hear us tell what we are. But we can
afford to bear with them, and when we
have nothing to say or do for a little time
we can "stand still and see the salvation of
the Lord."					L. G. R.

				TIME.

	Time is a measured portion of duration.
It is divided into three grand divisions, viz.,
the past, the present and the future. The
past we know. Memory, that faculty of
the mind that retains so vividly the knowl-
edge of all our past thoughts and actions,
does its duty faithfully in this regard. It
brings to mind the bitter remembrance of
things that are past and connects them with
the present and the future. It makes them
live through all time, and may, perhaps,
through all eternity. It shows us all our
failings, all our imperfections, and points
out to us wherein we have failed to come
up to the standard of our expectations. It is
therefore necessary that we remember the
past, to make us not unmindful of the pre-
sent, and to better prepare us for the future.
There are some things we would not forget.
Memory may bring many pleasant recollec-
tions, that we are as anxious to retain as
we are to forget all past trials and disap-
pointments. There is in every one's life
some bright oasis to which memory will
revert with gladness, and linger on, as a
sweet strain of music to the weary heart.
The present is now. We cannot live in
time that is past, nor in time that is to
come. We actually live only now. It is
now we must bend all our energies to make
life a grand success. Now we must work
and toil for the crown of reward we are
anxious to gain; now the spade must be
struck, the soil turned up and our crop of
good deeds sown, if we wish to reap a har-
vest. It is now we lay the foundation of
future usefulness and greatness. Our time
is our life; it is given us in trust, and like
all other things that are entrusted to our
care, we will be held accountable for the
use of it. Present time is all we can call
our own and it is wise to make the best use
of it we cau. Fill it with all the good
deeds, pleasant thoughts and kind actions
in our power, so that when it becomes the


past, it will be one we will charge memory
to retain. Of the flowery future we know
nothing, but dream much. It is the place
where imagination loves to live. It is
where we look for the fulfilling of all our
fond hopes and bright dreams. It is where
we expect the grand scenes of life's drama
to be enacted. What bright hopes and
rosy dreams we have of the future. God
grant they may all be realized.

THE WISE OWLS.

	[Apropos to the decision of the Supreme
Court refusing a woman the right to appear
before it.]
	In a certain place there were nine old
owls who sit all day on a bench and looked
very wise. They tried a great many hard
cases and listened solemnly to the chatter-
ing of magpies and the cawing of crows and
the twittering of tomtits. But one morning
when the blue jay came in and asked them
to hear her, they all hooted in horror and
cried out, "Go away, you are a female!"
	Moral—If you wish to succeed, do not be
a female.—A fable by Lillie Devereux Blake.
	Now that the Supreme Court have ad-
mitted Mrs. Lockwood, to practice before
that honorable body there is a good chance
for Mrs. Blake to write another fable with
a moral, and as her efforts in that direction
are certainly very pleasing we hope soon
to see one on this same subject.

	NEWTON, CACHE Co., Feb. 14th 1879.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	A brief report of the annual meeting of
Newton Relief Society, held in the school
house Feb. 10th 1879. We were honored
with the presence of Bishop W. H. Rigby
and his Counselors J. H. Barker and W. H.
Griffin, and there were several others of
the brethren who attended the meeting by
invitation of the President, M. A. Parsons,
there was also a good attendance of the
members. After singing and prayer, our
President made some encouraging remarks,
and stated the object of the meeting. Bish-
op Rigby then addressed the meeting, he
referred to various principles pertaining to
the Relief Society, which were very in-
teresting. He informed us that it was the
largest consolidated quorum of Newton,
hence its importance, and the necessity of
the members being wise in all their move-
ments.
	The financial report of the past year was
presented and accepted. Summary of the
same:
Cash on hand from 1877......................... $12 00
Received by donations from members	  31 58

		Total						  43 58
Wheat on hand from 1877.......................  6 bu.
Wheat donated by the sisters in 1878.....  38
Received on city lot....................................  26

		Total						   70
	To the Logan Temple $26 45. By labor
and means, with the aid of the brethren, as
well as the sisters, we have fenced and
cultivated our city lot to the amount of $72,
70.
	The meeting also passed a unanimous
vote in favor of upholding the principle of
plural marriage, taking the Resolutions on
the same topic as published in the "WO-
MAN'S EXPONENT" of Jan. 1st 1879.
	Mrs. Mary Griffin, a visitor from the
Clarkston Relief Society, of which she is
the President, expressed her approval of
polygamy as revealed from God, and prac-
tised among the Latter-day Saints, in a
very decided manner.
	Mrs. J. M. Welchman, who had passed an
experience of many years as a practical
polygamist, said: she would not like to
give it up, though there were many sacri-
fices to make, but it all tended to purify
the life and character of women, she real-
ized that it was a principle of temporal
and eternal salvation.
	Singing, Benediction by Bishop W. F.
Rigby.
	A party was held in the evening, which
was well attended, we had a very pleasant
time and all present seemed to enjoy
themselves.

	"MY SON GIVE ME THINE HEART."

She rocked her baby, singing low
	Love tones that mothers
To tender words that mothers know
	How fittingly to string.
And aye! the secret of her song,
	In each and every part;
Was ever all the sweet time long,
	'My child, give me thine heart.'

When nature's fairest beauties smiled.
	She loved with him to stray,
And taught the listening trustful child,
	To fairer realms the way.
And when amid earth's scenes of ill.
	Her anxious fears did start;
The pleading of her love, was still,
	My child give me thine heart.

My child give me thine heart, he hears
	The heavenly Father's call;
For fleeing to his throne, her tears
	And prayer before him fall,
When Christ hath died that thou might'st live,
	And facing evils dart,
Thy mother's love, no less would give
	Wilt thou withold thine heart?

Alas! how e'en Immanuel's cry
	Of desolation, pain;
Pleading for strength, with him on high,
	While midnight hour did wane;
Fell on disciples loved, who slept,	
	Though soon with him to part
While He in love, and anguish wept,
	'Father give me their hearts.'

Alas! for him, who had denied
	His Master's name and power;
Of whom, e'en nature testified,
	In crucifixions hour.
Spare me the cry of souls that wait
	The dread command depart,	
Remembering at last, too late,
	'My child, give me thine heart. 
		AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON.

		CONSISTENCY THOU ART A 
				JEWEL.

	Will our enemies please be consistent, if
only for the novelty of the thing? How
reasonable they are! they never turn their
coats, no never! A short time since the
cry was the poor, down-trodden, broken-
hearted women of Utah, who dared not
raise their heads, much less their voices,
for fear of the vengeance of their tyrant
husband, for instance tie them up in sacks
and throw them into the Salt Lake to
pickle. They said give the poor, ignorant
creatures the franchise, and they will turn
the tables on their tyrants by pitching them
into the big pickling tub nature has pro-
vided. Well, our husbands gave us the
right of franchise, showing how little fear
they had of our vengeance, and for eight
years we have gone to the polls with those
husbands to cast our votes for those we felt
the best qualified to hold the offices we had
the power to bestow; no quarrelling, no
rowdyism, all peace and harmony.
	All of a sudden we hear a cry. What's
the matter now? Why, those women that
we have liberated, instead of voting for us,
and giving us all power to crush their ty-
rants, they are as bad as their husbands; we
thought we had thrown sand in their eyes,
and blinded their reason, but we find we
have failed; we thought they were fools,
but find they have out-witted us. Now,
what are we to do? We will move earth
and that other place, where they keep the
furnace red-hot, but we will stop your vo-
ting. What have they done here in Tooele
County? Thrown out all the precincts but
two. What for? To use the words of one
poor sniveling creature of this most liber-
al (?) party, who put in a petition asking

[Column 2]
that all returns from all the precincts voting
the peoples' ticket be thrown out, "because
the WOMEN voted." How magnanimous!
poor fellow, I wonder if he ever had a
mother, a wife, or a daughter; if so, what
do they think of him? Is he not afraid
they will pickle him?
	What is all this hue and cry about? Is
it our religion? Oh no! we don't care what
you believe in, you can believe in anything
you like only you must not practice it.
They roll up their eyes in holy horror at
plurality of wives, yet practice it them-
selves, after their own manner. The old
saying is "those that make the greatest
cry of 'stop thief!' are those that need
watching." What do such men care for wo-
man but to abuse her? Nothing; less than
nothing. The vast majority of mankind, if
women will do as they wish, and keep
quiet, they will spend their money to adorn
their person—never mind their soul—until
they get tired of them and want a change,
then if married to one they will often treat
her to magnificent contempt, twirl a cigar
between their lips, and talk of the folly
and imbecility of women, and bestow time
and means upon other women to beguile
their idle hours; if unmarried, spend their
money and degrade their manhood in gam-
bling dens and other vile places.
	They call the Mormons vile, whose lives
are spent in caring for their wives and chil-
dren, both temporally and spiritually, who
endeavor to exalt themselves and all de-
pending on them, who work early and late
to provide for their household honestly; not
striving as our enemies are doing, no mat-
ter how, to get their hands into "Uncle
Sam's" pocket, to rob him in utter shame-
lessness, following the Quaker's advice to
his son: "My son, get money; get it hon-
estly if thee can, but get money." Any one
that reads the papers cannot help but see
that the amount of fraud inflicted on the
people of the United States is stupendous,
and every pretended investigation is but an
added robbery of the nation. If our de-
famers are sincere, let them commence at
home to set things right, and they will find
so much to do that they will forget to trou-
ble us.
	Then again, how very consistent are the
friends of the Chinese Bill (these same
men); their strongest arguments are that
they have no family ties; they do not amal-
gamate; they do not seek to become citi-
zens, but as soon as they obtain sufficient
money return to their own country. Now
they must be driven out of the country, or
exterminated for not having families. The
Mormons must receive the same treatment
for having families, and seeking to become
citizens of the United States. Oh, how
very consistent! They are like the man
that was going to be hung—could not feel
satisfied. Well, they may drive John from
the country, and do many other mighty
works, but we intend to stay here so long
as we or our posterity may live.
							MARY.

THE WASHINGTON CONVENTION.
CONTINUED.

	Friday afternoon session: On the platform
in Lincoln Hall were assembled more ladies
than at any previous session; Dr. C. B. Pervis, of
Philadelphia, and Federic Douglas occupied
front seats, with a crowded audience anxiously
awaiting. President Mrs. Stanton addressed
the Convention on the condition of the age
and the tendencies to a more one-sided suffrage
system. She said, "All are now admitted to
the ballot except idiots, Chinamen and wo-
men." that the existing state of affairs tended
to "irreverence, infidelity and corruption."
She pronounced it absurd, and added that under
man's rule the system of politics was such it
was impossible to tell whether the Chief Execu-
tive Officer of the land held his seat by fraud;
or otherwise; she referred to the cipher system
as looking exceedingly bad.
	Mrs. Matilda Joslyn Gage next delivered her
remarkable speech upon United States' Rights
vs. States' Rights.
	We consider Mrs. Gage's speech of so much
value that we intend to publish it in full, also
Mrs. Stanton's opening address, Miss Anthony's
lecture, Bread and the Ballot, Mrs. Lockwood's,
Mrs. Slocum's and our own delivered on the
first evening of the session; but our readers
will be so kind as to give us time, our paper be-
ing so small we can only publish one at a time.
	Mrs. Helen M. Slocum, of New York City, a
very finely educated and talented lady, with a
very intelligent face and an earnestness that
must have thrilled every woman's heart in
that large assembly, so impressive were her
manner and tone, plead as a mother would
plead for her own child, for the fallen and de-
praved women who sell their souls for bread,
and the innocent children born to shame and
reared in crime, if suffered to exist at all. She
appealed to the mothers of the land and asked
them if they did not think it was time they
had the privilege of owning their own chil-
dren. She informed them there were but few
States in the Union where the father had not
power to dispose of his children as he pleased,
by appointing guardians, taking them from
their mothers, and even willing away her un-
born babe if he so chooses. Mrs. Slocum stated
there were, according to the carefully prepared
statistics of the "Working Woman's Protective
Union,'. five hundred thousand women in New
York City and its immediate vicinity, outside
of domestic service, who are working for wages
which do not average over two dollars a week.
She vehemently exclaimed, "Can any one tell
how it is possible for these women to clothe,
feed and shelter themselves upon such wages
as these? *	* There is a great army of wo-
men called outcasts, whose ranks are constantly
being filled for want of bread. *	*	* The
men who withhold from women the power of

[Column 2]
self-protection through the ballot, thus pre-
venting them from being able to dictate terms
to their employers, are largely responsible for
this state of things." She appealed most elo-
quently and fervently to mothers. *	* "We
mothers cannot separate ourselves from the
great mass of women, and who of us are to f[-]r-
nish the recruits for this army whose [-]anks are
being continually refilled? for death goes hand
in hand with such foul corruption."

	Dr. Caroline B. Winslow, a resident physi-
cian and the able Editor of "The Alpha," pub-
lished in the interests and for the benefit and
elevation of women, made a thrilling speech
upon the same subject as Mrs. Slocum, al-
though Mrs. Winslow confined her remarks
more to the wickedness and corruption of the
District of Columbia.
	Mrs. Williams, of Utah, then gave a short
discourse upon Mormon women, their motives,
principles and efforts; in short made a strong de-
fense for her Mormon sisters.
	Mrs. Spencer styled Washington a Congress-
ional boarding house, and advised women
who desired the suffrage not to feed Congress-
men so well.
	Mrs. Blake emphasized "The protection of
women by men" by relating a story of a young
girl in Michigan driven out of town to die on
Christmas night, and referred to the murder
column of the newspaper. She declared
that man was not the natural protector of wo-
man, as alleged. She drew on the calendar of
crime to bear out her theory. Mrs. Lockwood
sprang up, interrupting Mrs. Blake, and said
such remarks only tended to widen the breach
between men and women, and for her part she
would not stand it; as Mrs. B. had mentioned
several cases where men had murdered their
wives, she could show half a dozen instances
in that vicinity alone where women had not
only murdered their husbands, but their chil-
dren. There was some sharp sparring between
these two, both of them women of wit and
versed in the learning of the world, but Mrs.
Stanton, with her uncommon tact, succeeded
in obtaining order and a truce, after which she
made a few remarks herself, speaking in strong
terms of commendation of men, and the after-
noon session adjourned.
	Second Evening session. The first speaker
was Mrs. Julia Browne Dunham, from Iowa,
who gave a concise annual report of the Wo-
man Suffrage Association of that State; she
thought the Iowa Legislature nearly ready to
vote for woman suffrage, and that public opin-
ion was largely in their favor. Women had
already been given many offices of trust and
honor, especially as trustees of schools.
	Mrs. Stanton rose, greeted with enthusiastic
applause, and introduced Mrs. Lockwood as
Lockwood versus the United States. Mrs. L ,
the celebrated lawyer, then made her earnest
and eloquent speech entitled "Waymarks."
She was frequently applauded, and at the
close received a very beautiful bouquet.
	After this there was a sharp discussion be-
tween Dr. Perivs, and Mrs. F. Douglas on the
fifth resolution, which referred to the Presi-
dent's message and his neglect to mention
twenty thousand women citizens. Dr. Pervis
is a very talented man and a very fluent speak-
er, and of course used strong language. He had
taken exception to the phrase "possible bull-
dozing of some of the hapless race in the
South," and felt insulted by any such connec-
tion with the President; he denounced the
policy of President Hayes. Of course there
were loud cheers and applause and hisses. Mr.
Douglas resented the saddling of everything
upon the President, and there was a great deal
of confusion created; people called out "What's

[Column 3]
this to do with woman's rights," etc. Mrs.
Stanton had to rise quite often to call to order.
During the colloquy between Messrs. Pervis
and Douglas, Mrs. Spencer arose and stated that
the resolutions had been drafted with special
care &c. Mrs. Stanton called for a copy of said
resolutions, and thought the offending clause
was in the wrong place. The construction of
the sentence was afterwards changed. Mr.
Pervis closed his remarks by saying; "I speak
for black people because I know that on this
platform they can be spoken of (applause).
Speak for them, because there is no power un-
der heaven to protect them."
	Mrs. Stanton arose and said from the events
of the evening it was very evident there was
need of women in the councils of men. That
we must never forget the right and sacredness
of free speech, which is a woman's right tenet,
and that women must teach their sons when
they go to public meetings to use their tongues
like men and not like serpents. *	* They
must learn the great American lesson of tolera-
tion of free speech.	*	*	*	*
	Mrs. Stanton was enthusiastically applauded.
She gave notice that next winter there would
be some new speakers, both men and women
Her youngest daughter, now studying in the
Boston school of oratory, would be there on
the platform and take part in the Convention.
It was then announced the Convention was
over.	[handwritten] E. B. Wells

enth Ward gave a grand Jubilee on Wed-
nesday evening, the 12th inst. Mrs. Louie

	RESPECT AND SELF-CONTROL.

	I am often led to reflect on the lack of
respect that is shown in our intercourse
with each other, especially among the most
intimately associated—husbands and wives,
parents and children, brothers and sisters,
and companions bound together by strong
and holy ties, are all more or less guilty;
and it always appeared strange to me that
disrespect should be more common among
those who have every reason to love, than
it is among friends or mere acquaintances.
It cannot spring from lack of affection, be-
cause it is often the case that those who
love most are the most guilty; as the poet
says,

	"Oft for our own the bitter tone,
	Tho we love our own the best."

	It does not spring from want of thought,
because we can nearly always be thoughtful
enough to avoid the error while in the pre-
sence of strangers; so I have to come to the
conclusion that the most fruitful source of
disrespect is the want of self-control. The
adversary often tries us sorely through our
nearest and dearest ones. 'Tis often they
who force us to drink the very dregs of bit-
terness, surely not because they design to
afflict us, but they do not gain the mastery
over the angry thought, or impatient word,
that always makes such deep wounds; for
though unkind words may fall apparently
unheeded, they never fail to make an un-
favorable impression.
	How often would we save ourselves the
humiliation of seeking forgiveness, if we
would seek to be governed more by princi-
ple and less by impulse. The older we
grow the more clearly will we see the beauty
and wisdom of self-control. It will shield
us from so many of the little vexatious
trials that spring entirely from our own
fault no matter how much we may think
others to blame. Some people pride them-
selves upon always "speaking as they feel;"
that is very good if they always feel right,
and if they do they must be exceptionally
good; but in case they should sometimes
feel as "cross as two sticks," gloomy and
impatient, they had better either be silent

[Column 2]
or "speak as they don't feel;" a little de-
ception might be better in such a case.
	We will never regret checking ourselves
when about to say something very sarcastic
and taunting, something which an evil
spirit would convince us was just the right
thing to say; but oh, how many times do
we regret the want of self-control and power
to resist the tempter. We are all capable
of inflicting pain, and often do even when
studying to avoid it, and the heart cannot
be right where there is premeditation. I
think we allow our personal feelings to in-
fluence us too much in our conduct toward
each other. We do not stop to consider a
person's age, their superior qualities and
the many claims they have upon our respect,
or even reverence, but should they offend
us, we consider ourselves perfectly justified
in treating them with any degree of disre-
spect that will satisfy a feeling of revenge,
for call it what you will it is revenge.
	Manifestations of our deep and tender
affection are not proper and wise at all
times, but respect and kindness are never
out of place; they are acceptable to all.
Especially should we be guarded in our de-
portment towards parents in the presence
of their children, for who feels capable of
bearing the responsibility of teaching, by
example, a child to disrespect its parents.
Doubtless all parents have failings that they
are striving hard to overcome, are they to
be discovered by their children? Who
would be cruel and heartless enough to
draw the vail aside, teach a child to disre-
gard that binding command to "Honor thy
father and thy mother?"
	Need I speak upon the duty of respect to
the aged? That should be among our first
lessons, and never lost sight of. There may
be things that we all understand, but it is
good and may be very profitable for some of
us to have our minds refreshed upon sub-
jects that so greatly affect our every day
happiness, and everthing that tends to our
peace and happiness is of the Gospel, and
should claim our study. May we each seek
to be guided by the Spirit of God in every
thought, word and action, and thus will we
be inspired to respect the rights, privileges
and feelings of each other, preserving peace
in our own hearts while we are laboring
here on earth for that never-fading crown
of glory that is laid up for those who falter
not by the wayside, but endure faithful to 
the end.
		LILLIE FREEZE.

MORMON LADIES CALLING AT THE 
WHITE HOUSE.

	On Saturday, when Mrs Hayes had her first
reception, the two Mormon ladies, Mrs. Em-
meline B. Wells and Mrs. Zina Young Wil-
liams, Miss Anthony, Mrs. Spencer, of this 
city and others called, and the President, when
they asked a hearing from him, appointed
Monday at 10 a. m.
	The two Mormon wives named, one of
whom, Mrs. Williams, is the daughter of Brig-
ham Young,and the other wife of Ex-Mayor
Wells, of Salt Lake, went to the White House
promptly at the hppolnted hour, accompanies
by Mrs. Matilda Joslyn Gage and Mrs. Spencer.
These two ladies and Mrs. Wells, of Utah, were
of the committee appointed by the Suffrage
Association. Miss Rachel C. Foster and Miss
Julia T. Foster, of Philadelphia, Miss Wood-

THE WOMEN OF UTAH. MRS. BLAKE'S 
ANALYSIS OF THEIR PLEA FOR PO-
LYGAMY.
	At the recent National Woman Suffrage
Convention in Washington there were present
two delegates from the Territory of Utah. They
were Mrs. Emmeline B. Wells, editor of the
WOMAN'S EXPONENT of Salt Lake City, and
Mrs. Zina Young Williams, a daughter of Brig-
ham Young. They were both of them women
of culture, well dressed and well appearing.
Mrs. Wells was a lady of perhaps fifty, with a
pleasing, intellectual face. Mrs. Williams was
young and very good looking.  They were both
Mormons, apparently from devout, religious
belief, and had come East principally "plural
marriages," as they preferred to call their social
institutions.  In pusuance of this wish they
have within the last few weeks had several
hearing before the Judiciary Committee of
the Senate, on which they have presented their
views with force and clearness.
* * * * It was quite evident
that both these ladies were by conviction firm
believers in the divine origin of their faith,
and that they had in a large degree the tem-
perament that may perhaps be described as
eminently pious, or spiritual, in the best sense
of the word--a temperament which causes its
possessors to cling closely to their faith, and, if
necessary, to suffer martyrdom for it.  In
speaking of their peculiar institution they both
admitted that there were elements in it which
were painful to a woman's heart, but added
that they were doing what was ordained by
their religion, which commanded as a sacred
duty that they should share those attentions
which a selfish human instinct would cause
them to wish to retain undivided.
* * * * Mrs. Wells was careful
to say that they did not consider all men fitted,
either by nature or outward circumstances, for
plural marriage.  It was only men of principle,
of large, kindly hearts, who were proper per-
sons to have more than one wife. Nor did she
feel that this portion of their system was adapt-
ed to all communities, but thought it would be
especially desirable for rural regions where
women are in excess in the population, and
urged that in New England, for instance, the 
army of celibate women would be better off
as wives of good men, even though they shared
the honors, than passing to their graves in the
childless coldness of single life. We can fancy
that the dignified old maids of Massachusetts
would indignantly resent so base a suggestion,
[Column 3]
but we give it in justice to a really conscien-
tious thinker. * * *
	The women of Utah have enjoyed the right
of suffrage for eight years, and it was at first
supposed that political freedom would at once
cause them to tear off the yoke of polygamy;
yet this was surely a hasty conclusion, since
there was no more reason to think that giving
them the ballot would move them to disbelieve
in their religion than to imagine that men, on
reaching manhood and being able to vote
would change their religious convictions. We
do believe that political freedom cannot fail,
with the increased facilities for education, and 
freedom of thought which will accompany it,
to lead women gradually to throw off the
shackles of superstition, while their more spir-
itual uatures will still cause them to cling to 
all that is true and pure in religion; but it is
not to be expected that such a result will follow
at once, or that Mormon women, with their
most sacred affections twined about the insti-
tution of plural marriage would violently re-
pudiate it.  Yet the right of suffrage has al-
ready had for these women the most beneficial
results. It has led to the formation among
them of societies for mutual education and im-
provement. These associations meet through-
out the whole Territory, and women are con-
stantly employed in visiting the different so-
cieties to inform their members upon topics of
interest and importance. Already the influ-
ence of the women as a power is felt in the
body politic, while it is quite evident that free
thought on all topics is doing its work among
them. Of course it could not be expected that
they would make the first use of their liberties
by tearing down their homes, and the fact that
these Mormon women cling to these homes,
poor as they are, ought to silence forever those
objectors who maintain that enfranchising wo-
men would destroy the home.

PIPSEY PAPERS.

	What extraordinary pains the Gentile la-
dies are taking to suppress that blotch on
the body politic, which we hold so sacred,
viz., Plural Marriage!  If there is anything
morally or religiously wrong in the practice
of it, it is to be hoped they will succeed;
that is, in view of their introducing a more
moral and elavating mode of carrying out
God's great command to multiply and re-
plenish the earth.  He endowed us with
principles and desires pertaining thereto,
and gave laws whereby it should be carried
out in purity.
	Marriage was instituted as a principle
whereby virtuous love might be fostered
and protected, all intercourse outside of that
being condemned as unlawful and unholy.
The marriage ceremony enjoins upon men
and women to be true to themselves and to
each other.  It is usual for either men or
women, after the death of their relative
spouse, to marry again, but according to
the doctrines of modern Christianity they
must not marry a second wife while the
first is living.  Death releases their obliga-
tions.  But the marriage tie as revealed in
these last days binds for time and for all
eternity; therefore, if a wife dies, her hus-
band must spend the remainder of his life
alone or must of necessity have a plurality
of wives.  It is not the law of God, neither
does it accord with woman's physical con-
stitution, that she should marry several
husbands at one time, but if the parties are
agreed and are true to each other, what is
there wrong or degrading in a man having
several wives?  If he supports them, ac-
knowledging their claims and raising their
children in respectability, what is there do-
grading in the principle?  In the olden
time men after God's own heart practiced
polygamy, and we read of jealousy and dis-
satisfaction sometimes arising, as is fre-
quently the case at the present time.  There
is also jealousy and dissatisfaction in mo-
nogamous life, the marriage relation being
frequently severed.  But that in no wise
condemns marriage, nor degrades the law
of home-ties and influence, of which mar-
riage, is the foundation, although the argu-
ment is often used by men who wish to live
single, for not entering into the marriage
relationship.
	Polygamy in a moral or religious view
we have no intention at present to discuss.
If people who practice it are all pure in
heart, or correct in their judgmont, are
matters we are not open to judge, and over
which we have no control; but when we
look around us and see the corruption in
the world, and our comparative free-
dom from licentious practices we are led to
infer that our systems and principles are by
no means to be condemned.  There is no
degree or condition in life but has its pecu-
liar trials connected therewith.  It is not
polygamy as a principle that our enemies
are exercised over, that is a mere pretence
-a handle with which to turn the rack
whereon they would stretch us morally, re-
gardless of the physical suffering it might
cause us to endure.
	It was long ago the cry that Mormonism
must be put down, no matter what the cost
of suffering, murder and rapine, it must be
put down.  But how?  That is a problem
they have vainly tried to solve for over forty
years, and the solution still seems a mys-
tery.  Do they not know that if not sup-
ported and protected by Almighty God, our
peculiar institutions must of themselves
die out in time, and that opposition but
fans the flame which they are trying so hard
to extinguish.  That by their slanderous
and unwarrantable attacks they bring us to
the notice of the thinking public, and peo-
ple will investigate our cause who have
never before given us much thought.
	Who are those that are coming to the
front with their abusive epithets and their
heroic efforts to overthrow our established
religion?  Pity their heroism was not ex-
ercised in a better cause.  The leaders en-
raged in this undertaking are many of them
of those who once professed to believe in
the same doctrines, who have borne their
testimony to the work; whose fathers,
mothers, or sisters have laid down their
lives, perhaps, while promulgating the doc-
trines they professed.  They have joined
hands with us in the bonds of sisterhood,
have sat at our firesides, and partaken of
our cheer; they have been protected and
sustained by the ties the bind a growing
community.  And now they would raise
their hands against us and strike a dagger
to our hearts.  Sisters, I know them, I am
personally acquainted with several of them.
My childhood was passed in their society,
and their names are connected with some
of my earliest recollections.  We danced
at the same parties, shared the same rustic
amusements, and the same religious ser-
vices.  We worshipped at the same altar
and prayed to the same God.  We saw
nothing then to shock our sense of proprie-
ty or interfere with our conscientious prin-
ciples.  Our paths have widened and sepa-
rated; what their experience has been I am
unable to say, but I will venture to suggest
that of all the strong language they use,
and the terms they apply to us, they know
comparatively nothing.  We have no home-
less waifs cast on the tender mercies of pub-
lic charity.  Our religion forbids infanti-
cide, and its institutions protect women
against it and its concomitant evils.  We
have no starving poor, no licensed dens of
gambling and infamy.  We are  sober, in-
dustrious people, trying to pursue the avo-
cations of daily life in peace and content-
ment; we ask only to be let alone, and that
we do most emphatically insist on.  We
want none of the modern vices which ouor
Gentile citizens are introducing into our
midst, but they are forced into our midst,
to the detriment of the manners and morals
of our young people.  I saw a pitiful sight
a few days ago while visiting in Salt Lake
City.  I saw two women locked in jail-
they had both been ruined and abandoned.
Was it polygamy that had done this?  I
assure you it was not.  Salt Lake City is
the only city in Utah that is to any extent
populated by the element that are so anx-
ious abont us-the pure (?) in heart who are
so shocked by our sense of morality that
they would fain bring a ranting mob into
our midst, and turn us from our homes and
firesides. And what for?  For advocating
those principles which protect virtue and
innocence. I would recommend that they
sweep the streets of Salt Lake City and
clear from among us the vices that attach
to their own apron strings, before they at-
tack a principle that in and of itself at least
protects its victims, if such they may be
called.  Let them watch their own hus-
bands, and find if their conduct will bear
inspection, before they take a course to rid
our "poor, deluded women" of their natural
protectors and open up an avenue in society
for more corrupting influences.

THE HARP IN HEAVEN.

	One of the sweetest recollections of my
girlhood is a beautiful reply my mother
once made me, when my heart was swell-
ing with childish grief.
	I had just returned from the house of a
wealthy neighbor, who had kindly given
me the use of their piano for a few hours
every day to gratify my extreme love for
music. Our own cottage home looked so
plain in contrast with t[-]e one I had just
left, and no piano within its walls, I laid
my head upon the table and gave vent to my
overflowing heart. I felt grieved, and per-
haps a little angry, that we were unable to
afford the one thing I desired above all
others—a piano—and expressed my feel-
ings to my mother.
	Never shall I forget her sweet, gentle
tone, as she simply replied, "Never mind,
daughter, if you cannot have a piano on
earth, you may have a harp in heaven."
Instantly the whole current of my feelings
was changed. Earthly things dwindled
into insignificance, and the "harp in heav-
en," with its golden strings, became the
object of my desire. I felt reproved for my
repinings against the Providence that had
placed me in a humble home, and from
that moment the enjoyment of heaven
seemed far to outweigh all the plesures of
earth. That beautiful reply has followed
me all my life, or rather has gone before
me like a bright guiding star—lifting my
thoughts above this transient life, and
opening to my spirit's vision the glorious
scenes in that "land of life and light." I
have a "piano on earth" now, but its charm
is gone. Its music no longer gladdens my
heart as it once did, for the ears that loved
best to listen to its sweet tones, are now
enraptured with the grand harmonies of


heaven. The dear fingers that so often
touched its keys now sweep the golden
harp-strings. O, that "harp in heaven!"
How my soul longs for one breath of its
rich melody!
	As I look upon the dear baby fingers in
the cradle near me' I think it matters little
whether my child be poor or rich—whether
her path be strewn with thorns or flowers—
if she may only have a "harp in heaven."
—Mother's Journal.


GENGHIS KHAN AND THE OWL.

	Nearly seven hundred years ago, a
young Mongolian prince named Genghis (or
Zinghis) while engaged in warfare with a
Tartar tribe, was defeated in battle, and so
hotly chased by his enemies that he was
like to be captured. His shattered defend-
ers lost sight of him, and he fled alone in
momentary peril of death. Exhausted at
last, and with scarcely a hope of saving
himself, he plunged into a thicket, and lay
listening with beating heart while the Tar-
tars came on, raging for his blood. They
knew he had disappeared somewhere in
the immediate vicinity, and to his horror
they stopped directly before his hiding-
place. Instead of searching the bushes,
however, they held a short consultation
and passed on; and from the words he over-
heard, Genghis learned the cause of his de-
liverance. As the pursuers approached, a
white owl was seen sitting calmly on a tree
directly over the thicket. It flew away,
frightened at their appearance and noise,
and the barbarian warriors said, "If the
man had been here, that bird would have
been disturbed before. Let us go on."
	And so Genghis was saved, to become
one of the conquerors of the world, and Em-
peror of all Tartary and Northern China and
Eastern Persia. In grateful remembrance
of his preservation, he and his followers
adopted the snowy owl as their guardian
ensign, and ever after the picture of the
bird was displayed on the Mongol and Kal-
muck battle-flags.				T. B.
