For the EXPONENT.
BY THE BROOK.

	I've been sitting by the brook to-day,
And listening to its flow;
And lightly on its laughing way,
	It gurgled sweet and low.
I sat down on its mossy bank,
	And leaned against a tree,
And asked, and answered, as I sat.
	Swift questions, it and me.

	"Why art thou still and sad," it said,
	"With brow knit tight with pain?
Why canst thou not the world enjoy,
	When joy chants high her strain?"

	"O brook," I answered, "thou canst tell
	Of joys and pleasures moving:
But how canst thou know aught, in truth,
	Of pain, born oft of loving.
Thou hast not had my baby's arms.
	Around thee close enfolded;
Thou hast not known her gentle power,
	Or nature, angel-moulded.

"Her dimpled fingers never clasped
Your own in baby pressure;

And how can you, a simple brook,
	Know why I love my treasure?"

	"But how can all that you have told,
	Bring sorrow to your heart?
Has death's dark hand, with cruel grasp,
	Compelled you two to part?"

	"Ah, no, sweet brook; and were it so,
	My brow were all unknit;
And here to-day with quiet eyes
	And calmest heart I'd sit.
For, knowing that my floweret rare
	Were in His careful keeping,
With patience I would pray and wait,
	And rest my heart with weeping.

	"By grasp more fierce than death's, sweet brook,
	She's been removed from me;
And all her loving ways and looks,
	No more, no more I see.
Man's cruel word betrayed my heart,
	And left it cold and bleeding.--
What cares that man, in all his pride,
	For the wife's tears and pleading! 

	"I prayed them with a mother's tears,
	And offered all I owned,
To only keep my little ones,
	That Heaven had to me loaned.
Soften, O God, the hardened hearts
	That flung aside my trust,
And heeded neither tears nor prayers!
	But said it was but just. 

	"But just! O God, didst Thou
	E'er ordain such thing?
That we must suffer all the pain,
	And only know the sting?
Must woman ever toil and weep,
	And travail for the world,
And man sit over her, and judge	
	With scornful lip upcurled?

	"And so, dear brook, my heart is sad,
	And crushed with rarest pain;
And I have come in solitude
	To pour forth my refrain."

	"Full well I see why thou art sad.
	With darkly-clouded eyes;
And surely more than human thou;
	If it were otherwise.
But let me tell you what I know,
	A brook though I may be;
The triumph of the right will come,
	For God doth all things see.

	"Too long has woman been the one
	To suffer and to weep,
The time has come when men will find
	That justice will not sleep.
And woman will uprise and show
	How cruel is her wrong;
And conscious of her pow'r for good,
	She'll move amid the throng.


WOMAN'S RIGHTS.

		THE THEME OF LATTER-DAYS

Dear Editor :--Although a native of dear
New England, I also claim to be a "Woman
of Utah," by virtue of a quarter of a cen-
tury's residence in these peaceful vales,
which are far dearer to me than even the
land of my birth. How often has my heart
gone forth in praise and gratitude to my
Heavenly Father, that I have been privi-
leged to rear my family in this goodly land,
so far from the strife, turmoil and persecu-
tion that surrounded me in the days of my
childhood; for my footsteps have ever
walked in the pathway of the Saints, and
in the early days it was ofttimes thorny
and tempestuous. O how I revere these
grand old mountains, that hold us so se-
curely in their peaceful embrace. But I
am forgetting my subject--Woman's 
Rights. As privilege has been given,
through the columns of your highly valued
paper, to the women of Utah, to express
their thoughts and sentiments, I for the
first time attempt to do so.
	I deem the most sacred right of woman
the custody of her children. To me this is
Godliness. If we look at the lower order
of creation we will see that the young re-
main with the mother, until, by maturity,
they become independent creatures, capa-
ble in turn of acting for themselves. How
much more should we, who claim to be in-
telligent beings, with the stamp of Deity
upon us, adhere to this sublime law of na-
ture. I rejoice to think by the laws of our
holy religion this tie is acknowledged as
binding and imperative; but as the laws of
the land deviate from this, it is against
those that I wish to protest.
	A just, righteous, God-fearing man is my
adoration; but that man alone has the right
to make laws to govern woman in matters
of such vital importance as this, I do not
believe; at least, not without a fair repre-
sentation on our part to sanction them. I
will admit that cases have come under my
observation, where women have seemed
unfit to take charge of their offspring, but
these cases were rare and isolated ones,
where all purity was crushed from their
souls by the cruelty and injustice of man.
In exceptional cases of this kind let there
be a remedy; but where a mother is at all
capable let her have her children. Let not
the holy and sacred bonds of motherhood
be torn asunder and laid on the altar of in-
justice. I do not sanction the separation of
husband and wife for trifling occasions; but
I think it the duty of each to bear and for-
bear until this ceases to be a virtue; but
when the crisis does come, and there are
children in the case, were I a legislator I
would frame a law for them in this wise:
The woman should be judged by her sisters.
There should be a petition, to be signed by
the women of the particular town, ward, or
neighborhood where the woman resides, and
if they, by their judgment, consider the
woman at all capable of the care of the
young, the children should be given to her.
When men are divorced from their wives,
they generally exhibit a certain degree of
spitefulness toward their wives, and in my
humble opinion, it is more to gratify this
feeling than from an overflow of philo-pro-
genitiveness, that they strive to retain the
children. That they do this is proof posi-
tive to me that they were uncongenial in
the domestic relations, and that the wife
was perfectly justified in leaving them to
their own wretchedness.
	Now, dear reader, I have no axe to grind
in this matter, for my half dozen children
cluster round the sacred altar of home, with
their own father and mother presiding; but
my heart has been pained by the sorrows
of others, and I have many times felt to cry
aloud in their behalf. Lest I weary your
patience I will close. If this is consigned
to the waste-basket, all right.
		MA. GOODWIN.

OUR WASHINGTON OFFICE.--In accord-
ance with our plan indicated in July num-
ber, we have opened an office for "Woman's
Words" in Washington, D. C., where we
shall catch the words of wisdom or folly
from men and women of all the nations
under the sun.
Our new location is in the marble build-
ing, northwest corner of 11th and Pennsyl-
vania avenue, recently leased by the Wash-
ington "Sunday Republic."  It is directly
opposite the ever beaming genial "Evening
Star," a little way from the "Daily Repub-
lican," on the road to the "Daily Post," in
sight of the Capitol building, and upon the
great national thoroughfare to the White
House.
 If more were needed to make the new
office desirable, it is directly under the
friendly wing of the bright and popular
weekly, the "Sunday Republic, edited by
the talented J. Brisben Walker, aided by a 
host of contributors well known to fame.

		WANSHIP, Sept. 12th, 1878.

EDITOR EXPONENT:
	The Branch Relief Society of Wanship
is in a prosperous condition, with twenty-
eight members.  The reorganization took
place Dec. 18, 1876.  Bishop Atwood and
Bishop Roundy being present, also several
others of the brethren, Mrs. Louisa J.
Roundy was appointed President, who has
been very active and persevering in her
duty up to this time, Mrs. Mary Reynolds
having previously resigned her office, being
called to remove from Wanship for a sea-
son.
	Our meetings are well attended, peace
and love dwell in our midst.  We have the
"Life of Brigham Young" and the "Wo-
men of Mormondom" to circulate and read.
There are several who take the EXPONENT,
and in doing our duty we feel well and en-
joy the spirit of the Gospel.  Some of our
sisters are good speakers, and all are gener-
ally on the improve in faith and good works.
We hope to be able to withstand the
scourges which await Babylon, wherever
she is found.
 Our Financial report for the past year is
as follows:

	We feel it important to be up and doing,
as we look at the significant signs of the
times, and we know that we must be hum-
ble and faithful to be able to stand.

	BLOOMINGTON, IDAHO, Aug. 7, 1878.

EDITOR EXPONENT:
	I think we are improving a little; as a
Society we number seventy members, about
forty average attendants.  Our meetings
are on the first Thursday of every month.
	The 24th of July was celebrated here in
a very pleasant manner; we had a dinner
provided at the schoolhouse, superintended
by the president of the Relief Society, Mrs.
Sarah Greenhatch, and the teachers, when
I believe every family in Bloomington were
invited to partake of the good things pro-
vided.  We had a meeting in the forenoon,
and were addressed by Presidents J. H.
Hart and George Osman, and Bishop
Hulme and others.  The Sunday School
children enjoyed themselves in the dance,
and we had a very good time.
	We have done very little about storing
grain yet; we have about thirteen bushels
of wheat and four hundred pounds of flour,
and intend, if we have health and strength,
to have more in a little while.  Our Presi-
dent has appointed a committee to purchase
wheat and store it up until a time of need.
A number of the young ladies of the Im-
provement Society, as well as some of the
older ones, go to the field and glean wheat
three days in each week, and they feel
blessed and happy in their work.  Bishop
Hulme called on the brethren to help the
gleaners by taking them in their wagons to
and from the field, and bringing their grain
home for them.
	Our financial condition for the past year
is as follows:

Disbursed to the Logan Temple............... $36 00
To the poor..................................................   28 60
Shares in Co-op store.................................   70 00

	The Lord has blessed the people in Bear
Lake Valley this year with a beautiful har-
vest.
	Praying that the blessing of God may at-
tend you, and that your beautiful little pa-
per may be more widely circulated, that
many others may rejoice in its good teach-
ings, we remain,
		SARAH GREENHATCH, President,
		ELIZABETH LANG, Secretary.

				SANTAQUIN, Sept. 22d, 1878.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	The Relief Society of this place held
their Tenth Annual meeting Sept. 5, 1878.
Our reports were all duly read by our Sec-
retary showing that we had received dur-
ing the year in donations, labor, &c., $210 82;
disbursed during the year to the Temple,
missionaries, the poor, and for charitable
purposes, $102 90.  A report of the past ten
years was also read, commencing Sept. 1st,
1868, ending Aug. 31st, 1878; showing that
we had received during the ten years, in
donations, dividends and labor, $1896 83;
disbursed during the ten years to the Tem-
ple, missionaries, the poor and for charit-
able purposes, $1092 29.
	Our meeting was well attended; we had
sisters present from Salt Lake City, Provo,
Goshen, Benjamin City, and Spring Lake.
The meeting was addressed by Mrs. M. T.
Smoot, Lucy B. Young, Pres. Stickney, and
our Bishop, George Halladay, all of whom
expressed their entire satisfaction at hear-
ing our accounts read, thinking for this
small place we were doing well, and alive
to our duties.  Our Schoolhouse was neatly
decorated for the occasion with articles of
home industry, consisting of quilts, cover-
lids, shawls, tidies, homespun cloth, and a
towel made of sweet clover, by Sister Mar-
garet Arleen, and also nearly three pecks
of cocoons were displayed for the inspec-
tion of our brethren and sisters, which ex-
cited a great deal of curiosity.
	We are striving in our weak way to do
all the good we can to help build up the
kingdom of God.  Wishing you great suc-
cess, we remain yours in the new and ever-
lasting covenant.
				E. J. STICKNEY, Pres't.
				E. CARTER, Sec'y.

THE FAIR.

	The Territorial Fair held in this City,
under the auspices of the D. A. and M.
Society, commenced Wednesday, Oct. 2, in
the City Market, and was open four days,
and each day was crowded with visitors. It
was undoubtedly the finest display of all
the varieties of exhibits that there has
ever been in this Territory. Those who
had the management of the several depart-
ments and the awarding of prizes deserve
great commendation for their tact, courtesy
and appreciation. The leather and shoe
department cannot be too highly praised,
and the excellence of the home manufac-
tures throughout deserve special mention.
The drawings, paintings, ornamental and
fancy work were worthy a place, and con-
vey the idea in a tangible form that there
is a love of the beautiful among the young
people, which deserves encouragement and
patronage. It is impossible to mention all,
or specify works of merit, the awarding
committee have already determined that
fact, but we will say the live stock on ex-
hibition was superior in beauty and quality.
Altogether it was a fine display, and it will
create a growing interest in the people of
Utah to excel and produce the very best
quality of fruits, flowers, manufactures of
all kinds, and live stock.


WOMEN VS. GIRLS.—Women ought to be
of more importance to society than girls,
but who does not know that the case is ex-
actly the reverse? How many women do 
we all know who shrink from society, give
their whole time to family duties, bury
themselves up at home, and seem to be of
no other use in the world than to dress their
girls for parties, and keep the house in or-
der for their beaux. Of course the chil-
dren grow up with the idea that mother is
of no consequence and don't know the 
world. They go abroad for their opinions,
and spurn all home influence.

THE MEMORY OF THE HEART.
If stores of dry and learned lore we gain,
We keep them in the memory of the brain,
Names, things, and facts — whate'er we knowl[-]dge call
There is a common ledger for them all;
And images on this cold surface traced
Make slight impression, and are soon effaced.

But we've a page more glowing and more bright,
On which our friendship and our love to write.
That these may never from the soul depart,
We trust them to the memory of the heart.
There is no dimming, no effacement there,
Each new pulsation keeps the record clear;
Warm golden letters all the tablet fill,
Nor lose their lustre till the heart stands still.
							DANIEL WEBSTER.

			AUTUMN LEAVES.

	Old friends and true, come you with me to-night!
	And in the shadow of the witching hours,
	We'll wander in the forests as of old,
	And there in clustering beauty, bright,
	E'en as the radiance of summer flow'rs,
	Gather those leaves of crimson and of gold,
	Such as we gathered in the "long ago,'
	Ere we had tasted of life's cup of woe.

	Those Autumn leaves are wound around my heart,
	With tendrils stronger than the touch of time,
	And bloom and brighten ev'ry season round;
	The mem'ries they enshrine cannot depart,
	But with each new affection intertwine.
	And each year is some precious token found,
	That brings the old-time forests to my gaze.
	And haunts me with "the scenes of other days."

	The [-]hestnut burrs we trod beneath our feet,
	Reminded us that life had thorny ways,
	But we were blooming then; like those bright leaves,
	And in our innocence, and joy complete,
	Trac'd not the shadow of the darker days,
	But only saw the harvest's golden sheaves,
	That lay before us in the coming years,
	And wot not we must sew the seed in tears.

	But tender hopes and sweetest fancies [-]ling
	Around the Autumn leaves along life's way,
	They gladden Winter, brighten home and heart;
	And when my children their rich clusters bring,
	And weave them garlands of the leaflets gay,
	The tears involunta[-]ily will start;
	I wonder if, like me, in golden sheaves,
	They'll bind with chords of love these Autumn leaves.

Salt Lake City, Oct. 11, 1878.					EMILE.

		TIRED MOTHERS.

	If there is any class of persons who need
tenderness and encouragement it is the
tired mothers; weary limbs, sad hearts, and
puzzled brains all attest to the fact.  There
ought to be special privileges granted to
tired mothers, as much as to invalids.
Those who have passed through this trying
ordeal know how to sympathize, know how
to excuse many of the shortcomings of
those who are passing through the narrow
gateways, the thorny paths, the rough high-
ways, where temptation holds high revelry,
and the angel of peace sits in shadow.
	Tired mothers! always anxious, schem-
ing, planning and economizing how they
can manage every detail of domestic life
with least expense, for children are such a
drain upon all the resources of one’s time,
heart and pocket.  Mothers doing double
work, triple work themselves, to save for
this, or that, until the nerves are strained
and shattered to a degree unbearable to
themselves, and particularly offensive to
others. Let me entreat you, fathers and
husbands, deal gently with the wife and
mother; cheer and brighten her life by all
the means in your power, for she needs
your help in many ways, to buoy her up
and sustain her, that she may be nerved
with fresh vigor to impart to the little ones
who are a constant drain upon her life and
[column 2]
energies. Give her smiles and kind words
and minister in kindly needs, and such at-
tentions as you have lavished on her in her
girlhood, ere she knew aught of the cares
and perplexities that wait on the footsteps
of after years, and cloud, offtimes, the
sunny home, sometimes bursting forth in
trememdous claps of domestic thunder, such
as deafen and deaden, perchance, the finest
and acutest capabilities of the human soul.
After these storms there sometimes comes,
'tis true, a dead calm, but it is not of that
nature of which contentment is born, but
rather that which paralyzes the best ener-
gies and truest, affections. Oftener still,
perhaps, there comes a series of these
storms, until it is impossible to quell them,
and the whole domestic drama is changed
from its pristine glory and strife; turmoil
and dissension take the places where peace
and happiness and love should sit enthroned.
A great deal is said of woman's power to
make her home pleasant, to guide her off-
spring, to cheer her husband; to strengthen
his hands, in short to be the constant star
in the horizon of home, always brightening
and gladdening the faces and hearts of 
those who assemble round the family hearth-
stone.  All this is true of woman's influ-
ence, but where does woman get all this
wonderful stock of power to diffuse, and 
why does she ever lose it?  These are ques-
tions to be answered.  We are told woman's
nature is especially adapted to the home
circle; granted--but has she an ever effer-
vescing overflow of amiability, and can she
pour forth in smiles, and songs, and works
and deeds of love from the fountain of her
inmost soul, without feeling the terrible
reaction that always follows a continued
drain upon any one source, when not re-
plenished in proportion.  I think not.
Woman wants man's help, his faith his
sympathy, his tenderness, his affection and
she appreciates any demonstration of his
constancy and devotion to the utmost.  In
the home where the mother is most con-
stantly occupied, to the exclusion of almost
every selfish gratification, there are contin-
ual trials of patience pertaining to her
daily cares and her little ones, and only
God for her to go to and unburthen her
weary soul; here is where the spiritual-
minded mother becomes strong; but occas-
sionally despondent feelings gain the mas-
tery, even of the most religiously inclined;
and if the husband is forgetful of the wife,
where shall she look for strength for cour-
age?  Many a mother, for the want of
counsel at the right time, has committed
indiscretions in the management of her
children, that has caused sorrow for all con-
cerned; whereas, if father and mother
were united as husband and wife should be,
much of this might be avoided.  But this
is too wide a field to venture upon at the
close of an article, and I forbear, but wish
to add that tired mothers ought to be ten-
derly cared for and carefully treated by
those whose name they bear, whose off-
spring they are nursing, tending and rear-
ing.  There are many little ways in which
the mother may be stimulated to renewed
efforts, but her time and brain are so en-
grossed by her positive condition and cir-
cumstances, that she often fails to gather
hope and grace equal to her needs.  Then
can the husband make himself specially
dear, if he but tender his advice and sym-
pathies, and let her see he realizes how no-
bly she is seeking to fulfill her part in the 
great programme of life.  I know it is often
said the husband is driven from his home,
and frequently resorts to the grogshop and 
the club for relief from trouble; but sup-
posing that the husband, in all the strength
[column 3]
and nobility of his glorious manhood,
should aid, by his own active exertions, in 
making home a heaven, where the tired
mother might find that stay and anchor
that is needed when the nervous energies
are exhausted by the wear and tear that is 
incident to the life of the mother.  There
is also a monotony in woman's life that is
as deleterious to health and activity as the
exertions she is required to make in fulfill-
ing home duties and obligations.  Women
often do not see that they are really not
only doing themselves a great injustice by
confining themselves too closely to the 
kitchen, the nursery, or the sewing ma-
chine, but they become disagreeable in con-
sequence of this very devotion.  They
want bracing up and invigorating, and as
those who look on, can see better and dis-
criminate clearer than the actor who is en-
gaged in the work, so the husband, seeing
the wife too much absorbed with domestic
affairs, might perhaps save her and himself
much unhappiness, by inducing her to go
out now and then and interest herself in
something that will conduce to health and
strength of body and mind.  This sort of
treatment, in many instances, would not
only save paying doctors' bills, but give a 
higher relish and tone to the enjoyments of 
home.  I would not wish to be understood
as dictating to men, oh no; but simply tell-
ing them some of the needs and longings
of tired mothers, without entering into de-
tails of causes and effects.
	An able senator of the United States, in
a public speech recently, said in the evils
which afflict our country today he "traced
the want of influence of the fireside vir-
tues," and appeals to the mothers of Ameri-
ca in glowing language, telling them what
great power lies in their hands.  All this
is very true; but although the home is the 
nursery of principles, and the mother the
first teacher, yet there is need of real co-
operation between fathers and mothers, and
mutual help from each, to lay the founda-
tion of such virtues and characteristics as
shall eventually ripen into glorious man-
hood  womanhood.  If the mother must
be made answerable, to a limited extent
even, for all the wrongs and transgressions
of which the world is full, then it behooves
woman herself to be strong, healthful, ac-
tive, with the largest available stock of gen-
eral intelligence on all possible subjects.  It
may be that this will qualify her to choose
for her companion and the father of her
children, one endowed with all the best re-
quisites for man's high and holy destiny,
and one who will possess not only the supe-
rior courage of true manhood, but also com-
bine with it the geniality and adaptability
that will give him prestige at his own fire-
side, that will be perceptibly felt by all, and 
especially the tired mother.

OUR RELIEF SOCIETIES.

SIMILAR ORGANIZATIONS WANTED BY
		CHRISTIAN MINISTERS﻿.

	Reading recently in the "Watchman," a
popular Boston paper, an article on "Christian
Women," we could not help constrasting the
perfect working order of the Relief Societies
of this Territory with the condition of those
highly educated, intellectual, noble women of
the Eastern States. The very words of that
Christian Minister should prove to him that
the system of charity established here is just
what he desires. Hear what he says: "In the
midst of the talk on Woman's Rights and Wo-
man's Wrongs, the church would do well to
take advantage of the hour, and map out a 
broader field of usefulness for the women in
our churches. A few have taken hold of mis-
sions, some have enlisted in the temperance
cause, but the great mass yet hug the old indif-
ference, and stand aloof from all public chari-
ties." * * The writer refers to Catholic Sis-
ters of Charity, and says they have done im-
mense good; but in continuation: "We want
no such sisterhoods as the Catholic Church has
founded, superintended and ruled over by a 
bachelor priesthood, but a sisterhood organized
and conducted by able and experienced Chris-
tian women, might find a place of work as
wide as the circumference of Christian char-
ity."
	In Utah such an organization really exists,
founded by Joseph Smith the Prophet, and
these organizations have made Mormon women
spiritually strong, and given them more exalted
views of true charity. There has been more
real good effected through these organizations
than one would think, were the facts not liter-
ally apparent. This minister says: "We can
imagine that a Protestant sisterhood, with
such a person as Selina, Countess of Hunting-
don, Madame Guyon, Mrs. Frey, Florence
Nightingale, or some of the Christian women
whose names we could speak; who live around
us here, at its head, might be a great and won-
derful blessing to Protestant society."
	Here there is just what this minister calls
for, and noble Mormon, Christian women at
its head—women who have borne persecution,
and been purified by suffering, until "their very
failings lean to virtue's side;" women of the
best blood of Puritanical New England, and
Old England; women who would take their 
lives in their hands, as it were, to sustain cor-
rect principles; women, in fact, who have done
it. Such women are Eliza R. Snow, Zina D.
Young, and M. Isabella Horne; these are only
three of the great army of noble women who
work continuously in the interest of Relief So-
cieties—one of them a literal descendent of the
very Huntingdon family to which this writer
alludes, a Huntingdon of New England


The writer referred to says; "There is a want
that our economy does not supply." The econo-
my of the Church of Jesus Christ has left no
such vacancy, for it is perfect in its organiza-
tion for men and women.
	Mrs. Sarah Andrews Spencer, in the edito-
rial department of "Woman's Words" for Oc
tober, says: "At our next Convention in Wash-
ington, let us by all means have one or more of
the enterprising, public-spirited women of
Utah present. If we mistake not our Gentile
sisters have much to learn from these heroic
women. What we read of their business,
ability, courage and patriotism is an inspira-
tion to us."	E. B. Wells


SEMI ANNUAL CONFERENCE.

	The Forty-Eighth Semi-Annual Conference
of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day
Saints, convened in the large Tabernacle in
this city, Sunday, October 6th, at 10 a.m.  The
gathering was the largest which has ever as-
sembled in the building.  On the stand were
President John Taylor, Elders Orson Pratt,
Orson Hyde, Lorenzo Snow, George Q. Cannon,
F. D. Richards, C. C. Rich, Joseph F. Smith,
Brigham Young, Albert Carrington, D. H.
Wells, and other officers of the High Priest-
hood, among whom were many of the Presi-
dents of Stakes.  There was a good attendance
of the Tabernacle Choir, conducted by Prof.
Careless, and Prof. J. Daynes, at the organ.
President Taylor called the attention of the
Conference, and it was opened by the choir
singing "I saw a mighty angel fly." Opening
prayer by Elder George Q. Cannon.  Choir
sang, "Author of faith." Elders Orson Hyde
and C. C. Rich addressed the morning meeting,
Elder George Q. Cannon occupied the after-
noon.
	Monday, Oct. 7, second day of Conference,
Elder Orson Pratt, Elder Joseph F. Smith
and President John Taylor preached in the
morning. In the afternoon Elder George Q.
Cannon read the report of President John Tay-
lor, Trustee-in-ltrust, which report was endorsed
by his assistants, the Twelve Apostles, their
Counselors, the Presiding Bishop and the Au-
diting Committee.  Elder Lorenzo Snow then
spoke briefly, and the names of the mission-
aries who had been selected for various coun-
tries, were presented to the Conference to be
sustained by vote.  Elder Albert Carrington
made some closing remarks.
	Tuesday, October 8, Third day. - Elder Geo.
Q. Cannon presented the Authorities of the
Church in their proper order, all of whom were
unanimously sustained by the Conference.
Then followed a report from Morris & Evans,
in relation to funds collected by them for the
benefit of the Welsh Saints; also a report in
relation to the Manti Temple.  These reports
were accepted by the vote of the Conference;
after which short speeches were made by El-
ders Brigham Young, Franklin D. Richards,
Wilford Woodruff and Joseph Young.
	In the afternoon Elder George Q. Cannon
read the report of President John Taylor, re
lating to the condition of the various Stakes,
Missions, Temples,Sunday and District Schools,
Brigham Young Academy and Deseret Univer-
sity, Relief Societies, Young Men's and Young
Women's Mutual Improvement Associations,
and referred to Zion's Co-operative Mercantile
Institution as being a stepping stone to the Un-
ited Order.  This excellent document was fol-
lowed by the reading of the reports of sub-com-
mittees in the various departments of mercan-
tile and manufacturing interests.
	President John Taylor then reviewed the vari-
ous subjects which had been brought up by the
different speakers, giving much instruction on
revelation, as well as explaining the duties of
the Presidency and the Bishopric, and
stated that all the various quorums and au-
thorities of the Church were under the direc-
tion and dictation of the Presidency and
Twelve Apostles.  He then made some state-
ments in relation to the future intentions of Z.
C. M. I., and the necessity of establishing a
board of trade, that all the interests of the en-
tire community might be fairly and honorably
represented.  At the close of his remarks he
blessed the entire congregation.  Elder George
Q. Cannon then presented the names of other
missionaries to be voted for by the Conference,
and the Conference was then adjourned until
the 6th of April, 1879, at 10 o'clock a.m. The
choir sang "Te Deum."  Benediction by Elder
D. H. Wells.
	This Conference has been one of unusual in-
terest, and was most numerously attended, the
Saints having come from all parts of the Terri-
tory.  The weather has been exceedingly fine
and everything indicated the presence of pros-
perity, peace and harmony.

	GRAND Sunday School Union; The Sunday
School Union held a mass meeting in the Theatre
in this city, Sunday evening at 7 o'clock p.m.
The Tabernacle Choir and Union Glee Club
were present by invitation. On the platform
was Pres. John Taylor, and several of the
Quorum of the Apostles, Elder George Q. Can-

Superintendents. Choice selections were sung
by the choir and Union Glee Club, which
added greatly to the enjoyment of the evening.

"Utah's Best Crop." The sight presented by
the large assembly was charming, and spoke
well for Utah's Sunday School organization.
The meeting was dismissed with prayer by
Elder Joseph F. Smith.

		TERRITORIAL CONVENTION.

	The delegates to the Territorial Convention
assembled at the City Hall per appointment,
at 6 p. m. Monday, Oct. 7. D. O. Calder, Esq,
Vice President of the People's Territorial Cen-
tral Committee, called the Convention to order
and a temporary organization was effected


with Hon. Orson Hyde Chairman and John
T. Caine, Esq. Secretary.  The first business
of the Convention was the appointing of a 
committee of seven on credentials, who re-
tired to examine credentials; meantime short
speeches were made by Hon. W. H. Hooper,
Judge Snow and E. D. Carpenter, Esq.
   The committee returned and presented the
report to the Chairman, which was read by the 
Secretary; and after amendment by the admis-
sion of some alternates and proxies in place of
absentees, the report was accepted.
   The total delegation consisted of 77 delagates,
among whom were the following  ladies: Han-
nah T-King, Zina D. Young, Sarah M. Kim-
ball, Sarepta M. Heywood, Emmeline B. Wells,
Priscinda Kimball, and Mary E. Carter.
   On motion of Pres. A. M. Cannon, the Con-
vention agreed to proceed to a permanent or-
ganization, to consist of a President, six Vice-
Presidents, three Secretaries, a Chaplain and
Sergeant-at-arm. On motion of Col. J. R.
Winder, the chair was authorized to appoint a
committee of seven to nominate candidates
for these offices.  During the absence of the
committee, Hon. L. E. Harrington being called
upon responded in a very appropriate speech.
Mr. H. began by saying the most important
item in political economy was to have a good
foundation; then we want a good government,
and in order to have good government we must
have good representatives to make good laws,
plain and simple, so the people, the common-
ality, can understand them and not lose the
meaning in the verbiage.  To accomplish this,
it is necessary to create intelligence, and to do
so we must educate.  The speaker avowed
himself an advocate of education; said no man
was fit for a ruler until he was qualified to be
ruled over; with him the question was not
what the world thought of us, but what God
knew of us.
   Mrs. S. M. Kimball was called upon and 
made a few concise remarks upon the plank
Woman's Suffrage in the platform of govern-
ment, and briefly referred to the 16th Amend-
ment.
   Mrs Hannah T. King was then called, and
spoke of woman's peculiar position before the
public at the present time, felt it was fraught
with a deep significance, and certainly was no
child's play.
   The committee's report on permanent organ-
ization was adopted as a whole.  President,
Hon. W. H. Hooper; Vice Presidents: First
Judicial District, L. E. Harrington and Canute
Petersen; Second Judicial District, William
H. Dame and J. D. T. McCallister; Third Ju-
dicial District, F. D. Richards and M. D. Ham-
mond; Secretaries, John T. Caine, Emmeline
B. Wells and F. M. Lyman; Chaplain, Wil-
ford Woodruff; Sergeant-at-arms, William
Hyde.  Hon. W. H. Hooper assumed the chair,
and declared the Convention fully organized,
and the Chaplain opened with prayer, after
which the Chairman thanked the Convention
for the honor conferred upon him, and made a 
few remarks upon the object of the assembly,
which was to nominate a delegate to represent
Utah in the Forty-seventh Congress.
   The Convention then went into a committee
of the whole, for a nomination of delagate,
with Hon. F. D. Richards in the chair.  A
brief recess was allowed the delegates of each
county to confer with each other, after which
the Convention was again called to order, and
reports of the chairmen of the various delega-
tions were made.  Each county returned a 
solid vote for Hon. George Q. Cannon, and the
chairman on the committee of the whole stated
that the Hon. George Q. Cannon was duly
nominated as Utah's delegate to the Forty-
seventh Congress, to be voted for at the No-
vember election.  This report was unanimously
adopted by the whole Convention, without one
dissenting voice.  Ezra D. Carpenter moved
for resolutions, short and crisp, expressing the
sentiments of the Convention on the subject
of woman's suffrage and in regard to the peo-
ple's delegate, which was not deemed expe-
dient as the hour was late, and further busi-
ness had to be transacted; but Mr. Carpenter
said some very good things in favor of women,
which the ladies present fully appreciated; he
remarked that it was comparatively easy to
speak on woman suffrage here in Utah, where
women had the franchise extended to them,
but it was an entirely different thing in the
world, where a few noble-hearted women were
battling, as it were, alone on this ground.  He
thought the first true step made towards wo-
man's suffrage had been made in this Territory.
This remark brought to our mind the letter we
received from Mrs. Lydia E. Beecher, of Man-
chester, editor of the "Woman's Suffrage Jour-
nal" of that city, and a prominent leader of 
the woman's party in England: "It has been
reserved for the government of your Territory
to set the world an example of real, political
justice, and the women of Utah should hold
their political suffrage as a sacred and divine
trust, held not for their benefit alone, but for
the sake of their sisters."  Hon. W. H. Hooper
made some most excellent remarks upon wo-
man's suffrage; said he had always advocated
it strongly, and related the circumstance of
Congressman Julian introducing the bill en-
franchising Mormon women.  Hon. Orson
Hyde spoke of the speech of Mr. Hopper in
the House of Representatives on the question
of Plural Marriage, and said it had never been
answered. Mrs. Zina D. Young made some
concise remarks upon the position of this
people in the eyes of the world, and prayed
God to bless the honorable body there assem-
bled.
   A committee on information was appointed
by the Chair, to wait upon Hon. George Q.
Cannon and inform him of his nomination.
This committee consisted of Hon. Abraham
Hatch, Hon. Orson Hyde, Hon. Wilford Wood-
ruff.  The Convention then proceeded to re-
elect the People's Territorial Central Commit-
tee, with some changes.  As it now stands it
consists of John Sharp, President, David O.
Calder, Vice-President, Theodore McKean,
Secretary, Mrs. B. W. Smith. Feramorz Little,
John T. Caine, H. W. Brown, John R. Win-
der, A. M. Cannon, George Crismon, A. M.
Musser, Mrs. E. B. Wells, Salt Lake Co; W.
N. Dusenberry, Utah Co.; Joseph O. Robison,
Millard Co.; John R. Murdock, Beaver Co.;
Edward Dalton, Iron Co.: Isaac Duffin, Kane
Co.; A. B. Whitehead, Washington Co.; I. W.
Pierce, Sevier Co.; W. T. Reid, Sanpete Co.;
Joseph Hatch, Wasatch Co.; W. W. Cluff,
Summit Co.; Samuel Francis, Morgan Co.; W.
R. Smith, Davis Co.; A. Christiansen, Box El-
der Co. F. M. Lyman, Tooele Co,; F. D. Rich-
ards, Weber Co; Moses Thatcher, Cache Co.;
Wm. H. Lee, Rich Co.; Wm. King, Piute Co.
Benediction was then pronounced by the Chap-
lain, and the Convention adjourned sine die.
The spirit of the convention was most excel-
lent throughout, and the business accomplished
was entirely satisfactory.  We feel especially
gratided with the feeling [-] existing here in
regard to woman's voice as well as vote in sub-
jects of civil government, and return our sin-
cere thanks, in behalf of the women of Utah, to
our brethren who have generously extended to
us the privilege of acting in political gather-
ings, and trust that the women of this Terri-
tory will use wisely and discreetly for good, the
power they hold through having the ballot.

IN MEMORIAM.

	DIED, Sept. 11, 1878, Anna, daughter of
George and Elizabeth Campkin of Salt Lake
City, and wife of Willis D. Fuller of Harris-
burg, Washington Co., U. T., at which place
she has lived since her marriage in 1867.
	Deceased was born in the village of Wadden,
Cambridgeshire, England, Aug. 12, 1843. Emi-
grated to the valley of Salt Lake with her
parents in 1850, and became a member of the
Church of Latter-day Saints. Her untimely
departure, which was sudden, almost without
warning, a few hours after giving birth to an
infant daughter, cast a gloom over our entire
community. For the six little ones (the eldest
but ten years of age), thus bereft of a mother's
tender love and care, our hearts are especially
moved, and we pray that though absent in
body, the memory of the beloved one gone may
be to them and the father of these dear children
as a sacred link, to lift their hearts upward,
and aid in leading their footsteps through life's
devious paths, until they shall again be reunit-
ed in that land where death cannot enter and
parting is unknown.

SANITARY USE OF FRUITS.—Fruit never
does harm unless it is eaten at improper
times, in improper quantities, or before it
is ripened and fit for the human stomach.
A distinguished physician has said that if
his patients would make a practice of eat-
ing a couple of good oranges before break-
fast, from February to June, his practice
would be gone. The principal evil is that
we do not eat enough of fruit; that we in-
jure its finer qualities with sugar; that we
drown them in cream. We need the medi-
cal action of the pure fruit acids in our sys-
tem, and their cooling, corrective influence.
This is a fact worth remembering at the
proper season.

economy,="" enables="" hus-="" band="" .t="">	THE EXTRAVAGANCES OF WOMEN.

	Hard times, financial difficulties, defalca-
tions,  embezzlements, defrauding and
swindling everywhere!  Why not?  There
is sufficient cause for this state of affairs.
	See the extravagance of the women. That
alone is sufficient to bankrupt the nation.
	Do the women of these days work do
they econoomize? do they deny themselves
luxuries?  Certainly not.  Are they will-
ing to live in the style their mothers and
grandmothers did?  No. Is there anything
less than the most luxurious homes, elegant
attire and costly equipages that will satisfy
their [-]thetic tastes?  Do we not all know
that our women are proud, extravagant,
wasteful - a dead weight upon our business
interests, crippling our energies and ruin-
ing our trade, by foolishly throwing away
our money for gew-gaws - money which is
needed to carry on business successfully?
And the husbands are willing to gratify
their expensive tastes, even though it lead
to bankruptcy and fraud.  Their extrava-
gance is doing even worse than bankrupt-
ing the nation; it is destroying our homes,
leaving our daughters unmarried, a burden
upon the fathers.  A young man cannot
afford to marry; it costs too much to keep
a wife.  The establishment that would suit
his income would not suit his wife's tastes.
She must dress as well and have as elegant
a home as the ladies in whose society she
has moved.
	Who does not hear these and like expres-
sions from all classes of men?  Intelligent,
truthful men believe they are stating facts
when they make these assertions.
	Through the press, from the platform
and occasionally from the pulpit, the people
hear of woman's extravagance.  When the
financial crash of 1857 came, the New York
"Tribune" and other leading papers asserted
that it was the extravagance of the women
that had caused the trouble.  Again and
again was this repeated, until the people
became convinced that such was the fact.
 A few foolish women, whose husbands
were glad to lavish money on them, had
bought high priced camel's hair shawls,
and this was sufficient evidence that all
others were extravagant, and straightway
woman was made the scapegoat for the sins
of the whole nation.
	Do you wish the public to believe a state-
ment, even though erroneous?  That is
easily done.  Make your assertion, repeat
it frequently, induce a few others to do the
same, and how soon it is believed by the
multitude; for "What everybody says must
be true." Who thinks of asking for the
proof?
	But let us look at the other side of this
question of extravagance.  Suppose women,
through the press, from the platform and
in private conversation should assert that
the extravagance of men has brought on
our financial difficulties and caused hard
times - that men, unwilling to economize
as their fathers and grandfathers did, spend
so much on themselves there is nothing
left to make home comfortable, and the
young ladies cannot afford to marry such
spendthrifts.
	Should they assert that men will not deny
themselves luxuries, that they wear costly
suits, carry expensive watches, pay high
prices for rooms in first-class boarding
houses and hotels, hire fast teams, attend
the opera, theatre, and other places of
amusement; that their extravagant habits
are destroying their health, morals and in-
tellect - might not be almost as much
truth in the statement as in those we so,
often hear concerning women spending
money foolishly?
	In every city of our land we see fine
mansions that cost tens, even hundreds of
thousands of dollars.  We see jails, court
houses, city halls, custom houses and other
public buildings which cost from one hun-
dred thousand dollars to one million - all
built by men.  But who charges extrava-
gance?  This is enterprise. 
	At the lowest calculation we find there is
more than five hundred millions of dollars
spent every year for intoxicating drinks,
and one hundred millions worth of tobacco
consumed by the men of the United States.
The money paid out for liquors, tobacco and
a few other luxuries men indulge in, would,
in two or three years, pay the great national
debt.  This could't be called extravagance
- but the expenses men have to incur.
	One objection we have heard urged
against women having a vote in the gov-
ernment is their extravagant habits.
"Why," said a good, honest legislator to
us not long since, "it would not do for wo-
men to vote; they would soon ruin the na-
tion by spending its money so lavishly;
they wish to have everything so grand."
"What did the refurnishing of these two
legislative halls cost?" we asked.  "Forty
thousand dollars.  The curtains at each
window cost seven hundred dollars." "What
did you pay for the carpet, desks and spit-
toons in the room in which the constitu-
tional convention met?" Eighteen thou-
sand dollars."  "What do you pay for
cleaning the treasury building in Washing-
ton City, to say nothing of the other public
buildings?"  "Fifty thousand dollars each
year."  "Now, my dear sir, do you think
women could be more extravagant than
this?"  "I do not believe they would have
spent so much in these instances," he an-
swered.
	If men were to thoroughly investigate
this matter they would cease charging wo-
men with lack of frugality.  They would
find the great mass of their countrywomen
exceedingly economical, saving even to
meanness, we fear.  Go into the houses of
the middle and working classes, and you
will see this verified.  The furniture, the
children's clothing, the dress of husband
and wife all prove there has been no money
thrown away foolishly.  You find these
have been made over and over again.  The
mother's bonnet, the daughter's hat changed
to suit the fashion, costing about one fourth
the price of new ones, are worn two, three
and four years - picture frames, ottomans,
sofa pillows, afghans, chairs that have been
cushioned without the aid of an upholsterer,
stands and tables made out of boxes, so
neatly covered that one might be excused
for not detecting the commendable sham-
all the work of women's hands.
	Travel on steamboat and railway cars,
and again you have evidence of the fru-
gality of woman.  The dinner bell rings on
the steamboat - who responds to the call?
Down both sides of the long tables are men
- two or three ladies at the head of the
first one.  Enter the ladies' cabin and you
will see the women eating  cold lunch
they carried with them from home.  Travel
by rail - who gets off and takes a good warm
breakfast, dinner and supper?  Men.  Who
eats a lunch in the car and saves seventy-
five cents or a dollar each time? Women.
	When men have learned to appreciate
woman's industry and economy - when
they have learned to enumerate the amount
of money saved by their wives and sisters,
they will cease to talk about their expen-
sive tastes and habits.

The majority of men know nothing of
the cost of a lady's dress.  One that costs
ten dollars would appear to them quite as
expensive as one that cost two hundred.  It
would be impossible for them to distinguish
the difference between a hundred dollar set
of laces and the cheapest of imitation, which
could be bought, perhaps, for one dolllar. A
bow of pink ribbon at the throat would ap-
pear to them as a more extravagant outlay
than a fifty dollar pin.
 By the industry, economy and good taste
of the women of America our homes have
become the pride of the nation and the
wonder of the world.  The wife of the
laboring man in this country makes it possi-
ble for him to get a home of his own.  The
wife of the mechanic, who, by her frugal
habits and strict economy, enables her hus-
band to keep his family of five or six on
that which would not board and clothe two,
if she did nothing, deserves a better reward
than being told she earns nothing and is
supported by her husband.  The woman
who cooks, mends, makes clothing, washes,
irons, sweeps and dusts, who does an untold
amount of labor that would cost the hus-
band hundreds of dollars annually, if others
performed the work, should not be insulted
by being told she is wasting her husband's
hard earnings, if she buys a good dress oc-
casionally.  The farmer's wife who milks,
churns and keeps the family in groceries,
and, as is frequently the case, clothes her-
self and daughters with her butter money,
should at least be permitted to call one
dime her own, and not be told she belongs
to the class of non-producers.  The educat-
ed woman who says to her husband I can
earn at paintings, teaching music, or writing
more than will pay a girl for doing all our
work, and I wish to do it should not be
compelled to cook, bake and wash because
the law, which she had no voice in making,
says she owes her husband service; neither
should she, if she adorns her house with
flowers, paintings and statury; be told she
is bankrupting the nation.

THE AMERICAN Woman Suffrage Asso-
ciation will hold its Ninth Annual Meeting
in Indianapolis, Ind., at Masonic Hall on
Wednesday and Thursday, Nov. 13 and 14.
There will be sessions each day, morning,
afternoon and evening, commencing Wed-
nesday at 10 a. m. The call for this meet-
ing, published in the "Woman's Journal,"
says, "Let the auxiliary societies, state and
local, at once call meetings to choose their
delegates, who should, if possible, be pres-
ent early, with credentials regularly signed
by the officers of their respective societies."
The call is arranged in parliamentary form,
and specifies minutely how people in dif-
ferent localities may make a proper repre-
sentation. All this agitation and holding
conferences on the subject of suffrage is 
significant of the future, and speaks in
plain terms and almost in trumpet voice,
of the great contest for right that is being
fought all over the enlightened world.

WANTED.
 We want that ingredient in religion that
shortens the steps, tunes the voice to melo-
dy, fills the eye with sunshine, and checks
the impatient exclamation and the harsh
rebuke.  A religion that is polite, deferential
to superiors, and courteous to inferiors, and
considerate to friends; a religion of heart,
that goes into the family, but rises superior
to it, and keeps the husband from getting
cross when dinner is late, keeps the wife
from fretting when the husband tracks the
newly-washed floor with his muddy boots,
keeps the mother patient when the baby is
cross, and amuses the children as well as
instructs them, cares for the domestics as
well as promptly paying them, projects the
"honeymoon" into the "harvestmoon," and
the happy home like the eastern fig tree,
bearing in its beauty at once the beauty of
the tender blossom and the glory of the
ripened fruit.  We want more religion that
interposes between the ruts, the gulleys,
and the rocks of the highways of life and
the sensitive souls that are obliged to travel
over them.--Woman's World.

HYGIENIC NOTES.

	Cure for Diptheria.—Take two ounces each of
olive oil and coal oil; mix well by shaking. Ad-
minister, internally, every hour, a quantity accord-
ing to the age of the patient. Dose for a child
five years old half a teaspoonful of the mixture;
half that age, half the quantity, and so on.
	Twenty minutes afterwards, give the same quan-
tity of dry sulphur. Twenty minutes after that
the same quantity of dry cream of tartar.
	This treatment is to be repeated for three hours,
when great improvement is generally perceived, in
which case the time between the doses is to bə
gradually increased, until a cure is effected. A
little common sense and appreciation of the differ-
ence of constitution, &c., in various cases will
suggest modifications of the treatment.—Deseret

	Bathing Children.—Some mothers, writes a
physician, think when their children get beyond
two or three years of age the frequent entire bath
can be dispensed with. If some of the main
facts of physiology were well known and under-
stood, every one would perceive that cleanliness
of the skin is one of the conditions of good health.
It happens when bathing is disregarded that the
lungs, kidneys or bowels have more than their ap-
portionment of work. If these are strong and
healthy, they may bear the tax with little appa-
rent injury, but, in most cases, a lowering of the
vitality and tone of the system ensues. Large
bath tubs are pleasant and convenient, but not in-
dispensable to the proper cleansing of the skin.
A speedy sponging of the body in pure water,
followed by friction in pure air, is all that is nec-
essary.
	When disinclined to use water, I find thorough
application of the flesh brush to the whole person
an admirable substitute, especially on retiring; it
relieves nervousness, equalizes the circulation and
induces quiet sleep[-] Mothers, above all, should

skins are kept active and healthy there will not be

eases. If your little one is cross or troublesome,

effect of a bath; sometimes it is magical, and, if
tried, he will go to sleep and awaken bright; cheer[-]
ful and happy. Do not, though, as I have seen
some parents do, plunge a child into water, when
he screams and shrinks from it, thinking they are
doing a good deed. Nature must be the guide; if

WE would say to our subscribers through-
out the country, as there has been some
misunderstanding in regard to postage,
that the postage for one year is ten cents to

to Europe, twenty-four cents,this may seem

 THE Lord has blessed this people with
one of the most abundant harvests that ever
crowned the labors of the farmer, and we
trust our sisters will not be unmindful of
the mission given them  store up grain
against a day of need.  It is now just two
years since this mission was given to the
sisters, and particulars published for their
benefit in the columns of the Exponent.
President Young had great faith in the
women of Zion and, with the organizations
as they now now exist it is a comparatively
easy matter to gather in means.  The sis-
ters have been liberal in their donations,
and quite a large amount has been stored in
different parts of the Territory, but this is
a work that is not done in a year or two, but
is a continous labor, and certainly the sisters
should not feel it a burden, but continue in
well doing, knowing that “the race is not
to the swift,” but to those who endure to
the end.  There are some granaries already
built, and others begun, and we trust that
the sisters will be earnest in this good cause,
for such it is; what could be greater or
grander than to disburse food to the desti-
tute in a time of need?  If the sisters are
striving for usefulness, and wanting a high
sphere to labor in, here it is.  Let there be
some Joseph’s in Utah even it they are
women: it will matter but little when men
are hungry if even a woman dispenses
charity to them.  Sisters let us be wise in
this respect, and lay up of the abundance
God has given us here in these valleys, and
manifest to Him and to all the world our
liberality in the things which we have been
commanded to do.  Never mind what those
say who are not co-workers with us, but
prove our faith in the prophecies of the an-
cient and modern prophets, by storing up
grain for ourselves and our friends, and
those who will come from afar to ask of us,
when the land is laid desolate with the ter-
rible scourges that await it.  Let all the
Relief Societies throughout the length and
breadth of the Territory see what can be
done in this matter, and send us word pre-
vious to the Annual Grain Meeting which
will be held in this city Nov. 16, in the in-
terest of the Grain Question.
