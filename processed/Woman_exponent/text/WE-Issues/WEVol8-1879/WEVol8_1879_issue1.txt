A NEIGHBOR.

	Written on the occasion of a Juvenile Concert for a
poor widow's benefit, and gratefully dedicated to all par-
ties, Jew or Gentile, that assisted therein.

'One touch of nature makes the whole world kin;'
Sorrow's a key all sympathies to win.
The privilege to give to all extend.
E'en as the dews of heaven on all descend;
Of those who much possess much is required,
If blessing in proportion is desired;
If small our means, if large our will and love,
Much will be credited to us above;
"The widow's mite," a gift exceeding small,
Exceeded others, "for she gave her all."
When little ones as volunteers enlist
The poor and helpless kindly to assist,
Ah! who shall say 'tis not a pleasant sight,
Or check their efforts in the cause of right?
Who aids the weary, succors the distressed,
The smile of heaven alike shall on him rest;
He shall be recompensed, whate'er his creed,
That helps another in his time of need.

Ages ago was information sought
From One who everywhere compassion taught—
Who is my neighbor? Who, that I should love
E'en as myself, or as the Lord above?

By Christ Himself, to suit the simplest mind,
Past controversy, neighbor was defined.—
"A certain man, his name it matters not,
Fell among thieves: such was his hapless lot
The desperadoes took his all and fled,
Leaving him bruised and bleeding, nearly dead.
While thus he lay, all wounded, senseless, sore,
A priest went by and calmly viewed him o'er,
Deigned not to help—so great his priestly pride,
So small his soul, he passed the other side.
A certain Levite also passed the place,—
One of a called and chosen priestly race—
He saw the man, but with averted sight
Soothed thus his conscience, that it served him right.
One of Samaria's sons next passed along;
He saw and pitied, knowing right from wrong.
With oil he soothed the poor wounds, and did cheer
With wine his spirits, faint from pain and fear.
On his own beast did set him, took him to
A neighboring inn, and something more did do:
He called the host, and as his bill he paid—
'Tend well for me this wounded man,' he said;
Soon as I come again, believe me, sir,
I'll pay whate'er expense he may incur."

Now," said the Savior, "tell me if you can,
Who was a neighbor to this wounded man?"

Ah, what a parable was this to choose—
A sermon full of meaning to the Jews;
Full well Christ knew their over-righteous zeal,
That with Samaritans they did not deal;
Full well he knew they did not deem them meet
To wipe the dust from off their sacred feet;
A double purpose, thus, his words supp[-]ied—
To show man's duty and reprove man's pride.

Oh, 'tis a lesson sacred and sublime,
And always will be to the end of time.
'Gainst cool indifference to another's fate,
'Gainst bigotry, that meanest kind of hate,
That makes men monsters, foolish, vain, unjust.
Unworthy even of the smallest trust.
For wh[-]t foul wrongs must bigotry atone!
Since Daniel in "the lion's den" was thrown;
Since Christ was crucified; and now,
*Because another Daniel made a vow
Unto his God, he must that vow betray,
Or in a prison cell the forfeit pay.
Justice and prejudice are not allied,
They cannot live logether side by side;
Whene'er in chnrch or court a bigot reigns
Justice expires, or but exists in chains;
For bigotry is so profoundly blind,
Outside itself it nothing good can find;
Integrity as well might hide its face,
For bigotry ne'er sees a single grace;
Sweet mercy, sorrowing, folds her pinions there,
Where virtue fails how can she but despair
O bigotry! when this fair earth is freed
From thy control, man will be blest indeed;
So quickly germinates thy baneful root,
That soon the persecuted persecute;
Wrong feeds on wrong, and hate of every kind

[Column 2]
With rapid growth o'erruns the narrow mind;
Alas! 'tis not confined to any place,
To any special nation, age or race,
To check its growth; we all of us have need,
To check its growth; let all of us take heed,
Lest through our prejudice we fail to be
Neighbors to all that need our sympathy.

The Gospel Founder, truth personified,
Huried all His wrath at sanctimonious pride;
His kindest language always was addressed
To cheer the humble, or the most distressed.
Blest with the wisest, purest, loftiest mind,
His love extended unto all mankind.
A moral standard was by Christ unfurled,
Noble and broad enough to bless the world.
"Do unto all as ye would have them do,
Give unto every man the honor due;
Do good to all men, whosoe'er they be,
As ye have means and opportunity;
Give unto those who make you no return,
Give when the rcompense you can discern;
The measure that you mete shall be to you
Measured again, and running over, too."
Do good to them that hate us—if we can,
Like Him we shall be something more than man,
Few love their foes, few ask ther Father to
Forgive their enemies the wrong they do:
Ah! if we could this Godlike standard reach,
Could we but practice what these precepts teach,
Whate'er our faith, we surely would agree
In all that claims our common sympathy.
He is a neighbor, whatsoe'er his creed.
Who helps another in his time of need.

						EMILY HILLOODMANSEE.

CEASE TO FIND FAULT.

	Don't quarrel with your lives, your des-
tiny, it will do you no good if you do; but
rather add to your unhappiness, as your
discontent will mar your face, and spoil
your disposition; and more than that, who
of you would really change your lot for
that of another, taking the good and the
evil together? Dont you think your should-
ers better fitted for the burden placed there
than any other one you could select. One
has more of this world's goods than an-
other, we have no need to fret for money;
but would you change the advantages of a
sound and healthy constitution with a
bright intellect, for a sickly one, and conse-
quently a mind less brillant. You may
not see others' sorrows, but remember
every heart knoweth its own bitterness,
then cease to find fault with fate; you must
if you have any true philosophy in your
nature; I don't see how you can very well
help it. Your fate will follow along by
your side just the same unless you have
the power within yourself to change it, so
you might as well be pleasant about it, it
will materially help both yourself and
others: then think more of your blessings,
and less of your sorrows and troubles and
the world will be better for your presence,
and you will be able to combat difficulties
more successfully, and gain an experience
that will be more advantageous than the
riches of the world that perish with using,
and which you can never take hence.
							MELISSA.

NOTES AND NEWS.

	MISS ANNA E. Dickenson has decided
not to visit California until autumn.

	MADAME ELIZABETH Bonaparte died in
Baltimore, Md., on the 4th of April last,
aged ninety-four years. She was the wife
of Jerome Bonaparte, brother of Napoleon;
her maiden name was Patterson.

	VICTOR HUGO is now seventy-eight years
old and is in robust health. A few of his
oldtime friends invited him to a dinner on
his birthday, served in the "Cafe Riche" in
Paris. "He spoke with his usual vigor and
point."

	MRS. C. M. Williams has been elected
President of the State National Bank at
Raleigh, N. C., in place of her husband,
now deceased. She is said to be the first
woman who ever held that office in the
United States.

	ABOUT A hundred and fifty names, most
of them belonging to what are called
"strong-minded ladies," are appended to
the preliminary prospectus to the "Political
Club for Women," which, I hear, is mak-
ing fair way. The project was inaugurated
at the close of last year, and the intention
is to open the club in a central situation as
soon as a thousand members shall have
been elected. There is no entrance fee,
and the subscription is only five shillings a
year, as it is desired to comprise all sorts
and conditions of women. It is calculated
that it will not be safe to open until an in-
come of £250 is guaranteed for current ex-
penses. I fancy there will be no difficulty
in finding the proper number of clubable
ladies in a political as well as in a social
sense; and social clubs for ladies are spring-
ing up all around like mushrooms.—From
an English paper.

	THE QUEEN IN ITALY.—A Turin journal
states that the Queen has already derived
benefit from her sojourn in Italy. On the
1st of April she took advantage of a lovely
morning to walk for a long distance along
the shore of the lake, accompanied by the
Princess Beatrice, and unattended. She
was dressed in the most simple manner, in
lusterless black barege, walked very rapid-
ly, and from time to time stopped to admire
a fine view. Before the Queen returned to
her temporary home, she visited the Villa
Durozzo, where a part of her suite is lodged
and inspected the rooms she had hired
there. Then taking the path to the left
she decended again to the shore of the lake.
Perceiving some washerwomen at work,
she sat down on a stone near them and in
excellent Italian conversed for some time
with them. The Queen and Princess were
taken for ordinary tourists. Her Majesty
was charmed at the error. On her way to
the Villa Clara she visited the stables. The
coachman, an Englishman, in charge of
them was so embarrassod at finding himself
in the Royal presence that he was not able
to reply to the Queen, who, taking him for
a Lombard, spoke to him in Italian. The
Italian Governmeut have organised at
Raveno a special telegraphic service to
place the Queen in intimate communica-
tion with Germany and England.

RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE.

JUAB STAKE.

	Fourth Quarterly meeting of the Juab
County Stake Organization of Relief Socie-
ty, held in the Meeting House at Nephi,
April 20. Officers of the Board present,
also Pres. John Taylor, Brothers Rockwood,
Jennings, Van Cott and Paxman. Meeting
opened by singing and prayer. Minutes of
last Conference read and accepted; also fi-
nancial report.
	Pres. Teasdale said: "Mrs. Amelia Golds-
brough has been President of the R. S. for
many years, and it would be useless to
speak of her unceasing labors; you all know
them. She is also President of the Stake,
and we feel to relieve her from the Presi-
dency of the branch, that she may be more
at liberty to attend to her other duties, vis-
iting the different societies in this Stake of
Zion. Her labors are appreciated. I move
that we sustain Mrs. Mary Pitchforth as
President of the Nephi Branch." The
motion was unanimously sustained.
	Pres. Goldsbrough made a few excellent
and appropriate remarks, expressing her
desire to aid in both temporal and spiritual
work.
	Pres. John Taylor said that he felt very
much interested in the Relief Societies;
they had exerted themselves very credita-
bly in the interests of the people, and were
great aids to the Priesthood, which they
hold, not in an independent capacity, but
associated with their husbands; as it is
said, "The man is not without the woman
nor the woman without the man in the
Lord." We are operating together in the
interests of Israel; and the Relief Societies
and the Young Men's and Young Women's
Mutual Improvement Societies have been
and are doing a great deal of good. The
Relief Societies are not new among the
Latter-day Saints. Joseph Smith organized
the first Relief Society at Nauvoo, at which
I was present. Emma Smith was chosen
President, and Sister Elizabeth Ann Whit-
ney and Sister Sarah M. Cleveland as her
Counselors; Sister Eliza R. Snow was Sec-
retary, and Sister Elvira Cole, Treasurer
(as my memory serves me), and they im-
mediately commenced their labor of love
among the Saints. Soon after their organ-
ization, through persecutions and other
circumstances, resulting in the death of
the Prophet Joseph and his brother Hyrum,
the Society suspended, until again organ-
ized by Pres. B. Young in Salt Lake City.
Let me here remark that while we appre-
ciate the labors of our sisters, it must not
be forgotten that the man holds the Priest-
hood, and is the head of the woman, as
Christ is the head of the Church. But are
they not set apart by the Priesthood to offi-
ciate in their callings? Yes. So are the
presiding officers of the Young Men's and
Young Women's Mutual Improvement As-
sociations; but setting apart and blessing
them is not ordination. This may answer
some questions that I have been asked on
this subject.
	The sisters, however, can work and do
work in their sphere as helps to the breth-
ren. They can do a great deal; they can
feed the poor, clothe the naked, instruct
the ignorant, comfort the distressed, and
even lay hands on and pray for the sick,
and do a large amount of good generally.
I consider the silk culture, in which the
sisters have engaged with such laudable
zeal, very essential; also the cultivation of
ourselves and families. And while the
brethren ought all to be gentlemenly and
courteous in their demeanor, the sisters

[Column 2]
should all act the part of ladies, be gener-
ous, kind, affable and courteous, maintain
virtue, purity and honor, and treat each
other as ladies. It would be proper also to
throw out influenees that would be refining
in their nature. We ought to teach our
daughters how to work, how to cook, adorn
our houses and make home pleasant and
desirable, that we may be elevated in our
feelings, aspirations and acts. We are only
jnst starting out now, but the time will
come when kings will come to Zion, as the
Scriptures say, to gaze on her glory. The
young men should educate themselves that
they can teach kings and queens; and our
sisters should be prepared to take their po-
sition in Zion. Our sisters are really one
with us, and when the brethren go into the
Temples to officiate for the males, the sis-
ters will go in for the females; we operate
together for the good of the whole, that we
may be united together for time and all
eternity. I am pleased to see my sisters
relieving the poor and comforting and min-
istering to the needy and unfortunate. Re-
lieve everybody of every creed that needs
assistance, especially the household of faith.
You sisters that go among the people visit-
iting, try to elevate everybody; they are
all the children of God and need your
beneficence and kindness. You can afford
to do good to all, but in doing this don't
neglect your own sisters. The Lord sends
rain upon the just and upon the unjust, and
we can afford to treat everybody right.
Shall we, in doing this, however, be one
with the profligate? No. Or with the
thief or liar? No. We are here to elevate
the human family to the standard of God;
we must not descend to their standard.
And I would say, My soul, enter not thou
into their secrets, and mine honor, with
them be not thou united;" but I would feed
and clothe them if they needed it. Again,
let me call upon you to educate your chil-
dren, teach them through proper channels.
We don't want to place our children in a
position to be taught by the heathen, and
have them dragged down to perdition. Do
you, as good Saints, fathers and mothers,
think that those who do not receive the
Gospel can go into the celestial kingdom of
God? Suppose a man and his wife should
be fortunate enough to get into the Celestial
kingdom of God, and see their offspring in
a Terrestial or Telestial state, and then, if
they had the power to do it, to hear them
say, "Father, mother, I have to blame you
for being here; it was you who put me un-
der improper influences, which caused me
to take the course I did. It is not my fault,
it is yours." For God's sake never put
yonr children in a position to be led astray.
Place them under proper influences, make
their home pleasant, have beautiful gar-
dens, and pleasant, pure and virtuous asso-
ciations.
	I am glad the sisters have a little grain
laid up, and I would advise you to plead
with your husbands to put something to the
pile.
	May the Spirit of God dwell in your
bosoms and rest upon your children and
all that pertains to you, in the name of Je-
sus. Amen.
	Bro. Rockwood said: "It is with pleasure
fhat I contemplate upon the great work that
is before you, my sisters, in clothing the
naked, feeding the hungry, &c. I have
faith in the administration of my sisters.
Your influence is felt in the world; you are
coming to the front. May God bless you.
Amen.
	Singing, "Lord dismiss us with thy bless-
ing." Benediction by W. Taylor.
	MRS. AMELIA GOLDSBROUGH, Pres't.
	MRS. MARY PITCHFORTH, Sec'y.

			WOMAN'S VOICE.

	For a long time I have been wishing that
the Spirit of the Lord would aid me in
writing a few lines that would harmonize
with the feelings of my sisters, and also
tend to develop my thoughts and give them
expression. I realize the more we exercise
the ability and knowledge our God has en-
dowed us with, the better we will be able
to convey our thoughts, and by seeking
unto Him in humble prayer He will enlarge
our understanding so that we may more 
clearly comprehend His work. I wish to
add my testimony to that of many others
to the divinity of this great work that we
are engaged in for the building up of the
kingdom of God on this earth. I know the
Lord has revealed His word again unto the
human family, and I know this is the true
and everlasting Gospel of Jesus, the power
of God unto salvation to all who believe
and obey. My heart is made to rejoice
while I am reading the testimony of so
many of my sisters in the EXPONENT. In
my youth I joined this Church—in the year
1833, in the State of New York. I had the
privilege of traveling with the Saints
through many scenes of affliction. I was
in Nauvoo at the time our beloved Prophet
was martyred, and have passed through
many trials; and even now, after all, though
being called to help in building up this
new country, I feel to rejoice, knowing that
if we endure to the end our reward will be
eternal lives. Is not this worth living for?
I am not weary in well-doing in the work,
but am determined, by the aid of my
Heavenly Father, to serve Him to the end
of my days, that hereafter I may enjoy the
society of the prophets and apostles and all
the glorified who have come up through
much tribulation. This is the prayer of 
your sister in Zion,
					OLIVE TENNEY.

THE BABY AND THE SHOWER OF
RAIN.

This morn my baby waked with strange surprise,
And to the window turned enquiring eyes;
For the first time the pattering rain he heard,
And all his wondering powers at once were stirred.

He's eight months old to-day, my baby "Le,"
Yet rain before he has not chanced to see—
At least to notice—and from window pane
He turned to me with looks of "Please explain."

You know, dear sister, how we mothers think
We understand the baby's nod and wink,
And learn as much from "coos" and cunning looks
As scientists from nature and irom books.

"It is the rain, my birdie pet," I said;
He raised his hand and sagely shook his head;
"It looks like waters"—then he stopped to sneeze-
The same that runs along below the trees;

The same you use to make me clean and sweet,
In which I love to splash with hands and feet;
And how you call it—"what[--] "The rain, my love."
"Well, I can't see what brings it from above:

"Of how it stays there till its time to fall—
I cannot understand the thing at all!
I think that I must wait until I grow,
And when I'm big like you, then I shall know."

And so he dropped the subject for a time,
And clapped his hands and sang a baby chime.
How much, I thought, we older folks appear
Like this meek child with matters not quite e'ear.

Things are presented which are mysteries grand,
We see and hear, but cannot understand;
Yet to our Father they are all as plain
And simple as the falling of the rain.
									LULA.

MRS. SARAH J. HALE.

	Mrs. Sarah Josepha Buell Hale, one of
the oldest of American writers, died in
Philadelphia, April 30, leaving as her me-
morial a mass of writings covering a period
of nearly sixty years, and which every-
where show painstaking industry and a su-
perior literary capacity. She was born in
Newport, N. H., in 1789. She married
David Hale, a lawyer, retaining, however,
her maiden name of Buell after assuming
that of her husband. Mr. Hale died in
1822, and his widow began her literary life
a year later, a book of poems, "The Genius
of Oblivion," Concord, 1823, being her first
venture. "Northwood," a novel in two
volumes, appeared in 1827, and in 1828 she
became editor of the "Ladies Magazine,"
published in Boston. In 1837 this periodi-
cal was united with "Godey's Lady's Book,"
a Philadelphia magazine of the old school,
and Mrs. Hale was its editor until 1877.
Her name has been most widely known in
connection with the "Lady's Book," but
her pen was busy with other subjects than
these growing out of her editorial duties.
She wrote, first and last, more than twenty
books, mostly of that peculiar type that one
associates with the round table, with its
red and black spread, the high-posted bed-
stead, the wooden mantel, with its gilt
china ornaments, and the white muslin cur-
tain of the "spare room" in the low farm-
house where, perchance, one has passed a
few careless vacation days. American wo-
men have written a whole library full of
this kind of literature. They were the
books that mothers gave their sons and
daughters as birthday presents thirty years
ago, bound commonly in full morocco, with
sides and backs gilt in arabesque patterns,
but sometimes in simple muslin, with the
same ornaments, and in silken cases, with
gilt edges. Such titles as "Flora's Inter-
preter," "The Ladies' Wreath," and "The
Poet's Offering," some of Mrs. Hale's best
known books, will suggest the whole class.
"Woman's Record, or Sketches of Distin-
guished women," is a very useful manual,
and takes its place with the best books of
reference. This is the work by which she
will be longest remembered, and perhaps
the only one that will hand her name down
to future ages. Her remains rest at Laurel
Hill.—Woman's Words.

SELF-IMPROVEMENT.
	There are many opportunities for improve-
ment that are passed by without thought, and
it is very frequently remarked by young peo-
ple (and we might say elder ones), I would
like to improve myself, but I’ve no chance,”
when chances and opportunities are being
thrown away all the time.  There are young
men and young women who are doing nothing
in particular, because they cannot find that
which is sufficiently remunerative.  And even
men who hold responsible positions, heads of
families, husbands and fathers, spend their
time not unfrequently in idleness, because
there ls no work for them to do that suits them
in this “work-a-day world.” And these same
men, some of them, debate upon the corners
of the streets about women usurping rights and
so forth.  The Scriptures say the man “who
does not provide for hisown house has denied the
faith and is worse than an infidel.” Of course
women who are left to take care of themselves,
and there are thousands in the world, have to
earn an independent living for themselves, and
sometimes for their families; such need some
preparation beforehand for the active pursuits
of life.  And who can tell what may be the
circumstances of the future?  The mother who
sends her daughter forth without some thor-
ough preparation that she can rely upon in an
extremity, does her an injustice, as we view
the matter.
	It is often remarked that every boy should
learn a trade, whatever his circumstances may
be as regards property, and we believe that is
right; but it has usually been the confirmed
custom of the times in the past that a girl's
fortune is to marry.  It is the opinion of many
people that if girls are trained in a spirit of
financial independence they would not be be
likely to marry, because they could support
themselves.  In New England, where girls are
trained in more industrious habits than in al-
most any other part of the world, there are to
be sure a very large percentage of old maids,
but it is not because of their energy, or indus-
try, or independence, but from other causes
well-known, but which we have not space
now to explain.
	Girls are not suitable for married life unless
they are energetic and industrious, and they
should be well informed on all general subjects:
and much of this they can do themselves, if 
they will.
 Girls, do not indulge in idleness! how often
we have heard the exclamation: “I’m not go-
ing to work, I’m going to have a good time;
drudges are not appreciated,” We are quite
willing to admit there is some drudgery con-
nected with a woman’s life.  But what about
yourselves, girls?  What will you find to ap-
preciate in your own conscience, if your life is
spent in frivolities and in trying to make your
path easy.  Perhaps the Great Creator has en-
dowed you with beauty, with a perfection of
face which is always more or less charming,
but too frequently these charms pall when the
real life of the individual is not consistent with
the fascinating exterior.
	This is an age of progression marked by
many new and fresh inventions and innova-
tions, in consequence of which changes are
taking place that are calling forth from the
minds of individuals advanced thoughts upon
subjects that were heretofore almost ignored,
so strong seemed to be the chain that bound
the opinions and customs of the past.  But
this is an e ra when woman must accept her
true position, and it is one of active work, and
it is needful she should prepare herself: do not
think you are to occupy positions, unless you
fit yourself for them, if you do you will de-
ceive yourself.  Put a limit to your desires,
the wisdom of life is acquired little by little,
by patient application and a strong devotion to
the best interests.  We know that it has been
considered by some that girls are toodelicate and
fragile, and many are reared by their parents
so tenderly that they are almost unfitted for
the actual experiences of womanhood. “Well,”
says, one, “so many women are physically in-
disposed for the arduous duties and labors that
seem to be their lot in life, and can take no ac-
tive part in the duties of their own household.”
Why is this?  Do they not early in life violate
many of the laws of health, and do not moth-
ers and fathers look upon it almost indifferent-
ly until it is too late.  Tight-fitting clothes,
shoes and gloves; too much dancing; wearing
thin dresses to evening parties, and coming
home without sufficient wrappings; these and
many other neglects that are overlooked at the
time, but become serious matters when the
health is wholly undermined.
	A temperate manner of life, with plenty of
outdoor exercise, and suitable clothing, a mod-
eration in the pursuits, or avocations, engaged
in, will in most cases secure health to those
who have not some inherent weakness which
baffles all skill.  Health is almost a necessity
to success in any enterprise.  It is possible to
improve in observing the laws of health,
and this will help one to attain other de-
sirable gifts and acquirements more readily.
	Our advice to young ladies is, qualify your-
selves for some noble purpose in life. “Well,
supposing I should get married?"  So much
the better for you and your husband and espec-
ially your children, for of all the positions
woman is called to occupy, the one that needs
the most careful preparation, the hsghest en-
dowment, is that of being a mother.  There is
not a positive necessity of certain conditions in
life to make you what you may aspire to be;
you can cultivate yourself under almost any
circumstances, if you have the will and deter-
mination.  Those who have been the most cele-
brated in literature, in science and in art, and
those who have acquired most wealth have
begun by cultivating themselves; and have
risen by degrees to the rank they occupy.
	In all that relates to life much is expected of
woman, therefore her need of cultivation is
great, and it should be not only in the graces
and embellishments that beautify and adorn,
but in the sound and profound truths by which
the greatest problems are solved.  If woman
gives to home the tone that attracts the hearts
of all the household, if woman gives to society
its character, should not the mothers of the
race have every avenue open to them possible,
that they might dedicate themselves to a life
of self-improvement?
	E. B. Wells


HOME AFFAIRS.

	THE PRESENT number commences Volume
Eight of WOMAN'S EXPONENT. Through its
agency Latter-day Saint women have become
better known to the world and have obtained
some prestige through this channel in ex-
pressing their ideas and reporting their
labors for each other's elevation and
improvement. We hope the increased
subscription list for the present year may prove
that the ladies appreciate the little paper as an
educator among the Saints, and also the good
missionary work it does at home and abroad.

	WE REGRET that the young people of Ogden
have given up publishing "The Amateur;" that
a journal in the interest of the Mutual Im-
provement Associations is needed must be ap-
parent to all who consider the subject. "The
Amateur" has done good work in its field of
labor, and its pages have always been filled
with expressions of true principles and pure
sentiments, and of this class of reading there
is a lack in the world. We are sorry to have
"The Amateur" suspended.

	THE BOOK of Abraham, by Elder George
Reynolds, is indeed a great acquisition to the
home literature of the Saints. It is not a crude,
unfinished work thrown together hastily, but
all who read its pages will understand it is
compiled with thought and carefnl attention
to facts, and is an embodiment of a series of
historical facts that possess a significance to the
Latter-day Saints. We commend it to all as
a work of genuine merit. The book is for sale
at the office of the Deseret News; it can also
be obtained here.

	WE WOULD respectfully solicit the Secreta-
ries of the Y. L. M. I. A. in the various organ-
izations everywhere to send word to the editor
of WOMAN'S EXPONENT if they edit a paper in
their Society; and if so the name of the paper
and how long it has been edited, how often it
is issued, the character of its articles, and who
are the editors and regular contributors. We
should be glad to publish these items in our
paper, and to ascertain what exertions are be-
ing made among the young ladies connected
with these societies to improve themselves in
writing.

IN MEMORIAM.

	Sister Diantha Morley Billings departed this
life at Provo on the evening of May 14, at fifteen
minutes to ten o'clock. She was familiarly
known as "Mother Billings," and was truly a
mother in Israel, beloved and respected by all.
She was amiable, gentle, tender, truly benevo-
lent, and her kindness and deeds of mercy ex-
tended to the stranger, as well as the friend or
neighbor.
	She was bo n August 23, 1795, at Montague,
Mass. In the year 1815 she, with her relatives,
moved to Kirtland, and there was married to
Titus Billing[-], with whom she passed her mar-
ried life mo[-]t happily. Patriarch Isaac Morley
(now deceas[-]d), her husband and herself were
among the first bapt[-]zed in Kirtland. It was
at the house of her brother, Isaac Morley, that
the Cambellite meetings were held previous to
the advent of the true faith, which took away
so many of its disciples. She was an intimate
friend of the Prophet Joseph Smith and also of
President Brigham Young, President Heber C.
Kimball, Bishop N K. Whitney and of the
families of these brethren. Bro. and Sister


Billings were among the first Saints who re-
moved to Missouri, whence they were driven
and plundered of all their worldly possessions
by the mobs that arose in that State against
the Saints.  Her husband was in Crooked
River battle and stood beside Apostle David
H. Patten when he fell.  Sister Billings moved
to Nauvoo about the same time as the Prophet
Joseph Smith, and while there was blessed
and set apart to administer to the sick and act
in the capacity of nurse, in which calling she
has been skillful and reliable, until her health
rendered it impossible for her to act the part of
an accoucher longer.  She with her husband
and family emigrated to Utah in 1848, and were
soon after called upon to go and help build up
settlementsin Sanpete County  They returned
to Provo in 1864, and in 1866 Bro. Billings died.
Sister Billings has been a widow for thirteen
years.  Her life has been a most exemplary
one and worthy of imitation.  Her name will
ever be held in honorable remembrance, and
she will be esteemed as a woman of God.

		ERROR vs TRUTH.
	If I felt competent to write anything like
my more gifted sisters, I would often take my
pen to help fill up the pages of our valuable
paper, the WOMAN'S EXPONENT--the name it-

the time when she is beginning to comprehend
her true position side by side with man, her
brother, companion and friend, not master.
That she is a helpmeet, to help on the work of
redemption. The Lord our God has revealed
these things through our worthy brother and
prophet of the 19th century, Joseph Smith.
Then shall we fear man or woman whatsoever
they shall strive to do against this kingdom?
No, verily no!  They little know with whom
they are contending, the Lord God Almighty,

very shame.  Can they not see the Lord is
working with the nations? What turmoils,
trouble and distress do we read of in every
quarter of the globe!

vant, the prophet, that be would make the
wrath of man to praise Him, the rest He will
restrain; and thus we find it the case in every
instance.
 Our so called Christian ladies meeting to-
gether to conduct measures to combat Celestial
Marriage? E en those who sit in darkness and
the shadow of death, marvel when they read,
and become more anxious to find out the why
and wherefore of the case which has become
the theme of so much di[-]cussion, and search
to understand the matter more thoroughly,
and then truth comes to the surface and many
honest in heart will learn the paths of peace,
and walk therein. I rejoice when I read my
paper, that we have be many noble, honest,
good women in our midst, who will beard the

keep on in the good work, sound the trumpet

Zion, we will obey his behests, help fight her
battles, for be it known error is inferior to her

it is error in marriage covenants or in any
principle which relates to our eternal happi-
ness.  Truth must eventually be the victor.

RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE.
WEBER STAKE OF ZION.
	Quarterly conference was held Friday
and Saturday, May 9 and 10, in the Taber-
nacle in Ogden City.  On the stand were
Pres. Jane S. Richards, Counselor Hattie C.
Brown, and Secretary Amella M. Frodsham,
of the Stake Relief Society organization;
local brethren, Apostle F. D. Richards,
Pres D. H. Peery, Coun. L. J. Herrick,
some Bishops and Elder David M. Stuart;
ladies from Salt Lake City, Pres. E. R.
Snow, Mrs. Phebe Woodruff, Mrs. S. M.
Kimball, Mrs. E. B. Wells; also several
presidents of Relief Societies of Weber
Stake.  The Conference opened with sing-
ing and prayer, as is usual.  Pres. J. S.
Richards arose and made a few remarks,
alluded to the illness of the Secretary and
offered an apology for her being seated
while reading the minutes and the reports
of the several branches.  On nomination of
Pres. E. R. Snow, Mrs. E. B. Wells was
appointed Secretary pro tem.  Minutes of
last Conference were read and accepted;
statistical and financial reports from each
ward in the city and the various towns in
the county were also read, showing the good
condition and prosperity of each of these
branch societies, and commendatory of the
sister’s labors temporally and spiritually.
After the business of the morning session
had been concluded, Mrs. E. B. Wells ad-
dressed the assembly; among other things
casually mentioned, she alluded to the op-
portunities afforded to women now, as com-
pared with the past, the advantages to be
derived from the increased facilities open
to women's progression in the various ave-
nues of life; rejoiced that women had the
privilege of speaking in public for each
other's mutual benefit and enlightenment;
also in the privilege of the ballot which
had been given to Mormon women, and the
obligations and responsibilities attending
the use of it, and that it was necessary wo-
men as a class become intelligent upon these
subjects and understand their true position.
Pres. D. H. Peery made a few remarks;
urged the people to come to the meetings
to hear the sisters talk; he had great faith
in woman’s ability to do good, believed in 
the powerful influence of the mother, in
her teachings to the little ones, knew from
experience that it was remembered and was
a sort of talisman through life.  He spoke
of the great work accomplished by Sister
Eliza R. Snow; said probably there were
few men in this Church who had done as
great a work.  He entreated the brethren
to assist the sisters in building a granary in
Ogden to store the wheat in that the sisters
had gathered, and felt it was a mistake in
the brethren not saving more wheat.  He
had more confidence in the integrity of
women than men, and of all men on the
earth he realized the truth of what he said,
for to the good teaching of his mother and
the spiritual influence of his wife he as-
cribed the credit of being a man of God.
He talked of the co-operative manufactories
they were trying to establish to strengthen
the people and help them to become self-
sustaining.
Afternoon session:  A much larger con-
gregation assembled in the afternoon, and
after usual exercises of singing and prayer,
and the reading of a few other reports
omitted in the morning, Pres. E. R. Snow
addressed the people.  She expressed her
gratitude to God for the organization of Re-
lief Societies; she had been connected with 
the firs[-] organization by the Prophet Joseph
in Nauvoo on the 26th of March, 1842, and
remembered he said that it should become
a great power and have great influence, and
it had extended not only throughout Zion,
but into Great Britain.  She realized that if
the sisters honored their calling as daugh-
ters of the Most High God, all would be
fulfilled that had been promised.  She said
it was easy to profess to be a Saint, but not
so easy to live up to every principle; this
required firmness and integrity to principle.
Her heart ached for some who had been
obedient, but were now using all their in-
fluence to destroy; they could only be com-
pared to blind people in the midst of light.
It was necessary for Saints to set their faces
as flint against any indication of evil pow-
ers, or influences.  The Saints are asleep,
but Satan is on the alert, and he seeks to
decoy the innocent.  We should have no
individual interests, we should realize we
are the daughters of God, bought with the
blood of His Son; if we would reflect upon
these things we should do our duty with
all our souls.  Never shrink from a duty,
be it ever so trifling; this will refer to
young and old; it will apply to me.  There
are many duties which require to be per-
formed co-operatively; we can do these
things if we have the Spirit of God with
us, the Holy Ghost, and does any one need
better company?  If we have this there is
no danger of hatred or jealousies, it is long-
suffering, gentleness, patience, meekness,
it will make us powerful helpmeets: some
are, but are all?  In the world some mo-
nopolize to the injury of others, but we
want to come together, consult together and
accomplish great purposes for the good of
all. Miss Snow also made some remarks
upon storing grain, building granaries, the
manufacture of straw, the making of artifi-
cial flowers, and the home interests gener-
ally in Ogden, giving the sisters there, with
Sister Jane S. Richards, as prime leader,
great credit for their energy and persever-
ance in that direction.  There were many
other excellent sentiments advanced.
Mrs. Frodsham said it was by the power
of faith she had come to the meeting; she
wanted to attend to hear the instructions
and meet the sisters from Salt Lake City,
she hoped to be able to attend on the mor-
row; she felt the people were blessed.
Mrs. S.  Kimball related, some inter-
esting episodes of her life, told the story of 
teaching school in her own sitting room,
and how at a certain time she said to her
husband, “If you and the little boys will
go to to the cañon and get some timber we
can build a schoolroom;" and how by a
great deal of faith and exertion on her part
and diligent labor by the husband and 
sons it was accomplished.  She had the
honor, too, of making the first move in
building Relief Society edifices, and told
her experience in getting it finished; since
then they had put an addition to the House
and built a granary,  fire-proof building
with tin roof, cost $500.  She said we are
all surrounded by influences and circum-
stances, and out of them we each of us
work out our own individuality.  She felt
there was a great future opening up before
the young women of to-day, for which the
elder sisters had laid the foundation.
Pres. Jane S. Richards made  few clos-
ing remarks, and showed some home made
gingham, spun, colored and woven by a sis-
ter in Huntsville from cotton raised in the
southern part of the Territory; also a piece
of linen goods and linen thread, the flax
having been grown in Huntsville and
manufactured there.  Conference adjourned
until Saturday morning, May 10. Singing
and benediction.
 Saturday morning session:  The officers of

counselors to good men and mothers of no-
ble children.  Do not associate with men
who will not honor God and his holy ordi-
nances.  It is important that marriages
should be consummated in holy places, and
then the covenants will be recorded in
heaven and the family relations will be
eternal.  She then gave good advice in re-
gard to the reading and studies of the

ladies of the great advantages they enjoyed;
said the more good they could do the more
their faculties would become developed.
Politeness is always comely; cultivate it in

God for wisdom.
 Apostle F. D. Richards said he felt inter-
ested in the movements and teachings of
the sisters.  We are all fond of variety in

try to be content with that which was for

royal line; such are the noble of the earth.

Sister Snow has desired the knowledge of

in some there is no love of the truth.  Meet-

MRS. GAGE'S SPEECH AT THE WASH-
INGTON CONVENTION.

CONCLUDED.

	Let me show you one good that has come
to woman through her ballot in Wyoming.
The payment of men and women teachers
has been adjusted upon the basis of the
same pay for the same qualifications, and
been thus equalized by direct statute for
political power always benefits the parties
holding it. In reading ancient history we
find political power shown by one person,
re-acting upon a whole class. For instance,
the ancient Roman women worshiped the
goddess Viriplaca, whose special mission
was to appease husbands; but after Volum-
nia had shown power to stop the conquer-
ing march of her son Coriolanus, at the very
gates of Rome, the whole sex were treated
with more consideration, and a temple was
erected to Woman's Fortune.
	Milman calls attention to the disrespect
with which Greek women were treated,
because of their total want of political
power, their "political insignificance."
	Now we women of the states know that
the women of the Territories of Wyoming
and Utah are not secure in their political
rights, because the women of the nation
have no political rights. Scarcely a session
of Congress but some political busy-body
introduces into that body a bill to disfran-
chise the women of these territories. We
are tired of it! we are tired of so much at-
tention being paid to the wrongs of men

[Column 2]
and so little to the wrongs of women.
When such a speech as that of Mr. Blaine
is made, we wonder where political consis-
tency has flown.
	Then there was another point of Mr.
Blaine's speech that was bitter to the think-
ing women of the nation. That is, his
pointing out the fact that members of the
House hold their office in defiance of the
rights of representation.
	We know and feel that every Representa-
tive and every Senator, the Hon. Mr. Blaine
himself not excluded, hold their seats in
defiance of woman's rights of representa-
tion. Women have been counted in the
basis of representation, and are not allowed
to exercise political power.
	Mr. Blaine, in his argument, quoted that
portion of the 14th Amendment which
says, "Representatives shall be apportioned
among the several states, &c., &c., but when
the right to vote, &c., is denied to any of 
the 'male' inhabitants, &c.
	Mr. Blaine declared the patent undenia-
ble intent of this provision was, that if any
class of voters were denied, or in any way
abridged their right of suffrage, the class
so denied or abridged should not be counted
in the basis of representation.
	The women of the nation notice this an-
nulling provision, because through it they
are cunningly made to count in the basis of
representation.
	It is an underlying principle of true
statesmanship that constitutions should be
general in their character, securing in a 
general way the rights of all members of 
the nation. But the women of the nation
are indebted to the Republican party for a 
principle in opposition to general rights, by
the introduction of the word "male" three
times in the constitution.
	I shall make use of Mr. Blaine's very lan-
guage, and say that "wherever a feeble pro-
test is made against this injustice," to one-
half the people the taunt comes, "What are
you going to do about it?" and "how do
you propose to help yourselves?"
	As Mr. Evarts so graphically said in the
great Tilton-Beecher trial, "man holds wo-
man in the hollow of his hand to protect or
to crush," so the great political parties seem
to say to the women of the nation, "We
hold your rights in our grasp; we can pro-
tect you or crush you. What are you going
to do about it? and how do you propose to
help yourselves?"
	That they hold power we acknowledge—
that they hold it rightfully we deny.
	Let us look at a few other ways in which
the United States has touched the rights of
women where protection has been secured
her by legislation outside of itself. One in-
stance that has come to my knowledge
since I have been in your city, is in the
case of pensions for colored women. The
United States not only secured the ballot to
the black male citizen outside of state
authority, but it has touched the family re-
lation with its powerful hand. It has as-
sumed that the woman with whom a col-
ored soldier was living at the time of his
death was his wife, notwithstanding he
may have lived for many years in recog-
nized married relations with another wo-
man, and become the father of children by
her during this period. In one case com-
ing under the cognizance of our Washing-
ton woman lawyer, Mrs. Lockwood, a pen-
sion was by United States authority thus
granted to a woman living with such col-
ored soldier at time of his death, although
she had no other claim upon it. This sol-
dier had, during the period of slavery, been
married in his master's house by a regular-
ly ordained clergyman to another woman,

[Column 3]
and by that wife had become the father of 
five or six children. This woman was his
lawful widow, according to state and re-
ligious law. These children were his law-
ful children, according to state and religi-
ous law, but the United States stepped in,
and made of this married woman a thing I 
will not name, and cast her children into
the world with the brand of illegitimacy.
	What a strange thing is law! what a
strange power the United States wields !
and how unjustly it always manages to
touch women, for now comes up Utah with
similar complaint.
	I am not careful to touch the religious
aspects of this Utah question. I do not
care for it only so far as it touches, or is
touched by woman's political rights, al-
though I do know that woman's political
wrongs and her religious wrongs have been
very closely intermingled in the past. I
recall a Papal Bull of Urban II, in the 12th
century which compelled priests to discard
their wives, making of thousands of wo-
men in England, wives who were not wed,
of children, offspring who had no recogniz-
ed fathers.
	We of the National Woman's Suffrage
Association have nothing to do with the 
religious rights of women in Utah, except
in so far as they intermingle with, and
touch woman's political rights. But the
Utah question, which now comes up again,
is not simply a religious question. The 
government is continuously striving to
touch the political rights of the women of
this territory. Its Governor is a United
Statas officer, and in his last report to the
Secretary of the Interior, he so far trans-
cended the duties of his office, as to sug-
gest the disfranchisement of Utah women.
Almost every session of Congress sees some
bill of similar import introduced.
	The general government did not confer
this right, did not secure even the exer-
cise of this right. The territorial legisla-
ture the same as in Wyoming, secured to
women the exercise of the right of suffrage;
the United States according to its own
theory, has nothing at all to do with the suffrage
question. It proposes to disfranchise those
women as a punishment for their religious
belief; it proposes to make social outcasts
of them, as it has already done with the
wives of some of its black soldier voters.
	Looking back through history we find no
act of the Romish Church more vile than
that which compelled its priests to disown
their wives and legitimate children—none
which so utterly demoralized society, and
destroyed its tens of thousands of women.
And although as a body of reformers, I
again say we do not touch religion except
where it, and politics together, infringe up-
ou the rights of women, I do not hesitate
to say for myself individually, that I have
no faith in any form of religion, be it what
it may, Christian, Mohammedan, Buddhist,
that receives revelation only through some
man; or farther than that, I will say, I have
no faith in any form of religion that does
not place man and woman on an exact
equality of religious rights. Two forms of 
religion of the present day which have
risen through women, or as revelations to
her, namely the Shaker and the Spiritual,
do give us equality in religious rights, for
men and women. But I close by again
calling your attention to the inconsistency
of United States laws, and their especial
injustice to women by interference with
those rights secured them by state or terri-
torial laws, as in case of the colored soldier's
wife, as in case the assumption that the


United States had a right to prohibit the
exercise of the suffrage by a woman in
New York, although New York itself did
not interfere: as in case of the virtual pro-
hibition by the United States of jury rights
to the women of Wyoming; in case of the
presumptuous suggestion of the Governor
of Utah that its women should be disfran-
chised; as in case of such bills so often in-
troduced in Congress.
	And Senator Blaine with his eye on the
Presidency—with his primal admission
that the right of suf[-]rage belongs to all citi-
zens of the United States, devoting his
recent great speech entirely to the denial
of black men's rights of citizenship, his
talk about the black men being the "un-
witting instrument" of increasing the po-
litical strength of a party inimical to him;
his wish that the basis of representation
should be lessened in those states where
the black man is deprived of his right to
vote, exhibits an inconsistency that all
thinking women notice. In this speech he
said not a word about lessening the basis of
representation on account of woman's being
deprived of her right to vote.
	The country was wild over Mr. Blaine's
speech. All Washington was agog to hear
it. The gallery and floor of the Senate
Chamber were filled; the diplomats were
present; Democrats and Republicans were
alike disturbed by it; but really it was a 
very small matter in comparison to the de-
nial of representation to women. Woman
is not only denied the right of representa-
tion, but she is actually counted in the basis
of representation, and thus to use the words
of Mr. Blaine, is the "unwitting instru-
ment" of electing men to make laws against
her. She is doubly powerless, because not
only has her own actual representation been
taken from her but a forced representation
is given her by her being counted in the 
basis of representation and she has never
been allowed to select her own representa-
tives. The only analogy I have ever been
able to find, is the old three-fifths representa-
tion of slaves; a system which allowed
masters to use slaves as a means of tighten-
ing their own bonds.
	But to borrow Mr. Blaine's form of ex-
pression, let me tell Mr. Blaine and all poli-
ticians, that I know something of the 
opinion of the women of the nation, and I 
know they intend to be recognized as citi-
zens of the nation secured in the exercise
of all the powers and rights of citizens of 
the nation. If this security has not come
under the 14th Amendment, it must come
under a 16th, for woman intends to possess
"equal personal rights and equal political
privileges with all other citizens." She
asks for nothing outside the power of the 
United States to secure, she asks for noth-
ing outside the duty of the United States 
to secure.
	Politicians may as well look this fact
squarely in the face and become wise after
the wisdom of the world, for in just so far
as they ignore and forget the women of the 
country, in just so far will they themselves
be ignored and forgotton.

HYGIENIC NOTE.

	The Method of Rearing Children.—The Sani-
tarian publishes a letter from Dr. Noyes, who is
in medical charge in the Oneida community, de-
scribing their method of rearing children—in
which they have been pre-eminently successful.
He says:
	Our system of nursing young children, if it can
be called a system, is nothing more than the ordi-
nary one, with some perfcction in detail, which


is made possible by our combined mode of life.
An infant is cared for by the mother in her own
room during the nursing period, and for some
time longer. The nursing period varies from
eight months to a year or fifteen months, accord-
ing as the season permits. We do not think it
advisable to nurse longer than a year.
	When a child begins to drink we sometimes
begin giving it oatmeal, boiled two hours and
strained through a fine cloth, or pearl barley, with
the cow’s milk, which is generally from a selected
cow. Whether it takes these preparations or not,
we get it as soon as we can on a diet containing
more or less coarse unbolted wheat flour, and as
soon as possible we come down to three meals a
day, with nothing between meals. We give our
children ripe fruit freely after they havs learned
to eat solid food, and avoid the habitual use of
the bland, starchy food, arrowroot, &c., as much
as possible. They eat no meat until five or six
years old.
	At about fifteen months old the mothers put
their children into a nursery during the day, re-
taining them nights for some time longer. In
this nursery they are cared for by experienced
nurses; and at about four years they enter the
children’s department. The rooms the children
occupy have a south exposure, plenty of light, no
trees within one hundred feet. Our house-
drainage is as good as we can devise, and the
entry of sewer-gas in any form is vigilantly guard-
ed against. Slop closets are disenfected every
morning after using, and locked the remainder of
the day, being connected with a, draught-chimney.
In a word, we aim to give them pure air, plenty
of life in the open air, pure food of a plain quality
with very little fine flour, with little or no medi-
cine.

rescues the day from idleness,and he that is
never idle will not often be vicious.
