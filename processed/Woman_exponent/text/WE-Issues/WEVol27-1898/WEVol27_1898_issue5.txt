CHILDHOOD.

Airy, fairy, little lass,
Tripping through the dewy grass,
Picking flowers from the mass,
Lying round you as you pass.
Softly, sweetly trill a lay,
Waking music all the way;
Forest echoes round you play,
Ringing all the live-long day.
Lightly, smoothly speed along,
Quickly as the agi e fawn,
Leaving naught behind whereon
There can smile another dawn.
Happy elfin careful tread,
Where the forest joys are fled,
And the winding river’s bed,
To the sea its waters [-]ed.
Smiling, careless lassie dear,
Changes in your path appear,
Making all the world seem drear,
Filling your blithe heart with fear.
Noisy, turbid waters ring,
Foaming spray they outward fli g,
Fleet as birds upon the wing,
While they angry sonnets sing.
Strongly, firmly weave a strand,
Binding like an iron band,
Where the waters meet the land,
Grasping with a mighty hand.
Fleeting fading, oh, how fast,
Glides the present to the past;
Now your life its spring has passed,
And in summer’s mold is cast.
Forest whispers nevermore,
Breathe the song they did before;
Childhood dream[-] for thee are o’er,
Woman’s scepter’s at your door.

MADAME MOUNTFORD’S LETTER.

	You ask what I am doing here. Trying
to rest, is my reply; but it is not an idle
rest, but of that kind that says “a change
of work is as good as a rest,” hence I have
only changed my work. Though I am not
lecturing to thousands in halls as is my
custom to do, from afar I am daily coming
in contact with the hearts of people of var-

[Column 2]
ious religions, races and complexions, liter-
ally speaking, small and great, rich and poor,
free and bond—so that every day my recep-
tion room is crowded with people from
every station in life, who come to call upon
me and have a chat after my long absence.
Religion predominates everything in this
land, and as each race or organization here
in Jerusalem stands alone, proud and un-
bending with a strong disposition to des-
pise all but one’s own creed, it is very dif-
ficult for the members of these conflicting
creeds to associate much together, hence my
work is no small one in having them treat
each other in the most cordial manner in
my drawing room. I am glad to say that
my personality among them seems to have
the effect of removing all feelings of rancour
that might exist between them religiously,
and we have delightful conversations and
each goes away glad to have met the other
and surprised to find that there is so much
good outside of one’s own immediate circle.
I am, therefore, kept so busy all day and
even in the night hours, that I get very
little time for correspondence. My life here
is just as busy as when I am at work in
England or the United States, only that it
is of a different nature in its detail work. I
must have been born under a perpetual mo-
tion state, I am always on the move and the
day is never too long for me but rather flies
too fast.
	I am glad to hear that you are going to
interest your girls in mission work, the
glorious time is at hand when there will be
no difference between male and female, all
are one in Jesus Christ. “Say ye to the
daughters of Zion: Behold thy salvation
cometh.’ ”
	We get very little news here regarding
the war and I hope ere this reaches you
peace will have been declared. What is
my opinion of war? that it is one of the
greatest curses on earth. I hate the very
idea of it. War brings every evil in its
trail, it arouses the worst passions of man
to shed man’s blood. To me the jubilation
aroused by regiments going to battle, and
the enthusiasm and plaudits of the multi-
tude in cheering their soldiers is still a relic
of barbarism in humanity. There is some-
thing in my nature that revolts at this glee
at the idea of shedding human gore—it is
like the savages dancing with delight at the
sight of the blood of their captives. To
slay the temple of the living God in any
shape, form or manner is to me a crime, and
the thirst for human blood once aroused in
a country is not so easily quenched, especi-
ally among the vicious and depraved.
Everything beautiful in literature and art
gets a set back in times of war; peaceful
pursuits are stifled—the land is filled with
orphans and widows who are left to fight
the battle of life single handed, and they in
their turn become degraded and slaves to
those who pretend to fight for their freedom.
I am more than surprised that so many
American women advocated this war, I
thought they would rise up as one man and
demand peace. What good is our educa-
tion if we cannot settle matters by arbitra-

[Column 3]
tion? Oh, the hideousness of war in these
civilized days! In freeing poor Cuba how
many noble American women and children
will become slaves in the factories and
dwell in the slums of our large cities. But
enough! You may not have the same
views as myself on the subject. To me
war is an abomination. Perhaps it may do
Cuba good, but it is sure to bring to Amer-
ica much that is bad. The war taxes are
diminishing the strength of the people, for
when bread and all eatables become higher
in price the poor have to suffer and eat less
and hence poverty and crime are grafted on
a country. God save America. I think
her intentions were good—but has she alto-
gether gone into this war for unselfish
reasons, simply and solely to free Cuba?
The people yes! but how about the politi-
cians that always sway the people?
	Oh for the true Christ spirit! Oh for the
the peace on earth!
	But all has to b[-] until righteousness shall
come upon the earth. God bless you all. I
am one and part of your country. I love
your stars and stripes, I am a loyal Ameri-
can! but I love to see justice done to every
human being, and I hate war.
Yours truly
L. M. VON F. MOUNTFORD.
Jerusalem.

my imperfections. I don’t know that there

united is my prayer."

THE MASON AND THE STONE.

BY E. K.
The stone lies foursquare in its place,
Cold and black and bare of grace,
The sun shines on the barren square
And wakes no life or beauty there.
Bitter the b[-]ow of steel on stone,
Bitter the granite’s grating groan:
The mason’s cu[-]ning hand is set
Upon the stone to smite and fret;
And deeper as the chisel goes
The beauty daily grows and grows,
For lights and shades begin to fall
About the carving on the wall,
And angel-wings and faces leap
Obedient to the chisel’s sweep;
Flowers and fruits of paradise
Beneath the fretful hammer rise,
Until the house of God has grown
More worthy through that carven stone.
Thou, too, great Mason hast forewilled
That all the stones whereof Thou build
Thy house not made with hands shall so
In beauty as in suffering grow.
The heart to which Thy tools are laid
Shall never lack of sorrow’s shade,
That from that background there may shine
Outlines of beauty most divine.
—Speaker.

CLARA BARTON TELLS OF THE
RED CROSS.

	Clara Barton, president of the National
Red Cross; George Kennan, first vice-
president and D. L. Cobb counsel, appeared
before the Executive board of the American
National Red Cross Relief committee
yesterday afternoon in the Chamber of
Commerce to answer questions concerning
the standing of the organization they rep-
resent. The meeting was behind closed doors.
	Doubts heretofore expressed by members
of the committee, notably by Vice-Chairman
E. Orr, as to the exact status of the Red
Cross, were according to Secretary John P.
Faure, completely dissipated by Miss Barton
and her associates. Mr. Orr was not pres-
ent, but William G. Low appeared as his
proxy.
	A week ago two members of the National
Red Cross asserted that the members of the
association were ten in number. Miss Barton
quoted it as a society of over thirty, whose
meetings were merely for work in the field,
"entirely unhampered by elective commit-
tees." This hitherto regarded as an ob-
jection was apparently sanctioned by the
Relief committee.
	"Miss Barton's telling address," said Mr.
Faure, "descriptive of Red Cross work car-
ried every man off his feet. Many wept."
	Mr. Kennan spoke especially of the busi-
ness machinery of the Red Cross—its care-
fulness of distribution, economy of adminis-
tration and completeness of record of its
doings.
	The committee was entirely satisfied and
commended the methods and practice
of the Red Cross as described by Miss Bar-
ton and the others. The assertion that
Miss Barton had appeared before the Relief
Committee to vindicate the Red Cross is in-
dignantly scouted, and the further announce-
ment that a special staff would be appointed
from its ranks to accompany her on her re-
turn journey southward was also denied.
For herself Miss Barton said:
	"The committee and I have perfectly
understood each other. All actions of the

[Column 2]
National Red Cross have been fully endorsed.
I am glad of the opportunity to thank the
press of New York for its attitude toward
me and the organization I represent."
	Mr. Faure reported a $2,500 contribu-
tion from August Belmont & Co., and other
donations amounting to $641.
	The New York Ambulance Red Cross
Auxiliary at the home of Mrs. W. C. Cowles,
No. 689 Madison avenue, reported yesterday
$2,695 as the total recent collections, mak-
ing a total of $46,121.
	Eighty thousand dollars was reported as
the aggregate sum collected by the various
auxiliaries of the Red Cross at yesterday's
meeting of the Woman's Committee on
Auxiliaries at the home of Mrs. Butler Dun-
can, No. 1 Fifth avenue.
	The General Committee, which serves as
a clearing house for all the auxiliaries, re-
ceived representatives from sixteen of the
twenty-four already formed, with a detailed
account of materials and funds on hand.
Each auxiliary will turn over a certain
amount of linen, hardware, hospital equip-
ment, cots, etc., that the various associa-
tions have undertaken to furnish. The
new organizations are:
	No. 20—West Side Auxiliary, under
Mrs. Charles R. Shaw, No. 130 West
Seventy-fifth street.
	No. 21—Hazleton Pa. Auxiliary.
	No. 22—Westchester Land and Sea Aux-
iliary, under Mrs. Frank K. Hunter.
	No. 23—Staten Island Auxiliary, under
Mrs. May Myrick.
	No. 24—Princeton Auxiliary,  under
Mrs. James Morgan, Mrs. W. E. D. Scott
and Mrs. Laurence Hutton.


AN APPRECIATIVE LETTER.

Downer’s Grove, Ill.,
May 15, 1898.
Mrs. E. B. Wells:
	Dear Sister—If you had received the
materialized expression of my thought in
writing as many times as I have wished I
could send, you would realize my apprecia-
tion of your courtesy in sending me the
“Woman’s Exponent.” I always find
something of interest in it, and I enjoy the
reports which disclose the fraternal spirit of
your social institutions. In April 15th
issue I was much pleased with the report
of Julia A. Pace “How to Build a Granary
on Nothing.” Could we have the same
co-operative spirit actualized in our econ-
omic systems, we should have no unem-
ployed, no tramps, no children in factories,
no billions of interest bearing debt, no ten-
ant farmers in mortgaged houses. Nothing
is needed in this country of rich resources
but the working together for the good of
all, and to fill the purpose for which our
Constitution was adopted, “to establish
justice and promote the general welfare.”
We should then banish poverty and bring
peace and good will. I rejoice that such a
spirit of actualization of co-operation is any-
where in operation to any degree. The
colony of Ruskin, Tenn., seems to promise
successful realizations of this principle
which alone can solve the problem of civili-
zation. I am looking for a reconstruction
of our system, a federation of the nations
of the earth, a people’s government in this
country, and, when the present war is
closed, an end of war on this planet.
	What may arise in connection with this
conflict cannot be foreseen, but that the


time is near when fighting on the battle-
field or in industry aiid trade will cease, I
have no doubt. Humanity must learn that
“to love one another” is not only true
Christianity, true religion, but true states-
manship and true economics.
	I am to spend the summer in this delight-
ful suburb. Thanking you for sending me
the “Exponent” I am
Fraternally yours,
LUCINDA B. CHANDLER.


U. W. P. C.

	The regular meeting of the U. W. P. C.
was held in the "Exponent" office Tuesday
evening, May 31. The president being ab-
sent the vice-president, Mrs. R. H. Doolan,
presided. Opening prayer Mrs. Phebe C.
Young. The secretary called the roll, re-
sponses being given with original patriotic
sentiments. Mrs. E. B. Wells proposed
the name of Mrs. Kate B. Anderson as a
member, and she was unanimously elected.
Under unfinished business the Curfew law
was discussed with no definite result. The
resignation of Mrs. Miller as alternate to
Mrs. Brown was accepted and Mrs. Rhodes
was appointed in her place.
	Mrs. Luella Rhodes read a carefully pre-
pared historical paper on the "Royal Fam-
ily of Spain and the present Generals."
Mrs. M. J. Lambert kept the members
deeply interested in her eloquent paper on
“Current Events,” including letters from
women’s organizations concerning the war,
and a mother’s letter whose son was killed
on the Maine. General discussion of papers
followed.
	On motion of Mrs. Schiller the meeting
adjourned. Closing prayer by President R.
B. Pratt, after which volunteer refreshments
were thoroughly enjoyed by all present.
AIMIE C. SCHILLER, Asst. Sec.


WOMEN IN ART.

	Rosa Bonheur is over seventy-nine years
old. She is as active as ever, and still
spends five hours a day perched upon the
substantial ladder which has become the
most historically valuable object in her
studio. With its help all her most famous
work has been achieved, from the "Horse
Fair" to the picture which she justly con-
siders her magnum opus, "Horses Thresh-
ing out the Corn." Mlle. Bonheur has
commissioned Miss Anna Klumpke of Bos-
ton to paint her portrait, and has invited
Miss Klumpke to be her guest this summer
while the work is being done. This is an
exceptional honor conferred by the greatest
woman artist upon one of the most gifted
of American women artists. Miss Klumpke
studied under Robert Fleury, Bouguereau,
Villefroy and Lefebvre, and she has borne
off numerous honors and medals—a third
medal from the Salon, the silver medal at
Versailles, and a gold medal from Philadel-
phia. The Historical Society of Albany,
N. Y., invited Miss Klumpke to hold an
exhibition in their galleries, and during
the last week of April a collection was ex-
hibited of some forty oil paintings and past-
els by Miss Klumpke, loaned by their
owner.
—Ex.

THINGS OF INTEREST.

	The daily events now transpiring are full
of interest, so much so that one watches the
bulletins hourly as the dispatches come in,
and an ever restless feeling pervades the
atmosphere about and around, and men ask
each other what the next move will be in
the semi-tragic drama of occurrences.
	Although Utah is remote from seaports or
river navigation, yet the same general feel-
ing prevails in regard to naval expeditions

UTAH WOMEN AND NEWSPAPERS.

	This is the first issue of a new volume,
the "Exponent" having entered upon its
twenty-seventh year. Let us ardently hope
that it may be a more prosperous one than
the last few have been, and that the wo-
men of Utah in whose interest it is pub-
lished and particularly the women of the
Church, may be more zealous in sustaining
their own official organ, and that it may
continue to be a source of encouragement to
the organizations of women throughout this
and other lands.
	In times such as are now upon us, when
history is being made so rapidly and one
momentous event after another follow in
such quick succession, it behooves all wo-
men to be alive to the general good and to
know in what way best to serve each other
and all mankind.
	The newspapers of women throughout
the land are indeed most helpful in each
locality where they are published especially,
and Utah and Salt Lake City should not be
behind in this respect. Utah was the first
western territory or state to establish a wo-
man’s paper except Oregon, and has done
much in the way of political freedom for
women, and her women have not been be-
hind other states in the West intellectually,
notwithstanding the hardships and priva-
tions of pioneer life in a new country.
The women of Utah have acquit-
ted themselves creditably in this respect, and
have shown a courage almost unsurpassed
in the obstacles they have had to surmount.
	In philanthropic work they are second to
none; their largest and most flourishing or-
ganization of this kind, the National Wo-
man’s Relief Society, is known far and wide,
at home in our own country and abroad in
other lands, as one of the most successful
and enterprising organizations of women en-
gaged in philanthropic work for the better-
ment of humanity. We fear the members
do not in many instances appreciate the
greatness of and the benefits to be derived
from this important Society.
	Such great societies need printed instruc-
tions regularly, such as come through an
organ of their own, and should take a pride
in maintaining such a periodical for reliable
information and historical references in re-
gard to matters pertaining to the growth
and advance of the Society as a whole
wherever the locality may be, that all may
be aware of conditions and needs and thus
by interchange of ideas through the paper
as well as in various other ways be mutu-
ally helpful.


Our sisters here and elsewhere are invited
and urged to write for the “Exponent,”
and to cultivate whatever talent they may
have in this line, thus by a few words per-
haps blessing and comforting others,
sowing seed broadcast, casting bread upon
the waters, (perchance the bread of life)
that may feed spiritually those in direst need
of comfort, whose lives may be darkened by
sorrow and adversity.
	The motto of the N. W. R. S. is “Charity
never faileth,” and this spirit should per-
meate throughout the organization, for it is
the spirit of the blesssd Savior who left  to
the world the Golden Rule which if applied
would make us all better and truer followers
of Him who said: “If you love me feed my
sheep.”

WELCOME TO MISSIONARIES.

Levin, New Zealand,
April 11, 1898.
My Dear Mother:
	Here we are in this Nick ’o the woods
with a little time for writing home mail,
and first that goes to mother and Mother B.
the rest I will write to as I can, but to these
two I must say a word you know.
	There is so much to say that I hardly
know where to begin, but first I am well,
we having arrived at Wellington Sunday,
April 3, at 11 o’clock a. m., all fine and
glad to get ashore. Our voyage from Suva
Fiji was not quite so pleasant as from
Honolulu, having met a nasty gale for .two
days, but after that it was pleasant again
and our hearts were made glad on Saturday
when the East Cape of New Zealand came
in sight. That meant that our sea voyage
was nearing an end and that soon we could
be actively engaged in the work for which
we have come forth. It was interesting,
too, to see the glad smile on Hirini’s
countenance as he looked at the land so
familiar, so dear to his memory. Although
he now looks upon Salt Lake City as his
home, his Zion, still there must naturally
be a strong emotional sensation as he looks
upon the land of his fathers and his birth
and the experiences of a long life. Then
of his friends whom he was to meet. No
wonder a smile lit up his dear old
countenance. But I find that the sea trip
and the excitement of meeting have been
quite a tax on him, and we will have to
husband his strength and ask the Lord to
bless him for the labors and travels before
him.
	On our arrival at Wellington I was hand-
ed a letter from Elder Richards stating that
conference had commenced that morning at
Popawai, Wairarapa, and for us to proceed
there as soon as convenient. Being Sunday
no trains were running, only excursion
boats and bicyles, so we had to content our-
selves by waiting till morning. In the
meantime, however, we got our trunks
through the Custom House, but not with-
out some difficulty, as one brother very
providently inclined, had three pairs of new
shoes in his trunk, which had never been
worn and of which I knew nothing. These
were held, of course, also two packages of
underclothing on which we had to pay
some duty.
	Next morning at 7:15 we had all our
baggage transferred and were on our way to
the “hui tau” (annual conference,) where
we arrived at a little before noon. I had
wired President Richards and we were met
at the train with sufficient conveyances to
take us all over. Brother W. C. Castleton
was there with a one-horse buggy for me,
and I very much enjoyed the three mile
drive to the Maori fra with him. And
what a royal reception! About a fourth of
a mile from the fra we found a brass band,
(Maori) waiting for us which formed in
marching order and we were requested to
dismount and fall in which we did, two
and two, there being eight of us. Thus
we were escorted under fine music and the
Queen’s own flag, (God save the Queen.)
A little further we were met by a party

[Column 2]
who gave us a reception haka (dance) when
the band fell to the rear, the dancing com-
pany falling back gradually till the space in
front of the large building was reached.
Then the dancing party fell out and a “tan-
gi” commenced. This part I felt it was not
necessary for us all to endure, so as soon as
we could politely do it we left Hirini to re-
ceive that attention and we broke ranks
and fell upon the necks of the twenty-eight
Utah Elders assembled. You see our com-
pany swelled the number to thirty-five, and
I just can assure you we did have a good
time.
	Our arrival rather broke into the conduct
of conference and the afternoon was given
up to welcome speeches, regular meetings
being resumed again at 7 p. m., followed
by three meetings each day, Tuesday and
Wednesday. On Thursday the chief of
the village, an outsider, invited us to re-
main for a holiday and feast at his expense.
We could, of course, not do less than ac-
cept after his kind treatment of us in assist-
ing to entertain our conference, we being
on his ground. Some of the boys got up a
matched game of baseball, and when Elder
Richards and I walked out a little later
they insisted upon my acting as umpire.
Well, so long as it was a non-running posi-
tion I accepted, and had the thanks of the
winning side.
	The big dinner occurred at 5:30 p. m.,
and I can just tell you it was a spread.
About two hundred sat down to tables
loaded with good things. Roast beef, pork
and mutton, fish, fowl and potatoes, first
course; then plum pudding and pastry
galore.
	Elders Richards, Hirini, and yours truly.
occupied the places of honor at a table with
the chief and a friend. The decorations
were the whole tops of trees, ferns and
large bouquets; and the guests did full just-
ice to the viands prepared. After supper
and evening prayers the tables were cleared
out and a dance indulged in by the Maories.
In this we did not participate, as the mis-
sion statutes rule the Elders out of such
intimacy with the opposite sex. We could
only sit and look on. The music was
furnished by the Maori band, and was
really good. Some of our Elders varied
the amusements with a sort of an orchestral
program, there being a violin, mandolin,
claironet, two harmonicas and a piano.
	Elder Richards and I left early going to
the room above, which was the Elders’
quarters, attending to other and more im-
portant matters. About 1:30, just as Brother
Richards was getting into bed, the call for
“after the ball supper” was made, and I
not having undressed was deputized to rep-
resent the president. Thus it was past 2
a. m. when I retired, and some of us did
not turn in till 4 or 5 o'clock, and we were
out again at 5:30 getting horses up and
ready for those who came that way, and
the dispersing commenced. Many of the
Maories took the early trains and the place
began to look lonesome. But we have had
a glorious good time; have had some most
excellent meetings, and I can say for my-
self I enjoyed it immensely. But we were
very busy. Between meetings and at night
planning and arranging appointments,
answering questions, instructing the Elders,
so that we never got to sleep till 1:30, and
out at 5:30 or 6 in the morning. Also I
had little sleep the two nights before reach-
ing there and when we left on Friday hold-
ing a meeting with Europeans in Carterton,


I was just struggling to keep awake. But
Saturday night and last night we had a
chance to catch up, and are now ready for
work again.
	It was really more of a pleasure even than
I had anticipated to meet old Maori friends
again. To remember and be remembered
so kindly was very nice. I have not yet
seen any of my Waiapu district people, as
they did not get down to conference, it is a
long way for them. But I am glad to [-]ay
that our next April conference will be held
up there. Also I expect to take in the dis-
trict on my way to Auckland, spending
most of the month of May in that and ad-
joining districts, reaching Auckland early
in June, with conferences appointed from
that on to September and October, so you
see I shall be busy. We are now on our
way to the Mabia, making a final trip for
Elder Richards. The Mabia is Hirini’s
old home, and we desired to make it con-
venient to take him up there early. We
expect to go on tomorrow to Napiere.
	In all probability Elder Richards with
W. C. Castleton, clerk of the mission, and
three other Elders will leave Wellington on
April 28th on the same boat on which we
came out. They will reach home about
May 25th and you may expect a call from
them.
	Now mother, do please take care of your-
self. I am looking for home mail due in
Auckland tomorrow. This should leave
Auckland on the 16th. I am quite anxious
to hear how matters have gone on at home.
But we are all in the hands of the Lord,
and we must do the best we can leaving
the rest to Him.
	Please remember me to all my friends.
Love to Lizzie and Charlie and all the
folks. Send this over to Mother Burton to
read as I must write her but briefly this
time.
	The News is not furnished the mission,
so it you will get the Semi-Weekly and
send me in one bundle each San Francisco
mail, I will try and arrange for the pay of
same. With love I am, your son
EZRA T. STEVENSON.

BIOGRAPHICAL SKETCH.

PATTY SESSIONS.
	Patty Sessions was born in Bethel, Ox-
ford County, Maine, 1795, was married to
David Sessions, June 28, 1812, and settled
in the adjoining town of Newry.
	She was a member of the Methodist
church for seventeen years, and was bap-
tized into the Church of Jesus Christ of
Latter-day Saints in 1834. She was the
mother of eight children and survived them
all but two; she has fifty-three grandchil-
dren, one hundred and thirty-seven great
grandchildren and twenty-two great great
grandchildren, making in all two hundred
and fourteen posterity.
	They sold their property and left their
native land in June 1836, to share in the
troubles and persecutions of the Saints.
They traveled two thousand miles, camp-
ing out all the way, to Caldwell Co., Mis-
souri, they then purchased land and re-
m[--]ned in that place until 1838, when they
were driven out, they then went to Han-
cock Co. Illinois.
	They went to Nauvoo in an early day
and assisted in building up the city and also
the Temple. She was present at the meet-
ing when the Prophet Joseph Smith or-

[Column 2]
ganized the Relief Society; she joined the
Society then and remained an active mem-
ber as long as her health would permit.
	While at Nauvoo she was set apart by
Brigham Young and Heber C. Kimball as
a doctor for women. Leaving Nauvoo she
went to Winter Quarters where she was
very efficient in waiting upon the sick,
especially the women. In 1847 she left
that place for Salt Lake City, driving an ox
team across the plains, where she arrived
on Sep. 26, 1847.
	Soon after she arrived in Salt Lake there
was an organization formed which was
called the Council of Health. Mother
Angell was president, Sister Sessions was
one of her counselors, and she acted in that
capacity as long as the society lasted. Dr.
Willard Richards, counselor to Brigham
Young, took a great interest in this Coun-
cil of Health.
	She was the president of a benevolent
society which was organized on June 10,
1854 for the purpose of clothing the Indian
women and children.
	She acted as a teacher in the Relief Soci-
ety from about the first of its organization
in the 16th ward until she left there and
moved to Bountiful in 1872, she there
opened her home for the benefit of the Soci-
ety and the teachers held their meetings in
her house for quite awhile.
	In 1883 she erected a school house for the
benefit of her grandchildren and the poor
of the ward who were not able to pay their
schooling. She left money enough in the
Parent Co-op so that the dividend will run
the school continuously ten months in the
year and pay all the expenses pertaining to
it. She took a great interest in the school
as long as she was able to take interest in
anything.
	She has donated very liberally to the
building of the Temples, she put eleven
hundred dollars in the Logan Temple and
gave a very great deal to the Salt Lake and
St. George Temples. She also donated
very liberally to the Perpetual Emigration
Fund, as much as two hundred dollars at a
time.
	She died a true and faithful Latter-day
Saint on Dec. 14, 1892, aged 97 years.
“Blessed are the dead who die in the Lord.”

ROYAL FAMILY OF SPAIN.

READ AT THE U. W. P. C., MAY 31, 1898.
	When we catch our first glimpse of the
Mediterranean about 1500 B.C., it is dot
ted with the sails of the Phoenician navi-
gators. They crossed the Mediterranean
in every direction distributing the manu-
factures of Asia.
	The longest voyages that they made were
to procure tin. which was in great demand
for the manufacture of “Bronze.” The near-
est regions where this metal was found was
the Caucasas on the eastern shore of the
Euxine (now called the Black sea.) To-
wards the close of the 11th century B. C.,
the Grecians were jealous and closed the
Ægean sea against these navigators. The
Phoenicians pushed out into the western
Mediterranean and opened the tin mines of
the Herian peninsula (now Spain.) A
hasty sketch is all I can give of the general
features of this period upon which we are-
now entering.
	The Phoenicians established naval sta-
tions and trading posts. Articles of Phoeni-

[Column 2]
cian workmanship are found in the earliest
tombs of the Greeks and Etruscans. The
Phoenician navigators were enterprising.
They built the Hellespont bridge for Xerxes,
they furnished ships for the King of Baby-
lon for naval operations, and they circum-
navigated Africa, an undertaking remark-
able considering the age, 604 B. C., 2’000
years before the Portugese navigator,
Magellan.
	The political strifes between 1500 B.C. to
133 B. C., when Spain became a Roman
province are too numerous to mention.
Nearly eight hundred years elapsed when
Alaric held his sword over Rome and threat-
ened its destruction if his demands were
not granted. Alaric was obliged to force
his way into the city, and Rome paid the
penalty of not yielding to him.
	For several years after the Saracen in-
vasion Spain was broken off into a number
of small but independent states. The king-
dom of Asturia, or Leon, had been growing
in power and in 758 A. D. a second king-
dom was founded in Sobrave. Thirty-six
years afterwards was founded the third
Christian kingdom, that of Navarre, and in
933 another independent monarchy was
formed in Castile and soon became the
most powerful of the Spanish states. The
kingdom of Arragon was the last founded.
	In 1035 A. D. there was a division in
Castile and Arragon, and they were not
united until the marriage of Ferdinand
and Isabella. King John II of Arragon
was greatly pleased at this match; he was
deeply sensible of the importance of consoli-
dating the monarchies of Spain under one
head. The marriage articles were signed
on the 7th of January, 1469, but the cere-
mony was not performed until the 19th of
October, 1469. So great was the poverty
of the parties that it was found necessary to
borrow money to defray the expenses of
the marriage.
	There were five children born to Ferdinand
and Isabella. The eldest daughter, Isa-
bella, was married to Alonzo, the heir to
the crown of Portugal, in 1490. Prince
John, heir to the Spanish crown, married
Margaret, daughter of Emperor Maxmillian.
Johanna married Phillip, son of Maxmillian.
Donna Catalina the youngest daughter,
married Arthur, Prince of Wales, (after-
ward to his brother, Henry VIII.) Prince
John married in April and in October of
the same year was stricken down with
fever and died. The prince dying without
an heir the crown of Castile and Arragon
descended to the son of Isabella of Portugal
who was confirmed by the Cortez of Castile.
Thus the three crowns, Castile, Arragon
and Portugal were suspended over one
head. This young heir died when about
two years old. Isabella of Castile died in
1506, and before she died she exacted a
promise confirmed by an oath from Ferdin-
and (of whom she was always jealous) that
he would not marry again. The death of
Isabella was the signal for disorder which
the genius and authority of Ferdinand were
unable to suppress. While the queen’s re-
mains were scarcely cold Ferdinand took
the usual measures for announcing the suc-
cession and renounced the crown of Castile in
favor of Joanna and Phillip, and to their
eldest son, Charles I. of Spain, on the
death of his mother. The reckless extra-
vagance and licentiousness of Phillip so
troubled the queen that she grew morbid
and finally her malady became incurable.
Phillip long since ceased to treat her with
[-]ffection of even decency, and fain would


[Column 1]
have persuaded the Cortez to authorize her
confinement as a person of disordered intel-
lect. Phillip’s reign was short as he died
on the 25th of September, 1506. Ferdinand
held the reins of government for Joanna;
finally his desire for a son to succeed him
was so great that shortly after Isabella’s
death he married Germaine, daughter of his
sister, (Leanora the queen of Navarre,)
His wish had nearly been realized, Queen
Germaine was delivered of a son in March
1509, but the infant only lived a few hours.
Ferdinand began to be impatient and mor-
bid and his health declined so rapidly that
it was evident that his days were numbered.
He passed the last month of his life at the
country seat of the Duke of Alva, and died
in January, 1516.
	By the death of his grandfather, (Ferdin-
and) Charles I. took possession of the throne
of Spain by the title of Charles V. On the
death of his paternal grandfather (Max-
millian) he was elected Emperor of Ger-
many. Under the reign of Charles V.
the nation continued to advance higher and
higher and he seemed to take everything
before him, until in 1552 he was obliged to
flee before Duke Maurice of Saxony. The
season of the most brilliant results is not
that of the greatest national prosperity, the
boasted reign was dearly purchased by the 
decline of industry and the loss of liberty.
Charles V. celebrated his own obsequies,
his domestics marched in funeral procession
with black tapers, he followed in his
shroud and was laid in his coffin with great
solemnity. The length of the ceremony or
the impression which this left on his mind
so affected him that he resigned his crown
and retired to a monastery and died in
1558.
	Phillip II. was the son of Charles V. and
Isabella of Portugal, he was an accom-
plished linguist and mathematician and a
connoisseur in architecture and fine arts.
In 1554 he married Mary Tudor, Queen of
England, daughter of Henry VIII. and
Catherine of Arragon. After Mary died he
made an attempt to gain the hand of Eliza-
beth but was unsuccessful, and finally mar-
ried Isabella of France. His life  was no-
toriously wicked and licentious, his suffer-
ings were terrible, his body was entirely
covered with ulcers; his death must have
been a great relief to him and all around
him.
	In 1598 on the death of his father Phillip
III. came to the throne, he was married to
Margaret of Austria, his second wife was
Isabella of France. His reign of twenty-
three years was chiefly noted for the cruel
expulsion of the Moors from all parts of
Spain where they had sought refuge during
the last reign. In 1609, 600,000 individuals
were forcibly driven from their homes and
landed on the shores of Africa, there to be
treated even worse by the most cruel and
perfidious of the human family.
	Phillip IV., son of Phillip III. and Isa-
bella of France, began to reign in 1621. In
1615 he married Elizabeth of France, his
second wife was Marie Anne of Austria.
He ascended the throne when but seven-
teen, he was extravagant and dissipated,
his reign was most unfortunate, he was at
war with France, Holland, Sweden and
England. In 1640 Portugal freed herself
from Spanish yoke. Since the days of
Roderick the Goth, a more disastrous reign
than his had not darkened the annals of
Spain.
	Charles II., son of Phillip IV. and Marie

[Column 2]
of Austria was feeble in body and almost an
imbecile in mind. His first wife was Louise,
niece of Louis XIV., and his second wife
was Mary Anne of Newbery. Charles was
the last of the Austrian dynasty. Glorious
as the condition of Spain was under the
earlier sovereigns it was brought now to the
verge of ruin. For nearly three centuries
it had held the foremost rank in Europe as
a military nation; now it began to decline
both in influence and glory.
	Charles II. was childless and bequeathed
his throne to Phillip V., Duke of Anjou.
Phillip V. was the grandson of Charles II.
half-sister. He was the second son of
Louis the Dauphin of France and Mary
Anne of Bavara. He assumed the title of
King of Spain by virtue of the will of
Charles II. His throne was contested by
the House of Austria in favor of Arch Duke
Charles. Phillip was the first of the Bour-
bon dynasty who occupied the throne of
Spain, and this was the occasion of the wars
of the Spanish succession. Phillip was
married twice, first to Princess Marie Louise
Gabrielle of Savoy, his second wife was
Elisabeth Farnese of Parma. Phillip be-
came a victim to melancholia and abdicated
in favor of his son Louis. Louis died a few
months after of small pox, and Phillip was
obliged to take the reins of government,
In 1731 Phillip invaded Naples and took
possession of that kingdom and gave it to
his son Don Carlos.
	Phillip V. was succeeded by his son
Ferdinand VI., surnamed the “Wise.” He
ascended the throne in 1746. Ferdinand
did not share his ambitious step-mother’s
desire for conquest in Italy, and withdrew
from war, recalling all his armies so quick-
ly from Italy that all the northern part fell
into the hands of the Austrians, who treat-
ed the people with the greatest inhumanity
and even attempted to harness them to
their heavy-artillery. During the thirteen
years of his reign he was one of the most
just and humane of the monarchs who ruled
over Spain. He promoted the welfare of
his country, reorganized the navy, en-
couraged and established manufactures,
and by his judicial and political conduct
placed his brother on the throne of Naples.
Ferdinand married Magdalene Theresa of
Portugal. He died in 1759. 
	Ferdinand left no heir so the throne
descended to his brother, Charles IV. of
Naples, and Charles III. of Spain. Charles
was a very good man, for promoting educa-
tion, reforming and expelling the Jesuits in
1767. Before this time England became
alarmed at the naval preparations of Spain
and declared war in 1762, and took Havana
in Cuba and Manilla in the East Indies.
After several years peace was declared and
Havana was restored.
	Charles III. died in 1788, and was suc-
ceeded by his second son Charles Anthony,
Prince of Asturia (the eldest son was in.
capable of inheriting' the crown.) Charles
IV. was married to Maria Louise Theresa
of Parma. Charles was governed entirely
by Manuel Godey, the lover of his wife and
an instrument of Napoleon’s. The second
revival of the country commenced, trade
aud commerce began to show signs of re-
turning activity. During this reign war
broke out with Great Britain, resulting in
disaster to Spain. In 1804 another war
arose and resulted the same. In 1808
Charles abdicated in favor of his son
Ferdinand VII. but two days afterwards
privately protested.

[Column 3] 	
	Shortly after the prince was elevated to
the throne, he and his father were induced
by Napoleon to take a journey to Bayonne,
but instead of pleasure to find themselves
prisoners in the hands of Napoleon. They
surrendered the crown to Napoleon and he
transferred it to his brother Joseph. The
Supreme Council of Seville declared war
against. France and Napoleon. England
recognized Ferdinand as king and sent an
army to aid the Spanish. After many
bloody battles the French were driven from
the country. In 1813 Ferdinand VII. was
restored to the throne. He married the
notorious Christina who bore him two chil-
dren, Isabella and the Infanta Maria Louise.
Having no sons Ferdinand revoked the
Salic law which excluded females, and es-
tablished a regular succession to the throne
of Spain, females as well as males.
	Ferdinand died in 1833 and bequeathed
his crown to his daughter Isabella. His
brother Don Carlos contested the legitama-
cy of the throne, and this led lo a long and
disastrous war. Isabella II. was only three
years old when she was proclaimed queen.
Don Carlos and his son refused to take the
oath of allegiance and a civil war broke out
which raged for seven years. Don Carlos
reduced to the last extremity fled to France
for refuge and renounced his claim to the
throne of Spain. Isabella’s mother Christina,
and Esparten the “Duke of Victory,” were
appointed regents during her minority. At
the age of thirteen she was proclaimed of
age and at sixteen was married to her cous-
in, Don Francisco de Assis. Married
against her will to a man said to be unfit
for the conjugal state, the private character
of Isabella has been exposed to much cen-
sure and scandal.
	Ever since Isabella ascended the throne
disorder and dissatisfaction seem to be
characteristic of Spain, and in 1845 Don
Carlos made another attempt to gain the
throne of Spain for his son, Charles Louis
“Prince of Asturia.” Isabella with her
royal daughter set out for Barcelona but
was hooted and insulted through the streets.
The cause of this was that she wanted a
bill passed which would place the chief
municipal appointments in the hands of
the crown and deprive the inhabitants of
of the towns of any control over their func-
tionaries. She carried her measure but
there was such an uproar she was obliged
to yield. In 1841 quite a dispute arose
between Christina and Esparten, each one
wanted to be sole regent. Esparten was
elected and continued at the head of the
government until the 14th of July, 1856,
then Gen. O’Donnell was appointed presi-
dent. O’Donnell did not reign long; he
was obliged to resign on the 13th of Octo-
ber, and a new ministry was formed under
Gen. Narvaez. On the 28th of November
the queen gave birth to a son who as heir
to the kingdom took the title of Prince of
Asturia. The insugentsand royalists were
always fighting and in 1868 Isabella was
dethroned and with the king and her four
children took refuge in France. A new
president took the field, Marshall Serrano,
there was much dissatisfaction and all kinds
of parties formed, and an unforeseen diffi-
culty arose, the rebellion of Cuba. It be-
gan at Mauganillo on the 10th of October.
Manuel Cespedes was chief of the Cuban
forces and asked for recognition for the 
new Republic of Cuba, this rebellion re-
mained throughout 1869. Then the Spanish
began to look around for a king.


In 1871 the Cortez elected Prince Amedeo
of Italy to be king. Finding the task a
hopeless one Amedeo abdicated in 1873,
then the form of government was changed
to a Republic. In 1874 Spain was the
scene of general anarchy and much blood
was shed.
	In December 1874, Alphonso, (Isabella’s
son) was declared king. In 1877 Alphonso
married Mercedes, a daughter of the Duke
and Duchess of Montpensier, thus fulfilling
a part of the scheme of Louis Phillip of
France. Mercedes was a beautiful woman,
an angelic disposition, and the first and
only love of Alphonso. The honeymoon
was scarcely over when Mercedes began to
droop and fade and died in 1878. The
Spanish people insisted upon an heir to the 
throne so the following year Alphonso mar-
ried Marie Christina, the daughter of the
Austrian Arch Duke Ferdinand. In 1885
Alphonso died and six months after a son
was born. Little Alphonso was not only
born a king but came into the world in
the presence of his Ministers of State, Cabi-
net officers, members of the regency and
Privy council. A rumor had been circu-
lated that a bogus heir would be placed up-
on the throne, and that was the reason that
every consideration of privacy due to a wo-
man at such a time had to yield to the de-
mands of political situation. Marie Chris-
tina is regent during the minority of Al-
phonso. According to the laws of Spain
the king attains his majority at the age of
fifteen. The little king is twelve now, what
will be left for him to rule over remains to
be seen.
	The queen regent is very much disliked. 
She began by losing the friendship of her
people and then lost their sympathy, now
she is about to lose their respect. She is
said to follow the advice of her mother, the
Arch Duchess Elizabeth of Austria, when
any difficulty arises. She surrounds her-
self with cardinals and bishops who are the 
masters at the Court. She almost remains
in retirement when there are requests for
her presence. The queen was not present
a few weeks ago at the patriotic "Gran
Co[-]rida," the receipts of which were des-
tined for the national subscription. The
Spanish people do not know her, conse-
quently can have no sympathy for her.
	Don Carlos has never been so near the
throne as now, his partisans grow daily
more numerous and powerful. If the queen
cannot keep her people together and the
little king has to abdicate, will it be a
Monarchy or a Republic? War abroad
and revolution at home—such is the condi-
tion of Spain now. A movement against
the queen regent has been started and seri-
ous complications will probably arise before
long. It is said from a reliable source that
a majority of the Spanish people would like
to be rid of the Phillipines and Cuba, and
that they ought to be given to the Ameri-
cans without war; however, that will be
left for those who come later.
	We will glance over the interval since
elapsed, during the first half of which the
nation wasted itself on schemes of mad
ambition, and the latter half has sunk al-
most into a state of oblivion. In glancing
over this period we must confess that the
reign of Ferdinand and Isabella was the
most glorious in the annals of Spain.
				LUELLA M. RHODES.

NOTES AND NEWS

	Lady Henry Somerset was re-elected
president of the British Woman’s Temper-
ance Association at the recent annual meet-
ing, by a large majority.
	Mrs. Elizabeth Smith Miller of Geneva,
N. Y., has republished a remarkable speech
by her father, Gerritt Smith, urging the
liberation of Cuba. It was delivered July
4, 1873, but reads as if written today. It
seems as if the veritable spirit of prophecy
had rested upon the famous New York
philanthropist.
	Mrs. Ida H. Harper is now in Washing-
ton, D. C., with her daughter, resting after
the completion of Miss Anthony’s biogra-
phy. She writes: “I shall spend most of
the summer reading proof. Our book will
be ready for the public Sep. 1, but the war
may delay its publication.”
	Mrs. Julia Ward Howe, on one occasion,
presented herself at a club of which she is a
member with her bonnet wrong side in
front. After some hesitation lest Mrs.
Howe should feel hurt, a sister member in-
formed her of the mistake. “What a blow
to my vanity!” said Mrs. Howe with an
amused smile. “I thought I was receiving
an unusual amount of attention as I came
down town on the car, but attributed it
solely to my own attractions.”
	Lady Aberdeen has received a letter from
St. Petersburg, saying that Russian women
are developing a lively interest in clubs, due
largely to the growth of the woman’s club
idea in England and America. Until re-
cently no move in this direction has been
possible, as Russian laws forbade the
formation of women’s clubs, and it is only
after strenuous efforts and repeated appeals
to the empress that the embargo has been
removed. Now there has been established in
St. Petersburg the “Russian Women’s
Mutual Help Society.” The president is
Dr. Anna Shanbanoff, a woman of much
ability and energy. The club contains
about 1,400 members, including doctors of
medicine, mathematics law and authors,
and it is said most of the intellectual society
of St. Petersburg.

INDIVIDUALITY.

Each being wins his way alone—by choice;
Each must unfold his temper, worth a[-]d power,
Amid the grind of circumstance and time
Great needs, ambitions, purposes plans,
Press forward their demands imperative,
And clamoring on the threshold of the heart,
Create the force to push out free and bold,
In untrod realms where we must hew a way;
Where kingdoms wait for those who bear
The natural heart of royalty!
Companions true are those who keep our pace,
And yet encroach not on our liberty; 
The human soul so hungers to be free
No tie can hold it back. And so it is, 
Our kinsmen oft become but memories;
And should we stand eyes meeting old time eyes.
The knowledge sure would flash from soul to soul,
I know thee not!
NAN WILKERSON WOOD

GOV. ADAMS’ ADDRESS.

AT THE BIENNIAL FEDERATION OF CLUBS
IN DENVER.
	“Livingston found in the mountains of
Central Africa a tribe who placed over the
doorway of every hut a sign that meant ‘she
ruleth here.’ Savage instinct and impulse
grasped a principle of equality and justice
that it has taken ages of Christian civiliza-
tion to discover. Over the gateway of
Colorado stands the sentiment ‘she ruleth
here.’ Are men of the West more chival-
rous or more just than their Eastern
brothers?
	“Is it a coincidence, or is there an ele-
ment of truth in the boast of Switzerland,
Montenegro and other mountain lands, that
liberty has a clearer vision in higher alti-
tudes? Certain it is that it is the mountain
states Colorado, Utah, Idaho and Wyoming
only whose statutes recognize that the
Creator made no mistake when he placed
man and woman side by side in Eden.
Westward the course of justice, as well as
empire takes its way.
	“It is fitting that this great confederation
of women’s clubs should meet in Colorado.
While you are not a suffrage association
and may not care to vote, the ultimate of
your intellectual aspirations must be the
elimination of sex from the statutes of
equality. Colorado has added a soprano to
the symphony of political liberty, and we
offer our laws, our homes, schools, institu-
tions as object lessons to those who would
doubt the results of equality before the law.
(Great applause.)
SUFFRAGE NEEDS NO DEFENSE. 
	“Woman suffrage needs not the defense
of results; it is not a question of expedi-
ency but of abstract justice. Yet no evil
has come from the experiment. My testi-
mony may be biased, for had I received no
women votes some one else would have had
the pleasure of greeting you this morning.
But to me there has come no just criticism
to the participation of women in public af-
fairs; no awaking from the fond dream that
the union of the vigor, physical force
and courage of man with the higher moral
and spiritual qualities of woman will weave
for our children a fairer destiny than has
yet come to the race.
	“While here gather your own testimony.
The state superintendent of schools is a wo-
man, and no office in the state house is con-
ducted with more ability. Half the county
superintendents of Colorado are of the same
sex, nearly all of the teachers are women,
yet we challenge comparison with the pub-
lic school system of any state in the Union.
WOMANLY WOMEN.
	“Upon our streets even counting our
Buffalo Bills and Arizona Kids, you will
not see as many long-haired men and short-
haired women as in Boston. Our women
have not diverted their patronage from the
dressmaker to the tailor. Dry goods and
millinery stores still prosper. Politics and
oot ball still have their season. The set-

[Column 2]
ting sun glints the mountains yonder with
its beams of glory as in the days of old
when man was king. Now and then we
meet a man whose spirit gropes through the
murky atmosphere of personal appetite. A
member of this class went home last night,
and at supper protested against cold pota-
toes and a tough beefsteak; his wife who
had just returned from her club, asked him
if he measured the universe by a good meal.
She had spent the afternoon at a woman’s
club discussing the weighty problem and
potentialities of the human race, and cold
potatoes and a poor steak were but an in-
significent local issue. Go into our homes
and you will find, certainly, no more ex-
amples of heavy bread, of neglected duties
and husbands, of ragged and unwashed
children, than in states which women’s
clubs and suffrage have not invaded. No
fair, just man can look into the eyes of the
mother who bore him, or the wife who
guides the destiny of his own sons, and
deny her any club or political right which
is his.
	“Selfishness will pipe its lament, and
when the politicians realize that equal suf-
frage doubles the number of candidates for
office without increasing the number of
offices, they will anathematize the expan-
sion of the ballot with as much vigor as
Lord North cursed the memory of Colum-
bus for discovering a land that had cost him
so much in trouble, treasure and defeat.
	“If Mrs. Browning were living and in
Colorado she would have no occasion to
write of ‘women sobbing out of sight be-
cause men made the laws,’ for the influence
of women voters, and especially of the wo-
men’s clubs, can mould—almost dictate—
any new legislation.
WOMEN AS STATESMEN.
	“Women may develope the arts of a
statesman, but they can never be politicians.
When their personal ambition is at issue
they cannot dissemble. Since our consti-
tution women have voted in all school elec-
tions, and that is one department of
government in which politics and venality
have never found lodgment. What her in-
fluence has been in our school life is a hint
of what it will be in every direction where
morals and the public good are concerned.
	When the Israelites were broken with
corruption and dissension God gave a wo-
man to judge them; Deborah ruled them
for forty years, and the Bible adds ‘then
they had a rest.’ I have never been clear
whether the rest came as a result of her
rule, or because her reign was ended. But
in our land come rest or come dissension,
Deborah is to reign. The women’s libera-
tion movement which under the shadow of
the Rocky mountains has crystalized into
legal enfranchisement, is not a transient
impulse; we stand at its cradle, but few of
us will live to see the limits it will reach.
Liberty when unchained at Runnymede,
made the circuit of the globe. So with
equality, it is not a summer ripple upon the
political sea, but a strong, swelling tide,
whose waves will wash every civilized
shore.


“The trial has been called and it will
never be thrown out of court until the logic
of experience has proven whether the past
inequalities of man and woman were the
invention of man’s legislation or of divine
authority.
WOMAN IS HUMAN.
	“Woman’s clubs and her interest in pub-
lic questions have been a revelation to
many. Woman is found to be neither di-
vine nor Satanic, neither a slave nor an
angel, simply human. She is neither the
soulless creature of Homer or Milton, nor
the burlesque divinity of Cervantes, but a
being dowered with common sense and an
exalted purpose. Every attainment but in-
creases her moral and intellectual force
without damage to tile sublime character
of mother and wife. She may break some
of the chains of prejudice and conventionali-
ty, but she will not lose her womanhood.
Fear not that equality will make her mas-
culine, ‘man and woman created he them,’
and no belated act of justice on the part of
man can change the ordinance of God.
	“Proud as we are of the public’s past the
Higher and purer influence of woman in
public affairs should give a future still more
radiant and fair. Mrs. Stanton said that if
Miriam had been in charge of the children
of Israel that they would have reached
Canaan in forty days instead of wandering
like Gypsies for forty years. Perhaps had
woman been placed in our national consti-
tution it would not have taken one hun-
dred years and the first sons of a genera,
tion to eliminate slavery. Had the single
word ‘sex’ been inserted in the fifteenth
amendment, I believe that our nation
would be a generation in advance of its
present position on the problems of moral
and municipal reform.
INSPIRATION OF WOMAN.
	The nation owes a debt of gratitude to
women’s clubs. They have been an in-
spiration to its members, they have been
centers from which radiate electric currents
of moral and political reform. They have
broadened their own horizon and that of
the race. They have organized the eneigy
and intellect of woman and directed them
into every field of good, upon which falls
the smile of God or man.
	“Men have clubs for sport, pleasure and
dissipation. If we omit the Woman’s
Morning Whist club, which is as wasteful
as any club man can invent, women’s clubs
are universities, colleges, training schools
where post graduate degrees are taken in
citizenship, art and literature.
	“Woman’s sphere is no longer confined
to the washtub, the kitchen or the parlor.
	“Her sphere is now the grand whirling
globe with all its responsibilities and its
glories. The growth of woman’s clubs is
the greatest movement of this generation,
and as representatives of that beneficient
influence we greet you today. 
	“The faith Rome had in her vestal vir-

	An important meeting of the American
National Red Cross Relief Committee was
recently held at the Chamber of Commerce
in New York City. Bishop Potter presided
and on the recommendation of Mr. W. T.
Wardwell the following appeal was issued:
	The American Nation[-]l Red Cross Relief Com-
mittee, acting und[-]r the au[-]pices of the Ameri-
can National Red Cross, has for its objects the
col ection of funds for providi g medical and
surgical attendance, nursing, medical supp[-]ies,
food, c othing and such necessary assistance as
may be required by the American National Red
Cross, u[-]on call of the United States Government
in order to unify all endeavors to that end during
the pres[-]nt war.
	Under p[-]o[-]isions of the Geneva Conference
from which every Na[-]ional Red Cross Society de-
rives its authority, the American National Red
Cross is directed to provide such relief as may be
required by all, wi[-]hout recognition of friend or
foe, who may suffer from the calamities incidental
to war pestilence or famine
	The Red Cross here, and throughout the civil-
ized world, by a wide and varied experience in
recent wars, recognizes by international treaty the
sacred obligat[-]ons of h[-]lpfulness for the suffe[-]ing
wherever found, has so perfected its organization
that it becomes the recognized and legitimate
channel for contributions from all classes of in-
dividuals and every variety of auxiliary associa-
tion.
	For the purpose of properly systematizing the
benevolent impulses of the general public and of
giving proper directions for efficient Red Cross
work, the Committee solicits the cooperation of
individuals and auxiliary associatio s throughout
the country, confideut that through such means
the various funds and articles collected can most

UTAH VOLUNTEERS.

		Oakland, Cal., June 14, 1898.
Editor Exponent:
	Perhaps some of your readers would like
to hear from the brave soldier boys who so
gallantly offered their services to their
country and who arrived several weeks ago.
I went across the bay to see those boys
whom the papers said were the finest equip-
ped, the most highly educated and alto-
gether the best specimens of manhood that
had volunteered for Uncle Sam.
	The city of tents was located on the Bay
District race track, adjoining Golden Gate
park and in full view of the ocean, where
they had the full benefit of the cold fogs
that greeted them every day and their soft-
est bed was sand. The Utah artillery had
decidedly the best of any of the regiments,
for they had a whole block to themselves
with cannon in the center and tents around
the edge, whilst the others were packed
closely.
	The Utah boys with their war accou-
trements sailed for Manila this morning
thousands turned out to cheer them and
wish them a pleasant voyage and a safe re-
turn. The California people have treated

[Column 2]
the twelve thousand men here royally.
They have been guests of the city as far as
Uncle Sam would permit, they have had to
eat pork and beans and do their own wash-
ing. And oh, those boys and men doing
their own washing! A whole month’s wash
would consist of one shirt, one towel and
three pairs of socks with the heels rubbed
off when they were washed. As for baths,
they will remember those they had and
look forward to those they expect to get.
They seem to think they are on a picnic
except when they roll in a blanket and lie
on the sand. I asked a friend of mine if he
had an allumium badge, he said “No, what
is it for?” I answered softly, “So you can
be identified if anything should happen.”
The tears came into his eyes and he said,
“Oh they can identify me, but nothing will
happen to me.” Then he inquired about
the badges and I told him the good, patri-
otic people of San Francisco and Oakland
had made arrangements to supply every
soldier with a badge, his name and country
and state engraved upon it, to be placed
upon a buckskin string and worn around
the neck, then there were flannel bandages
for every one and comfort bags with things
that soldiers need, where they have no
mother or wife near.
	Everybody has been very busy here and
are hard at it to feed this army of men as
they arrive, it is a very big undertaking,
and so many of the companies came with-
out even a change of underclothing and no
shoes, absolutely destitute, but the people
have supplied every want and I feel sure
San Francisco will be remembered when
other things are forgotten.
	There are many funny things happen out
at camp. One of the boys committed a
misdemeanor and a policeman took after
him, but the soldier ran into a tent with
the officer in close pursuit. The guard
told the officer to halt but he thought he
was the law, but before you could wink he
was taken to the guard house and locked
up for the night. Whilst one of the com-
panies was drilling in double quick time the
Captain fell into a gopher hole and as each
man came on he fell till fifty were piled
there. Every day there is something funny
happens and I am glad they do have a good
time. You would hardly expect it on
$13.00 per month salary, but they don’t
have to treat either a lady or a gentleman.
If a soldier goes out with a lady she pays
for everything and is honored to do it. Ten
soldiers got on the car when I did and a
wealthy girl paid their fare, and that is the
way it is all the time. If the war don’t do
another thing, it has made us all more
patriotic and self-denying and has brought
us all closer together. I am proud of the
volunteers and I am proud of the Utah
boys and of the people who provided for
them so liberally that it has been comment-
ed upon a number of times. I only hope
they will have an opportunity to distin-
guish themselves at Manila. They are the
only artillery company that came and the
only cavalry company so that they may
make a hit, let us hope so. My heart
gees out to them as they sail away, let us
hope they may all return safe.
						NELLIE.

THOUGHTS ABOUT THE WAR.

	The war news is revolting, appalling
and blood-curdling, and the time has most
assuredly come when we cannot endure to
even read the details of the heart-rending
occurrences transpiring from day to day,
one turns away from the bulletin boards faint
and sick at heart, knowing too, sometimes,
that their very own if not wounded or dy-
ing are among the rank and file likely at
any moment to be in the midst of the dis-
tress consequent upon war. O, that the
day might speedily come when war should
cease and nations be at peace one with
another; but while we are in the midst
of these terrible sorrows and calamities it
behooves us all to exercise faith in God and
pray earnestly that the white emblem of
peace may soon o’ershadow those engaged
in this fearful conflict though it were under-
taken to free the oppressed from the yoke
of bondage.
	Even now the losses on both sides are
fearful to contemplate, and none know
what may yet transpire before this “cruel
war” is over. The heat of the tropical sun
is overpowering, and the wounded who suf-
fer from agonizing pain are in a condition
more deplorable than those who have passed
over to the great beyond, where we are
told there is no more pain or suffering for-
ever.
	When we consider that among the volun-
teers is the very flower of American man-
hood (many of them only boys) patriots
and sons of patriots, it is indeed terrible to
think how great is the loss to the country
as well as to the homes made desolate and
the heartstrings torn with anguish, and to
those of us who feel this war might have
been averted by arbitration had wisdom
held her rightful sway, the fearful results
already recorded are overwhelming. One
can scarcely rejoice in a victory that re-
quires such sacrifice of human lives in this
enlightened age when civilization makes
such boasts of mercy and of charity and
brotherly love. O, the pity of it all, that it
had to be! And if as so many assert, un-
due ambition and a spirit of revenge is
prompting it then, indeed, “How have the
mighty fallen.”
	President McKinley’s Proclamation that
we should “give thanks unto the Lord”
for the nation’s success is well-timed, and
that we beseech the Lord in all humility
for continued guidance and protection is the
proper spirit to manifest; meantime that we
bow to His will and in all sincerity conse-
crate and dedicate our armies to His ser-

[Column 2]
vice, putting away all selfishness and de-
sire for vengeance, and pray that no more
human blood be spilt, but peace be soon de-
clared and a better and a higher lesson
be taught to the barbarous nations than to
slay their enemies. The Savior’s lesson to
all mankind was “love one another.”
	There may be some rejoicing over such
victories as Dewey’s and the heroism of
Sampson, Schley and others, but after all
must it not necessarily be rejoicing in sup-
pressed grief? and is there real cause after
all—we admire physical bravery on land or
sea, but God knows whether this be a
righteous conflict and He will one day
bring all the nations to judgment. And
now with heads bowed down and hearts
sorely stricken we mourn the nation’s loss
and the untimely death of heroes whose
lives were freely given for their country’s
sake and the oppressed of other lands. And
the end is not yet. “And the mourners go
about the streets.”
	But let us hope that this is not all, and
exercise faith in God for our grand army of
the Great Republic that He will have com-
passion upon those, who have gone in obe-
dience to authority, and preserve in the
hour of danger the honor of this Western
continent that it may indeed be an asylum
and a haven of refuge for all nations, as has
been declared by ancient and modern pro-
phets.

VISIT TO SAN LUIS STAKE.

	On Friday morning, June 3, we left Salt
Lake City to visit the sisters in San Luis
valley, accompanied by Sister Minnie J.
Snow who represented the Y. L. M. I. A.
	We reached La Jara Saturday morning,
where we met some of the sisters and drove
over to Sanford with them. Mrs. Cornelia
Mortensen, president of the Relief Society,
took us home with her, and that afternoon
and evening we had the opportunity of
seeing a number of the sisters of that place.
We dined at Mrs. Mary E. Crowther’s, the
secretary of the Relief Society of the Stake,
whose husband is away on a mission. Sun-
day morning quite a number of the Sanford
people went over to Manassa where the Re-
lief Society conference was held. The morn-
ing was fine and the ride enjoyable, and the
country everywhere looked delightful and
prosperous.
	Our party went direct to President
Smith’s for instruction and lunched there,
then to the meeting house where after the
sacrament had been administered the con-
ference opened. There was an excellent
representation from the several branches,
and an opening address by President Corne-
lia Mortensen. The secretary read the
minutes of the previous conference which
gave a good idea of the work of the Society
in that locality.
	The same evening there was a meeting
of the Y. M. and Y. L conjoint and a gen-
eral invitation extended to all—there was
a full attendance and a good spirit. Some
young missionaries from the city were pres-
ent and bore faithful testimonies, they are
doing effective work among the people in
Colorado, organizing branches of the Church
under the presidency of Apostle John W.
Taylor who has headquarters in Denver.
We were delighted to hear from Brother
Taylor, whom we met on the train in go-
ing over, in company with Brother Willie
Clive, that he had organized a Relief Soci-
ety in Denver and also in Colorado Springs

[Column 2]
and expected to organize soon in Pueblo,
which has since been done, Mrs. Susa
Young Gates being present and assisting
Apostle Taylor and the Elders.
	An interesting conference of the Y. L.
M. I. A. was held in Manassa on Monday,
there were three meetings held, the Relief
Society conference occupying the morning
hours, and the following day we met with
the sisters of Mountain View at which place
we were literally feasted; to look at the
tables, one would have imagined it was
Christmas, and the puzzle was where it all
came from, roast turkey and every other
imaginable luxury away out on the open
fields where the ward house was situated,
miles away from the dwellings. The warm
welcome and generous hospitality will never
be forgotten. The same afternoon we held
meeting in Sanford also in the evening,
both were largely attended.
	Afterwards we held meetings in Morgan
and Richfield and Eastdale, the last twenty-
five miles away across the Rio Grande
river over which has been built a stupen-
dous iron bridge. The drive to Eastdale
was most interesting and there were novel-
ties to be seen in Spanish and Mexican
houses which are anything but artistic,
though situated as they are might be called
picturesque. Eastdale is beautifully situ-
ated and at present prosperous under the
direction of Bishop Jenson who seems to
possess the elements of character necessary
for colonizing. We enjoyed this place
very much, held two meetings, one at 4 p.
m., another at 8 p. m. We left early in
the morning and drove near to a rugged
mountain on the borders of New Mexico,
en route we saw several wolves who seemed
very bold and fleet of foot. President
Smith was sorry he was unarmed, for he
could easily have killed one or two of them.
	The drive across the lonely country was
charming in several ways, particularly so
to the writer as there was desert land where
one would expect to see mirage, and we
were not altogether disappointed, at another
time we should be very glad to write about
it. We reached Manassa as we supposed
in time for an afternoon meeting, but were
surprised to find a banquet had been pre-
pared instead in the new hall just purchased
by the sisters there for the use of the Relief
Society and Y. L. M. I. A. The decora-
tions with flags and bunting as well as
plants, vines and flowers was very hand-
some and impressive, and the tables fairly
“groaned with the weight of the feast.”
About two hundred and fifty people par-
took of the bounteous repast provided, the
lack of time prevented speech-making, al-
though the writer made a few remarks and
Sister Mortensen really rose to the occasion
in a few appropriate words.
	Our visit will long be remembered, Sis-
ter Snow always seemed able to suit the occa-
sion in her gentle way at all meetings, and
we both felt blest in our labors and enjoyed
the people we met as we traveled hither 
and thither up and down.

	Mrs. Mary S. Gist and Mrs. Kate Tan-

in the Templeton, Tuesday evening, July

and of the Cuban Relief Committee, both
of Washington, D. C., and Mrs. Woods
read some of her celebrated poems and gave

ning.
	The Mothers’ Congress which was held
in the Asssmbly Hall in this city commenc-
ing June 30, and closing July 2, was a
complete success, and being the first of its
kind in the West demonstrated the fact that
Western women are alive to the best inter-
ests and welfare of the nation of which they
are an important part. Later we hope to
give a condensed account of the proceed-
ings, but our people, the Latter-day Saints,
have ever been alive upon the question of
motherhood, and through the Council of
Health and the Relief Society and other or-
ganizations of a later date have been incul-
cating the knowledge and insisting upon
the holiness of this condition, and its mani-
fold duties, obligations and responsibilities.
	Mrs. Clara B. Colby, editor of the Wo-
man’s Tribune, Washington, D. C., has
been duly accredited to the war Depart-
ment as a correspondent for her paper. It
is the first and only woman’s paper to re-
ceive such a pass, and only five or six wo-
men in all have been thus favored. War
correspondents have admission to the camps
and to the field, but have to report at head-
quarters and have their passes counter-
signed by the General in command. Their
passes enable them to be included in ar-
rangements for transportation, etc., they
bearing their own expenses. Their
movements are subject to such restrictions
as the commanding officers may impose
from time to time.

ardson gave us many facts, also many of her

for the future. Mrs. Fabian read the re-
port, which was most encouraging. The
work appeals directly to the nation’s honor
and to each loyal individual man, woman

THE SABLE MESSENGER.

There was grief in the heart of the mother—
	A sorrow unspeakably deep,
And the loving young sister and brother
	Stole out of the chamber to weep.
Each friend that stood by felt the shadow,
	The cold, creeping shadow of woe,
As in trembling and tears they awaited
	The moment when Leah must go.

Afar to some beautiful country
	Beyond the wide horizon’s rim,
(So far and so lovely none ever
	Returned from its boundaries dim;)
Her father had years ago journeyed,
	Left wife and sweet little ones all—
But here is a messenger from him,
	His dearly loved Leah to cal[-].

“Thy father has sent for thee, Leah,”
	The messenger whispering said,
And stood with his long sable mantle
	Enveloping even his head.
’Twas rumored that never a mortal
	Dared look in the face such as he,
Lest eyesight be blasted or even
	He cease of the living to be,

How black was that long shrouding m[-]ntle!
	How hollow that voice and how low!
And Leah clung close to her mother,
	And shuddered, and dreaded to go.
“O mother, I fear this dark stranger,”
	She panted in tremulous tone,
“I know not the way—is there danger?
	Oh, must I go, mother, alone?”

Close, close to the heart of the mother
	Her tender arms folded her child,
And, torn with unspeakble sorrow,
	She pleaded in agony wild—
“Oh, spare me this trial, I pray thee! 
Oh, break not a mother’s fond heart!
Return by the way which thou camest;
	And alone, oh in mercy! depart.”
 
There were tears in the eyes of the mother,
	And sobbing outside on the stair,
And sympathy saddened each o[-]her,
	Who waited expectantly there.
But sternly unmoved stood the stranger, 
As still as a statue of Fate;
He stirred not a fold of his mantle
	But icily whispered “I wait ”

The moon hid her face in a cloud-vail
	The tremulous stars ceased to shine,
The wind with its eerie low moaning
	Swept mournfully through the dark pine.
All sound faltered low into si[-]ence—
	Fond nature seemed holding her breath—
The thought sped from bosom to bosom,
	“How dreadful how cruel is death!”

[Column 2]
At length with a slow, stately motion,
	The stranger a step drew more near,
And Leah her startled eyes lifted,
	All darkened and heavy with fear.
He opened his arms to enfo[-]d her.
	The pall drew aside from his brow,
And lo! the dear face of her father
	Smiled lovingly over her now.

What glorious flash no[-] illumines
	The sweet face so pallid and worn!
What luster those heavy eyes brightens,
	Light sunshine above a dark storm!
“Papa!” cried the sweet voice, and lifting
	Frail hands like the wings of a bird,
“Goodbye, dear mamma,” low she whispered,
	And sprang to his arms at the word

There were sighs from the lone hearted mother,
	Caressing the pale, lovely clay;
There were tears from the sister and brother,
	For Leah had vanished away;
But the smile of her joy softly lingered
	Around the cold, beautiful face,
That seemed to her dear ones remaining
	A token of heavenly grace.

CLARA BARTON’S REPORT. 

	New York, July 16.—A telegram re-
ceived today by the executive committee of
the Red Cross from Miss Clara Barton dated
Playa Del Este, July 15, is as follows:
	Santiago has surrendered. I came from
the front yesterday in a pouring rain.
Fever is suspected here. Siboney is burned.
Dr. Lesser and Mrs. Lesser and all their
nurses are in the fever hospital two miles
distant. I saw them today and all are doing
well. Four army surgeons of the St. Paul
are with us, there being no other provision
for them.
	We are feeding the refugees of Sibony,
and many thousands at Eurmisea, by rail.


Elwell is feeding them at Caney by army
wagons, and 20,000 by pack mules, 15
miles travel.
	Commander McCalla of the Marblehead,
called yesterday for 100,000 rations, medi-
cines and clothing for the refugees in the
woods in the country surrounding Guan-
tanamo. Nothing is known of boats or of
aid of any kind from any other source.
State of Texas has fed all the wounded at
the front, and is helping them home.
	All members of the Red Cross are in per-
fect health, thoroughly organized.
	A telegram has been received from Red 
Cross Agent Kent at Jacksonville who
states that Col. Maus, surgeon-in-chief,
has just returned from Miami and reports
much sickness there, with a great need of
milk and ice. The Red Cross launch
Moyner has been repaired and will be
ready to leave Jacksonville Monday, for
Key West.
	Advices just received from Key West in-
dicate that the Red Cross will reach Santia- 
go Tuesday next with the much needed
supplies and surgeons.


MRS. GATES’ LETTER.

Provo, July 19, 1898.
My Dear Aunt Em:

	After the convention closed in Denver on
June 27th I remained in Denver, as you
know, as the guest of Apostle John W.
Taylor. I had a most delightful visit with
Brother Taylor and his kind, gentle wife,
receiving many attentions also from the
Elders in that mission; I must mention the
kindness of Elders Ensign and Graham,also
the unfailing courtesy of Elder White, not
forgetting the thoughtfulness of Brother
Pack and the exceeding sweetness of Elder
Clive’s magic violin. In short all the
Elders were as good to me as if I had been
their own mother, and I shall not soon for-
get my visit nor the pleasant friendships re-
newed and formed.
	Apostle Taylor called the sisters of the
Relief Society together in Denver and we
spent an hour or so at Sister Taylor’s talk-
ing upon the duties and privileges of this
organization. Sister Lowery, a lady from
Boston, is the president of the organization
there. She is of a very mild and yet strong-
character with perfect manners and a will- 
ing, helpful disposition. She is very anx-
ious for more light and assistance in her
duties, and I recommended her to take
the "Exponent" and communicate with
you. There are thirty sisters in this branch,
who are very earnest and faithful members.
	The next branch visited was Colorado
Springs which has just been organized since
last February. There are only four fami-
lies in this branch and all the women be- 
long to the Relief Society. Sister Lenora
Adams is the president with Sister Dena
Myers as first and Alice Johnson as second 
counselors and Susie Johnson as secretary
and treasurer. I gave them some iustruc-
tion as to the duties of officers, mode of con-
ducting meetings and blessed them as the


Spirit suggested in their labors.  It was
very interesting to examine thus the be-
ginning of the great work which grows al-
ways with uplifting power. All these
branches and the branches of the Church
are of very recent origin and in great con-
trast to the old, well established organiza-
tions which I have attended in my life.
	At Pueblo Apostle Taylor joined us and
we held three public meetings. Also or-
ganized the Relief Society.
	We have great reason to rejoice in the
great blessings meted out to the Saints and
for the rapid spread and increase of the
work of God. I trust you yourself will
some day be able to visit these faithful and
earnest sisters of the Colorado mission.
			Yours lovingly,
				SUSA YOUNG GATES.

THE MOTHER’S CONGRESS.

Dear Sister Wells:
	Now that the Congress festivities of the
“Mothers of America” are over, both in
national capacity and state as far as our own
dear Utah is concerned—and I have been
privileged to be an active participant in the
one and a silent observer in the other—I
feel impressed to write a few words of in-
formation for those who may be interested
in the move to advance motherhood.
	As far as our Utah Congress of Mothers
is concerned, I was a silent observer and of
course am not informed enough to express
my views; but at the National Congress of
Mothers I had the honor of being an active
participant, and as such can speak facts.
The only conclusion I can come to (according
to my silent observations) is that too few
of our Utah people are acquainted with the
effort that has been made here in regard to
educating women for motherhood. In gen-
eral terms this manner of education has
been in operation here in Utah from the be-
ginning of its existence, through the Relief
Society and later the Young Ladies Asso-
ciation, but later still a special work for
the education of motherhood has been in
operation, and of which I give myself the
credit of being the originator. It is now
between nine and ten years since I started
this special work here in Utah for the sole
purpose of better qualifying women for
motherhood, and one of the consequences
was that I was invited to attend the Nation-
al Congress of Mothers, at Washington, D.
C., this spring as delegate to represent my
work. I went there to represent about fif-
teen hundred women of Utah and bring
their greeting to the Congress. 
	About a year ago blanks were sent to me
from headquarters at Washington, which
would unquestionably give an understand-
ing of the nature of my work.
	In brief, I will say that I went to that
blessed Congress at Washington, and the
privileges I enjoyed, the honor and respect
conferred upon me was something that I
never dreamed would ever be my lot. I
went with comfort and hope in my soul,
having been set apart for the mission and
the blessings promised were literally ful-
filled. The first day of Congress I gave
my report like the other delegates. It was
listened to with great interest which was
proven from the enthusiasm manifested
through applause. It was the only work
of its nature reported and seemed to fill a 
vacant place. I gave my report verbally,
which was very much appreciated, so much

[Column 2]
so that the president took me warmly by
the hand saying: “Oh I am so glad, that
was from the heart; that is the way I wish
you to speak tomorrow.” I was on the
program for the next day, to treat the sub-
ject, “The Physical Side of Motherhood.”
	I went to the meeting with my soul full
of gratitude that I would be privileged to
present to this important assemblage of wo-
men the principles that are so dear to me,
and which I think so important to the true
adornment of the human race. I went
there fasting and praying, and my Heaven-
ly Father blessed me, I know. I had no
paper, I spoke of my subject as I knew it.
I know it was appreciated by the audience
from the enthusiasm manifested. After the
applause ceased several written notes were
sent up to me (by the ushers) of congratula-
tion and appreciation, asking for interviews
with me at the close of the meeting. One
lady from New York wrote me: “I am go-
ing right home and organize such work. I
would give anything if I could get you
along with me to start us out with your
counsel and advice. Please let me have an
interview with you after this meeting.”
When I came down from the platform and
the meeting was closed it seemed that every
one wanted to shake my hand and be intro-
duced to “the delegate from Utah,” and to
tell me who they were. The next morning
ing when I came and took my seat a beau-
tiful bouquet of flowers was handed over to
me with the words: “This is in apprecia-
tion of what you said yesterday. I am
delegate from Arkansas.”
	I would not have you think that I am
speaking of these things in a boasting spirit,
oh no, far from it! but it is in honor of the
noble women, and with the fulness of my
soul’s gratitude to my Heavenly Father. I
have no honor for myself, it is all His.
	It was so intensely desired that I should
have held a special conference, they called
it, in my line of work, and it was tried
hard every day during the week to get that
brought about by all those who were so en-
thusiastic—the president as much as any
one—but the program was so extensive and
time so very crowded, that it was not pos-
sible to get the time for it, and when I said
goodbye the beloved president said warmly
to me: “I am so sorry we could not get to
hold that conference, but you must be with
us again, we must hear more from you.”
And she added, “I am so glad we have just
received a telegram today from Salt Lake
City stating that they are organizing a
Mothers’ Congress, so you will be right
in it when you get home.” I thanked her
very heartily for her kind feeling and the
way she had treated me, and was silent for
the balance.
	When it was found that it was not pos-
sible to hold that special conference, I obli-
gated myself by promise to many delegates
to send them after I returned all the infor-
mation I could in writing concerning my
work. I have now done so, in as much as
have sent to eleven states in America
long, long letters of explanations, accom-
panied by my little book “What Women
Should Know.” I have done it on the
missionary plan, thankful if I can in this
way be instrumental in the hands of God to
bring some light to those who wish to have
it.
	I say in honor of that blessed woman, the
president of the National Congress, Mrs.
Theodore W. Birney, that she is a model
of grace, humility and symplicity. One is

[Column 3]
drawn towards her with the love of a sister.
God bless her in her noble calling!
	I have a word more to say in honor of
our esteemed Senator at Washington,
Hon. Frank J. Cannon and wife, for their
great kindness and consideration towards
me while at Washington. I shall never for-
get it and my soul is full of blessings for
them and all theirs, that God will reward
them for their manifestations of love and
respect to their humble sister.
			HANNAH SORENSEN.
P. S. I forgot to say that I have had a
most beautiful letter from the president of 
the National Congress of Mothers since I
came home—a letter of sympathy and en-
couragement asking also for information in
my line of work, and it finishes with the
words: “You must be with us again.”

	While on my sojourn in the East I had
also the privilege of visiting Chicago for a
period of five weeks. It was a very plea-
sant experience I had there in many ways,
but what I especially wish to refer to there
is the organization, or branch of our Church
organization there. I did not expect to find
such a flourishing little branch there as I
did, so I received very unexpectedly a very
pleasant impression. Our Elders laboring
there are types of intelligent men full of
ambition and energy, but the most beauti-
ful ornamentation I found in these dear
brethren was the unmistakable stamp of
humility. It was so gratifying to me to
silently notice the interest, love, grace and

	I attended several of the meetings of the

there, held in private houses among the

who as a rule are all present except in case

mable woman, is a Sister Smith who a few
years back was a resident of Nephi here in
Utah, but on account of circumstances re-
sides at present in Chicago. She is well
qualified for the position she occupies, in-
telligent, modest and warm-hearted, and
has the respect of those who preside in the
branch. Their secretary, Sister Ericksen,
is a very efficient secretary, punctual and
orderly, and in short it was a group of sis-
ters in whose company I enjoyed myself
very much. Their methods of meeting
were of the same order that we have here
at home with two exceptions (and these ex-
ceptions were very pleasant features) name-
ly, the members were regular attendants
and of the Elders of the mission two or
three were also regular attendants.

	Lingering on the dewy air,

	To my heart bowed down with care,

Ever trust the God of Battles!
	Live thou near Him day by day.

HOTEL METROPOLE.

Avalon, Santa Catalina Island, Cal.
					July 8, 1898.
Dr. Pratt, President, and ladies of the
		Press Club:
	I send you my most cordial and affection-
ate greetings; my sincere wishes for a
pleasant and restful summer.
	I will tell you as briefly as I can in a
letter what I have been doing since I left
Utah. Two weeks were given to San Fran-
cisco and vicinity, with frequent calls at
Camp Merritt. The boys were quite well
and a credit to their state, but disappointed
that they were left as home guards when
they expected to sail soon for the Philip-
pines; but they need not be impatient they
may have their chance for active service
and destruction. While it looks now as if
the end were not far in the future, I am one
who believes the war will be long, I have
never felt quite sure it would end in a few
weeks. My sojourn was pleasant as I have
many dear friends there, while much time
was devoted to sight seeing.
	The last of June we came to Los Angeles,
“City of the Angels,” the garden of Eden
as one friend remarked, and truly with
Pasadena, Riverside and Santa Monica it is
the garden spot of California, though not so
beautiful as when I visited the place four
years ago—owing to the drouth. As usual
we had an earthquake, light said the people,
but it wakened me from a sound sleep and
my bed rocked three times and it was noisy.
A friend at Redoude said there was a wall
of kelp several feet high in front of their
cottage, so there must have been quite a
disturbance somewhere. We visited the
ostrich farm, not nearly so interesting as
the one at Phoenix, Arizona, the birds are
not so large.
	One of the most lovely halting places of
my journey was at Avalon, on Santa Cata-
lina Island. The train took us to San
Pedro harbor, we embarked on the steamer
Falcon and were soon plowing the blue
waters of Santa Catalina channel, the island
is about twenty miles distant. The trip
across is a delightful experience, we were
told, that a person must be extremely sen-
sitive to suffer with sea sickness, but a large
majority were sensitive. The sky is clear,
the water blue, the air balmy, the flying

AN EMINENT VISITOR.
	Miss Sarah E. Hunt of Boston, first vice-
president of the Daughters of the Revolu-
tion general society, and regent of the
Massachusetts State society, visited our city
during the Mothers’ Congress. Miss Hunt
is a leading woman and Mrs. Wells was
fortunate in arranging a meeting between
her and the Wasatch chapter of the D. R.
	The “Exponent” office was beautifully
decorated with roses. Miss Hunt talked in
a simple, charming manner, enthusing her
interested listeners. Miss Hunt said:
“The North and the South in the Revolu-
tion went to war together and fought for
one common cause side by side, and we are
their children. Between the North and the
South, the East and the West there is that
bond that cannot be broken, this is the
grandest part of our work. The D. R. has
a large field. Just now there is a chance to
show what we can do in instilling patriot-
ism. The Massachusetts State society is
very successful, we have a large member-
ship. Our best work is in repairing and
preserving the Paul Revere, Bunker Hill
and others, but we feel that our greatest
strength is in our State society.
	“Do not feel disappointed, Mrs. Wells,
if mothers feel that they are so busy that
they cannot attend to joining, keep a brave
heart and never give up even a poor ground.
We desire Mrs. Wells to be State Regent
of your society. Be Democratic so that all
eligible persons may come in. If there are
any daughters of men who fought in the
Revolution find them and admit them free
of fees into the D. R. The General Soci-
ety will present them with a gold badge.
We want to especially honor them.
	“Our Massachsuttes State society found
an old lady aged 104, who was the daughter
of a soldier of the Revolution and we took
great pride in presenting her with a gold
badge.
	If any of you ladies come to New York
be sure you call at our headquarters.
Thursdays we have tea. ” MissHunt has a
charming presence and won the hearts of
the members of the Wasatch chapter.
	After the goodbyes were said Mrs. Wells
asked “How many of the roses will you
take with you?” and the reply came, “As
many as you will give me, and as many as
my hands can hold.” Then with the
pleasant and good wishes for a delightful
trip through the Yellowstone whither she
is going, Miss Hunt carried away a great
bunch of the lovely roses.
A. M. H.

MAINE MARTYR MEMORIAL DAY.

To the Presidents of Stakes and the
Bishops of the Church:
	Dear Brethren:—In view of the fact that 
the management of the Maine Monument
Fund has disclaimed any intentional slight
on the part of the national committee to-
wards the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-
day Saints by omiting on the 8th day of
May, to call upon the Church to join the
other churches in the land in holding
memorial services on the 8th day of May,
in honor of the brave men who lost their
lives in the Maine disaster; and now ask us
through its management to appoint a suit-
able day on which the members of the
Church may hold Memorial services and
take up collections for the Maine Monument
Fund, we have decided to designate Sun-
day, the 24th of July, as the day on which
such services shall be held.
	It is our desire that the Church of Jesus
Christ of Latter-day Saints should be iden-
tified with all such patriotic movements as
the erection of this monument to the
nation’s honored dead, that all our fellow
citizens may know that our sympathies
both in its joys and in its sorrows, are with
our country. We therefore ask the Presi-
dents of Stakes and the Bishops of wards
throughout the Church to make the neces-
sary arrangements for holding special
Memorial services at the time designated,
and at the conclusion thereof to take up a
collection from the people to assist in the
erection of a monument to the Maine mar-
tyrs.
	All money collected should be promptly
forwarded by the Bishops to Elders Heber
J. Grant and B. H. Roberts, who have
been appointed a committee to take charge
of this business. Address them at 20—26
south, East Temple street, Salt Lake City,
Utah. Your Brethren,
WILFORD WOODRUFF,
GEORGE Q. CANNON.

WOMAN’S WORK IN WAR.

	Nowadays one has daily the war and the
news pertaining thereto with all its varia-
tions of battles fought and unfought on sea
and on land. To publish a paper that con-
tained no war news at all would be an ex-
ception to the general rule, for we have
war news the first thing in the morning
and the last thing at night, and one dreams
of the war waking or sleeping; and yet to
give anything like a summary of reliable
war dispatches in a little paper only pub-
lished at intervals of two weeks or more is
out of the question.
	The news which makes one fear and
tremble at present is the sickness prevailing
in those hot climates where our soldiers are,
and if the people ever need to exercise
mighty faith it is in view of the fevers pre-
valent in the camps of the soldiers in hot
weather. When one reads that Gen. Shafter
reports 3,770 cases in the army in Cuba,
which it is said gives the war department

[Column 2]
concern, how is it with the mothers, wives
and sisters as well as sweethearts of the
men and boys who have gone forth in the
full strength and glory of their physical
health to fight for their country? It is in-
deed a serious matter and when women feel
they can only watch and pray at home how
difficult it is to be reconciled. Tennyson
says:

			‘More things are wrought by prayer,
Than this world dreams of. Wherefore let thy
	voice
Rise like a fountain for them night and day.”

Joanna Bailey:
					“A goodman’s prayers
Will from the deepest dungeon climb heaven’s
	height
And bring a blessing down”

Young in his “Night Thoughts:”

“Prayer ardent opens heaven, lets down a stream
Of glory on the consecrated hour
Of man in audience with the Deity,”

	Coleridge in the Ancient Mariner: “He
prayeth best who loveth best.” And wo-
man it is conceded, loves most and truest
her dear ones therefore should never cease
to pray, God grant us grace to do our part
as women if it be only in prayer, and let us
also pray for peace.
	But there is work to be done for the sick
and distressed and all who can should lend
a helping hand to the Red Cross and War
Relief Association in some of the various
methods and ways in which actual help is
being given to those who have not the op-
portunity of doing or getting necessary
things for their own comfort and relief or to
prevent pain or suffering.
	It is difficult to understand how women
can sit idly by and not endeavor to aid in
some way those who at this particular time
need positive help, and so as well as to
watch and pray, there is also work; and to
be at work is sometimes a panacea for pain
of mind or heart. Those who want to
give and have not the means, some of them
at least can give time and thought and by
the needle and in various other ways assist
in the Red Cross or Relief work. A few
women are giving of their time, their
means, their hearts to this noble work for
those deprived of home and friends and
the things they are now needing and to
which they have been accustomed when in
the home circle. The widow’s mite is just
as acceptable now as when our Savior was
upon the earth, and the beautiful saying
holds good everywhere and at all times,
and so one should not hesitate to give a lit-
tle.
	The overtures from Spanish government-
al powers are somewhat encouraging in
favor of an armistice but not altogether ac-
ceptable, the taking of Manila, the victory
of Santiago, Dewey’s wonderful achieve-
ments, and all the advantages so far known
do not give us peace and there is great un-
certainty as to the future, and the sorrows
and cruelties and barbarisms of war make
one shudder at the very thought, and to be
in the midst of such scenes is more than
terrible.
	We had hoped to give here an account of
Red Cross work in the State but could not
get an entire report nor an approximate, we
know, however, that it has been very credit-
able and will give the figures and particu-
lars later.

PIONEER PARK.

	On Monday, July 25, fifty-one years after
the arrival of the Pioneers in the valley of
the Great Salt Lake, the square on which
the first human habitations were built in
this desert land (now a flourishing state)
was publically dedicated with appropriate
and impressive ceremonies, conducted un-
der the auspices of the Mayor and the City
Council. President Wilford Woodruff, the
veteran pioneer now ninety-one years of
age, was seated upon the platform, also
President George Q. Cannon, who came in-
to the valley a few months later, and a few
members of the old City Council of long
ago and other citizens, also a few promin-
ent women, among the number being Mrs.
Zina D. H. Young, Mrs. Jane B. Taylor,
Mrs. Maria L. Nebeker; the two last named
came in 1847. The patriotic music was
inspiring, the singing excellent, opening
prayer by Dr. T. C. Iliff and address by
Mayor Clark both particularly well adapt-
ed to the occasion, the Mayor rehearsing
the most notable events. Our revered
President made the speech of the day, heart-
felt and replete with memories of the time
alluded to.
		ADDRESS OF PRESIDENT WOODRUFF.

	President Woodruff was then presented to the audience by Mayor Clark and was
received with the warmest demonstration
of applause. The venerable Pioneer and
leader said in substance:
	It was fifty-one years ago yesterday a
little before 11 o’clock a. m., that he drove
President Young in his carriage on to the
spot near where the Knutsford hotel stands.
On his arrival in the valley, the speaker
had, he said, one bushel and a half of pota-
toes in his possession. He had covenanted
with the Lord that he would neither eat
nor drink until they were planted, and he
kept his promise. Others of the Pioneers
also had potatoes with them. They, too,
promptly attended to the work of planting.
And they were planted in the earth, not in
the moon, (dark or light) as Brother Orson Pratt objected to that. (Laughter.)
	There was a man in Nauvoo who built a
carriage to suit his own fancy. It had a
bed eleven feet long and was entered by
means of steps and door. This vehicle the
speaker bought and brought to this valley.
On the Monday morning immediately after
the arrival of the first Pioneers President
Young took some of the Twelve Apostles
and with them walked on a short distance
in a northwesterly direction; a halt was sud-
denly made and President Young striking
his cane upon the ground, exclaimed,
"Brethren, here will be the Temple of our
God.” "I didn’t ask him,” continued
President Woodruff, “who told him the
Temple would be there. I didn’t care. I
put a stake there and the Temple is there,
a monument to President Young’s foresight
and prophetic accuracy.”
	After passing over the ground which is
now Pioneer square the party entered
wagons and started for the point of the
mountain, which appeared to be about five
miles to the westward. The distance, how- 
ever, was much greater, being about fifteen
miles. They continued on to Black Rock 
where President Young plunged in the brine
and took a bath, being the first Mormon to
lave in its waters. He was followed by
other members of the party among whom
was a colored man. When the latter em-

[Column 2]
erged from the waves the salt brine dried
upon his skin and instead of being a black
man he was perfectly white. (Laughter.)
This transformation, Porter Rockwell de-
clared to be the first miracle performed in
the valley. (Renewed merriment.) But
when he washed himself in fresh water the
salt disappeared and once more the white
man was black. (Laughter and applause.)
	On reaching here the Pioneers were im-
portuned to continue their journey to Cali-
fornia, where it was believed their condi-
tion would be bettered. President Young
said he knew he was in the midst of a des-
ert with his people, but that would make
no difference. They would build a temple,
a city and a commonwealth here. When
the first message flashed across the new
electric means of communication the words
were, “What has God wrought?” and so
regarding the declaration of President
Young the speaker felt constrained to say,
‘‘What has God wrought?” in these
mountain vales that are now cities and
towns bedecked with temples of worship,
school houses, colleges and the various
conditions of an advanced civilization.
	Here President Woodruff paused briefly
and then said: "Wondrous changes have
come to this land and people since the arri-
val of the Pioneers, and I want to say that
the changes, progress and increase will be
just as great during the next fifty-one years
as during a similar period that has just
closed. The destiny of this people is be-
fore heaven and earth and nothing can stay
it."
	President Woodruff said he would speak
of one incident and only one connected
with the journey of the Pioneers hither. It
was suggested to him by the presence on
the stand of W. C. A. Smoot who was the
youngest teamster in the advance guard. It
occurred near the Platte river. One day
the little band of home and liberty seekers
were startled by seeing to the west directly
ahead of them, what appeared to be a great
black cloud close to the earth. To their
amazement they discovered on approaching
nearer that it was not a cloud, but a solid,
mighty, moving body of buffalo, probably
the greatest herd that had ever assembled
in the western world. There was great
danger of being crushed to death by the
mighty horde. What to do was a problem.
But after consultation a wedgelike column
was thrown out and an opening effected in
the herd. Among this animated mass of
the monarchs of the mountains and plains
they struggled on for their own personal
salvation for three days and nights. At one
time Brother Smoot lost a horse which
got mixed up with the buffalo which were
traveling not in a scattered condition but
just as close as animals could crowd togeth-
er. For a time it seemed as though the
loss would be permanent, but after a while
the horse was recovered.
	"It always was,” said President Wood-
ruff, in his closing reference to the incident,
“a mystery to me why that great body of
buffalo should have gathered together and
marched from its roaming place on the
slopes of the Rocky Mountains and moved
down on to the Platte. It never occurred
before; it never occurred since, and will
never occur again.”
	As a finale to his address President
Woodruff said he felt that it was his duty
to give President Brigham Young the
credit of having laid the foundation of this
city and state. The streets of Salt Lake

[Column 3]
City, for instance, were the widest and 
most beautiful of any city on the earth. In
the historic city of Jerusalem two loaded
camels found it difficult to pass each other.
In this city thirty of these caravans of the
desert could do so. President Young be-
lieved in fulfilling the commandments of
God. He believed in doing things well.
That was a characteristic of the Pioneers,
no matter what it cost. For instance, a
mountain was moved at a cost of a half
a million dollars for a foundation for the
Manti Temple.

	Congressman Wm. H. King spoke, after a
quartette "When the swallows homeward
fly.” The speaker believed somewhat in
hero-worship and added "All Hail to such
men as Brigham Young, Wilford Woodruff,
Orson Pratt, Erastus Snow and George Q.
Cannon for what they have wrought;” and
he closed his eloquent tribute to the Pion-
eers with the expression: "Today all hon-
or and adoration should be given to Wilford
Woodruff, the Pioneer of pioneers.” Judge
C. C. Goodwin spoke last and was, as usual,
very humorous.
	President George Q. Cannon offered the
dedicatory prayer which was in that happy
spirit which goes to the hearts of the audi-
ence. This was followed by three cheers
for the Pioneers and also three cheers for
the Mayor and City Council who had been
instrumental in having this spot of conse-
crated ground set apart for the benefit of
the people.

HOW TO CLEAN WINDOWS.

	In the annual work of spring cleaning
there is nothing more important to have
properly done than the washing and polish-
ing of the windows. Unlike other branch-
es of house cleaning, a dull cloudy day is
best for window cleaning as experienced
housekeepers say it cannot be so well done
when the sun is shining on the glass.
When ready to begin the glass should be
well wiped with a soft, dry cloth to free it
from dust, then should be quickly washed
with warm water to which powdered borax
is added and then dried. To polish the
glass after cleaning, fill a small muslin bag
with whitening to which a little powdered
borax is added, and dust thickly over; then
wipe off with a soft cloth and rub with old
newspapers Windows thus washed will
be beautifully clean and bright and will
keep clean a long time.
