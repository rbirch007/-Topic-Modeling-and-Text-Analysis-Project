				WOMEN.

	Brought up in a town where the greatest
pains were taken to educate women, and
graduate them with high honors, in con-
nection with the colleges—marrying a man
that I could look up to, and rely upon his
clear judgment—coming into this Church at
the age of eighteen, and finding that the
power of voting was given to women as
well as to men, I have not felt that bitter-
ness of spirit that many have felt, who have
been wronged by those of the opposite sex.
	I have said, and still say, that if women
can do a piece of work of any kind as well
as men can, they should receive the same
amount of pay, and not, because they are
women, receive a less stipend.
	Women in many nations are but the
pack-horses of men. Rude, uncivilized
and barbarian, men lord it in idleness,
while women labor to raise bread for them
to eat. Women in civilized countries, of
the laboring classes, have had many do-
mestic duties to perform, which to them
have generally been pleasant; generally I
say, for I believe, as women educate them-
selves and move in a higher sphere of
thought and action, the daily routine of
household employments becomes irksome.
But in the present state of society, how
can they be carried on differently? Those
who are able can hire help to have their
work done, but if all move in the same
plane of thought, or are not obliged to
work to earn their bread, who will there be


to hire? There is, it seems to me, but one
remedy for the women; to unite their la-
bors, as the United Order can unite them,
and as the Relief Societies have made a
beginning, whereby there will be more
time for intellectual improvement, than
can be now taken in the majority of house-
holds. I often hear the remark, "I really
do not find time to read," and I have some-
times felt as if domestic duties were so
urgent, in my own case, that I have to
forego the pleasure of reading and writing,
although every odd moment is spent in one
of those pursuits.
	"I spend half my time in thinking what
to cook," is another remark I sometimes
hear. Now, if the planning and cooking
came only once in a while, it could be en-
tered into with renewed zest, and the re-
mainder of the time devoted to something
else. The every day practical duties of
life should not be discarded, but every girl
should be taught to perform them with
ability, while at the same time they should
not be the whole work of this existence.
The dawning of a future era for women is
lighting up the world, and women will re-
joice to be emancipated from many cares
that now hinder increased usefulness. The
Lord is blessing the women of Zion; more
and more responsibility rests upon them,
and more is required at their hands.
	Then let us be faithful, even unto the
end, that we may receive our reward in
the presence of our Father.
	St. George.		EMILY B. SPENCER.


AN ANECDOTE

ILLUSTRATIVE OF A GREAT MAN'S LIFE.

	There had been a "Twenty-fourth of
July picnic" in one of the canons east of 
this city. The sun was declining in the
west, the festivities of the day were ended,
and the merry company had started home-
ward.
	At the side of the canon road trudged a
young man, with a bundle of bed-clothes,
an ax and a dinner sack upon his back. He
had, evidently, been stopping some days
and nights in the canon, chopping down
timber ready for hauling. One after an-
other of the vehicles, which were convey-
ing the merry-makers to their homes,
passed carelessly by, their inmates scarcely
noticing him. But after a while, a fine,
portly-looking gentleman put his head out
of a carriage and called to his driver,
"Here! stop and take that boy in." Ac-
cordingly, the poor wood-chopper was taken
up and carried a good portion of his way
home.
	I do not know who all the gay people
were who passed him by so thoughtlessly,
but the gentleman in whose carriage he
rode was Brigham Young.		L. G. R.

	A PARENTAL HINT.—When an accident
occurs, learn whether it was through mis-
fortune, carelessness, or wilfulness before
you pass sentence.  Accidents are fre-
quently of great service, and children often
learn more caution and real informatin
from their occurence than from fifty les-
sons.  Be it remembered that the perfec-
tion of science is owing to the occurrence
and remedy of its early accidents.
	DECISION of character is one of the most
important of human qualities, philosophi-
cally considered.  Speculation, knowledge, 
is not the chief end of man; it is action. 
We may by a fine education learn to think
most correctly, and talk most beautifully;
but when it comes to action, if we are weak
and undecided, we are of all being the
most wretched.  All mankind feel them-
selves weak, beset with infirmities, and
surrounded with dangers; the acutest 
minds are the most conscious of difficulties and
dangers.  They want above all things a 
leader with that boldness, decision and en-
ergy, which with shame they do not find
in themselves.  "Give us the man," shout
the multitude, "who will steph forward and
take responsibility."  He is instantly 
the idol, the lord, and the king among men.
He, then, who would command among his
fellows, must excel them more in energy of
will than in power of intellect.—Burnap.

WOMAN'S VOICE.

EDITOR EXPONENT:	
	Being much interested in the work now
in progress among the sisters, and also a 
reader of the  EXPONENT, I would like to
say a few words through its columns to all
its numerous readers. It is time that we,
as sisters and mothers in Israel, should
wake up and be alive to our duties. As
there are great responsibilities resting upon
us, I realize that we have no time to lose,
but should be diligent and zealous in doing
good and helping to build up the kingdom
of God. We are doing very well in this
part of the vineyard of God; we have over
fifty bushels of wheat stored away against a
day of need. We have got the young sis-
ters organized into a Young Ladies' Mu-
tual Improvement Association. Our Bish-
op, John T. Hart, and his First Counselor,
James F. Hunter, were present at our meet-
ing, and gave us some very valuable in-
structions upon being united, home indus-
try, and to let our clothing be the work-
manship of our own hands, and not follow
after the fashions of Babylon. Sisters, let
us awake from this slothfulness, and see
what we can do, by faith and prayer and
active work, in the line of our duty; bury
not in the earth the faculties assigned us
to improve upon, but trusting in the
power of God, who has promised to sustain
all those who strive in humility and meek-
ness to keep His commandments. Let us
come forward and do our duty, heedless of 
criticism. Do not let us content ourselves
with the idea that our help is not necessary,
that we have no ability, or talent, that can
be of any service in rolling forth the king-
dom of God. How many women have
thought they had no influence, that their
words and ideas were not of sufficient con-
sequence to make or mar another's happi-
ness, or that they were capable of helping
to establish those principles of truth re-
vealed from heaven in these last days; who
have lived and died without benefitting
those with whome they are connected.
Meanwhile, have they left any impression?
Yes, they certainly have! And this is, that
they let the golden opportunity pass, the
precious time go by, without developing
those inherent gems of worth and richness,
which the God of heaven implanted within
their souls to beautify and elevate mortal-
ity. Sisters, let us who still live, who have
yet a day of grace granted unto us, take
warning by those who have regretted in
their dying moments that they had not
lived better lives. Let us strive by faith to
do some good, to help our fellow creatures,
embracing within our grasp the living and
the dead—for this is one of the great fea-
tures of OUR TIME, that we can minister
for those who have not been favored of God
as we have been.	FRANCES B. HART.
	Alma, Weber Co., March 22, 1878.

		FILIAL DUTY.

BY HANNAH T. KING.

Parents are the richest treasures
	Heaven on children can bestow;
Purest passions, sweetest pleasures
	From those sacred fountains flow.
Nor love's soft sigh, nor blush of beauty,
Are so sweet as filial duty.

Not the scent of budding roses,
	Not the tints of early day,
Nor all the fragrant spring discloses
	Breathing all the balm of May,
Nor love's soft sigh, nor blush of beauty,
Are so sweet as filial duty.

Lovers' words are very tender,
	Lovers' words are very sweet,
But through all they cannot render
	Joys so full and so complete.
Not love's soft sigh, nor blush of beauty,
Are so sweet as filial duty.

Blessings, many flow from wealth,
	Giving mighty power for good;
Legion are the sweets of health,
	Giving life its richest food;
But riches, health, and youth and beauty
Fail, if lacking filial duty.

		SCRIPTURE WOMEN.

	       BY HANNAH. T. KING.
			[continued.]

   "And the Syrians had gone out by com-
panies, and had brought away captive out
of the land of Israel a little maid, and she
waited on Naaman's wife."  Naaman was
greatly in favor with the king of Syria,
being Captain of the host, and was honor-
able, because by him the Lord had given
deliverance unto Syria; he was "also a
mighty man in valor," BUT "he was a
leper!" How often that little word BUT
causes the skeleton to walk out of the se-
cret closet, that every household, however
favored, possesses in a greater or less de-
gree! and it seems that the "little maid"
mourned over his affliction - for doubtless
they were kind and good to her, and she
loved them, and was grateful to them - and
she said to her mistress, "Would God, my
Lord were with the prophet that is in Sa-
maria, for he would recover him of his
leprosy."  These words were told to Naa-
man and led to an inquiry; this little maid
preached one of the gifts of the Gospel to
her mistress - the glorious gift of healing,
and the sequel was marvelous, for it brought
the afflicted man in contact with the pro-
phet of God, and by his obedience to his
words, he was healed!  The little maid be-
ing an instrument in the hand of God in
bringing about that glorious result; her
name is not even given, but her sex and
occupation is all that she is designated by
--but the meditative mind will draw her
picture, as a pure, affectionate, grateful and
appreciative child of God, and it is sweet
and beautiful in its brief simplicity, and
in its glorious immortality, and a subject
for contemplation by the young of every
age, and as an example to them to follow
and imitate.
   I have omitted some minor characters in
the Old Testament, though they are very
interesting and might be elaborated, but in
a small paper, brevity should be the motto
of every writer, as far as possible; it has
become an aphorism that "brevity is the
soul of wit."  I now pass on to the Apocry-
pha, and describe a noble woman, whose
deeds of valor have immortalized her
name.
   I refer to the character of Judith, - a
most remarkable one.  In these days, when
women have become degenerated and ef-
feminate by ease, refinement and luxury,
we can hardly realize the grandeur of such
a character as Judith, where piety of the
highest order, elegance of manners, dress
and address, are all blended with the un-
flinching hardihood of the boldest heroism,
and where every step is taken as in the
very presence of her God and under his
sanction and approval!  Her diplomacy for
the honor and salvation of her nation and
people, is of the most refined and exalted
character; her tact, her address, her
prayers, her movements are all made as
with the All-seeing eye upon her!  All this
and much more is subject for deep contem-
plation; and be it ever borne in mind there
was no murder in her act; she shed not one
drop of innocent blood; Holoferness was the
avowed enemy of her people, waiting only
for the opportunity to destroy them; he
was a foul-minded man, meditating her
seduction.  But in all her interviews with
him, she held him by, as it were, a fasci-
nation; her beauty, her heroic virtue, made
him virtually her slave!  Let woman ever
bear in mind that a pure, chaste and good
woman, with tact and talent, can hold even
a libertine at arm's length, and he can no
more touch her rudely than he could touch
an angel!  Such a character awakens the
dormant divinity in the nature of man,
and has been often known to wield a power
over him that has called out the glory of
his manhood, crushed down the low pas-
sions that have obtained the mastery over
him, and raised him upon a platform from
which he has never again descended.  Has
woman this mighty power?  Yes, indeed,
she has, if she only knew the nature God
has given her!  Judith delineates this char-
acter in a remarkable degree; of course,
the Spirit of God ever attends such charac-
ters, and in His strength they conquer.
In her glorious song of triumph she says,
speaking of what Holoternes intended to
do, - "But the Almighty Lord hath disap-
pointed them, by the hand of a woman!"
The women crowned her with an olive
wreath - the emblem of victory - and "she
went before all the people in the dance,
leading all the women; and all the men
of Israel followed in their armor, with gar-
lands, and songs in their mouths."  Then
follows the song of Judith, which is full
of all that constitutes a Saint of God, and a
soldier of Christ!  And the people gave
her all the riches of Holofernes, which she
gave "for a gift unto the Lord."  And after
the great feasting and rejoicing which
were made by the people in her honor, she
returned into her own possessions, being
held in honor by the whole country.  She
remained a widow to the day of her death,
when they buried her in the love of her
husband, Mauasses, she being one hundred
and five years old.
   In contemplating this character, Joan
D'Arc, Charlotte Corday and others of the
same caste, rise before us, and we feel they
are of the same constellation.
   Mary, the mother of Jesus, is the first
woman named in the New Testament, but
few words are used to introduce her, but
how full are those words! a volume in every
sentence!  There is something very strik-
ing in the sublime brevity of Scripture; in
a few verses her history is given, her line-
age, the salutation of the angel, and nearly
all is told.  A few words are used in after
times respecting her, which give a key to
the purity and contemplative tendency of
her mind and character.  She appears once
with her Savior-Son at the marriage in
Cana of Galilee; and she is at the foot of
His cross on Mount Calvary, in the last tre-
mendous hour of His earthly pilgrimage!
Yes, and she is among his last thoughts - in
almost his last words he committs her to
the care of One whom he knew would carry
out His last Will and Testament.  When
the heart is full, but few words are ever
used!  And when addressing One in whom
we have entire confidence, a look, accom-
panied by a few words, conveys a volume!
Hence the Savior only said, looking at His
chosen disciple and friend, "Behold thy
mother!" and turning to her, "Behold thy
Son!" "and from that hour that disciple
took her to his own home."  After the cru-
cifixion we hear no more of her - when
she died, or where interred, no mention is
made! - But millions, in their mistaken
zeal, have worshipped her, and do this
very day, addressing her as Mary, Mother
of God!  The angel's salutation is enough--
"Hail! thou  art highly favored! blessed
art thou among women, for thou has found
favor with God!"

	Mary Sullivan, the poor girl in New

shawl on a stoop, and pleaded before the

proprietor of a low liquor saloon in the

crazed woman in the street, Mrs. Addie
Oakman, enticed her into his shop and out-
raged her, was sentenced by Judge Gilder-
sleeve to "eighteen months imprisonment."

bigamy, who admitted in the Court of Ses-

ruining an innocent woman for life, under
circumstances of the basest deception and
falsehood! Only four years for ruining

children on the world, disgraced, nameless
and homeless! These are the fruits of
men's laws for women!	E. A. A.

mind, which is the fruit of resignation.—

A TRIBUTE OF AFFECTION.

	That buds and blossoms may spring forth from tears;
And bursting into beauty fragrant flow'rs
	Twine with the cypress bough through coming years,

									EMILE.

	Death has stolen from the wreath of our
friendship one of its fairest and most fragrant
flowers. Oftentimes the heart's deepest foun-
tains are opened and the pitiful sorrow, the
love, the friendship, the undying affection, the
promises of a life, the burial of hopes cherish-
ed for years, all—all find expression in scald-
ing tears that well up from the depths of
life's fountains. And so our hearts have been
opened, and one near and dear to us taken
from our midst.
	Last Monday evening a few minutes before
eight o'clock, the spirit of Miss Emma Wells,
daughter of Counselor D. H. and Mrs. E. B.
Wells, departed from its earthly tabernacle.
The sickness that had such an unlooked for
and melancholy termination, was of about
three weeks duration, and until but a few
hours before her demise had not assumed any
very alarming phase, and even until it was
positively ascertained that she was no more,
the faith of many in her ultimate recovery re-
mained unshaken. It would be a vain endeav-
or to attempt to picture the scene at the bed-
side of death, or to describe the feelings
awakened by the tears, moans, sobs and al-
most hopeless grief of her parents, sisters, broth-
ers, and friends, as in the depths of sorrow,
they awaited her release from earth, nor
would we desire to do so were it in our power;—
heaven forbid that we should awaken in other
hearts, the sorrow of our own, which must
necessarily result from a description of the
scene. Quietly she passed away and a smile of
sweetest expression o'erspread her pale features.
The funeral services took place on Thursday
morning, at 11 o'clock, from the residence of
her parents, and a large assembly of relatives
and loving friends gathered to attend the last
rites over the remains of this loved one. The
coffin was literally covered with beautiful and
fragrant flowers, the emblematic tokens of
friends, artistically arranged in wreaths, in
bouquets, and in festoons, draping the coffin in
Spring loveliness. Beneath the coffin lay her
guitar, garlanded with delicate flowers and
evergreens intertwined. A few of her dear
friends of the Tabernacle choir opened the ser-
vices, their voices mournfully pouring forth
music eloquent with sorrow. Bishop Woolley
offered the opening prayer, again the
choir sang, and Apostle Joseph F. Smith

[Column 2]
arose to deliver the funeral discourse. He
took his text from the book of Doctrine
and Covenants, quoting at some length from
the revelation pertaining to the different de-
grees of glory awarded in the life to come.
This he followed by bearing an undeniable tes-
timony to the truth of our faith, and spoke
with such beauty, compassion and force upon
the certainty of a future life that his remarks
thrilled every one present. The address was
brief, expressive and appropriate. Again the
choir sang, after which Bishop Lorenzo D.
Young offered a prayer which was most beau-
tiful and touching; and the services ended. The
friends then slowly passed to take a last look of
their beloved companion, whom they could
scarcely see through their tears; the coffin was
borne by the pall bearers to the hearse,
the pr[-]cession formed in quietness; selemni-
nity and order, and all moved toward the si-
lent resting place of the departed. At the
grave, Prest. of Stake, Angus M Cannon deliv-
ered a dedicatory prayer, consecrating the
spot and praying that God would comfort the
hearts of those who mourned. Perhaps of
all, this was the most trying scene, for many
who had contained themselves up to that mo-
ment could restrain no longer, and broke forth
into tears and piteous sobs, mingling with
those already weeping, and making it, indeed,
a most heart-rending picture. The sexton ful-
filled his office, the last tributes of bleeding
hearts were paid to the deceased, and all slowly
and sadly turned to depart.

		"Death, ere thou hast slain another,
		Fair and learned and good as she,
		Time shall have thrown a dart at thee,"

	Miss Emma Wells was born in this city,
Septemper 10, 1853, and was one of the pioneer
children of Utah. Even as a child she evinced
those qualities of heart and mind which, dur-
ing later years, endeared her to all whose for-
tune it has been to enjoy her society; and as
she developed into womanhood it seemed that
every attribute which draws all hearts towards
its possessor, had found a home within her
breast. She early became a teacher in Miss
M. E. Cook's graded school, continuing so for
a number of years, and the same kindness, in-
genuousness and humor that made her beloved
by her scholars and won for her the love and
admiration of her associate teachers, character-
ized every act of her life. Not only in this but
in every other respect was she cherished and
esteemed. In all societies for improvement
and intellectual development she ever took a
most active and prominent part, and from the
incipiency of the Wasatch Literary Associa-
tion had been one of its most active and lead-
ing members. And is it wrong that we should
weep for such a loss? Can there be any sin in
mourning for one whose compassion for the
poor had become proverbial? whose nature was
such that it entered wholly into the afflictions
of others and forgot individual grievances in
solicitations for their weal? Whose charity
was such that,

	"E'en her failings leaned to virtue's side."

whose sympathy was ever laboring for the wel-
fare of her race? whose appreciation of all that
was beautiful in nature, art music and poetry
was the most exquisite? whose enjoyment of
life and happiness inspired all with a kindred
feeling? whose earnestness of purpose, integrity
of character, adherence to principle, love of the
Gospel, filial duty, and implicit faith in and
reverence for the Eternal Father were the out-
growth of an angelic nature? No! We cannot
think so. God gave these to us, and expects
that we shall enjoy them and mourn their loss;
but with a moderation and faith in His All-

[Column 3]
wise purpose which she herself would have
evidenced and admonished.
	It is said "The bier is the cradle of heaven,"
and we who believe this should find consola-
tion in the faith. The holy dead! how many
have gone, whose walk in life has been the ad-
miration of all who knew them? How good,
how pure, how happy are they—the blessed
dead. "It might make one in love with death,"
to know that we shall meet them when our
time has come to go. Is not, oh! is not this a 
balm for woe?

	"These taught us how to live, and oh! too high
	A price for knowledge, taught us how to die."

	Is not the hope, the faith, the knowledge
that if we live aright we shall again be partak-
ers of the pleasures of their society, sufficient
to induce us to emulate one whose sweetness of
disposition, like the fragrance of flowers, per-
vaded the atmosphere, and shed an indescrib-
able balm, that was felt and acknowledged by
all who came within her sphere? Whose kind-
ness of heart was

					"Like the sweet south,
		That breathes upon a bank of violets,
		Stealing and giving odor?

and whose purity of soul was such, that, com-
pared with it, snow would lose its whiteness.
Here is a noble incentive, and oh! let us labor
for so bright and promising and so joyful an
end. So long as virtue is admired, so long as a
love of our fellow creatures is cherished, so
long as compassion warms the heart, so long as
patience, truth, intelligence, principle, charity
and contentment exist within our hearts, so
long as we have faith in the innate goodness
of the human, and are loth to think or say ill
of another, so long as sympathy for distress
can move blue-eyed sympathy to tears, so long
will her memory remain engraven on our
hearts; for they but serve as a remembrance of
one who was possessed of them all.

HINTS TO THE YOUNG.

	Often, in this life of toil and preparation, we
find those most gifted in many respects, accom-
plishing the least amount of good to them-
selves and for the world. The chief causes of
these unsatisfactory results, may be considered
want of application and misapplication.
	Time is allowed to pass almost unheeded by
some, who vainly imagine they find as much
happiness in ease and idleness as they would
in anything else. Yet the merciful Creator has
given them brains to exercise, and hands to
work with, just the same as the most industri-
our possess.
	And again, many studious thinkers and hard
workers are thinking and working in the
wrong direction. Even here among the cho-
sen and favored people of God, may be found
those who labor incessantly for the vain and
perishable things of earth. We do not antici-
pate, in this short article, to approach any who
are thus, already turned away from the
"straight and narrow path." But if we may


chance to reach the hearts of some of our
young brothers and sister, to whom a few
hints on these subjects may not come amiss,
we shall have accomplished our purpose.
	To our young friends then—children of Lat-
ter-day Saint parentage,—we will offer an ex-
hortation: That you try to realize the high pos-
ition to which your birth entitles you. You
have no need to seek, through any talents or
graces with which God has blest you, to gain
wealth or power or popularity in the eyes of
the world. Let your high ambition and noble
aims be to sustain the position you already oc-
cupy as members in the kingdom of God.
Give cheerfully the time and talents, with
which He blesses you, to His service, and sure-
ly no good or desirable thing will be withheld
from you.
	While you are yet young and inexperienced,
let the voice of your parents be the voice of
God to you; follow its dictates and you will
not be led astray.
	Let your religious duties be first with you in
all things. When you undertake to accom-
plish any good thing, begin with asking the
blessings of the Lord upon your efforts. And
when you have succeeded, before congratula-
ting yourself, thank Him for His divine assist-
ance.
	Sometimes people talk about the sacrifices
they have made for the Gospel's sake. But the
Gospel contains so mnch that is desirable, that
to gain a knowledge of it—a testimony of its
truth—is worth everything beside.
								L. G. R.

OBITUARIES.

					KAMAS, March 26th, 1878.

EDITOR EXPONENT.

	It is our sad duty to record the death of our
dearly beloved sister Lavina B. Mitchell, who
died in Kamas March 22nd, 1878, of inflama-
tion of the lungs. She had been a member of 
the church for many years, and had passed
through many of the persecutions of the
Saints. She had embraced the principle of
Celestial Marriage in which she lived a true
and noble life. Rearing children and teaching
them the true principles of the gospel, was a
duty that she dearly loved and cheerfully per-
formed. One orphan child she took at the age
of three months and nursed him as her own,
the maternal love was scarcely discernible
from that which she felt for her own offspring,
and deeply and truly was her love returned. She
bore twelve children five of whom died in in-
fancy One, her eldest daughter had died two
months previous to her mother's decease. This
was a dreadful blow to the fond mother, she
had often expressed herself satisfied with life
but now she prayed for life and strength that
she might help to care for the five motherless
children that her daughter had left. But God
had ordered otherwise and she has gone to
meet the reward of the noble deeds done in
the body. Her kind affectionate disposition
made her beloved by young and old. She
leaves a large circle of relatives and friends to
mourn her loss.
					MRS. LAURA C. PACK.

sympathies are with this young lady. The fu-
neral services were conducted at the 13th Ward

NIGHT.

	Night is welcomed by the tired and
weary. Where one has been working hard
all day long, with what eager anticipations
the night is looked forward to, when our
weary bodies can rest in sweet sleep, until
the dawn of another day. The young and
the gay like to spend the evening hours of
the bright moonlight nights in pleasure of
some kind, and some take delight in tak-
ing "a ramble on a starry night;" but night
is e best appreciated by those who have
worked hard all day; they feel like ex-
claiming "Blessed boon! given that we
might rest from the toils and cares of the
day just past, and lie down through the
calm, still night, in sweet repose, forgetful
of all that is past, or that which is to come."
	Nights are sometimes really beautiful
and majestic, and others are dark and give
a solemn awe unto all nature. If we go
out on a clear, cold night and look at the
dark blue heavens, what a picture of perfect
beauty meets our eyes. There, stretched
out to our gaze, is one vast polished mirror,
with countless millions of twinkling stars
bedecking the whole; and perchance while
you are gazing at the wonders of that 
splendid scene, one or two brilliant orbs
may shoot across the heavens with light-
ning speed, leaving behind a streak of
golden light.
Then comes the bright, full moon slowly
creeping behind yonder mountain. Blessed
light of night! Thou art welcome. thrice
welcome to the weary traveler, who is toil-
ing wearily along o'er rugged mountain
roads, where it would be unsafe to go, if it
were not for the silvery light of the blessed
moon.
	I remember going upon a short trip, with
my father, to Pine Valley. After we had
passed the Twist and traveled some dis-
tance along the sandy road, I (being very
timid of the natives, and my mind wrought
up by imagination) felt alarmed. The
bright moonlight shone upon the white and
red sandstone hills and mountains, which
here and there would be left in utter dark-
ness by the solemn shadows. I laid down
in the wagon and tried to sleep, but in
vain, for every dark shade of a mountain,
tree, or shrub, would be shaped into Indi-
ans in my mind; I could distinctly hear

[Column 2]
their tread, and with almost breathless
specch would tell father that there were
lots of Indians around. He looked out but
failed to discover any. Then I would sit
up and strain my eyes to find their hiding
place, and wondered where they had fled.
Thus imagination kept my mind in the 
greatest horror and dread. That night was
not one of pleasure but one of terror, until
we camped, and sweet sleep visited my eye-
lids.
	In the colder North, on winter evenings,
there are many joyful crowds of young
folks, with warm blankets and furs, snugly
seated in sleighs and cutters, the horses
well decked with numbers of tiny bells, as
away they bound over the crust of the
beautiful snow, the bells filling the air with
music, making, as it were, more complete
joy for the merry crowd.
There are many enjoyments for the young
on winter evenings—lectures and other lit-
erary entertainments are devised for their 
benefit, and on such occasions they may
learn many good things; the dance and
theatre are great sources of amusement.
Home must not be forgotten, for around
the cheerful fireside, there are many hap-
py times, with the good counsel of father
and mother, plenty of good books to read,
and many social pleasures for those who
appreciate "Home, sweet home."
								E. S.

CORRESPONDENCE.

	SALT RIVER, March 26th, 1878.
DEAR AUNT EM:
	We have been encamped on the beauti-
ful river now nearly two months, it is about
twice as large as Bear River, clear and
beautiful to look at, but brackish to the
taste, though not unpleasant now that we
have become accustomed to it, and do not
mind it. The company have had good
health generally speaking, but some are
complaining now, I suppose because they
are getting acclimated.
	There have been born to Brother Cris-
mon on the journey, a daughter, a grand-
daughter and great granddaughter, they
are at present three beautiful babes and are
growing finely.
	This country affords a broad field for re-
flection and thought, there are traces of its
having at some period been a densely pop-
ulated country; and there seems to have
been three distinct grades, or races of peo-
ple, which the present tribes know nothing
of. There is an old ruin about twelve miles
from here, built something after the man-
ner of an old English castle, though it is
now nearly destroyed; the mote around the
pile is clearly defined. Our company are
taking a ditch out of the river to intersect
an old canal which runs twenty or thirty
miles through what has been farms and cul-
tivated lands, so level and even that one
can see away down to the Gila. The land
sloping gradually from the Salt River to
the Gila. The canal is as clearly defined as
the Cottonwood canal is, except near the
river which has changed its course and
washed the land upon its banks. The pres-
ent people know nothing of the builders;
they can only tell us that they lived and
died many hundred years ago. The native
population are mostly Mexicans, or a mixed
race; they are a very indolent, thriftless
people, one frequently sees houses looking
as though they had been built many years
without evidence of ever having had eith-
er doors or windows; naked and bare, with-
out vine, tree or shrub for ornament or com-

[Column 2]
fort, barely a shelter and nothing more.
There are some white men with Mexican
wives but they seem to have imbibed more
or less of the habits and tastes of the
"Greasers." There are a few wealthy white
men in the country who own and control
some very good farms.
	There are two or three Indian villages five
miles from our camp, the Indians are farm-
ing, but in a very primitive way; they plow
with a crooked stick, and lash a stick to
the oxens horns' for a yoke. I cannot see
as they are in any way superior to the sav-
age tribes of the north, either in intelligence
or cleanliness; they seem harmless, except
in thieving small things, and killing some
poor, worn-out stock for food. They are
the Pimos and Maricopas with a few oth-
er small tribes, and splits from these, each
having a name of their own.
	In a country where vegetables will grow
the year round, there are scarcely any to be
bought; an old resident told me that she
had gathered tomatoes from the vine for
her Christmas dinner, by throwing a light
protection over the vines. The climate is
indeed beautiful, plants, trees and shrubs
are blooming and lovely, and it seems
more like June than March to us; although
we are living in tents and wagons, we suf-
fer no inconvenience from the weather. I
have not seen an EXPONENT since I left
Utah, I would feel like meeting an old
friend to see one.
	With kind love and best wishes to all I
remain,
	Yours, as ever,
		FRANCELLE E. ROBSON.


MEDICAL SCIENCE FOR WOMEN.

	In reading, the other day, a speech on
the Medical Education of Women, it struck
me that the most important reason for it
was, after all, one which the speaker had
not mentioned: the fact that the medical
profession stands for science; and that wo-
men peculiarly need science, since their
natural bent is supposed to be a little the
other way. The other professions repre-
sent tradition very generally; the lawyer
must be bound by precedents; the clergy-
man generally admits that he must go
back to his texts. But the physician claims,
at least, to be a man of science, and stands
for that before the world. Hence the sa-
credness with which his position has always
been surrounded. The Florida Indians, ac-
cording to the early voyagers, not only took
the physician's medicine, but they took
the physician himself internally, after his
death. All other men were buried, but the
body of the physician was burned and his
ashes mixed with water, for a permanent
prescription.
At any rate, the physician popularly
stands for science; and in this point of view
his position is really noble. I have known
physicians whose very materialism was
more elevated than most of what the world
calls religion. To trace that wondrous
power called life, which takes these parti-
cles of matter and makes them think with
thought or glow with passion or put forth
an activity so intense as to be the parent of 
new life from generation to generation, this
study is something sublime. He who rev-
erently ponders on this may call himself
theist or atheist, he is yet worthy to be re-
vered; if he can teach us, he blesses us.
"I touch heaven" said Novalis, "when I
lay my hand on a human body," and the
popularity among physicians of that fine
engraving of Vesalius standing ready for
his first dissection shows that they take a


higher view of their vocation than the
world sometimes admits.
	It seems to me peculiarly important that
women should have a share in these stud-
ies. They certainly often have time
enough. It takes more time for a woman
to make herself charming than to make
herself learned, Sidney Smith says; and he
thinks it a pity that she should often hang
up her brains on the wall in poor pictures,
or waft them into the air in poor music,
when they might be better employed. And
that they have the requisite gifts for medi-
cal study will be plain to any one who
reads the celebrated Essay of Buckle on
"the influence of women on the progress of
knowledge" and sees how he traces some
of the leading scientific discoveries of mod-
ern times, as those of Newton and Goethe,
to the exercise of that very faculty of quick
witted combination which is the character-
istic of women's minds. Nay, a great phys-
ician, Dr. Currie, says in his letters that
he always preferred to have an ignorant
patient bring his wife with him, because
he could always get more careful observa-
tion and quicker suggestions from the wo-
man. This point lies directly in the line
of medical education.
	This study lies also directly in their path
as prospective wives and mothers, and this
alone would furnish a sufficient reason for
it. A woman of superior gifts, who had
studied medicine, but never adopted it as
a profession, told me that the mere domes-
tic use of her knowledge had more than
repaid her for all the trouble it had cost.
For a man who should thus abandon the
pursuit, it would be of comparatively little
use, apart from the general training; but
for a woman, if she fulfills the more com-
mon duties of a woman's life, this early
knowledge will always be a source of direct
strength. This applies in a degree to sur-
gery also; and I have always wondered, in
view of the old proverb that a surgeon
should have "a lion's heart and a lady's
hand" why our professors did not oftener
aim at developing this heart, if need be,
in those who have the hand without train-
ing.		T. W. H.

THE LITTLE HEALTH OF LADIES.

BY FRANCES POWER COBB.

*	*	*	*	*	*	*	*	*

	We have now touched on the subject of
dress, which plays such an important part
in the health of women that it must here
be treated somewhat at length. A little
girl in a London Sunday School, being ask-
ed by a visitor "why God made the flowers
of the field?" replied (not unconscious of
the gorgeous paper poppy in her own bon-
net), "Please, ma'am, I suppose for patterns
for artificial flowers." One might antici-
pate some answer scarcely less wide of the
mark than that of this sophisticated little
damsel, were the question to be put to not
a few grown women, "Why do you wear
clothes?" Their most natural response
would obviously be, "To be in the fashion."
When we have visibly wandered a long
way from the path of reason, the best thing
we can do is to look back to the starting
point, and find out, if possible, where we
have diverged. In the matter of raiment
that starting-point is not hard to find—in-
deed, to mark it is only to state a series of
truisms.
	Human clothing has three "raisons

[Column 2]
d'etre," which, in order of precedence, are
these:
	I. HEALTH.
	II. DECENCY.
	III. BEAUTY.
	HEALTH demands—
	1. Maintenance of proper temperature of
the body by exclusion of excessive heat
and cold.
	2. Protection from injury by rain, snow,
dust, dirt, stones to the feet, insects, etc.
	3. Preservation of liberty of action to all
the organs of the body, and freedom from
pressure.
	DECENCY demands—
	4. Concealment of some portions of the
human frame.
	5. Distinction between the habiliments of
men and women, sufficient to avert mis-
take.
	6. Fitness for the age and character of
the wearer.
	7. Concealment, when possible, of any
disgusting personal defect.
	BEAUTY demands—
	8. Truthfulness. The dress must be gen-
uine throughout, without any false pads,
false hair, or false anything.
	9. Graceful forms of drapery.
	10. Harmonious colors.
	11. Such moderate consistency with pre-
vailing modes of dress as shall produce the
impression of sociability and sauvity, and
avoid that of self-assertion.
	12. Individuality: the dress suiting the
wearer as if it were an outer body belong-
ing to the same soul.
	Glancing back over the above table, we
find this curious fact: The dress of men in
all Western nations meets fairly all the con-
ditions of health and decency, and fails on-
ly on the side of beauty. The dress of wo-
men, on the contrary, ever variable as it is,
persistently misses the conditions of health;
frequently violates the rules of decency;
instead of securing beauty, at which it aims
first instead of last, achieves, usually ugli-
ness.
	It is to be remembered for our consola-
tion and encouragement that men have ar-
rived at their present good sense in dress
only within two or three generations. A
hundred years ago the lords of creation set
beauty above health or convenience, just
as the ladies do now, and peacocked about
in their peach-blossom coats and embroid-
ered waistcoats, surmounted by wigs, for
whose stupendous discomfort even a seat
on the judicial bench can scarcely reconcile
the modern Englishman. Now, when the
men of every European nation have abjur-
ed such fantastic apparel, we naturally ask:
Why have not the women followed their
example? Why is the husband, father
and brother, habited like a being who has
serious interests in life, and knows that his
personal dignity would be forfeited were
he to dress himself in party-colored, be-rib-
boned garments, and why is the wife,
mother, or sister, bedizened like a macaw,
challenging every observer to note how
much of her time, thoughts and money,
must have been spent on this futile object?
The answer is one which is not pleasant to
make, discreditable as it is to both sexes.
The women who set the fashions dress for
admiration; and men like women who dress
to be admired; and the admiration given
and received is a very poor and unworthy
admiration, not much better than a salmon
gives to a glittering artificial fly, and hav-
ing very little more to do with any real
aesthetic gratification—as is proved too
clearly by the thoroughly unbeautiful de-
vices to which fashion has recourse. It is
the well-got up woman (to borrow a very

[Column 3]
expressive phrase,) not the really well-
dressed woman, who receives by far the
greatest share of homage.
	And now let us see how all this concerns
the health of women—how much their
"petite sante" is due to their general neg-
lect to make health the first object of dress,
or even an object at all compared to fashion.
	Tight-lacing among habits resembles envy
among the passions. We take pride in all
the rest, even the idlest and worst, but
tight-lacing and an envious heart are
things to which no one ever confesses. A
small waist, I suppose, is understood to be-
long to that order of virtues which Aris-
totle decides ought to be natural and not
acquired, and the most miserable girl who
spends her days in a machine more cruel
(because more slowly murderous) than the
old "Maiden" of Seville, yet always a[-]sures
us, smiling through her martyrdom, that
her clothes are "really hanging about her!"
It would be waste of time to dwell on this
supreme folly. Mrs. Haweis, in her very
noteworthy new book, "The Art of Beauty,"
has given some exceedingly useful dia-
grams, showing the effects of the practice
on the internal organs and skeleton—dia-
grams which I earnestly recommend to the
study of ladies who may feel a "call." to
perform this sort of English suttee for a 
living husband. Mrs. Haweis says that
sensible men do not love wasps, and have
expressed to her their "overallishness"
when they behold them. Considering how
effectively they have hitherto managed to
display their disapproval whenever women
have attempted to introduce rational at-
tire, it is a pity, I think, that they do not 
"pronounce" a little more distinctly against
this literally moral folly.
	I have already alluded to the brain-heat-
ing chignons, just gone out of fashion after
a long reign of mischief; and along with
them should be classed the bonnets which
expose the forehead to the cold, while the
back of the head is stewed under its cush-
ion of false hair, and which have the still
more serious disadvantage of affording no
shelter to the eyes. To women to whom
the glare of the sun is permanently hurt-
ful to the sight, the necessity of wearing
these bonnets on pain of appearing singu-
lar, or affectedly youthful, constitutes al-
most a valid reason against living in Lon-
don. And the remedy, forsooth, is to hold
up perpetually a parasol!—a yet further in-
cumbrance to add to the care of the drag-
gling train, so that both arms may be occu-
pied during a whole walk, and of course
all natural case of motion rendered impos-
sible. In this, as in a dozen other silly
fashions, the women who have serious con-
cerns in life are hampered by the practice
of those who think of nothing but exhibit-
ing their persons. And ladies of limited
fortune, who live in small rooms and go
about the streets on foot or in cabs, are com-
pelled (if they wish to avoid being pointed
at) to adopt modes of dress whose sole
"raison d'etre" is that they suit wealthy
"grandes dame" who lounge in their ba-
rouches or display their trains over the
carpets of forty-feet-long drawing-rooms.
What snobbery all this implies in our whole
social structure! Some ten millions of wo-
men dress, as nearly as they can afford, in
the style fit at the most for five thousand!
	The practice of wearing "decollettee"
dresses, sinning equally as it does against
health and decency, seems to be gradually
receding—from ordinary dinners, where it
was universal twenty years ago, to special
occasions, balls, and court drawing-rooms.
But it dies hard, and may kill a good many
poor creatures yet, and entail on others the


life long bad health so naturally resulting
from the exposure of a large surface of the
skin to sudden chills.

THE LITTLE ONES AND THE OLD
FOLKS.

	People in the strength and pride of youth,
and the vigor of life's prime, are liable to for-
get that the weaker portions of the human
family—the children and the aged—are great-
ly dependent upon them for care and comfort.
	We have even heard young mothers speak to
their children as though they were actually
great troubles, and as if to wait upon them and
attend to their little wants were among the
crosses and vexations of life.
	We have seen the aged and infirm neglected
by those who should have been their most pa-
tient and grateful ministers; and their desires
being looked upon as childish and whimsical,
treated with positive indifference.
	It is well for us to remember, when we are
grown to manhood and womanhood, and
have more or less to do in caring for children,
and aged friends or kinsfolk—that only a few
years ago, we were ourselves little children;
and then we had our little faults, and our ne-
cessities had to be looked after the same as
children in these days. And again, but a
short time hence, if we live, we shall be "old
folks," and need the same care and attention
that our grandpas and grandmas do now.
	LULA GREENE RICHARDS.


How a dyspeptic was cured.—"I was turned off
by the doctors as incurable, but one was kind
enough to give me some good advice, and it, to-
gether with my own judgment made me a well
man. That was fifteen years ago, and I am well
yet. The rule I followed was to avoid entirely
pork and lard, and anything cooked or fried with
it; also hard-boiled eggs, doughnuts, mince pies,
or any kind of pie, tarts, or pastry; also white
wheat flour, no matter how cooked tobacco, liquor,
molasses of all kinds, corn bread, pickles, and all
kinds of nuts. I lived on the following: Sound,
unbolted wheat flour, stirred into boiling milk,
and thickened to suit the taste, and eat with fresh
butter and white sugar, or the same made into
light bread, twelve hours old at least; cracked
wheat, boiled or roasted potatoes, warm, never
cold, fruit of all kinds, raw or cooked, soft-boiled
eggs, broiled and boiled beef and mutton, rice, rye,
honnny, with moderate use of vegetables, beets
and cabbage very moderate. Eat no cucumbers,
and if you need acid, use lemon juice, or unsweet-
ened lemonade. Have your meals regularly, and
eat nothing between meals. Stick to this twelve
or eighteen months, and you will be a well man.
