THOUGHTS AND MEMORIES OF A
SUMMER HOUR.

Mem'ry is busy in her haunted bow'r
	Clad with bright thoughts the senses to beguile;
So softly falls the touch of passing hour,
	That dreamy fancies linger near the while.

I hear soft voices mnrmur'ng low and sweet;
	I feel the touch of gentle hands again;
And in the shadow of some lone retreat,
	I e'en forgot I ever suffer'd pain.

Tears fall and mingle with the pearly dew,
	And flow'rs spring up afresh where all was drear;
And something whispers of a life more true,
	Where perfect love will reign, devoid of fear.

There seems a rapture in the summer breeze,
	Borne from some region ever bright and fair;
How lovingly it plays amid the trees,
	Breathing "enchantment in the azure air."

O memory of the years now past away,
	Buried and dead, but living still in thought;
Nature was then as prescient as to-day,
	And her conceptions were as grandly wrought.

Earth rob'd herself as queenly then, as now,
	And summer mantled hill, and field, and plain,
And garlanded with flow'rs her blushing brow,
	And wreath'd her sceptre with the golden grain.

Yes, other summers have in glory woke,
	And sunshine flooded nature with its kiss;
But time's relentless hand the spell has broke,
	Though earth were in an ecstacy of bliss.

In the weird rustle of the falling leaves,
	There is a sadness touching to the heart
For summer her departing glory grieves,
	Yet nobly doth she with her treasures part.

She sighs her requiem to the passing breeze;
	All nature joins her in the mournful strain;
A thousand echoes whisper in the trees,
	Repeating o'er and o'er the sad refrain.

And is there in our hearts no sad regret,
	No mem'ries of the last year's falling leaves?
Shall we the sweetness of the past forget?
	Or glean, and garner it in mem'ry's sheaves.

Where fancy's magle wand can recreate,
	And live again the happy seasons o'er,
Food for the mind, 'gainst bitter, adverse fate,
	Harvest of thoughts to gladden evermore.

	E. B. Wells	EMILE.
Salt Lake City, August 11, 1877.

WOMAN'S RECORD.

	MISS Rose Hall, of Middlesex, England,
has allowed her goods to be distrained for
Queen's taxes, as a practical protest against
the exclusion of women householders from
the Parliamentary franchise. Twelve sil-
ver forks were seized and put up at auc-
tion.
	MRS. M. B. C. Slade is to be the editor of
a new monthly magazine, entitled "Good
Times," which will be issued August 1st,
devoted to entertaining, amusing and in-
structive dialogues, recitations, declama-
tions, and selections; tableaux; motion-


songs, and other musical varieties, for exhi-
bitions and public exercises.

	MISS Bettie Van Lew occasioned a slight
flutter in the office of the City Collector,
day before yesterday, says the Richmond,
(Va.) "Enquirer." Her appearance there
was perhaps explained by the following in-
dorsement on a paper which she passed
over to the Collector, inclosing $200, the
amount of her tax bill: "Richmond, June
29.—Taxes paid under protest, having bit-
terly realized the need of the ballot, and
being, as a woman, denied that right.—
Elizabeth L. Van Lew." This Miss Bettie
Van Lew is the plucky Republican post-
mistress of Richmond, Va., lately dis-
placed by President Hayes.—Ex.


NOTES AND NEWS.

	DR. SCHLIEMANN; the celebrated explor-
er of Trojan and Greek mines, is expected
to visit America.

	AN EXPEDITION, consisting of Lieut.
Wood, U. S. Army, and Prof. Taylor of the
University of Chicago, with some assist-
ants, has gone to Alaska, to ascertain the
height of Mt. Elias.

	A STATUE of King Alfred the Great was
unveiled at Wantage, England, on Satur-
day, July 31st, by the Prince of Wales. It
is sculptured in marble, and was executed
by Count Gleichen, the queen's cousin, at a
cost of about $10,000. Wantage is the place
where King Alfred was born.

	IT SEEMS to be conceded, that among the 
members of Parliament most likely to be-
come future Premiers of the British gov-
ernment, is Mr. Chamberlain who, having
accumulated a fortune in manufactures, is
now devoting his fine abilities to public af-
fairs. Just now the most rising member of
the House of Commons is Mr. Leonard
Courtney, one of the ablest and most schol-
arly of the leader-writers on the London
"Times." Both Mr. Chamberlain and Mr.
Courtney have distinguished themselves by
their advocacy of the Woman Suffrage Bill,
which seems to be rapidly gaining strength
in England.—Ex.

	The laboratories for women at the Insti-
tute of Technology are well worth the at-
tention of those who are interested in the
higher education of women. They were
established about six months ago, at the re-
quest and with the assistance of the Wo-
man's Education Association, and are de-
signed "to afford facilities for the advanced
study of Chemical Analysis, Mineralogy,
and Chemistry, as related to Vegetable and
Animal Physiology and to the Industrial
Arts." Any subject may be studied sepa-
rately; there are no classes, each pupil
working by herself under the immediate
and practical instruction of Prof. John M.
Ordway or of Mrs. Richards. The micro-
scopic work, and physiological chemistry,
seem especially adapted to women, and
offer honorable employment to those who
are skilled.—"Boston Correspondent Wor-
cester Spy."

			R. S. REPORTS.

	The General Retrenchment Meeting in
the Fourteenth Ward Assembly Rooms on
Saturday, August 4th, was largely attend-
ed. President M. I. Horne conducted the
meeting in her usual dignified manner, and
after the customary exercises and reading
of minutes by the young lady secretaries,
Mrs. Horne addressed the sisters in an ear-
nest and impressive manner, exhorting
them to live their religion, attend to all du-
ties, whether at home, or in whatever posi-
tion they might be called to fill. Counselor
Zina D. Young related the outlines of her
recent trip to Bear Lake valley; alluded
to the smiling farms and fields of grain,
and said that although the grasshoppers
had been plentiful there, yet the people still
had an abundant harvest. Mrs. Dr. Hard-
ing, a lady from St. Louis, Mo., who was
present, made some remarks upon what
she had observed here in Salt Lake City,
and the great kindness and courtesy which
had been extended to her by the Latter-day
Saint sisters. Mrs. M. R. Thompson spoke
upon the the great trials and persecutions
through which the saints had passed, and
the blessings which were in store for the
faithful. Mrs. E. S. Barney made some
good remarks, and read a chapter from the
"Women of Mormondom." Some busi-
ness was transacted and the meeting ad-
journed to the regular time. President
M. I. Horne announced that it was expect-
ed some of the brethren of the First Presi-
dency would be present at the next meet-
ing.

	MONROE, SEVIER Co., July 19, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	There is a Relief Society in this place in
pretty good condition, under the presiden-
cy of Mrs. Clarinda Washburn, who is alive
to the duties of her office. Although we
are a long way from the seat of govern-
ment of the Church, yet we rejoice in the
same truths of the Gospel; are very glad to
hear the good tidings the servants of God
bring from time to time. We have just
had a two days' Conference in this county,
received much good counsel, and been more
fully organized in the priesthood, and trust
under the blessings of God to be able to do
more in the future than in the past for the
building up of the kingdom of God. We
are a small community in this place, but
when the counsel of our venerable and be-
loved president came, for the sisters to store
up grain, we went to work with a will and
cast in our mite. We are also trying to
do the best we can to help roll on the great,
grand cause in other ways. Please pardon
us for not communicating with you before,
but will strive to keep you better informed
in the future. The instructions and gen-
eral information we get through the EXPO-
NENT we hail with delight .
	We remain yours truly,
			C. WASHBURNE, President,
					C. COOPER, Secretary.

						July 28, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	Enterprise having become a ward in the
organization of the Morgan County Stake
of Zion, the ladies of this place, who have
heretofore formed a part of the Peterson
Relief Society, under the presidency of
Mrs. Ann Peterson, met on Sunday eve-
ning, July 22d, according to appointment
and direction of the Bishop, J. K. Hall,

[Column 2]
and organized the Enterprise Ward Relief
Society. Mrs. Margaret Green, being
unanimously voted in as president, chose
for her counselors Mrs. Emma Croft and
Mrs. Mary Hall. The organization was
completed with a Secretary, Treasurer and
a corps of Teachers. The Bishop and his
counselors then gave much good instruc-
tion, explaining the object of Relief Socie-
ties, and the manner and spirit in which
the meetings should be conducted.
	Hoping that God will bless our Society
and labors, and the labors of all who are
working for the advancement of truth and
righteousness upon the earth; and that the
EXPONENT may continue to be a welcome
visitor to our meetings, realizing that we
have derived much lasting good from its
pages, we subscribe ourselves,
	Your sisters in the covenant of peace.
		MARGARET GREEN, President,

				BEAVER, July 10th, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	It has been some time since I wrote you,
although my heart is with my good sisters
in this Latter-day work, yet my means are
limited. I feel that myself and all that I 
possess is on the altar. I was down at St.
George to the April Conference, and en-
joyed it very much. My husband and my-
self were down since, and commenced the
work for our dead. My heart is filled to
overflowing when I contemplate the great
plan of redemption, and the great privi-
leges we have that no other people have
ever had. The saints are being endowed
with keys and powers in the temple of the
Lord, and the evil one is raging outside,
knowing his time is short. I felt as though
I would like to live there. The Kingdom
of God is rolling, and the puny arm of man
cannot stop it. Do the Latter-day Saints
realize the responsibilities resting upon
them, and the days in which we are living?
We have no time to trifle away.
	Our Relief Society meets regularly and a
good spirit generally prevails. We are in-
tending to build a rock granary and store
for home products, and do all we can for
the Sanpete Temple. The weather is very
warm here, and there is a great deal of
sickness among children.
	Maria Louise, daughter of Amasa Lyman
and formerly wife of Elder Wm. Clayton,
died here a few days ago. When she was
brought to death's door, I was told she be-
gan to ask what chance there was for her,
after pursuing the course she had taken.
	I remain your sister in the covenant,
						RUTH W. TYLER.

	The 20th Ward branch of the Relief So-
ciety has lately been reorganized, and is
now in successful operation, and, we trust,
CO-OPERATING with all other branches of the
Society, whose motives and efforts are to
accomplish good and assist the great Latter-
day work. We hold our meetings once in
two weeks—a working meeting and a
speaking or testimony meeting alternately.
Our teachers are energetic, earnest women,
and we have a goodly number of lively,
diligent members in our Society. Our ob-
ject is to make ourselves useful in looking
after and relieving the wants of the poor,
and also to aid in every enterprise which
the women of the Latter-day Saints are
called to engage in, such as assisting the
brethren in saving means for building Tem-
ples, storing up grain for times of scarcity,
promoting home industries, sustaining

[Column 3]
home productions and all these great co-op-
erative schemes. On the 13th of April last
we had a feast prepared for the poor, the
aged and infirm of the Ward, and a most
enjoyable time was spent by all who partici-
pated. Circumstances prevented our Bish-
op, Brother John Sharp, from meeting with
us, but his counselors, Brothers Dunbar and
Allen, and also Brothers C. R. Savage and
C. W. Stayner favored us with their pres-
ence, and assisted with appropriate hymns,
songs and music in cheering the hearts of
our aged and afflicted brethren and sisters.
The meeting was adjourned for one year.
In the evening a pleasant and very sociable
party was given for the younger members
of the ward.

		FILLMORE CITY, Aug. 7th, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	With pleasure I send you a report of our
second Annual Fair, held on the 24th of
July, 1877, by the Ladies of Fillmore,
which added great interest to the pleasures
of that memorable day, and all who partici-
pated in the day's recreation expressed en-
tire satisfaction with the articles on exhibi-
tion, as they were almost exclusively home
manufacture, and the handiwork of the la-
dies of the Relief Society—carpets, quilts,
rugs, knitting, netting, needlework of all
kinds; fancy articles, such as wax, zephyr
and hair flowers; bead work, mats, baskets,
and indeed almost all articles, both useful
and ornamental, were tastefully arranged
through the different apartments of the
State House, which was profusely decora-
ted with flowers and evergreens.
	The lively strains of music, and the
merry voice of song were heard at inter-
vals during the day, while the constant de-
mand for refreshments, so courteously
served by the ladies, did credit to that de-
partment. Everything passed off harmo-
niously, each countenance stamped with an
air of freedom, coinciding with the memo-
ries of the day.
	The object of the Fair was to exhibit be-
fore the young, such articles of home in-
dustry, as will lead to the cultivation of
taste and refinement, and for the more ex-
tensive promotion of home-manufacture,
which we are so urgently requested to sus-
tain; also to procure means for the storing
of grain, and for establishing sericulture
more permanently, as well as to raise funds
for distributing necessaries to those needing
assistance.
	Financially and satisfactorily our humble
Fair proved a complete success, as will
show by a profit realized of $160, (one hun-
dred and sixty dollars,) to add to our treas-
ury. The sisters in this branch of the Re-
lief Society are energetic and willing to
respond to the various duties devolving
upon them, and as we now have the privi-
lege of renewing our covenants, feel to act
with unceasing diligence in the great work
we have espoused.
								D. K.

				FAMINE. 

	Oh, sisters rouse[-] and sleep no more,
	A warning voice is at the door!
	Shall Famine be a welcome guest
	Where erst with plenty all were blest?

	Shall this gaunt being look upon
	Our happy children, sparing none?
	Shall this dire skeleton sit down,
	And we abide its fearful frown?

	Oh no! oh no! We still have power
	To make stern Famine fairly cower,
	And take its journey to a land
	That has not known a guiding hand.

	For we've been warned, and warned again,
	That Famine, such as ne'er had been,
	Should spread destruction round the ones
	That gave no heed to inspir'd tongues.

	So store up now the golden grain,
	And save yourselves from future pain;
	For many unto you shall come
	For work, for food, for peace and home.

	Go glean the wheat, you maidens fair,
	And for the days of want prepare:
	Lay up this treasure while there's time,
	Nor bow, nor bend at Fashion's shrine.

	Nor let the pride of Babylon
	Secure the wheat your labor won:
	Save up the fruit, save up the grain,
	That you God's blessing may obtain.
							EMILY B. SPENCER.
St. George, 1877.

		BY MRS. E. J. HARDING.

	A good woman is a jewel of far more
worth than all else we may conceive of.
Let her be wife, mother, sister or friend, if
noble in her aims, gentle and forgiving in
her manner, self-sacrificing yet self-reliant,
she is to society a refiner of all influences,
an ornament, a treasure.
	But if her education, her early training
be neglected, and she is allowed to mingle
with promiscuous company or companions,
whose minds and hearts have been neglect-
ed, her whole future will be maimed if not
wholly blighted by the contact. Hence the
necessity of early and proper training of
daughters to fit them for future usefulness.
Woman has a glorious mission to fulfill, a
grand and important duty to perform, if
she would reach her highest attainments
designed by Him who gave her, her being
and capabilities and intended her to pre-
pare herself with the necessary qualific[-]-
tions.
	If a wife, she is to so order herself as to
be companionable, and lend a charm to that
home over which she has been chosen to
preside, making it the most attractive pos-
sible, so that he, her husband, the compan-
ion of her choice, may love to dwell there-
in, because of his home being a peaceful
habitation to which he may always retire
when the perplexing duties of his daily
routine of business is over, and be re-
freshed by home comforts. Such an wife,
if her companion be intelligent and appre-
ciative, will be by him prized above rubies.

immeasureably increased, and the necessity
for mental culture two-fold. Then to her

		PATRIARCHAL MARRIAGE.

	Knowing that the order of marriage exist-
ing here, furnishes one of the popular subjects
of journalistic writers, and having read some
of these articles written by persons  unacquain-
ted with the subject, we propose to offer a few
ideas as briefly as possible, believing it is con-
sistent for woman's voice to be heard in her
own defense.
	Celestial Marriage, as the Latter-day Saints
term it, means something more than the sim-
ple condition of husband and wife, as that rela-
tion is understood by the modern world; it em-
braces in its covenant the ties of endless asso-
ciation, and eternal progression; this is what
is comprehended by those who enter into this
holy order of matrimony.
	With Mormon women it is not the idea of
many wives which so horrifies the world, but
it is the goal to be attained, the promise at-
tached to the covenant, which reaches far
away into the countless ages of progression,
and stretches backward to the redemption of
the dead, who passed away without the saving
ordinances. This is the dispensation, in which
the hearts of the fathers are to be turned to the
children, and the hearts of the children to the
fathers. These are the ambitions which stim-
ulate the saints to endure hardships, ignominy
and the world's disgrace. The world judges
Mormon women from their own standpoint,
and had they no more laudable ambition than
the world have, they would be just as wretched
as they imagine them to be; but they have a
greater aim in view, and live for a higher pur-
pose; and in order to rise above all things it
is necessary to descend beneath all things.
	The world does not understand the Mormon
Sisters; they stand as a race unprecedented in
history; an enigma to their contemporaries;
but their history is being written imperishably
in their works; they have not only assisted in
pioneering and founding a new territory in a
desert land, but in ushering in a new social
and religious dispensation. They have main-
tained their integrity and demonstrated their
sincerity, heroically, yet unassumingly, by
sustaining practically the principles of plural
marriage. All who advance new light upon
any subject are sure to encounter opposition
from the masses; it is one of the fixed laws of
that old inveterate tyrant CUSTOM; and more
especially is this the case, when traditions and
conventionalities are swept away, and a new
phase of things presented, without permission
of the "powers that be." Mormon women
have borne much for their religion; they know
in what estimation the world regards them;
they also know that God has spoken from
heaven, and true to the divinity of their na-
ture, they "stoop to conquer."
	Some of those noble few who first entered

[Column 2]
into these sacred marriage covenants, were wo-
men of strong individuality; women who held
themselves too sacred to descend to the level 
of marriage such as exists in modern society;
women of such axalted ideas of virtue and 
chastity, that they demanded the same purity 
of life from the opposite sex. In fact they
were of that strong-minded Puritanical race of
New Englanders, who inherited independence
of character sufficient to the emergency. They 
were taught the principle of patriarchal mar-
riage and they comprehend it in its fullest
and highest sense, as it appealed to the higher 
moral and spiritual nature; and on these
grounds they accepted its conditions, knowing
that it would eventually, more than repay the
sacrifice of the world's good opinion, if they 
could but suffer and be strong.
	We do not believe in destroying any of the
properties of our God given natures; but in
disciplining and cultivating all the holy affec-
tions, and emotions which will prepare us for 
the society of angels, and to dwell with celes-
tial beings. God gave to woman the blessing 
of maternity, and an abundant capacity for
love: not that she should be forever barren; not
that she should sacrifice her womanly instincts
for unholy purposes, but for the fuller perfect-
ing of herself, and her love through the powers
of motherhood.
	Plural marriage elevates woman, by develop-
ing more perfectly the sentiments of true love,
than any other marital relation, for its aim
and bearing is continually toward a higher 
life; and it enjoins the keeping of a higher
code of laws. None but pure-minded and high-
souled woman can sufficiently comprehend 
the design of these covenants, to endure the 
trials and temptations which are incident to a 
higher spiritual development. The actual ex-
isting circumstances which are consequent up-
on the relationships and obligations of plural
marriage expand the soul, and clothe it with
the Christian graces of character which make
life beautiful and honorable; patience, long-
suffering, gentleness, forbearance, meekness
and above all that charity which helps one to
love her neighbor as herself. these virtues
must be practical in the lives of those who ful-
fill the responsibilities which celestial mar-
riage implies. It does not narrow, but widens
woman's field for usefulness.
	The very genius and spirit of the age is in
keeping with the cry of woman for recogni-
tion of her position by the side of man. What 
is it purport? It is the consciousness in wo-
man everywhere, if even a latent spark of her
inherent divinity lingers, that the hour is has-
tening when the curse will be removed, "Thy 
desire has be to thy husband and he shall 
rule over thee." What is the burden of this 
cry, and what are "the signs of the times?"
"Behold the bridegroom cometh go ye out to
meet him!" Mormon women are practically
solving the questions which will result in a 
more complete purity of life.
			[handwritten] E. B. Wells.

	CITIZENS of Salt Lake seem drifting into the
habits of other cities in regard to summer re-
sorts. Several have built country houses for
summer use in healthful localities or near
springs considered beneficial. Others camp out
in the canyons or seek lodgings at the various
local hotels which have been built in these
mountain passes for the accommodation of
travelers.

	THE SALT Lake Theatre has been opened
the past week and some very celebrated plays
have been performed. Miss Rose Eytinge a
pleasing and popular actress has been perform-
ing "Rose Michel," "Miss Sarah Moulton"
and "Lady Macbeth," each of these characters
being represented in a natural and graceful
style. Miss Eytinge is one of Salt Lake's fav-

	THE LADIES EXCURSION to Lake Point Thurs-
day. Aug. 9, was a very enjoyable affair. The
day was fine, the lake was sufficiently calm
and placid and the bathing was truly delight-
ful. Notwithstanding the party was a very
large one, the enjoyment was not lessened, for
all seemed determined to enjoy it to the ut-
most. The bowery was very pleasant and com-
modious; and those who wished could obtain
refreshments at the hotel or on the steamer
General Garfield. Much credit is due to the
ladies' committee who had charge of the excur-
sion, for their successful tact and management.
These baths are wonderfully exhilarating, and
delicate persons or invalids should avail them-
selves of the present cheap railroad fares.

	THE "WOMAN'S Commission House" has
been undergoing some change in the arrange-
ment of goods etc., all of which it is expected
will be an advantage to the institution. They
have divided the store in the centre and all
the assortment of goods belonging to the store
previously, is on the south side of the large and
spacious room, and the other side is filled with
goods from Provo woolen factory, for whole-
sale and retail, which will probably bring
many customers to the establishment who will
patronize the Woman's Store by purchasing
from its variety stock. This store has been es-
tablished and sustained for the sake of encour-
aging home-industries and manufactures, an
object which should call for the special atten-
tion of all who desire the future welfare of
Zion. It is to be hoped the sisters will not let
their interest in this institution flag, but use
every effort possible to make it a complete suc-
cess.

were being planted all over the Territory
wherever the climate would permit them to

	"The Women of Mormondom" is the title of
a new work just out from the pen of E. W.
Tullidge, the author of the "Life of Brigham
Young or Utah and Her Founders," and other
creditable writings, as well as of several plays
which have been placed upon the boards here
and in other cities. This writer is original in
his style of composition and as Mormon Wo-
men form an entirely new subject, the writer
has an abundant field for the expression of his
originality. He has written some most glow-
ing and inspirational chapters on the themes of
Mormonism and Woman. Mr. Tullidge in his
characteristically happy and appropriate style
has addressed the preface of this work to the
Women of America:
	"Long enough, O women of America, have
your Mormon sisters been blasphemed! From
the day that they in the name and fear of
the Lord their God undertook to build up Zion,
they have been persecuted for righteousness
sake; 'A people scattered and peeled from the
beginning.'
	The record of their lives is now sent unto
you, that you may have an opportunity to
judge them in the spirit of righteousness. So
shall you be judged by Him whom they have
honored, whose glory they have sought, and
whose name they have magnified."
	A most striking preface, and one which be-
speaks the tact of the author. The book
scarcely needs the benefit of a Review for it
recommends itself more forcibly than it is pos-
sible for any writer to do in a newspaper para-
graph.
	Mr. Tullidge has so arranged the Table of
Contents that one can easily find whatever
they wish; the book contains 552 pages divided
into 60 chapters. This work is a record of in-
teresting incidents and episodes in the lives of
Mormon women from the earliest days of the
organization of the church. The author in
his closing chapter has thrown out some beau-
tifully suggestive and convincing ideas that
will almost force the reader to accept the Mor-
mon view of the subject. He reasons thus, "A
certain manifest destiny has made the marriage
problem the supreme of Mormonism. How
suggestive in this view is the fact that Con-
gress, by special legislation, has made polyg-
amy the very alpha and omega of the Mor-
mon problem. The Mormon women, there-
fore must perforce of circumstances, by their
faith and action greatly influence the future
destiny of Mormonism.

[Column 2]
	If it was Brigham Young who gave to them
that unparalleled power, no matter what should
be declared by the enemy as his motive, then
has he done more for woman than any man
living. But Mormon apostles and representa-
tives executed this grand charter of woman's
rights; and George Q. Cannon's noble declara-
tion at the time—that the charter of female
suffrage ought to be extended to the entire
republic—is deserving the acclamations of the
women of America.
	There is a providence in the very attitude of
Mormon women. The prophesy is distinctly
pronounced in the whole history of their lives,
that they shall be apostolic to the age. A new
apostleship is ever innovative. The Mormon
women have established an astounding inno-
vation in polygamy. It has been infinitely
offensive. So much the better[-] for it has made
a great noise in the world and has shaken the
old and rotten institutions of Christendom.
That shaking was not only inevitable, but nec-
essary, before a new civillization."

A FEW IDEAS.

	T﻿he newspapers and journals throughout the
country now abound with dreadful accounts of
strikes, and all the horrors attending them.
In my opinion this is only the beginning of
sorrows. This nation must drink of the cup
they have filled to overflowing, drink it to the
dregs. Ye have wrought us wrong, ye have
brought us woe, shall ye triumph much lon-
ger? We answer, no!
	It seems the governments are powerless to
bring order out of chaos, when will it all end?
When each one is willing to love his neighbor
as himself! Is there a remedy? Yes! When
man is willing that God shall rule on the earth.
What are the causes that have led to this state
of things? "Mans inhumanity to man!"
Those who have been blest with intellect and
energy, have, in many instances used those
gifts to trample down their less fortunate
brethren. They have vaunted their education
and despised those who were not book-learned;
they have trod them under foot, or made them
their stepping-stone to money, place and pow-
er, and when they have attained that which
they sought for, they cast them away as worth-
less.
	Will these Railway Kings who are now in-
volved in the striking, even look on their
employees, would they sit at the same table
with them? No! Yet they are the ones who
fill their purses, they furnish the bone and
sinew, have built their railroads and given in-
to their hands the fat of the land, and what is
the workingmen's reward? Reduced wages
until they starve, while they look on and see
their employers enjoying every luxury that a
pampered appetite can crave and the famished
wolf must look on contented with his lot. How
long can this state of things last? From the
highest to the lowest it seems like a struggle
to prey on one another's property. Those who
have been in power have shown the uneduca-
ted what position and influence of wealth can
do. It can rob a whole nation of honest work-
ers, shamelessly ride over every obstacle to fill
some of the highest places in the nation, and
when rascality is discovered enables the perpe-
trators to slip through the hands of justice un-
punished. The honest laborers cannot do as
the influential class can, so they use such wea-
pons as they possess, then they take fearful
vengeance on their oppressors; they are like
an unchained tiger knowing neither friend
nor foe, all are alike to them, they are unreas-
oning, fearless alike of heaven or hell.


It has been declared that education and en-
lightenment would save the world, and bring
the reign of peace. Who have been the ones
to inaugurate this awful state of things in
these once United States? It has been such
polished men as Belknap and Boss Tweed who
with refining insolence have robbed the na-
tion.
	Last conference President Young said they
would have blood enough before they got 
through, and his words seem coming to pass
already.
	Let us be thankful we are here in Utah and 
strive to be faithful to the end.
								MARY.

A LETTER TO WOMEN.

				CONCLUDED.

	I wish ladies would give this subject the at-
tention it deserves, and adopt, if not these ar-
ticles named, some others well devised, that
will, if not perfectly, partially meet the want.
I am convinced that if ladies only knew the
detrimental results or effects of weight and
heat in this region of the body, the spine, hips,
and abdomen, they would hasten to devise
some means of removal. I am not alone in
my convictions as to the great need, but have
the indorsement of the best medical authority
throughout the land.
	Every article of dress should fit loosely, and
tight, girting bands of any kind or form, be
wholly discarded, as they hinder or impede
free respiration, free circulation of blood, and
even easy, graceful movements of the body.
	When the ill effects of these stays and bands
that injuriously compress, are known, what
lady of even ordinary knowledge, will con-

sufficiently large so as not to confine either the
hands limbs or feet; and the latter, shoes,
should have always low broad heels to insure
comfort, not to say health, as the injurious ef-

given the matter an earnest thought.  The
high, narrow heel, prevents elasticity of step,
and with any attempt to step, the body re-
ceives a jar or jolt, very injurious to females
that have worn them, and in consequence been
obliged to discard them, as many can attest.
	Leather belts are decidedly injurious and
should never be worn. It would indeed seem
that style, fashion and present custom, all
strive to invent and offer for sale articles of
dress especially injurious, and that we blindly
adopt and continue their use in view of all the

SILK MANUFACTURE.

	In view of the growing interest in this
industry in Utah, the following sensible
article from the August number of the
"American Agriculturist," may prove pro-
fitable to some of our readers—
	In 1860 the production of silk in Amer-
ica was valued at two million dollars. In
1876 the value of the silk made in this
country was not less than twenty-five mil-
lion dollars. No branch of manufacturing
industry has advanced more rapidly than
this, and none promises to grow more stead-
ily in the future. But it is worthy of note
that the raw material for this manufacture
is wholly imported, or the exception is so
trifling that it need not be taken into ac-
count. At the same time no country in the
world possesses a finer climate and soil for
the rearing of the silk worm, and the cul-
tivation of the trees upon the leaves of
which it feeds.
	Unfortunately, the light, easy, agreeable
industry of breeding and rearing the worms
and reeling the fibre from the cocoons, is
not considered as being worthy the notice
of farmers, or farmer's families. Yet the
day of small things has not wholly passed
away, and indications seem to point to a
necessary return to the smaller industries
of the family, which have been abandoned
or neglected during the flourishing times
of the past few years. Everything is tend-
ing towards the exercise of economy in
small as well as great things, and an indus-
try which may profitably employ the chil-
dren on the farm, is not to be overlooked or
despised. The exhibition of silkworms
and their cocoons at the Centennial, did
much to inform the public as to the charac-
ter of this employment. The exhibit of
Mr. Crozier, of Silkville, Kansas, was very
interesting in this way, and it is to be
hoped may be effective in turning the at-
tention of farmers' wives who have more
than usual leisure, to the advantage of
learning the methods of managing the
worms and of teaching their daughters.
The reeling of the silk is somewhat diffi-
cult, but the machine needed for it is very
simple, and can be worked by a little girl.
	Several million dollars a year might be
distributed among farmers' families, that
are now sent abroad for the purchase of raw
silk, if only the attention of those who are
able to do this easy work were effectively
turned to it. In California, the raising of
silkworms's eggs is an important industry,
but in this the silk is sacrificed, or at most,
the cocoons from which the moth or parent
insect has escaped, have a very low value
for carding purposes.

EDITOR EXPONENT:
 A feeling of interest in the children
growing up around us, prompts me to ven-
ture a few words.
	Does every mother in Israel early teach
her  children to pray night and morning?
I feel that we do not, or have not realized
the importance of this, or many of our 
young who are now going astray, might,
through this measure, have been kept in
the line of their duty.  Mothers, are we
careful enough in regard to the examples
we set before the rising generation?  If I 
backbite my neighbor, can I consistently
reprove my child for doing the same?  In
dealing with others, if I fail to be strictly
honest, or if in conversation I am not ex-
actly truthful, shall I expect my  children
to act and speak in a rightous and just
manner? Not if I am a reasonable wo-
man, surely.
	Some mothers are actually so thoughtless
  to speak lightly of the Improvement
Institutions which have been organized for 
the benefit of our young people. Yet what
a vast amount of good these organizations
are calculated to do!  And many mothers
have left much to be taught their sons and
daughters, in the meetings held by these
societies, which they might and should,
themselves, have instructed their children
in, advancing them in the Latter-day work,
and rendering less necessary the many
meetings about which some of them com-
plain.
 The mother who unnecessarily leaves her
children to the care of others, is liable
through so doing, to meet with misfortunes
for which she will never cease to reproach
herself.  Think of a mother going from
home for pleasure and leaving her little
daughter in the care of a nurse who wants,
alsp, to take her pleasure and does not wish
to be bothered with the child.  So she fixes
some clothes to look like a person standing
at the foot of the bed into which she puts
the little one, telling her if she cries that
person will take her off!  The mother comes
home to find her darling positively dead
with fright!  Now she may rail at the 
cruelly, heartlessness and dishonesty of 
the nurse, who was paid for taking care of
the child and has proved unfaithful.
What comfort does this give her stricken
heart?  She can but reflect that she too has
proved unfaithful and unworthy of the 
priceless treasure which God trusted to her
care.
 Oh! mothers be true to your high and
holy callings, and find no pleasures so sweet
as that of ceasless care and watchfulness
over your children.  Govern them in kind-
ness- do not whip them!  We all have
pains enough inflicted upon our bodies be-
fore we get through with life, if we avoid
all harshness toward each other.
 We should teach our children as far as 
possible to understand and keep the laws of
life and health.  These are important prin-
ciples contained in the Gospel of Jesus Christ
which embraces all good.  I am satisfied
that keeping late hours in dancing and 
other amusements is very injurious; and I
shall be glad when the counsel which has
been given concerning these things, is prac-
tically carried out in all the cities and set-
tlements of the Saints.
	We move slowly as a people, but we do
progress; and I know that one great item is
to have charity one for another, to forgive
each other's failings as we wish our own to
be forgiven.  I pray that wisdom may be
given to parents in Zion, and that their
children may prove worthy to bear off the
Kingdom."

LONGFELLOW'S HOME.

	In 1843 the house was bought by Mr.
Longfellow, and from that time, with ten-
der love and reverent care he has adorned,
beautified and perfected it.  There is not
another house in America so full of fascina-
ting interest.  The very atmosphere is
different from that of other houses.  It has
a sacred hush, due in part, it may be to the
all-subduing power of association:
		"Once, ah once within these walls,
		One whom memory oft recalls,
		The Father of his country dwelt."
	Perhaps the most interesting room in the
house is Longfellow's study.  Here most of
the poet's hours are spent the quiet only
broken by the chimes of the old clock in
the corner.  It is one of  those antique
time pieces higher than a man's head, with
a round moon-face at the top, such as are
found in some old New England houses, and
are sufficient guarantee of the respectabil-
ity of the family.  An open fire burns
cheerily in the grate.  An orange tree
stands in the window, and near it an
Egyptian stork keeps watch.  A table in
the centre of the room is heaped with
books and papers, and has a look of orderly
disorder.  Its choices treasure is Cole-
ridge's inkstand.  Here, too, is an early
volume of Coleridge's poems, annotated in
his own handwriting, which is as scraggly
as that of a genius ought to be.  On this
table are piles of unanswered letters; only
a sharp-toothed mouse could get through
them in a month's time.  That which fu-
ture generations will regard with most in-
terest in this room is a bookcase filled with
Longfellow's own works in the original
manuscript.  They are handsomely bound
as befits the clear, beautiful writing, and
make a noble collection.  It is a pity to di-
vide them yet what one should menopolize
such a heritage!  Perhaps public gift will
eventually be made of them.  Some baby
now unborn, may donate them where they
will be safe through the generations.
Among the pictures here are crayon like-
nesses of Emerson Sumner and Hawthorne,
all taken when these famous men were in
the flush of youth.
 Passing through the hall we enter "Lady
Washington's Drawing Room."  The fur-
niture is white satin covered with gay
flowers in vines and clusters; arm-chairs
and sofas are heaped with soft cushions cov-
ered with the same material.  The carpet is
a bed of flowers.  The effect is heightened
by a large mirror opening another gay vis-
ta, and a picture in gorgeous colors extend-
ing from floor to ceiling.  It is one of Cop-
ley's—"The Grand-children of Sir William
Pepperell."  A quaint little maiden in a
high cap, and stiff bodice, a youth with
flowing curls, and a wooden-looking poodle
compose the group.  The picture is set in a
massive burnished frame, and the effect
would be oppressive in another room, but is
in admirable harmony in this state apart-
ment. On an ETAGUE, laden with treasures
is an agate cup, from the hand of no less a
master than Benvenuto Collini—clear, ex-
quisitely carved, graceful in shape and guar-
ded by two tiny open-mouthed dragons. It
was sent to him from the collection of the
poet Rogers and therefore, has a double
value in Longfellow's eyes.  As he holds it
in his hands and points out its various beau-
ties, one can but think what a crowd of pleas-
ant associations are gathering in its deli-
cate cup.
 In the dining-room we see rare old china,
a modern picture of a cardinal in red, walk-
ing in the Borghese Gardens, and several
family portraits.  Among them is Buchanan
Read's picture of "Longfellow's Daughter,"that has been photographed so often; the
"blue-eyed banditti" that the poet father
has so charmingly apostrophized in "The
Children's Hour:"
		"Grave Alice and laughing Allegra,
		And Edith with golden hair."
	The library is the most beautiful room in
the house; dark and rich in tone, with a
look of spacious elegance and home-like
comfort.  On three sides the walls are lined
with books.  The bronzes and screens are
studies. Here hangs a portrait of Listz.
The background is dark, and he is dressed
in the long black convent robe.  High
above his head he holds a lighted candle.
The rays shape themselves like a halo
round his head, and throw into fine relief
the thin, spirited face.  Mr. Longfellow
saw him thus for the first time as he stood
in the convent door peering out into the
night. The vision impressed itself upon
the poet, and he persuaded Liszt to have
his picture painted.
	Longfellow is now nearly seventy years
of age. He is of medium height, well-
made, with no sign of age in figure or walk.
His head and face are eminently poetic.
His forehead is broad, benignant and full.
The great charm of his face centers in his
eyes; of an unclouded blue, deep set, under
overhanging brows they hold an indescrib-
ble expression of thought and tenderness.
Though seamed with many wrinkles, his
face is rarely without the rosy hue of health,
and would appear that of a much younger
man but for its frame of snow-white hair.
Hair and whiskers are long, abundant and
wavy, and give the poet the look of a pa-
triarch.
	His manner has a child's simplicity, yet
is of an impregnable dignity.  Tolerant to
all opinions, courteous to all men, he is ap-
proached nearly by only the few.  When
with friends there is a dash of gentle hu-
mor in his talk more mirth-provoking than 
livelier sallies from wittier men.  In his
home his hospitality is proverbial.  Bret
Harte has called him the ideal poet, and
he is ideal host as well, entertaining with
gentle tact and exquisite courtesy.
 He is emphatically the poet of the beauti-
ful, and his life is as rounded and complete
as one of his own sonnets or a Beethoven
symphony.  He is not one of those great
men who must be seen, like an oil painting,
at a distance, but the nearer one approaches
the finer show the outlines and shadings of
his character.  He is still a great worker
composing with great care.  Time has taken
from him only the gold of his hair and
smoothness of his brow, and has given him
year by year, added grace, and sweetness,
and strength.  Wide-spread as his influence
has been, yet his mission has hardly begun;
for as long as the heart of humanity shall
beat, his voice shall be heard in tones of
music, singing words of consolation and
hope.—"Boston Times."

	A DREAM AND INCIDENT.

	My parents lived at Nauvoo and that was
my home. The incident I am about to re-
late occurred in the year 1846, just at the
time the Saints were moving out of Nau-
voo, and leaving their beautiful homes, and
the magnificent temple which they had
erected, at a great sacrifice, to the God they
worshipped, and in which they had hoped
and expected to administer for their dead

[Column 2]

friends, and receive the fulness of blessings.
On the night of the 7th of February, I re-
tired to rest early, and dreamed that I must
go to the Temple; I awoke in the night
and thought over my dream, went to sleep
and dreamed the same thing over again. I
arose in the morning and went about my
duties; something seemed to tell me that I
must go down to the Temple that day; the
impression was very strong—I felt as if im-
pelled by some hidden power. Excuse for
going, I had none; I told my dream and
feelings to my mother; she said I could go
down while the ground was frozen, but
thought I would have a bad time getting
home through the mud in the afternoon,
for we lived one mile east of the Temple,
and that was quite a long walk for me; but
I resolved to go, and started on my way. I
wondered to myself what I was going there
for, or what excuse I could make to those
whom I might meet for my appearance
there at such an inopportune time. At last
I remembered that Brother Bolton and his
wife were staying there, that they had
promised to go over the Temple and out on
the roof with me, and I thought this ex-
cuse enough. When I got there Bro. Bol-
ton was very busy and said I would have
to wait a while. The sisters were there
that day washing and cleaning up the house,
preparatory to leaving it; I went in where
they were washing, and conversed with
several acquaintances.
	Sister Bolton and I concluded to go up
into the steeple; walked around the dome
outside, wrote my name in several places,
among the thousands that were already
there, came down where Bro. Bolton was
still busy and somewhat excited, for the
news had just been received that the ferry
boat loaded with our brethren and sisters
had sunk while crossing the Mississippi
River. I then went out on the roof alone,
walked around there, viewed the city and
all its surroundings, until I was tired and
thought it was about time for me to be
going home. I was sitting down musing
and observing, when all at once I noticed a
little blue streak like smoke coming out
from under the shingles close to where I
sat. It frightened me; I arose in great
haste and ran to tell Bro. Bolton, but in a
second I thought perhaps it was only the
steam from the hot water where the sisters
were at work, then I comprehended that
could not be; but I would not needlessly
alarm any one, so I returned and sat down
again to discover the reality. The Temple
was on fire and no mistake. I hastened
down and told Bro. Bolton; he only laughed,
said such a thing was impossible, and would
not even go and see, until his wife went
and saw the smoke curling out in great cir-
cles; when she told him he started in a
hurry, and gave the alarm to some of the
brethren who were at work in the base-
ment. The bell was rung, which startled
the whole city; the scene that followed
beggars description. I did not have pres-
ence of mind enough to get out of the
house before the way was completely
hedged up, and I was obliged to stay there.
The brethren formed in two lines up the
great winding stairs, one to pass the buck-
ets of water up, the other to carry the
empty ones down. In the meantime a mob
had collected outside the door, to keep the
water from going in; the police had to come
to the aid of those engaged in extinguish-
ing the flames, before they could disperse
them. In the midst of this confusion, I
noticed an oldish woman, rather small in
stature, dressed in dark red clothes, run-
ning here and there wringing her hands
and at last disappearing no one knew where;


she was a stranger to all in the house, no
one could tell anything about her.
	Since I have been living in Utah I met
with Bro. Bolton; we were talking about 
that fearful circumstance, and he said he
could always see, in fancy, that woman;
she was always before his eyes, whenever
he thought of her; but he had never found
out who she was, where she came from, or 
why she was there.
	The fire, which was really alarming, was 
at length extinguished, the Temple saved,
and I had learned the meaning of my
dream. All this took place many years ago,
still the recollection is as bright and fresh
as if it had happened only yesterday.
	MATILDA P. KILLIAN.
Salem, Utah Co., May 23, 1877.

	Many of the laws on the statute books of
the several States deny to a woman, in cer-
tain cases, the control of her own property,
or even her own hard-earned gains. More
than this, that very motherhood which the 
guardians of Man's prerogatives declare to 
be so sacred is invaded and outraged by
laws which rob the mother of her children.
Women's wages are so pitiably poor and 
mean that many are almost driven to star-
vation or the street, and that too because 
they are crowded into a few avenues of
employment and crowded out of others by
men, or deterred from entering them by
the frowns of society. Who gave us the
right, as men, to say to women, "There are
certain things you shall not do, even to
make an honest living!" What right, hu-
man or divine, have we to stand, like senti-
nels with pointed bayonets, at the polls, or 
any other of the gates of privilege and pow-
er, and say to one-half the inhabitants of 
the land, "Hither you must not come?"
	But, above all, what right have we to 
command Woman to keep quiet and retired,
to be shrinking and timid, to shut herself
up in her home, and hide her talent away
in a napkin? None, none whatever! The
Christianity for the times demands that all
the lovers of humanity, of both sexes,
should be employed for the world's re-
demption. Two-thirds of our church mem-
bers are women. In God's name, and for
His dear sake, as well as for the love of 
perishing souls, let us bring up this mighty
reserve army, and invite every woman to
a post of duty.

	It is time to stop jesting at "Woman's
tongue," and time to consider whether that
tongue cannot speak the words of gospel
truth better than the stammering tongues
which some of us carry in our own heads.
We can remember how, when we were
children, we loved to listen to our mothers
when they talked to us. We all love to 
hear the tender voice of sister or sweet-
heart or wife; and we recognize the pecu-
liar charm of a woman's conversation.
Then let these tongues and these charms be
consecrated to the Lord! Let them be
given to Jesus! The Lord hath need of 
them, make way for them! Clear the track;
and let the beautiful feet of the Lord's
great company of waiting handmaids run
with the tidings of salvation to the very
ends of the earth.


HOUSEHOLD HINTS.
	Apple Pie.—Take apples that are very tart,
slice thin; line a deep pan with rich crust, and
heap with sliced apples; sprinkle with sugar,
dredge flour over until white, grate in plenty of
nutmeg; put in half a teacup of hot water, cover
with crust and bake.
	Caraway Cake.—Take one pound of flour,
three-quarters of a pound of sugar, half a pound
of butter, a wine-glass of rose water, or some
other liquid, four eggs, and a half teacup of cara-
way seeds.  The materials should be well rubbed
together and beaten up.  Drop them from a spoon
on tin sheets, and bake brown in rather a slow
oven; twenty minutes or half an hour.  Unless
the eggs are very large scant the flour a little.
