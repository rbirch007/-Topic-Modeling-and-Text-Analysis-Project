A BIRTHDAY TRIBUTE,
	1st of June, 1877.

Brigham! Brigham Young! this honored name
Calls for no titles to enhance its fame;
When names are render'd mighty by good deeds,
Titles are dropt as husks that bore the seeds;
The name alone is all that need be traced,
By that the marble is in beauty graced;
And that alone an epitaph will form
To those now living, and to those unborn;

Assembled here upon this bright June day
We meet our fealty, and our love to pay;
"The June is in us," while we mingle here,
Our spirit-voice sends up a grateful cheer
To Him who dwells in yonder heaven above,
Almighty Majesty—but enthroned in love!
To Him our voices and our hearts we raise,
In adoration, and in grateful praise,
That He has given to us this wise, good man
To counsel Israel, and to lead the van;
Not on the battle field, but in the halls
Where God's theology sends forth her calls,
By Brigham's voice—the delegate of heaven,
Where all the masses can inhale the leaven;
And catch the spirit which his words infuse
The evil learn to shun—the good to choose;
And learn to be a Saint of latter days—
Learn how to live, and how to pray and praise,
And learn to think, and understand, and see,
The truth of God, that makes all people free!
Yes, glorious truth in nudity sublime,
Needing no veil around her, sacred shrine!
No chain encircling neck, or feet, or hands,
But in unfettered nudity she stands!
Yes, free from every trammel man has made,
Sublime in beauty is her form display'd,
That form erect, and towering to the skies,
God's glorious handmaid stands before our eyes!
Such truth is Liberty! her other name,
But in essential, is indeed the same!
Such is the theme that Brigham, far and wide,
Preaches to all the church—and all outside!
His voice is open and his teachings clear
To saint, or sinner, who will lend an ear;
Through life's best years this God-appointed man,
Has preach'd the gospel—God's appointed plan
To save, regenerate, and to blend in one
All dispensations that through time have run!
In one grand brotherhood of peace and love
Prepared to welcome from the realms above
Our great Redeemer to his purchased earth!
When all creation shall receive new birth!
And Christ, and God, and all the saints be one,
The warfare ended, and the vict'ry won!

Father! on this auspicious morn we humbly bear
Before thy throne, in one united prayer
Our Leader! and we ask that he
By thy good spirit may supported be;
Shield him from all that would engender strife,
Through all the coming years of future life:
While life can be enjoyed extend his span
To all the years that's meted out to man;
The evening of his days—Oh! may it close
In bright refulgence, and in sweet repose;
Like day's grand orb declining in the west
"Life's fitful fever" ended, all is rest.

	HANNAH T. KING.
SALT LAKE CITY, June 1st, 1877.

				HEALTH.

	How melancholy the thought when one
reflects upon the deplorable condition of the
world to-day; how far its inhabitants have
wandered from the ways of God, the laws
of nature, until the whole earth has become
one great house of disease; and surely it is
now that it has reached its zenith of cor-
ruption. Where you find one healthy, well-
developed body, you find hundreds, who,
though too ignorant in regard to these
matters, to be aware of the sad reality, have

[Column 2]
the seeds of death and disease so deeply im-
planted in their vitals, that nothing but one
grand revolution, one great change of their
whole lives will be able to uproot it. What
is this change, and how can it be accom-
plished? Simply by strictly observing the
pure and holy laws of nature. In no other
way can it be brought about; so far have the
inhabitants of the earth departed from
these laws that they have almost forgotten
them, and everywhere we see the result of
their folly. Instead of the fine, healthy,
bright-eyed baby, we see the pale, sickly,
ill-formed one; and instead of a robust,
well-developed maiden at nineteen, we find
her delicate and undeveloped, who after
taking upon herself the responsibilities of
wedded life, bears offspring still more puny
than the mother—they grow unflourish-
ngly for a few brief months, years perhaps
—and thus does one generation exceed the
other in disease and decay. But how are
we to become healthy? Air, water, diet,
dress, exercise, occupation and manner of
living must be duly considered; and climate?
—where can we find a healthy climate?
Our own, here in Utah, is delightful; our
pure mountain air is exceedingly conducive
to health; this accounts, in part, for the
"rosy-cheeked Mormon girls," as they are
frequently styled by strangers visiting our
city. Our mountain air associated with a
correct method of living, would tend to
make us the most healthy, vigorous class of
people on the earth. The following is what
Mrs. Helen Hunt says in regard to health;
taken from the "Laws of Life."
	"I have now learned that if no law of
health were ever broken, we need never
have a day's illness, might grow old in en-
tire freedom from suffering, and gradually
fall asleep at last, instead of dying terrible
deaths from disease; and I am all the time
wishing that I had known it when I was
young. If I had known it, I'll tell you what
I should have done. I would just have
tried the experiment at any rate, of never
doing a single thing which could by any
possibility injure or put out of order any
portion of my body. I wish I could see some
boy or girl try it yet; never to sit up late at
night; never to have a close bad air in the
room; never to sit with wet feet; never to
wet them were it possible to help it; never
to go out in the cold weather without being
properly wrapped up; never to go out of a
hot room into a cold out-door air without
throwing some extra wrap on; never to eat
or drink an unwholesome thing; never to
touch tea, or coffee, or candy or pie-crust;
never to let a day pass without at least two
good hours of exercise in the open air; nev-
er to read a word by twilight nor in the
cars; never to let the sun be shut out of
rooms. This is a pretty long list of "nevers,"
but "never" is the only word that con-
quers. "Once-in-awhile" is the very watch-
word of temptation and defeat. I do be-
lieve that the "once-in-awhile" things have
ruined more bodies and more souls too, than
all the other things put together. More-
over, the "never" way is easy, and the
"once-in-awhile" way is hard. After you
have once made up your mind "never" to do
a thing, that is the end of it, if you are a
sensible person. But if you only say, "This

[Column 3]
is a dangerous indulgence; I will be a little
on my guard and not do it too often," you
have put yourself in the most uncomforta-
ble of all positions; the temptation will
knock at your door twenty times a day,
and you will have to be fighting the same
old battle over and over again as long as
you live."

	A PETITION from the women of Louisi-
ana, containing the signatures of one
thousand real estate owners and tax payers,
presented in the House of Representatives
recently, inaugurates a new movement in
Louisiana, viz: the equality of women be-
fore the law. For several weeks a number
of the more prominent ladies of the city
have been quietly organizing this new de-
parture, and the leaders allege in their pe-
tition that fully one-half the real estate in
Louisiana is owned by women and one half
the revenues are of their contribution. But
for the late hour in which the petition was 
presented, it was proposed to submit a bill
requesting our Representative in Congress
to advocate a sixteenth amendment to the
constitution for the benefit of women.—
New Orleans Democrat.

			SPRINGVILLE, May 5th, 1877.
EDITORS EXPONENT:
	I enclose you a brief report of a meet-
ing of the Young Ladies Retrenchment
Association, held in the meeting house
April 25, 1877. After the usual opening
exercises, Mrs. Dr. Ferguson of Provo ad-
dressed the Association on the subject of
woman's dress and its relations; she said
the present fashionable mode of dressing
outraged all the laws of health, and is also
the cause of much valuable time being
spent foolishly in making clothes which are
elaborately trimmed, besides being a waste
of means which should be used to assist in
building temples, and for other charitable
purposes; she said that those Babylonish
fashions should not be practiced by a Re-
trenchment Society, that they should strive
to retrench from every thing that is evil,
not only from extravagance in dress, but
from every evil that gives no profit, and
seek to have pure, intelligent minds and to
take an interest in all matters that tend to
the development of good. She also gave
some very valuable instructions in regard
to tight lacing and its results. Showed the
necessity of us becoming a self-sustaining
people, and of storing grain. She express-
ed the pleasure she felt in meeting with the
young ladies, and prayed the Lord to blessthem. Other remarks were made and the
meeting was one long to be remembered by
all who were present.
			KATE HOUTZ, Cor. Sec'y.

			SPRING CITY, SANPETE Co..
						May 13, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT;
	We held our eighth Annual Meeting May
3rd. A good spirit prevails here among
the sisters, they are one with the President
M. A. P. Hyde, to do all they can in the
good work in which they are engaged, ac-
cording to their circumstances. The re-
ports are favorable for the past year. The
teachers and other sisters take great pains
in looking after and caring for the poor,
sick and needy, of whom there are quite a 
number, according to the size of the place.
Mrs. Frantzen is energetic in her duties as
treasurer, turning everything to advantage,
so that our means increase in her hands to
the benefit of the Society.
	We are gathering and storing away all
the wheat we can, and have about one hun-
dred and thirty bushels on hand. We have
one city lot fenced, and one hundred mul-
berry trees planted, and ground prepared
for five hundred more as soon as we can pro-
cure them. In our last meeting it was ar-
ranged for the sisters to unite in companies
to make cheese for ourselves and to donate
to the temple. The sisters are also making
articles of clothing for the brethren who
work on the temple, such as socks, gloves,
quilts and other useful articles; and all are
willing to exert themselves to forward the
work of the temples; and also willing to
comply with the laws of the UNITED OR-
DER, explained to us by Elders Taylor and
Pratt, and taught us from time to time
by President Hyde.
	Bishop Olsen encourages us to go on as
we have hitherto done, as he was very
much pleased with our proceedings.
	Another small matter which may be wor-
thy of notice: about three years ago, Mrs.
Hyde proposed that every member of the
society give one egg in every month and
an account be kept separately of it to see
how much it would amount to; some per-
sons think if they cannot donate liberally,
they will not do anything, and Mrs. Hyde's
idea was to give every one a chance that
none might feel slighted in not having their
names with the workers, but that all might

[Column 3]
feel free and equal and have a lively inter-
est in the sisters work. This fund during
the last three years has amounted to the
sum of sixty-five dollars.
	Praying the Lord to bless all the Relief
Societies, and the saints whereever they
are, I remain your sister in the Gospel.
					P. E. Benson, Sec'y.

THE SUMMER HOURS.

Balmy and fragrant is the breath of June,
	And summer days;
And nature's rhythm in harmony attune,
	Gladdens our ways.
The bright, warm sunshine cheers us with its beams
And joy comes gushing with the dancing streams,
	Which bode us good,
Giving sweet promise of the buds and flowers,
	And verdant wood,
And all the golden wealth of pleasant hours,

Fair as the rosy blush of early dawn
	Doth summer come;
Clover and daisies scent the dewy lawn,
	And insects hum,
Mingles with songs of birds in tuneful voice
And in the chorus all our hearts rejoice,
	And welcome now
Prophetical, these signs that summer brings
	Upon her brow,
Which speak to us of happier things.

O, sorrowing soul look up! Be glad once more!
	For hope shall rise
Triumphant, now the winter storms are o'er,
	And to our eyes
Earth seems like Eden, where ere long shall reign
The peace, which crowns the night of gloom and pain.
	O, child of fear!
Seest though not now, from out the withered sod,
	The grass appear;
E'en this, "betokens the dear love of God."

"Our Father" left us here some dear delight
	Our souls to greet
Great nature's splendor bursting on our sight
	Brings mem'ries sweet,
And summer breezes wafted from the skies;
Breathe whisp'rings of the realms of Paradise;
	And seem to kiss,
Our wearied heads with soothing tenderness,
	Foretaste of bliss,
When love supreme shall crown our happiness.

Summer, with roses blushing everywhere,
	And lilies sweet,
And all its wealth of flowers and foliage fair,
	Makes more complete
The yearnings for immortal fruits and flowers
Which erst we tasted in lost Eden's bow'rs
	And brings to mind
The tree of knowledge on that happy shore
	We left behind,
Which we must reach to hunger nevermore.

	EMILE.
S. L. CITY, May 29, 1877.

A FEW REFLECTIONS.

(CONTINUED.)

	To the honorable men of the world, and
especially those who claim to be the fathers
of the people and guardians of the law, and
to divines set in judgment to deal out jus-
tice to their constituents, to illustrate clear-
ly my subject, I will call this Gospel, you
so much desire to destroy, a tree, (if you
please) and one which attracts attention
by its rapid growth; one which has made
itself a history, which you should become
thoroughly acquainted with, for it betrays
poor generalship not to understand the pos-
ition the enemy hold ; and a laborer in the
vineyard must be a novice indeed who does
not know that in order to subdue a foul
plant or obnoxious tree, it must be taken
up by the roots and not a vestige of the
root be found in the ground, or it will as-
suredly, after a season shoot up again.
	Your predecessors committed the same
error you meditate, which will lead you as-
tray; they began by disrobing the tree of
its tender leaves and branches, despoiling
it of its beauty, but finding the tree still
stood and put forth new tendrils they did
all they could against it. The branches
were broken off and scattered and the tree
felled to the earth as they supposed, to
flourish no more. This was on the eve of
the Dark Ages. The tree was forgotten
and the spot where it once stood lost sight
of, or if ever recalled alluded to as a thing

[Column 2]

of the past; but like our trees which are
broken down and the ground left barren to
all appearance, through the cold bleak win-
ter, but when the spring comes and the
sun gives his warmth and light to the earth
and all nature seems to smile, the roots put
forth again and before we are aware of it
there is the exact similitude of the old tree.
Just so with this polygamic or patriarchal
tree; the sun has arisen in his majesty and
power and poured down his effulgence of
warm light; summer has come, that is, the
light of truth has again shed forth its pro-
pitious rays and the fruits of its effects are
seen, the branches are spreading abroad and
are very prolific, bearing much fruit; and
wise presidents, judges and divines, it is
your duty before you proceed further in this
matter to analyze and know without a
doubt that the tree is bad, and as I have
been taken from among the wild branches
and grafted into the tree and grown with
its growth and strengthened with its
strength, and have searched and reflected
much concerning it, perhaps you will allow
me to assist in your researches: I believe
the necessary knowledge can be easily ob-
tained, for it is written, "Can a bitter or
corrupt tree bring forth good fruit?" Doubt-
less you are all believers in God, and some
are ecclesiastics, and as such, without re-
serve acknowledge God as the Supreme
Judge, and as He claims this earth as His
vineyard, we must allow Him to accept or
condemn as seemeth Him good, and He
plainly says that He judges a tree by the
fruit thereof.
	In doing this we take sacred history, our
Holy Bible, which all the Christian world
profess to believe in, and as we turn its pa-
ges we find it contains a few items in re-
gard to this patriarchal tree, and learn
whether God ever accepted the fruit of it.
In Gen. iv Chap., 25 verse, we find the first
account of a man having a plurality of
wives, "and he said unto his wives Adah
and Zillah, hear ye my voice, ye wives of
Lamech, for I have slain a man to my
wounding and a young man to my hurt."
The spirit of his speech does not infer tht
he considers himself guilty of any crime
except that of killing the young man. We
will look at Gen. xv chap. 1 verse, here
we find a man whom God has spoken to
saying, "Fear not Abram I am thy shield
and exceeding great reward," and in the
18 verse it reads "the Lord made a cove-
nant with Abram saying, "unto thy seed
have I given the land," &c., and as he is
thus favored of the Lord, we will trace his
history and see what transpired with him
in his day. Gen. 16 chap., 3 verse, "and
Sarai, Abram's wife took Hagar her maid,
and gave her to her husband to be his
wife," and now what will the Lord of the
vineyard do to this man who has so willing-
ly become the husband of two wives. With
him we must begin the history of the po-
lygamic tree; read from the 7th to the 12th
verses and see how an angel of the Lord
conversed with a plural wife, but perhaps
one might say she was not so much to
blame as her mistress, as she was but a ser-
vant, but the man, is he not to be brought
to an account for such a rash act, or can he
justify himself by saying "the woman gave
her to me." Let us search further and
find what is said about them. In the 15th
and 16th verses is the account of Ishmael
being born. How different was Abram from
the generality of men now-a-days, he not
only calls Hagar his wife but acknowledges
the child as his son, thus shielding him
from all reproach. In the xvii chapt. there
is an account of the Lord appearing again
unto Abram and instead of chastening

[Column 3]

him He ratifies His former covenant with
him and said "thy name shall no more be call-
ed Abram, but Abraham, for a father of many
nations have I made thee," and in another
place He says "I will make you exceeding-
ly fruitful and will establish my covenant
between me and thee and thy seed after
thee in their generations, for an everlasting
covenant to be a God unto thee and thy seed
after thee." It appears to me, by this that
all who believe the Old Testament must un-
derstand that God loved the old polygamic
tree, for he does not condemn this man, but
on the contrary says that in this fruit shall
all the nations of the earth be blessed, and
He knows the tree and its fruit.

THOUGHTS SUGGESTED BY READING
THE EXPONENT.

EDITOR EXPONENT:
	When I read your excellent paper and re-
alize the great work you are striving to ac-
complish in removing prejudice and cor-
recting the many evil reports which gain
such wide circulation throughout the world
in regard to the women of Utah; my heart
is lifted with gratitude to the Supreme Ru-
ler, that I have been permitted to live to
behold this blessed day, when a few of the
noble, brave women of our church have had
the courage to lead out in the great work to
which you have devoted yourselves, that
of showing to the world our true position,
both socially and religiously; proving to
them that we are far from being the poor,
ignorant, oppressed women they have rep-
resented us to be. We believe we can rep-
resent ourselves much better than our
would be sympathizers, (but really covert
enemies) could or would represent us. Let
us be firm in our convictions for right and
defend faithfully the principles of our holy
religion, as revealed by our martyred
Prophet, and by his rightful successor Presi-
dent Brigham Young. We can show to the 
world that we are free to believe what we 
please without any coercion whatever, and 
are capable of judging matters intelligent-
ly and what we do receive is with a firm

SALT LAKE CITY, JUNE 1, 1877.


THE NEW VOLUME—AND THE
WOMEN OF UTAH.

	Our readers are aware this number ushers in
the sixth volume of the EXPONENT, and we
look forward with bright anticipation to the
glorious work it may accomplish as it advan-
ces from year to year. It has been dedicated
to the work of improvement, and consecrated
by thousands upon thousands of prayers and
blessings earnestly offered up by the women
of the Latter-day Saints. It not only finds its
way into the homes of the people of this Ter-
ritory, but it has gone as a glad messenger of
salvation to some of the most remote quarters
of the globe, and has cheered, comforted and
inspired to noble purposes those who have read
and comprehended the power and blessing now
being made manifest among the women who
are represented as the offscouring of the earth.
Women who have proved themselves true to
the requirements of heaven, and willing to lay
all upon the altar of their faith. Their star is
rising, and ere long they will take a higher
rank in the annals of history. Babylonian
fashions and customs with their pride and
pomp and sham, are beneath the notice of the
saints of God; they have been called to a high-
er and holier calling; to fulfill a mission which
will stand unparalleled in woman's records.
It will require all the ability, energy, faith and
integrity that can be brought into requisition.
The thorough system for the storing of grain
against a day of famine, which has been com-
menced and is still in operation among the wo-
men of Utah, is certainly one step in advance
of any set or organization of women upon the
face of the earth. Let us not be dilatory when
such great issues are at stake. There is noth-
ing which is of more consequence to this peo-
ple than the preparation which must be made
for the terrible calamities which are to come,
and will come upon the inhabitants of the
earth. The women of this kingdom should
awake to the great responsibilities which are
resting upon them, and be sure they are doing
all that is possible to preserve themselves and
those near and dear to them, from the evils
and temptations which surround them on
every hand.
	The very fact that woman is the subject of
so much discussion at the present day, shows
that there is a reality in the work she is ef-
fecting, and it proves, moreover, that men who
are so afraid of woman stepping out of her
sphere feel that the power she holds is po-
tent. If not, why should they care? They are
fearful lest woman's spiritual nature acting in
and with her, and forming a characteristic
feature of all the movements in progression,
should so improve the condition of the sex at
large, that their power over them will be sensi-


bly weakened, consequently woman will speak
and act for herself and mothers instruct their
daughters to fill higher places than to be the
menials only, or worse than that,of wicked and
corrupt men. The women of Utah know
something of these duties and obligations,
some things have already been revealed to 
them "and more remains to be revealed here-
after." If they carry out the instructions already
given them they will become all that the most
ambitious could desire.
	E. B. Wells

	THE WOMAN'S EXPONENT:—This number is
the commencement of Volume Six. The EX-
PONENT is gaining a name and place as well as
influence; and ranks creditably with other
periodicals. No pains is spared to adapt it to
the wants and interests of the people. The ar-
ticles are selected and arranged with the great-
est care that nothing should appear to shock
the most delicate organization. We have been
accused of throwing articles aside. We do;
we should be sorry for our readers if we did
not; and for ourselves, we must answer to God
and our own conscience for what we publish.
Our desire is to make the paper the EXPONENT
of true principles and suited in tone and
phraseology to the condition of the people
whose interests we represent. The women of
Utah occupy an important position, and one
which all the world is waiting to see analyzed.
Then it behooves them to be wise, and move
cautiously, and it is better to be a little slow in
making statements, or advancing ideas, than
like too many of the leading women of the
day, have to retract, or retire from the encoun-
ter signally defeated. The Mormon women
will lead the van in the questions which are
being agitated among the sex, whatever the
world may think to the contrary. Many are
afraid to speak and talk of charity, but it is
high time to speak, and write and act wisely
and well. Therefore we would say to our pa-
trons, write your best thoughts lay before the
readers of the EXPONENT your best devised,
and most economical schemes for progression
in all that pertains to temporal and spiritual


salvation. Prove to our enemies that we have
something superior to them, something dearer
than all the glitter and tinsel of sentiment the
world possess. It is to the best interest of
every woman in Israel to sustain the EXPO-
NENT, for it gives them a power that is being
felt far and wide for good. Elders have said to
us that when out performing missionary labor,
the EXPONENT in their hands was an ef-
ficient help, and accomplished silent mission-
ary work more potent than they could do by
preaching long and loud.

		SMITH, THE PROPHET.

	"April 28, 1842.—At two o'clock, p. m., I met
the members of the "Relief Society," and af-
ter presiding at the admission of many new
members, gave a lecture on the Priesthood,
showing how the sisters would come into pos-
session of the privileges, blessings, and gifts of
the priesthood and that the signs should fol-
low them, such as healing the sick, casting out
devils, &c., and that they might attain unto
these blessings by a virtuous life, and conver-
sation, and diligence in keeping all the com-
mandments: a synopsis of which was reported
by Miss E. R. Snow.
	Prest. Joseph Smith arose and called the at-
tention of the meeting to the xii chapter 1st
Corinthians. "Now concerning spiritual gifts
I would not have you ignorant." Said that
the passage in the 3rd verse which reads, "No
man can say that Jesus is the Lord, but by
the Holy Ghost," should be translated "no
man can know that Jesus is the Lord, but by
the Holy Ghost." He continued to read the
chapter, and give instructions respecting the

[Column 2]
different offices, and the necessity of every in-
dividual acting in the sphere allotted him or
her, and filling the several offices to which
they were appointed.
	He spoke of delivering the keys of the Priest-
hood to the church, and said that the faithful
members of the Relief Society should receive
them in connection with their husbands, that
the Saints whose integrity had been tried and
proved faithful, might know how to ask the
Lord and receive an answer; for according to
his prayers, God had appointed him elsewhere.
	He exhorted the sisters always to concen-
trate their faith and prayers for, and place con-
fidence in their husbands, whom God had
appointed for them to honor, and in those
faithful men whom God has placed at the head
of the church to lead His people; that we
should arm and sustain them with our prayers;
for the keys of the kingdom are about to be
given to them, that they may be able to de-
tect everything false; as well as to all the eld-
ers who shall prove their integrity in due sea-
son.
	He said if one member becomes corrupt, and
you know it, you must immediately put it
away, or it will either injure or destroy the
whole body. The sympathies of the heads of
the church have induced them to bear a long
time with those who were corrupt, until they
are obliged to cut them off, lest all become
contaminated; you must put down iniquity,
and by your good examples, stimulate the eld-
ers to good works; if you do right, there is no
danger of your going too fast.
	He said he did not care how fast we run in
the path of virtue; resist evil and there is no
danger; God, men and angels will not con-
demn those that resist everything that is evil,
and devils cannot; as well might the devil seek
to dethrone Jehovah, as overthrow an innocent
soul that resists everything which is evil.
	This is a charitable Society, and according to
your natures; it is natural for females to have
feelings of charity and benevolence. You are
now placed in a situation in which you can act
according to those sympathies which God has
planted in your bosoms.
	If you live up to those principles, how great
and glorious will be your reward in the celes-
tial kingdom! If you live up to your privi-
leges, the angels cannot be restrained from be-
ing your associates. Females if they are pure
and innocent, can come into the presence of
God; for what is more pleasing to God than in-
nocence; you must be innocent, or you cannot
come up before God; if we would come before
God we must keep ourselves pure, as he is
pure.
	The devil has great power to deceive; he will
so transform things as to make one gape at
those who are doing the will of God. You
need not be teasing your husbands because
of their deeds, but let the weight of your
innocence, kindness and affections be felt,
which is more mighty than a millstone hung
about the neck, not war, not jangle, not con-
tradiction, or dispute, but meekness, love, pu-
rity—these are the things that should magni-
fy you in the eyes of all good men. A change
must be brought to light, iniquity must be
purged out from the midst of the Saints, the
veil will be rent, and the blessings of heaven
will flow down—they will roll down like the
Mississippi river.
	If this Society listens to the counsel of the
Almighty, through the heads of the church,
they shall have power to command queens in
their midst.
	I now deliver it as a prophecy, if the inhab-
itants of this state with the people of the sur-
sounding country, will turn unto the Lord with

[Column 3]
all their hearts, ten years will not roll round
before the kings and queens of the earth will
come unto Zion, and pay their respects to the
leaders of this people; they shall come with
their millions, and shall contribute of their
abundance for the relief of the poor, and the
building up and beautifying of Zion.
	After this instruction, you will be responsi-
ble for your own sins; it is a desirable honor
that you should so walk before our heavenly
Father as to save yourselves. We are all re-
sponsible to God for the manner we improve
the light given by our Lord to enable us to
save ourselves.


ROYAL ART NEEDLEWORK.

	The English needlework exhibited at the
late Centennial Exhibition is again on ex-
hibition in the show room of the Royal

ident of the institution, the objects of

once held among the decorative arts. A

continuous and unwearied exertions made

curtains, portieres, screnes and ottomans,

repairing of ancient needlework. The spec-
imens exhibited are fine. A pair of velvet-
een curtains, with borders of conventional
sunflowers, made for the queen; a graceful
white lily border on red velvet, tastefully
designed by the Empress Louise; another of
cream-colored satin, a magnificent pattern,
evidently of Venetian origin, may be men-
tioned among the number.—The Watch-
man.


WOMAN'S RECORD.

	ONE of the noblest women of the age is
Mrs. Josephine Butler. Her good works,
and devotion to the cause of woman are
worthy of great honor. At Liverpool Mrs.
Butler instituted what was called a "Home
of Rest," now enlarged into a "Home for
Incurables" and an Independent Home to
train young girls for service, &c. In 1874-
75, she traveled through Italy, Switzerland
and France in the cause of benefitting and
elevating woman, and exerted such an in-
fluence as to bring about the British Conti-
nental and General Federation against Gov-
ernment Regulation of Vice. Her success
should be an incentive to action for others
who are laboring for good, and against mod-
ern conventionalities. She is spoken of as
"of slight frame and a voice not remarkably
strong; yet she is easily heard in large as-
semblies, and by her careful arguments and
her sincere eloquence convinces her audi-
ence." Her mission is self-imposed to lift
up the fallen, and aid everywhere and in all
ways possible, for the suppression of vice.
She speaks and writes and plans in behalf
of her glorious and exalted work. To all
such we can safely say, God speed them in
all noble enterprises.

			SIMPLICITY.

		TO THE SAINTS IN ZION.

	We are said to be creatures of habit.
We certainly are so to a great extent. We
are all, more or less, stamped with the dye
of education combined with the circum-
stances of early life, which constitute our
individual cast; by which we are fitted by
certain opinions and prejudices, and bound
to certain habits and customs, until we real-
ly become stereotyped in practice.
	When we enter the school of Jesus Christ,
the sooner we can divest ourselves, or become
divested of traditional cast—the sooner we
throw off the stereotyped edition of our-
selves; and like a plain proof-sheet, hold
ourselves subject to correction in all things,
and like little children, be ever ready to re-
ceive and adopt whatever tends to improve-
ment, the greater will be our attainment to-
wards that point which the Savior enjoined
on his disciples, saying "Be ye therefore
perfect, even as your Father in heaven is
perfect." To this end, we need to culti-
vate that truthful simplicity which like a
clear sunbeam, affords an extension of vis-
ion, far, far surpassing the utmost stretch
of all the telescopes of that ostentatious
formality, which clothes the affectation of
greatness with which the world abounds,
while simplicity exhibits the burnished
gem, with all its native lustre and brilliancy
undiminished, yet improved by the sculp-
tor's art. This feature was beautifully il-
lustrated in the character of Jesus Christ
during his life upon the earth. His origi-
nal worth and self-possessed dignity re-
quired no artificial support. Sensible of His
own superiority and greatness, and for the
time being, satisfied to be what he was; He
spared himself the trouble of endeavoring
to make every body understand and ac-
knowledge it. The life of Joseph Smith,
also, was a striking illustration of the
beauty of simplicity, as all will testify who
had the honor of his personal acquaintance.
	But we are under no necessity of refer-
ring to those who once lived; we have
plenty of living examples, both of male
and female; and they are in our midst, for
the greatest and the best on the earth are
here, the opinion of the world, to the con-
trary notwithstanding. For instance, is
there a greater than Brigham Young? Is
there one on whom rests a broader fold
of imperial dignity, as recognized by the
upper nobility? (By the way we do not con-
sult the opinion of the lower courts.) And
is there a more striking example of plain-
ness and simplicity? Does not this very
simplicity in him, beget love and admira-
tion in the hearts of this people?
	I always feel to shrink from the cold
formality—that heartless affectation of dig-
nity, where thought and feeling and every
native charm, are hid beneath the cum-
brous appendages of the so called etiquette
of fashionable life. It seems too much like
penetrating beneath, or unwinding the
sixty-four thicknesses of envelop to find an
Egyptian Mummy; and when you find it,
you only find a stiff, dried up, lifeless sub-
stance, at which every feeling would re-
volt to recognize as the representative of
once breathing, noble humanity. True dig-
nity is self-supported; and real greatness is
never straining for its own exhibition. It
certainly is desirable to be divested of that
kind of tall importance which requires con-
stant propping and splicing to sustain its
position. That is too expensive, it will nev-
er pay its own passage. I think it must
be one of those things that "perish with
the using."

[Column 2]	Ever delighted with the beauties of na-
ture, I like to see nature cultivated and im-
proved but not obliterated—however eleva-
ted and refined, let nature be nature still in
every department, both in the productions
of the earth, and in all the vast varieties
exhibited throughout animate and inani-
mate existence.
	I love simplicity. I like things to seem
what they are and be what they seem. I
love the artless exhibitions of unaffected
childhood; and regard simplicity in a child,
not as a frightful extuberance—a something
peculiar to, and only to be tolerated in the
infantile season, which, like the transient
verdure of spring, must disappear before
the summer solstice and give place to more
substantial productions; but as an inherent
attribute of real goodness, that should be
tenderly cherished—a plant that should be
cultivated for all eternity, and constitute a
prominent characteristic in every season of
life. If we are pleased with its charms in
childhood, it calls forth our admiration when
delineated in manhood; for when does
greatness appear so truly great, as when
clothed with the mantle of simplicity?
	We happen to be situated at the farthest,
lower extremity of that thing, called degen-
eracy and with all the boasted light and
knowledge of the age, the human family is
blind and ignorant with regard to genuine
worth, and true greatness. They call good
evil, and evil good; and I would ask, what
of all substances on earth, is more subject to
idiotic metamorphosing and artificial mum-
myfying, than that of the human kind? We
suffer our tongues to play the truant with
our thoughts—our bodies to be weakened
and disfigured by the arbitrary power of
de[-]enerating, corrupt Babylonish fashions
—our health destroyed and our lives short-
ened through a silly and guilty perversion
of dietic observances. The laws of nature
are set at defiance, while wisdom and sim-
plicity are laughed to scorn, and common
sense looked out of countenance. We all
have been and still are trammeled with
these mockeries of life. Yes, here in Zion
we are affectionately hugging to our bosoms
the prepossessions of Gentile tuition, cares-
sing the customs of the past, which have
been instrumental in lessening the tide of
human life to its lowest ebb, and at the
same time claiming, as we really are, to be
standing on the very threshhold of the dis-
pensation of the fulness of times, and ac-
tually living in the Era of the regeneration.
	As members of the church of Jesus
Christ, we each have high responsibilities
resting upon us; and surely quite as much
diligence and faithfulness are requisite in
Zion as in Babylon—if we needed a large
amount of moral courage to stand forth
amid the scoffs and sympathies of the
world, and of our unbelieving kindred, to
espouse the Gospel of Christ; we certainly
shall require no less to fulfill all its require-
ments in the midst of those who are called
saints; for remember, "all are not Israel
that are of Israel." In order to attain to a
fulness of promised blessings, we must be
found faithful, as well as to have been call-

the Savior said, "all the blood that has been
shed from the time of righteous Abel" &c..
"will I require at the hand of this genera-

bility of saving, or, in other words, of do-
ing the work for all the dead, from the days
of Adam, down to that time, rested upon

SURPRISE PARTIES AT SANTAQUIN.

EDITORS EXPONENT:
	I do really think the women in Utah en-
joy themselves the best of any women on
the earth. The world outside, say we are a
poor down-trodden set of women, but we
know that it is not the case; we enjoy our-
selves in a way that our consciences ap-
prove, and we feel that God approbates, and
our social parties and amusements never
leave a sting behind, as those do that are
after the manner of the world.
	I wish to say a few words about the Sur-
prise Parties that we have had in this place
during the past week. The first one was
in honor of President Elizabeth J. Stick-
ney's sixtieth birthday, and took place on
the 19th of March, 1877. The sisters of
Santaquin were determined to manifest ap-
propriately the respect they felt for her.
They met at her house at 4 o'clock, p.m.
laden with picnic and presents; she was
totally unaware of any manifestation of the
kind until ushered into the room where all
were seated. She is engaged in teaching
school at Spring Lake, and we arranged
that her husband should go down and bring
her home, telling him to be very silent;
judge then of her surprise on meeting so
many of her friends unexpectedly. Sever-
al pieces were composed for the occasion,
and many handsome presents given to
her by the sisters. She responded to all
in a humble and beautiful manner, asking
God to bless us all; her husband also thank-
ed the sisters for the respect shown to him-
self and wife, and Mrs Mary Stickney also
returned her thanks for the respect shewn
to our President. Bishop Halladay was
present, who always encourages and adds
zest to all our social meetings; also Elder
P. N. Andersen, president of the Scandina-
vian brethren. Many other brethren and
sisters were there. A splendid supper was
given, and an abundance left to distribute
among the poor. The evening was most
agreeably spent, and very reluctant were
we to disperse. Evenings spent in these
kind of enjoyments prove much more profit-
able than if we wasted our hard-earned
means in visiting traveling shows or cir-
cuses, which usually leave a dissatisfied
feeling.
	The other surprise party was managed
for a birthday festival to some new comers,
who have not yet become initiated into the
ways of this country; so our sisters conclud-
ed to carry picnic to their house and give
them a SURPRISE, the evening was spent
very pleasantly, and in this way the sisters
seek to cheer and comfort each other and
to enjoy themselves without wounding the
most sensitive or refined feelings. Time
passes lively with us, good feelings exist
all around, and we feel truly thankful for
the spirit of the Gospel which is imparted
to us.


Praying for success to attend your very
valuable paper; I remain yours in the new
and everlasting Covenant.
				ELIZABETH CARTER,

HOUSEHOLD HINTS.

	Rhubard Jelly Wipe, peel and cut quite small
some fresh rhubarb, put it into a preserving pan
with a very little warm water and the thin rind of
half a lemon to every pound of fruit. Boil until
reduced to a pulp. Strain the juice and weigh it
and allow one pound of pounded sugar to every
pound of juice. Boil up the juice, add the sugar,
boil, skim, and when it jellies on the skimmer
pour into pots, and tie down when cold. If
boiled too long it will become treacle.

	Stale Bread: Every person may make econom-
ical use of this article if care is taken in its be-
ing kept; it should be broken up small, dried slow-
ly in the oven, then grated or pounded in a mor-
tar to coarse powder and kept in wide-mouthed
bottles, well-corked. It will keep a long time in
a dry place, and is useful for many purposes, for
dressing for fowls and meat, for making the choic-
est puddings, or for mixing with flour for pan-
cakes.

INTEMPERANCE.

 There is a saying which if some of our
boys would think of in the right time,
would be the source of much pleasure in-
stead of pain; it is "when sinners entice
thee, consent thou not." If they would
think of this when they are tempted to in-
dulge in intoxicating liquors, stamp it on
their memory, burn it there, so it could
never be erased, it would be the means of
avoiding a great deal of pain, sorrow and
death. Intemperance is a subject that is
agitating the public mind considerably at
present, and I think it high time some-
thing should be done to put a stop to the
sale of liquors; for if it were not very
plentiful a great many who drink now,
would not, were it not before them all the
time. When men so far forget themselves
as to indulge in the debasing habit of drink-
ing, it seems to me that they forget that
they are rational being, accountable to their
God for the deeds committed here; if they
would pause to think of the many, many
broken hearts and desolate homes, made so
by the demon drink, they would surely
think a great many times before they touch-
ed it. If they would consider how they are
debasing themselves, how low and degrad-
ed they are becoming, they would not taste
it. When a man so far forgets himself as
to let whiskey be his ruling passion, he is
lost to all the better feelings that should
prompt his heart to actions of love and
kindness. I would that I could speak with
a voice of thunder, that all might hear,
but would be repaid, could I save one per-
son from a drunkards grave. I have heard
young men when remonstrated with for
tampering with the baneful stuff, say, "Oh,
I can quit when I get ready;" but the get
ready so seldom ever comes, even were the
saying true, that it is not often stopped. It
goes on from bad to worse, till their one
aim, the one desire of their hearts is drink,
that thirst which is unquenchable is made,
and cannot be satisfied. It gets so bad after
a while that they must and will have it,
whether it comes honestly or not. And
when a young man gets so low, he is lost to
honor, to everything that elevates the hu-
man above the brute, and is indeed an ob-
ject of commiseration. Do the young men,
middle aged or any ever think, when they
take the first glass what it may lead to?
Do they imagine a drunkards home? Let
us imagine one for a moment: The wife is
waiting in tearful anxiety for her husband,
the man who vowed to love and protect her,
(and what a protection indeed;) she hears
his unsteady steps on the walk and shud-
ders for very fear of—she does not know
what, she thinks, ah! what might have
been; she knows full well that instead of
the words of love and kindness she should
hear from him, it will be oaths and curses.
The children are huddled away out of
sight in horror of the man whom they
should love and honor; who should love and
cherish them, and when he leaves thisworld should leave an honorable name for
them to inherit, but instead of that, what
a stigmatized one there is for them to bear;
one which a whole life of good actions will
hardly obliterate.

themselves that when they sink into the
early grave, which nearely every drunkard
finds, it is not regretted. Boys, stop and
think before you touch it. Think, if you
want your friends, your associates to love,
honor and revere you; if you want to leave

THE SILK WORM'S WILL
	BY MISS H. F. GOULD

On a plain rush hurdle a silk work lay,
When a proud young princess came that way,
The haughty child of a human king,
Threw a sidelong glance at the humble thing,
That took with a silent gratitude
From the mulberry leaf-her simple food-
And shrank with half scorn and half disgust,
Away from her sister, child of dust;
Declaring she never yet could see
Why a reptile form like that should be,
And that she was not made with nerves so form,
As to calmly stand by a “crawling worm!"
With mute forbearance the silk-worm took
The taunting words and the spurning look;
A stranger alike to self and pride,
She had no disquiet from aught beside,
But lived of a meekness and peace possessed,
Which these debar from the human breast.
She only wished, for the harsh abuse,
To find some way to become of  use
To the haughty daughter of lordly man,
And thus did she lay a noble plan,
To teach her wisdom and make it plain,
That the humble worm was not made in vain;
A plan so generous so deep and high
That to carry it out she must even die.
"No more," said she, "will I drink or eat!
I'll spin and weave me a winding sheet,
to wrap me up from the sun's clear light,
And to hide my form from that wounded sight.
In secret then, till my end draws nigh
I'll toil for her; and when I die,
I'll leave behind as a farewell boon,
To the proud young princess my full cocoon;
To be reeled and woven in shining lace,
And hung in a vail o’er her scornful face;
She will softly smile as she draws her breath,
Through the very threads that have caused my
	death:
When she finds, at length, she has nerve so firm,
As to wear the shroud of a "crawling worm."
May she bear in mind that she walks with pride,
In the winding sheet where the silk worm died.


A FEW REFLECTIONS.

			    (CONTINUED.)

 Beside blessing Abraham and the lineage
of this patriarchal tree, God also blessed
Sarai and said she should be called Sarah
and should bear a son, and become a mother
of nations, and kings of nations should be
of her posterity, Genesis xv chapt., 15, 16
verses.
	In the xxxii chapt. of Genesis, Jacob is
spoken of journeying with his wives, and 
the angels of God met him on his way, and
Jacob called them God's host.  And this
same Jacob was one of the main branches
of this polygamic tree.  In the same chap-
ter beginning at the 24th verse there is an
account given of Jacob wrestling with a
man, and prevailing; and Jacob knowing
this man was one of great power, would
not let him go until he should bless him;
and he obtained the blessing he desired,
and his name was called Israel, "for as a
prince hast thou power with God and with
man and hast prevailed."  And Jacob asked
his name, but he evaded answering the
question by asking another, "and Jacob
called the name of the place Peniel; for I
have seen God face to face and my life is
preserved," Genesis xxxii chapt., 30 verse.
We find again in the xxxv chapt., begin-
ning at the 9th verse, God appeared again
unto him and made the same promises to
him which He made unto his grandfather
Abraham; that he should be fruitful and
a nation and a company of nations should
come of his loins.  And the land which
God had given to his forefathers, Abraham
and Isaac should be given to him and his
seed after him.  And the Bible expressly
says he talked with God.  These testimo-
nies of the respect in which God held these
ancient patriarchs with their plural wives
abound throughout sacred history.
 Moses whom God called to lead the child-
ren of Israel out of bondage was also a man
who had plural wives.  We find in the xii
chapter of Numbers that the Lord instead

EDITORS EXPONENT:

	I have often felt impressed with a great
desire to write my feelings and the thoughts
that pass through my mind while medita-
ting on the work of God, and as I am a 
great thinker I have been led of late to con-
template the glorious and most wonderful
workings of the Almighty, and it causes
my heart to rejoice that I live in this day
and hear the servants of God talk in plain-
ness of the things which pertain to our
eternal salvation and exaltation in the
kingdom of God; they speak with power,
and it thrills my soul with joy while listen-
ing to their teachings, and I feel willing to
put away all my selfishness and think, Oh!
that I could live by every word that pro-
ceedeth from the mouth of God through
His servants.
 The cares and duties of a large family
prevent me from filling responsible posi-
tions as some of my sisters are so faithfully
doing, but I can fill a place which calls my
attention daily and hourly and which I can-
not neglect without feeling remorse of con-
science. It is our binding duty as mothers
in Israel to watch over our little ones to
keep them as much as we can out of dan-
ger. Those precious gifts which God has
given me, words cannot express the anx-
iety I feel regarding their welfare, especial-
ly those who are old enough to be taught
those principles whicn will help them to
fill honorable places in society, and become 
useful wives and good mothers. Our child-
ren are our hope, and on us depend their
future happiness in life. Keep them from
the company of those who would lead them
astray; it requires patience and a prayerful
heart, and it is no easy task to keep our
children in the paths of virute; we must
pray for strength from on high to enable
us to fill that high and holy calling, and
great will be our joy if we are permitted to
have those jewels in a heavenly sphere,
throughout the endless ages of eternity;
then will it not reward us for all our watch-
care and trouble, our toil and sorrow, if we
can accomplish this, by the help of God?
although we may work with fear and trem-
bling, if we save our sons and daughters
we will have accomplished something great.
Home and children are a blessing to wo-
man, and it should be her highest ambition
to set a good example before them especial-
ly as it falls more on the mother to train
them when the father is absent from the
family circle. The mother should call her
children together and bow in humble pray-
er for the light of the holy spirit to lead
them in the path of duty, this will give
that mother strength that she may endure
faithful while performing the most arduous
tasks which devolve upon her; she who is
sometimes so tired, and weary and would
be glad of an opportunity to retire to some
place of solitude for rest from the noise
and bustle which seems almost distracting
and unendurable at times.
						S. L. C.

 How little woman's labor is appreciated
by the husbands and fathers, they shrink
from some of their most important duties,
and leave too great a responsibility upon the
mother, and apparently can enjoy ease and
comfort elsewhere, while their sons are
breaking the Sabbath day, or doing that
which grieves a mother to the hearts core.
Oh fathers in Israel, when will you see to

dom of sentiment, but "out of the abund-
ance of the heart the mouth speaketh."

PATRIARCHAL MARRIAGE.
	The subject of patriarchal or plural mar-
raige is generally interesting to every one
especially to the outside world. Almost
the first question asked our missionaries by
the Gentiles, is "how many wives have
you got?" when they become aware they are
Latter-day Saints and from Utah.  An im-
mense interest is created when plural mar-
raige is mentioned. There are very few
that understand the principle of patriarch-
al marriage in its true light. The beauty
and consistency of it is only seen by those
that possess the spirit of the Lord; which
spirit enables us to see and understand 
things pertaining to life everlasting, and it
will comfort and cheer us in all the trials
which we are called to pass through. This
is a principle through which Satan tries us
with all his might; he shows us (that is if
we allow him to) that mole hills are about
the size of mountains, and little things,
which at other times we would pass by un-
noticed; he makes to appear very dreadful 
to bear. Blessed are the women who will
sustain this principle in the face of all oppo-
sition: Such indeed are mothers in Israel,
and true daughters of the living God.
When men and women take upon them-
selves these holy covenants, they should be
so sure that they are in the path of duty,
that all  busybodies there are cannot in-
fluence them or cause them one dark hour.
Sisters when a person comes to you and
speaks against this holy principle and tries
to discourage you in fulfilling the law given,
do not yield to the influence, but endeavor


to show to them the principle in its purity,
and how perfect should be the practice of
it. A part of woman's mission is to com-
fort and cheer those around her, especially
those of her own household; the wife
should comprehend the greater glory she
is attaining to through every wife added
to the family. The Lord fits the back for
the burdens and He blesses those who obey
Him in this principle in ways that are won-
derful; and besides if we are faithful and
keep the commandments of the Lord we
lay on this earth the foundation of eternal 
progression.
 Our religion is a practical religion, we
carry it out in our every day lives, and by
doing, our duty and living up to our privi-
liges and blessings and keeping our eye on
the mark, we help to roll on the kingdom
of God, and fulfill the mission we were sent
here to do. We should so live that when
our children grow up they may feel that
Plural Marriage is far superior to the one
wife system, and that they may not have
the prejudices to overcome that we have
had through tradition, and that our child-
ren may be as reconciled to it as the child-
ren of Jacob were. 
 Then sisters let us sustain this principle
with our whole heart and soul, for there are
noble spirits anxiously waiting to take tab-
ernacles here, and if the women in the
kingdom of God don’t fulfill the first great
law given to them, then others must be
raised up who will.
 Asking the Lord to bless all the daugh-
ters of Zion, and that all may attain to Ce-
lestial Glory who are earnestly striving for
it, is my sincere prayer.
					LIZZIE LEAKER.

HOME MANUFACTURE, AND
STORING GRAIN.

	There are many practical subjects of vital
interest now to the women of this church and
kingdom which ought to be taken into consid-
eration, and acted upon, as well as talked
about or written upon. This people are called
upon to be self-sustaining; to produce all that
is possible from the soil and from the elements,
and to manufacture all articles practicable at
home, and invent improvements in all the de-
partments of industry, which will be labor sav-
ing or time saving, and encourage all attempts
made in that direction, that will help to make
us as a people in reality what we have been so
long entreated to be, independent of Babylon;
that when she shall fall, as she inevitably
must, the Latter-day Saints may not be left
destitute of necessary comforts and blessings,
because they were not willing to hearken un-
to the voice of the Lord. But we cannot be-
lieve this will be the case. Surely the faith
the saints profess is real, and will bear the test
of endurance, though great exertions must be
made, and much self-denial practiced. After
having succeeded in establishing civilization
in a desolate country, enduring all the trials
and hardships consequent upon such an enter-
prise, this people of all people upon the earth
should be the last to go back to those who
thrust them out from their midst to perish of
hunger or cold, or in any other manner, es-
teeming them too vile to occupy cultivated
lands and homes. This people owe no grati-
tude to the world outside, that they should la-
bor and toil and accumulate means to support
Babylon. What obligations are the saints un-
der to their enemies? None save such as all
creatures owe to each other to do good for evil.
If people could only believe it, only compre-
hend it in its fullest and complete sense, they
are not even doing the good to the outside
world which they would be doing were they
to combine together and determine to be self-
sustaining. If this people will make the one con-
centrated effort they are now called upon to 
make, and not think they will do it at some
future time, but now, they will not become
slaves to Babylon and her wares, but will be
well prepared to administer to her in the day
of adversity, when "the hour of her travail"
comes. What is there now in this territory if
the great crisis which the saints well know is
impending were to come suddenly? Nothing,
except it may be in one or two parts of the
territory where home-industries have been
practically tried for a short time, but for the
majority, poverty and wretchedness, and mis-
ery. Shall we all be able to realize these great
temporal truths?

the facilities are indigenous here in Utah

	Another subject of the utmost interest to the
women of this territory, is the saving and stor-
ing of grain. We hope none of the good sisters
have forgotten the counsel given to them by
President Young last fall on this subject. We
do not wish to relinquish OUR efforts in any
sense in this direction in the least. We feel
that it is something the sisters have been called
to do by revelation, and let each one of us
be wise and use all the energy we possess to 
save every kernel of wheat raised this season,
and if it is really necessary that men should
sell or dispose of their grain for other things;
let the Relief Societies in the different settle-
ments be prepared to buy as much as possible,
and store it away carefully using every precau-
tion to preserve it from damp and insects of 
all kinds. We hope the sisters will also be
wise in preparing suitable places for storage,
now before the harvest comes on; take into
consideration all due preparation, and do not
be afraid you will not get the grain to store for
you will. This is not a trifling matter nor a
thing of little consequence, but a work that
demands some of the best thoughts and exec-
utive ability that woman has ever been called
upon to put into action. Many of the sisters
and children have told us they intend glean-
ing: no doubt the wheat fields will be filled
with gleaners this season. If the faith of the
sisters is good, there will be no danger of their
not having large quantities of grain to store,
and remember it is food for you and your
children in the future when war, desola-
tion and famine shall come, and the saints
know it will come. Remember this is salva-
tion both in a temporal and spiritual sense,
and it is worthy the sacrifice of fine clothes
and all superfluities. May the Lord aid and
bless the sisters throughout the territory in
this noble enterprise.

	Our home is situated on a sloping hill.
Just above us on the south west is the re-

most beautiful shapes and forms of trees
growing out of rocks; water gushing out of
the top of a mountain so high and perpen-
dicular that you can scarcely believe your
own sight. We are on a knoll with a pick-
et fence around the houses of Elders Wool-
ley, Lambert, Laie store and Plantation

of the Sandwich Islands, had I seen more of

think it would take a person with excellent
language to portray the beauties of this is-
land scenery; how I would like to be able
to describe it.
	We had the pleasure of a short, but very
pleasant visit from the Queen on the 28th
of February. She with her suite comprising
eighteen or twenty, mounted on fine horses
came dashing up to the door, which was
the first to give us warning of their in-
tended visit, there being some mistake in
the sending of the notice. Her business
was to organize Relief Societies. The peo-
ple were called together and she made a
speech, showing the necessity of such or-
ganizations, that the wants of the sick and
destitute might be administered to, and
thereby preserve the lives of many of the

took supper with us, and continued her
tour around the Island. Queen Kapiolani
is a fine looking woman, pleasant and agree-
able in her manner, a little above medium

being able to mount her steed without as-
sistance.

spirits. The mission is progressing slowly.
The term used by our Savior would well ap-
ply to this people "forgive seventy times
seven." Our annual conference here is
over. We have had an excellent time, all
our missionaries being here and all feeling
well. We look forward to our conference
with much pleasure as we can all meet and
enjoy the society of each other for a short
time. Our elders enjoy the spirit of their
mission. They have given good counsel to
the Saints here, and all seemed to enjoy it,
and want to try to do better in the future,
and live nearer to the Lord. In looking
over the congregation, I compared them
with our saints at home. I could see their
faces light up and fairly glow with the good

our Elders. The elders have been appoint-

A PROTEST.

	Sister Mabel:—I hope you will excuse
me if I appear blunt, and perhaps severe,
in my manner of expressing opinions about
your "Wishes and Suggestions," which ap-
peared in the EXPONENT of May 15th. It
is my nature to be plain spoken, and I
write as I speak.
	I consider the article referred to, the most
objectionable of anything that has ever
been published in our Woman's paper. It
seems contrary to the spirit of our religion
so uncalled for, uncharitable and unjust.
	Speaking of the honey bee as being "dis-
honest" for merely following the bent of its
nature, in sucking sweets from your or-
chard and garden flowers. And as "impo-
lite," for coming through an open window
to take honey from your table. In the first
place, it is nonsense to think that the bees
harm the fruit. A curse and a blight has
come upon our fruit because in former years
we have let the surplus waste, instead of
dividing it liberally with our poorer breth-
ren and sisters. The Lord will not suffer
His people to waste the blessings which
He gives them, without being punished
for so doing; and thus their blessings are
cursed to them. In the second place, there
is no need for the bees to trouble you by
coming through the open windows. A
piece of musquito bar may easily be
stretched across the aperture, and all such
trouble will be obviated. It appears to me
absurd to think of banishing our honey
bees from the city. Why, all through the
States they are kept in and about people's
homes.
	You speak of children who admire and
ask for flowers, as "little beggers," which I
think is very ungenerous. I should take
much pleasure myself, in having the little
things toddle in to ask me for a rose; and I
should never think of refusing them so
small a thing, while to turn one away with
a harsh reply would seem cruel indeed. I
believe we should encourage in our child-
ren a love for flowers and music, and all
such things, which bear refining influences,
and tend to elevate their young minds and
draw them heavenward. Too many peo-
ple who are well-to-do, are too much afraid
of taking a little trouble or going to the
least expense to gratify other children than
their own. But this is no part of our re-
ligion and should not be the feelings of a
Latter-day Saint.
	Many people are poor, and not yet able to
have fine flower gardens. But this is no
reason why their children should not be
taught to appreciate beautiful flowers. I
would teach them that roses are expensive,
but not to be ashamed to ask for them. We
go down on our knees before the Lord and
ask Him for gifts both greater and smaller,
and why not ask favors of each other, and
grant to each other such blessings as we
have in our power to bestow?
	I should consider being called from my
work to give a child a rose, the merest
trifle. As to your being called on such an
errand twenty times a day, I think that in
this expression you have gravely exaggera-
ted.

UNISON.

	Jesus has said "If ye are not one ye are
not mine." Although we may consider our-
selves true Latter-day Saints, if we are not
united in doing that which has been taught
us by those holding the Priesthood we can-
not claim to be one. Are we one in faith?
Yes. Are we one in baptism? Yes, we can
all see that alike. Do we all have faith in
the healing ordinance? A great many, if
not all of us have: we all should have.
Then in what are we not one? In worldly
goods. Have we anything that the Lord
did not give unto us? From what other
source did we derive the blessings that we
daily enjoy? Why do we have health and
life? Why are we not cripples as many of
our fellow beings are? Is it because we
have the ability to take better care of our-
selves than others have? I don't think so;
I think the Lord in His infinite goodness
and mercy gives us life health and ability
to serve Him, and to do good to others and
assist in building up and establishing His
kingdom upon the earth, for this last privi-
lege we should be especially thankful.
Then if He has done all this for us will He
not require obedience at our hands, to the
principles that are taught us from time to
time by His servants which are taught us
for our own benefit, not theirs? Are we
united on all these subjects? If we were
we would have more power to do good;
wher there is Union there is strength.
The Lord can take from us at any moment
life, health and prosperity. He can sweep
all before Him in one breath as it were,
and still He allows us to live on in obedi-
ence or disobedience, whichever path we
choose.
	Will He always be merciful? He has
said that the wicked shall be swept from
the earth; if they do not repent and serve
Him. He has said that the kingdom shall
not be given to another people. But a few
will stand firm and true to the covenants
they have made with God and each other,
and will assist in the redemption of Zion,
but we will only do it by being willing to
make every sacrifice that is necessary to
the accomplishment of that end. We may
have to do many things that are not pleas-
ant, we must not say we cannot, we must
say that we will serve God, come what
may; come life or death; and earn the re-

STORING GRAIN.

	RICHFIELD, SEVIER Co., April 5th, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	I will endeavor to give some information
concerning the progress of our Society; we
assure you that the sisters of Richfield are
not altogether dead, although so long un-
heard from. We read with surprise the in-
structions, when first given through the
EXPONENT, for the Relief Societies to Store
up Grain, and our hearts were filled with a
a true desire to obey the call from the ven-
erable leader of our church. It is a proof of
his confidence in the sisters, and we ought
to rise up in our might, yet with all humil-


ity, to engage in this noble enterprise, re-
membering that thereby we shall be able to
make restitution for past wrongs, for had
we, as a general thing, been obedient and
wise, more could have been accomplished
than has been, towards building up Zion.

	HOLDEN, WILLIARD CO., May 21, '77.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	We esteem it a privilege to contribute to
your valuable paper, which in perusing we
find very interesting and encouraging, more
especially the situations of the different
Relief Societies throughout the Territory,
with whom we wish ourselves numbered.
We are endeavoring to carry out the coun-
sel of our beloved President Brigham
Young, in laying up grain, planting out
mulberry-trees and encouraging home man-
ufacture as much as lays in our power, us-
ing every advantage possible. Our facili-
ties for raising grain are not so great as
some other localities, yet we intend with
the help of our Heavenly Father, to perse-
vere, and gain the reward allotted to the
faithful.
	We hold our meetings monthly, and ex-
press our feelings and the gratitude of our
hearts for the many blessings we as a peo-
ple receive from time to time.
	We also had our annual meeting March
16 which day we celebrated, commencing
with the usual exercises (singing and pray-
er) after which the annual statements were
read showing the attendance, donations
and labor performed to be very creditable;
and now have on hand quilts, lumber,
cash and grain, amounting to five hundred
dollars.
	Occasionally we are visited by President
Thomas Callister and a few of our leading
Elders who are deeply interested in the
prosperity of the cause of Zion and for
which we are laboring; they impart many
very valuable and impressive instructions to
us.
	Yours very Truly,
		EMMA J. BENNETT.


CORRESPONDENCE.

					MANTI, MAY 24, 1877.

EDITOR EXPONENT:

	Feeling a deep interest in the silk cul-
ture, and having been accustomed to the
business from my earliest childhood, I 
thought if I could say a word to encourage
those of my sisters who are starting out in
this branch of Home-Industry, I should be
most happy.
	In the first place I will say that I am sat-
isfied by experience, that Utah is quite as
favorable for silk raising as Old New Eng-
land, my native land, and in my young
days there was a great deal made there.
Beside this, it has the advantage of being
a healthy occupation for young or old. I
would say to the young married sisters,
have mulberry trees set out; if you will do
this, in a few years you will have some-
thing for your family to depend upon, which
will supply you with many of the comforts
of life, without applying to your husbands
for means, and will help to make you inde-
pendent in little things and perhaps able to
render assistance in many ways. I have 
heard it remarked, and very truly that 
President Young lives in an atmosphere
above us; I know if we had carried out


his counsel in regard to setting out mul-
berry trees, as well as in everything else,
we should be in a very different condition
to-day. The women of Utah are different
from many of those in the world. We
wish to be helpmeets to our husbands in
very deed. We are willing to spend our
time and the talents, ability and strength
which God has given us in doing good.
	I think we should cultivate that noble
principle of self-reliance that our Father
has implanted in our bosoms, and also in-
struct our children that if they have any-
thing they must earn it, and in time to
come they will bless us for it.
	We feel that we are a blessed people in
Manti, in having the privilege of assisting
to build a Temple in our midst. The sis-
ters here are determined to do what they
can in that direction. We have a fine large
meeting house in process of erection, un-
der the direction of our esteemed Bishop,
J. B. Maiben, who is ever alive to the
wants of the people and willing to encour-
age them in every good move and work.
	Wishing success to the Exponent, I will
close for the present.
	Your sister in the Gospel,
		R. WAREHAM.


RUNAWAY MARRIAGES.

	In a great majority of cases, a lady's elope-
ment is unwise, giddy, ungrateful, immod-
est, and evinces a lascivious appetite and
reckless disposition. Why should she de-
sert and distress those who have loved, nur-
tured and cherished her through all her past
years to throw herself into the arms of a
comparative stranger, who has done noth-
ing for her, and whose protestations of af-
fection have yet to undergo the first trial?
It is every way unworthy of pure and gen-
tle maidenhood to do so.
	We can imagine but one excuse for
her elopement, namely—the efforts of par-
ents or guardians to coerce her into marry-
ing some one she does not love. To avoid
such a fate, she is justified in running 
away, for no parent has or ever had a right
to constrain a daughter to marry against
her will. But where the parents are will-
ing to wait, the daughter should also con-
sent to wait, until her choice is assented to,
or she attains her legal majority. Then if
she chooses to marry in opposition to her
parent's wishes, let her quit her home open-
ly, frankly, in broad daylight and in such a
manner as shall kindly but utterly preclude
any pretense that her act is clandestine or
ill-considered. No one should be persuad-
ed or coerced to marry where she does not
love, but to wait a year or two for the as-
sent of those who have all their life done
what they could for her welfare, no daugh-
ter should esteem a hardship.
	There is some truth to be told about the
"common run" of masculine prowlers by
night, about garden walls and under bed-
room windows, in quest of opportunities to
pour seducing flatteries into the ears of
simple misses; but we have not time to tell
it now. As a general rule they are licen-
tious, good for nothing adventurers, who
would much rather marry a living than
work for it, and who speculate on the chan-
ces of "bringing the old folks round" after
a year or two. A true man would not ad-
vise, much less urge, the woman he loved, to
take a step which must inevitably lessen
the respect felt for her, and violate the truth
reposed in her by those who had loved and
cherished her all her days.—Home Circle.

	NOT only is kindness due to every one,
but a special kindness is due to every one.
Kindness is not kindness unless it be spe-
cial. It is in its fitness, seasonablesness, and
individual application that its charm con-
sists.

	HE that wrestles with us strengthens our
nerves and sharpens our skill. Our antag-
onist is our helper. This amiable conflict
with difficulty forces upon us an intimate
acquaintance with our object, and compels
us to consider it in all its relations. It will
not suffer us to be superficial.

	YOUNG men are as apt to think themselves
wise enough as drunken men are to think
themselves sober enough. They look upon
spirit to be a much better thing than experi-
ence, which they call coldness. They are
but half mistaken; for, though spirit with-
out experience is dangerous, experience
without spirit is languid and ineffective.—
Chesterfield.

	AGE AND YOUTH.—Age that is tender
and maternal, age that remembers its own
youth and does not demand bread from the
growing corn, nor wine from the green
grapes, and youth that can feel for infirmi-
ties which it does not know and that can
believe in wisdom which it has not reached
—these two together make a whole, perfect
for what it is, and turn a chance companion-
ship into a mutual blessing, and out of a
temporary association create a life-long
affection.


HYGIENIC NOTES.

	Bare Neck and Arms.—An eminent physician
declared: "I believe that during the twenty-six
years I have followed my profession in this city,
twenty thousand children have been carried to the
cemeteries, a sacrifice to the absurd custom of ex-
posing their arms naked. And yet it is said the
low-neck fashion is coming in again. Do not fol-
low it.
	Mental Development.—It is coming to be well
known that mental development is the result of
properly directed physical training; that the brain
grows in size and power by the varied exercise of
the senses and the will in mechanical employments
quite as rapidly as by purely intellectual efforts in
study or otherwise. It is equally known to phy-
siologists that most men are one-sided in heads as
in their bodies. The two halves of the brain are
rarely developed symmetrically, as may be readily
seen in the "conforms," or head measures accum-
ulated by hatmakers supplying individual custom-
ers. To some extent the difference in the contour
of the two sides of the head may be due to un-
equal pressure on the nurse's arm, or to the habit
of lying chiefly on the one side while sleeping,
thus causing a permanent displacement of the
walls of the skull, but the main reason appears to
be our one-sided habit of education. Dr. Brown
Sequard observes that the study of the facts in re-
lation to the brain has led him to believe that
"each half of the brain—paradoxical as it may
seem—is a whole brain."—Scientific American.
	Failures and Health.—The effect of so much
financial trouble is, for the time, very distressing
on the health of those involved. Debts and ina-
bility to meet them depress vital energy and im-
pair physical integrity; hence we see so many tak-
ing their own lives. The brain reels, the nervous
system totters and falls, and the man is a wreck
who once was the noblest work of God. There
is little hope of the generation now on the stage
that they will ever all learn the laws of true living


so as to be able to stand up against misfortune, or,
better still, avoid it; but there is a better time
coming, when people will study the laws of life as
revealed in our bodies and minds, and then things
will be different. They will then be so instructed
that they will refuse to enter upon a course of life
calculated to end in ruin to body and soul.
	Those in business will do well to heed the signs
of the times, and to guard the body from harm by
right living, and to keep their business within the
limits of their ability, so in times of pressure to
be able to endure. There can be no great and
abiding success in business enterprises without
sound health, good bodies, firm nerves, a stomach
capable of doing its daily duty, and a nervous sys-
tem that can throw off care at night, and take
sweet, serene repose. Look well to these things,
and there will be little to fear.—Herald of Health.


HOUSEHOLD HINTS.

very best method is to wrap each article in brown
paper. It has been proven one of the best pre-
ventives from moths in the world. Stiff seam-
less sacks are excellent to keep out moths, and
put in the sack some camphor and tobacco.
	Dough-nuts: One pint of flour, one table-spoon-
ful of sugar, one teaspoonful of cream of tartar,
half teaspoonful of soda, and two eggs. Mix
well with milk; roll out the paste, and cut it out in
any shape. Fry in boiling lard until a nice brown.
