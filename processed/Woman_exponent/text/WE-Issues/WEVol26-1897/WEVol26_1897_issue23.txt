AMERICA.
MY country ’tis of thee
Sweet land of liberty,
	Of thee I sing;
Land where my father’s died,
Land of the p lgrim’s pride,
From ev’ry mountain side,
	Let freedom ring.

My native country, thee,
Land of the noble free,
	Thy name I love;
I love thy rocks and rills, 
Thy woods and templed hills,
My heart with rapture thrills,
	Like that above.

Let music swell the breeze, 
And ring from all the trees,
	Sweet freedom’s song;
Let mortal tongues awake,
Let all that breathe partake,
Let rocks their silence break,
	The sound prolong.

Our fathers’ God, to thee,
Author of liberty,
	To thee we sing;
Long may our hearts be bright,
With freedom’s holy light,
Protect us by thy might
	Great God our King.
				S. F. SMITH.

A WOMAN’S VIEW OF THE GREEK
		QUESTION.

	BY MRS. L. S. DICKINSON.
	GREECE, whose name is rich in past
glories, has gone down in defeat on ac-
count of the inefficiency of her leaders, and
the overwhelming number of her enemy.
Other nations have looked on and smiled
because the loss was not their own, but
here and there throughout the world, are
men and women whose hearts have gone
out in sympathy toward the little country
in southern Europe, whose inhabitants
number less than a million people that
belonged originally to a race of men whose
deeds of bravery and loyalty have been
acknowledged by the gods of war, and
placed upon the pinnacle of fame from
where the light of their patriotism has
shone the world over, and will continue to
shine till time is no more.
	Greece is celebrated in the history of
freedom, of literature, art, philosophy and

[Column 2]
civilization generally, has fought for hearth-
stone as well as her religion, since the first
pages of European history were turned.
	Though two thousand or more years
have passed since the Golden Age, we are
refered more than we realize to the glories
of that time and people.
	If we look for some especially beautiful
piece of sculpture, we will be refered to
the work of some Greek artist. If we hear
of some remarkably beautiful woman,
we will perchance hear that she has Grecian
features. If we listen to a scientific sermon
or lecture, we will hear of the greatness of
some Greek poet or philosopher whose
voice is still heard in the halls of modern
learning.
	Greece is truly "the home of the poet
and the sculptor,” and her lamp of learn-
ing has indeed kindled an exalted admira-
tion in the hearts of the sons and daughters
of all civilized nations, for the heroes who
have left to their descendants names that
were “not born to die,” but to live on in
the memories of those that may follow on
for generations to come. For it was there,
in the land of Greece, through the bravery
and intelligence of those same heroes, that
political freedom for man was attained for
the first time in the history of the world.
	The glories of Greece never waned until
her government was wrested from her and
placed in the power of a nation whose every
thought and act was contrary to her own,
then the cloud of sorrow and despair set-
tled over her people, robbing them of the
power to maintain the standard of civiliza-
tion their fathers had established.
	The Greeks for hundreds of years have
believed that when a Constantine should
once more be united in marriage to a
daughter of Hellas by the name of Sophia,
it would be a day of rejoicing for Greece,
for she would then be in possession of Con-
stantinople once more and Prince Constan-
tine, Commander in Chief of the Greek
forces, being married to Sophia, a true
daughter of Hellas at the present time,
might, or might not have had some effect
upon the enthusiasm of the irregular band
of impatient but patriotic Greeks who
unfortunately precipitated the Greco-
Turkish war of 1897, before the Greek
Government was prepared for the struggle.
	In 1822 the Greeks rebelled against the
Turkish rule and after a fierce war in
which the Greek leaders made themselves
famous for all time by the brave and fear-
less manner in which they led their little
army against every conceivable disadvan-
tage on to victory, they recaptured cer-
tain parts of Grecian territory which are now
known as Modern Greece, whose literature
is still in its infancy and whose people have
been slow to regain their natural inherit-
ance of independence, as well as being
dilatory in trying to better their condition,
on account of their not having thoroughly
rallied from the depressing effect of Turk-
ish rule to which they were so long sub-
jected.
	Naturally, the Greek people were Platon-
ic in their love affairs and are sensitive in
the extreme, possessing the capacity to en-

[Column 3]
dure great suffering or great joy. They
have very weird imaginations and are
easily discouraged, and just as easily in-
spired by the din of battle and are willing
to follow a brave leader in whom they have
confidence into the very jaws of death.
When the news of the certainty of a
Greco-Turkish war reached this country
there was both fear and hope in every
home in the land where Greek history was
understood. Each heart trembled for fear
the Mother Land of political freedom
would go down in defeat on account of
her comparatively defenceless condition in
not being prepared for a great military con-
flict.
	The one hope common to every liberty
loving heart was that Greece, the one
country that had taught all other nations
their first lessons in intellectual develop-
ment, might conquer the people whose his-
tory is one of massacre and bloodshed,
whose very name is a disgrace to the world. 
	Had King George been of strong mili-
tary ability himself and possessed less con-
fidence in some of his personal friends and
in his son, Prince Constantine, whose
courage seemed to have vanished ere the
war began, there would have been a dif-
ferent story for the Greek sympathizers to
tell; but whatever the other faults of King
George might have been, his greatest
enemies must of a necessity give him credit
for the prompt manner in which he re-
sponded to the cry for help that came from
distressed Cretes when the brutal Turks
began their massacre on the island.
	Though King George was deficient in
the ability to command a great army, he
deserves commendation for the stand he
took in behalf of humanity; for when the
Powers continually threatened Greece with
coercion if he did not withdraw his troops
from Crete, he answered, “Greece knows
she must be victorious or disappear; we
know that all the Powers are more or less
against us, but a great wrong has been
committed against right and humanity in
the Cretan question, and the chastisement
has commenced.”
	The thoughtful lovers of justice will al-
ways honor the King of Greece for defend-
ing his countrymen and fellow Christians
as he did, by refusing to withdraw his
troops from Crete and leave the field in
possession of the Turk for he knew full
well if he did so the people would be
massacred, the island destroyed and turned
over to anarchy and ruin.
	It cannot be said of the Greek soldiers
that they were not good or willing fighters,
for even the Turks have given them that
credit, and when they have been ordered to
retreat on account of the bad management
of the officers in command, they have fallen
back with tears of vexation in their eyes
and curses upon their lips.
	What could have shown their loyalty
any more than the ready manner in which
they gave up their money, their homes and
their loved ones in this and other countries
and went to the rescue of their own country,
and to certain death, for they must have
known that when their ships sailed beyond


[Column 1]
the peaceful bars of their adopted countries
there would be no returning for them, un-
less the fickle Goddess of chance chose to
bestow her smiles upon them.
	What could teach the natural bravery of
the Greeks more than the voluntary ser-
vices of their men who have made their
homes in a land of plenty and reared their
families under the Star Spangled Banner.
Men who have learned to love and appreci-
ate freedom in all that the word implies,
and have given up all and gone to die if
need be for their mother land. The old
flag that is so dear to every American heart
has taken upon itself new glories for the
hundreds of brave men who have sailed to
Greece under its protection, for many of
those men while fighting for their own
native land have gone to their death loving
the flag of their adopted country, where
they have left all that is dear to them ex- 
cept their love and veneration for the
Fatherland. People are rather apt to cen-
sure the weak side of any contest without
a proper appreciation of the circumstances
surrounding the case. They will, so soon
as the one side has suffered defeat, immedi-
ately jump to the conclusion that something
has been done or left undone that might
have won for them the victory. Such
criticisms have been made of Greece, 
whereas the greatest possible amount of
praise should be unhesitatingly bestowed
upon her for having the courage to resent
a wrong and go to war in behalf of justice
and right, with a people whose army was
well equipped and well officered, was
drilled, advised and upheld by the Powers
of Europe and largely outnumbered the
Greek forces.
	Had it been the good fortune of Greece
to have conquered her Turkish enemy,
whose men deem it a pleasure as well as an
honor to rush into instant death because
their religion teaches them that beyond the
grave there is for them “ A Paradise where
sensual pleasures shall know no satiety.”
She would have bestowed a priceless favor
on the civilized world and gained for her-
self new glories that would have marked
the beginning of a new and brighter era for
her people But fate willed it otherwise
and the Powers had their way, and the
money Kings of Europe were saved, for the
time being at least.
	Thessaly not being recaptured by the
Greeks in the war of 1822 and 1823 but
ceded to her by the Turks in 1881, has
caused more or less dissatisfaction among
the Turkish people and that, coupled with
their late victory, has undoubtedly caused
the arrogant Sultan to demand the annex-
ation of Thessaly to Turkey as part pay-
ment of the indemnity more than his love
of adding to Turkish Territory.
	The Greek women as well as the men
are very enthusiastic and often take part
in the wars of their country, but if all re-
ports are true they have excelled them-
selves in the present conflict, as more then
one of the unspeakable Turks have fallen
by the hand of women. France, Brazil
Cuba and Greece have all had their Joan of
Arc, but Greece perhaps has possessed the
greater number of women who have taken
an  active part in the war.
	Evidently there are but few people in
Greece at the present time outside of the
army, as Miss Helene Eliopouloy, a Greek
maiden who recently emigrated to this
country, says that she has a father, three
uncles, three brothers-in-law and twenty-

[Column 2]
seven cousins in the Greek army, and that
she knew of seven Greek girls who entered
the army and each of them have now risen
to the rank of captain. Miss Clara Barton
expressed the sentiment of hundreds of
liberty-loving people in this country when
she, in answer to a communication from the
president of the Greek National League,
telegraphed the following message: “ Will
be very glad to co-operate in any way we
can. How can we best aid the cause?”
Queen Olga, the wife of King George, is a
true Greek who loves her country and is
in full sympathy with her people and their
cause. but unfortunately she places too
much confidence in the military ability of
her husband. She possesses great personal
charms and is highly accomplished, and
her most intimate friend is an American
lady who was born in Washington D. C.
	A patroitic person scarcely finds it pos-
sible to imagine any but a true and brave
Greek occupying King George’s apartments
in the old palace at Athens that was built
in the time of King Otho, for they are dec-
orated with the soul-stirring scenes of the
wars of liberation, and the tattered and
blood stained flags that were captured at
those same wars.
	A true Greek never gets so far from his
native land, so rich or so poor, but he re-
members to send home his remittance to aid
in establishing institutions of learning for
the rising generations of Greece. The
Athens University is a magnificent struc-
ture, and was built principally by contribu-
tions from exiled Greeks, and for this
reason it is called the National University.
	The men and women who have lived a
lifetime of freedom and plenty, would not
be so ready to condemn the Modern Greek
for his want of progress if they would but
put themselves in the Greeks place and
imagine, if possible, the state of affairs in
the land of Greece, when she has freed
herself by a fierce and bloody struggle from
her four centuries of bondage to a Turkish
ruler. Now that she has suffered new losses,
acknowledged her poverty and accepted
defeat, there will be those who will smile
in derision at the results achieved, but
there will always be those who will have a
tender reverence for, and proper apprecia- 
tion of the people who sanctified the altar
of liberty by the shedding of Christian
blood hundreds of years before our own
beautiful land of freedom, was dreamed of,
and they would be glad to join in the shout
‘‘Down with the Turk;” but that may
never be, for Christian Europe is so far
divided, and in division there is no strength.
It is the dream of France to possess Greece,
and England desires Egypt as the British
highway to India, and each of the other
powers have some covetous design that
seems to preclude the union that is neces-
sary to vanquish the power of the Sultan;
beside the money power in Europe as well
elsewhere, demands its share of attention
in diplomatic circles, and the bond holder
must have his dividend, though he be paid
in money stained in Christian blood.
	Sooner or later there must come a des-
perate struggle for supremacy between the
great powers of Continental Europe in
which England must also become involved,
and when the smoke of battle clears, it
will be found that the map of Europe has
been changed and that Turkey has been
divided among the so called Christian
nations.

U. W. P. C. 

	THE U. W. P. C. held its regular meet-
ing May 29, 1897 at the office of the WO-
MAN'S EXPONENT, President Ruth M. Fox
presiding. Prayer was offered by Mrs.
Young; the roll was called members re-
sponding with sentiments from N. P.
Willis; minutes of last meeting were read
and accepted. Under the head of business
the matter was discussed as to whether the
members should be assessed for the Feder-
ation expenses,or whether it should be taken
from the club funds. Dr. Pratt made the
motion that each member be assessed
twenty-five cents, which was carried. A
motion was made by Mrs. E. B. Wells that
a critic be appointed at each meeting, the
criticisms to be read at the following meet-
ing, which was carried and Mrs. A. M.
Bradley was appointed critic for that meet-
ing Papers on the war between Greece
and Turkey were read. Discussion opened
by Mrs. Dickinson on Greece, Mrs. Fox,
Turkey, Mrs. Alder, Greece. Discussion
followed by the members, which was very
interesting and brought out many points.
	Mrs. Alder, Mrs. Lambert and Mrs.
Bradley were appointed a committee on
programme. Meeting was adjourned for
one month. Benediction by Mrs. Doolan.
			ELLA W. HYDE, Act. Sec.

NOTES FROM WILLARD.

Dear Sister Wells:
	THINKING perhaps you would like to 
hear from our Relief Society, I take pleasure
in sending a few lines to your paper.
	Our venerable president, Sister Mary
Ann Hubbard, now past eighty years, is
able to meet with us and give us good
counsel. She has been president of the
Society forty years, she was appointed
president January 10, 1857, and after the
move south and the Saints returned to their
homes, she was again appointed president.
Her faithful counselors try to make her
duties as light as possible and all are united
in trying to do all the good they can.
	On the 9th of April a few of the sisters
met at the home of an aged couple, Brother
and Sister Renshaw, to celebrate their six-
tieth wedding anniversary, taking picnic
with them, and gave them some presents
that were well appreciated and all had a
pleasant time, especially the aged couple.
	On the 22nd of April, our general meet-
ing day, we met to have a general sociable,
and after the opening exercises our beloved
President announced that the sociable was
held in honor of our secretary, Sister Beth-
iah Fordham Wells, who had performed
her duty as secretary faithfully for nearly
sixteen years. Suitable pieces written for
the occasion, recitations and singing, also
an address by the Bishop occupied a portion
of the time. Then came refreshments, and
last but not least, our President in behalf of
the sisters of the Relief Society presented
the secretary with a beautiful fur cape, and
all said and felt that she had earned it.
Her heart was too full of gratitude for 
utterance. All had an enjoyable time long
to be remembered. 
With kind regards we remain,
	Your sisters in the Gospel
MARY ANN HUBBARD, Pres’t. 
BETHIAH F. WELLS, Sec’y.
Willard, May 12, 1891.

	REPLY TO THE QUESTION. 

		"WHERE ARE YOUR BOYS?
Ah, where are my boys do you question;
	That I am thus left here alone?
Three of them passed over the River,
	Only one remains of my own!
One striving with time’s fitful changes,
	Amid its vexations and trial,
Conflicting with crosses and losses,
	Only one left one of them all.
My girls too passed over the River,
	In the summer of life’s fair dawn,
Away from earth’s limits forever, 
	And left me their absence to mourn.
When yearning their presence to cheer me,
	Repeating their names o’er and o’er,
I fancy they often are near me,
	Drawn back from the "Evergreen Shore."
So I strive to overcome sorrow,
	Look away from the shroud and bier,
In hope, to that blessed tomorrow,
	To sing the glad song "We're all here."
							L. M. H.


AURELIA

REST dear one rest! And O, may it be sweet
In the land of the blest thy Savior to meet!
Thy pathway of life was troubledand sad,
May this “Happy New Year,” make thy tremb-
	ling heart glad.

O, hard is the journey that leads to the height;
But o’er the bleak mountains the peak is in sight;
There too to the traveler green valleys appear, 
Where the tired feet rest, and the Father is near.

The streamlets are bubbling a beautiful song;
And the wind of the South plays the tall trees
	among;
And there where the skies are sunny and blue,
There is rest for the weary, and happiness too.

Bright song birds are warbling their rarest notes
Like vespers at eve, the sweet melody floats;
From the crystal clear fountains the pearl drops 
	are falling, 
So faint on the ear like angel’s voice calling.

O, there is the rest most soothing and sweet;
And there the dear Lord his ransomed ones greet;
O, they are not lost but forever more blest
To all whom He loves, He giveth sweet rest.

Then ring out ye bells! this Happy New Year,
And tho’ on thy page hath fallen a tear,
Yet spotless and fair it will fade from the sight,
Fast melting in mist to the land that is light.

Rest, O there is joy when, the wanderer comes
	home,
Though foot-sore and weary breathing many a
	moan;
Yet the Father who notes the wee sparrow’s fall,
Outstretches His arms to gather us all.

Then sweet be thy rest this Happy New Year !
Though stand we in sorrow around thy young
	bier;
Yet in the fair mansions where the glorious ones
	dwell,
At the feet of the Father you say “ It is well.”
   A member of our Sundav School class who
died New Year’s Day, 1897.
L. D. ALDER.

LADIES’ SEMI-MONTHLY MEETING.

	Minutes of the Ladies’ Semi-Monthly
Meeting held in the 14th Ward Hall,
May 15, 1897, President M. I. Horne pre-
siding. Opened by singing “Glorious
things are sung of Zion;” prayer by Sister

[Column 2]
Maria Wilcox; continued by singing
“Though nations rise and men conspire;”
minutes of last meeting read and accepted.
	President Horne was thankful to be here
with us again. Thanked the sisters for
their prayers and sympathy during her
bereavement. “We are organized and
thereby we are strong. We have had so
much revealed yet we will have more as
soon as we live up to what we already
have.” Felt thankful for the Gospel and
for our lives which are a school for us. to
learn lessons. “I hope we shall learn
them well. We must bear patiently, and
not sink under our tribulations, to regret
when we are worn out and go to rest. We
are making our own records which will
stand for us forever.” Referred to the
Psalmist David, how he washed himself
and then ate food, and quoted his answer
to the question why did he do this, “We
go to meetings and hear His servants speak
about Him, yet here we testify one to the
other how good God has been to us." Re-
ferred to her Patriarchal blessings and how 
true they were, and how good the Lord had
been to her through all her sorrows and
afflictions.
	President Zina D. H. Young said, “I am
happy to be with you to day. President
Young instituted these meetings and what
a blessing they are to those who come here
with sad hearts. By the word of our testi-
mony we shall overcome. If angels come
here, they have a way to take notes and
these will be part of our record. We want
to lay up good words to think of. It is
sacred to come here. Every step we take
in obedience the stronger we will become.
Those who seek to do the mind and will of
God will come out all right. If things
come that you do not understand, do not
worry it will be all right, I am thankful 
for the Gospel. I used to be a Methodist,
and I was sincere but when the Gospel
came I gladly embraced it. Do not find
fault with the authorities; up hill or down
hill, hold on to that staff it will be all right.
The Gospel is good in youth, in old age
and good to die by.
	How I wish the seats were full! How I
wish we all appreciated our privileges!
How I wish I could whisper to the spirits
of our sisters ‘Come to meeting!’ I love to
praise the sisters. We are blessed, but let
us be humble. I want to hear the sisters
speak of the loving kindness of the Lord.”
	Sister Leaker bore her testimony to the
truth of this work. “I would not dare
raise my voice against the Lord’s anoint-
ed. The Lord will bless all those who
trust in Him. If you are in trouble go to
the Lord, He will not tell any one else, and
He will bless you.”
	Sister Maria Wilcox thanked the Lord 
that we had mothers in Israel to give us 
such precious words as we have had today.
Said we should be ready to perform every
duty that devolves upon us. Spoke of the
Temple work and encouraged the sisters
to go and do their Temple work. 
	Sister Davis bore her testimony. Told
of a man who was born and raised here,
yet had never spoken to President Wood-
ruff, yet was so anxious to do so. “I feel
how privileged I have been and I am so
thankful for my many blessings.”
	Sister Bassett said she had a testimony
to bear; thought we should appreciate the
many blessings we have. Have no desire
to find fault. Spoke of her parents suffer-
ing and endurance at Nauvoo; made
many encouraging remarks.

[Column 3]
	Sister Margaret Steele spoke of her
pleasure in coming to meeting and seeing
the faces of her sisters. “I have never
doubted the truth of this work. I try to
live my religion and try to be faithful.”
Spoke of charity, and how we should look
at each other, perhaps we would do no bet-
ter in the same place.”
	Sister Jones said, “I know that God
lives and that He has spoken in these last
days, and that we still have His mouth-
pieces here with us. These meetings here
always have been such a comfort to me. If
the sisters only knew how glorious these
meetings are I know they would come."
	Sister Jane James bore her testimony.
She was thankful that she was in this
kingdom and desired to be faithful. “I
desire to do the will of my Father who is
in heaven. I know this work is true, it is
all we have worth living for but the Spirit
of God.” Concluded by speaking in
tongues; interpretation given by Sister Zina
D. H. Young.
	Sister Julia C. Howe said, “I have
been so delighted with the counsel given
and the testimonies borne, that I feel that
I would like to add my humble testimony.
I have a testimony that Joseph Smith was
a true prophet.” Spoke of her experiences
in England; how good her parents were,
and how glad she was that she ever heard
the sound of the Gospel. Spoke beauti-
fully on charity,
	Sister Gardner bore her testimony. Sis-
ter Margaret O. Brown felt to encourage
the sisters in Temple work. Said it was a
glorious work, and how glad we will be to
meet those whom we have officiated for
when we get behind the vail.
	President Horne made closing remarks
and meeting adjourned for two weeks, at
usual time and place. Closed by singing,
“’Mid scenes of confusion.” Benediction
by Sister L. D. Alder,
				L. D. ALDER, Act. Sec.


NOTES AND NEWS.

	La Ligue for January announces that an
International Congress for Women will be
held in Brussels in August next. The
Congress will be organized by the Ligue
Belge du Droit des Femmes.
	MRS. KATIE R. ADDISON, president of
the Kansas Equal Suffrage Association,
calls attention to the fallacy of the accusa-
tion constantly made that advocates of
equal rights are almost all spinsters. She
says: “I do not know how it may be else-
where, but in Kansas we have but one un-
married woman on the official board, and
she is a young woman (Laura Gregg)."
	A MASS-MEETING of nearly 2,000 wo-
men was held recently at Omaha, Neb., to
elect eleven of their number for the Board
of Lady Managers of the Trans-Mississippi
Exposition. Of the women chosen, one is
an author of some note, one a well-known
artist in china painting, one a musician of
acknowledged talent, another a promising
member of the Library Board, another was
for years the general manager of the For-
eign Missionary Societies of the Christian
Church, three are teachers of ability con-
nected with the city schools, and the rest 
are women prominent in both literary and
social circles.

SALT LAKE CITY, JUNE 1, 1897.


THE NEW VOLUME.  

	How glad we should be if we could say
the new volume commences under auspici-
ous circumstances or with a hopeful out-
look, but unfortunately it is not so, and yet
if the women of the community could only
realize the value of having a paper of their
own it might be very different. We know
there is always a faithful few who are prompt
in payments,and who would rather do with-
out something else so that they might help
sustain a home periodical. All our sisters
must be aware by this time, having been
frequently reminded through these columns,
that there is great need of extra exertion
on their part just now in the midst of finan-
cial depression, to maintain their own
journal.

BRIGHAM YOUNG’S BIRTHDAY.

	The anniversary of President Young’s
birthday was celebrated under the auspices
of the Young Family Association at the
famous Lion House in this city, Tuesday,
June 1. The grand old parlor and hall
were profusely decorated with stars and
stripes, and with beautiful flowers, and
portraits large and small of Brigham Young
were hung in conspicuous places and draped
with national colors and with small flags in
the most picturesque fashion; there was
also here and there a bust of President
Young, and these additional features gave
character to the rooms and to the gathering.
The party was composed almost exclusive-
ly of the wives and descendants of Presi-
dent Young himself, with a few of his
brothers wives and their children, and
some particular friends.
	There was a little delay in opening the
exercises which had been arranged by a
committee of the sons and daughters, as
President Wilford Woodruff was expected,
but when word came that he did not feel
quite able to attend, the program opened
with Captain Willard Young, Master of
Ceremonies, as Apostle Brigham Young,
the eldest son (who has recently been very
ill) was scarcely strong enough to pre-
side. The assembly joined in singing,
“We thank Thee O, God for a Prophet,”
and an eloquent prayer was offered by
Bishop H. B. Clawson. President Lorenzo
Snow made the opening address which con-
tained an earnest appeal to all present, but
especially to the family of President Young
to officiate in the ordinances for the dead in
the house of the Lord, to live lives of devo-
tion to the work for which he whom they
met to honor and reverence today had lived
and had died, leaving the inheritance of
this work to be done by his posterity.
	Apostle Brigham Young made a feeling
address which contained many old-time rem-
iniscences, and was full of affectionate
interest for all.
	Song “O, My Father,” by R. C. Easton
in his inimitable rendering, Spencer Clawson
Jun. playing the accompaniment; piano
solo, Spencer Clawson; song, Lulu Moore;
“Many happy returns of the day;” read-
ing (Humorous) Master Frank Fenton
Young; song “Mona,” by R. C. Easton,
a most exquisite melody; piano duet,
Emmie and Lyle Yeung; verbal report of
the Young Family Association, Susa Y.
Gates; song Lula Moore, “Hearts and
Homes;” recitation Miss Frankie Young,
written by Mrs. R. E. Monch, title, Brigham
Young; address, Mrs. Zina D. H. Young;
Negro song in dialect with mandolin, Alice
Clawson—followed by an encore; song,
“Tit for Tat,” Nellie Brown; Lulu Gates
piano solo and song “The Garden of
Sleep,” also a Kanaka song; recitation,
Zebulon W. Jacobs, words by Elder C. W.
Penrose on the death of Brigham Young.
After the program delicious refreshments
were served and conversation of a reminis-
cent character generally was indulged in.
One particular feature of the day was the
singing of the old song “Twenty Years
Ago,” by about twenty of the daughters
and grand-daughters of the President.
This was considered appropriate on account
of its having been the twentieth anniver-
sary since the day was celebrated last by
the President himself, June 1, 1877.
	There is no place in Salt Lake City per-
haps of a more historic character than the

[Column 2]
Lion House, where the greater part of the
family of President Young lived so many
years, where so many social events have
been celebrated, and where hundreds of
strangers have been entertained by this re-
markable man and by members of his fam-
ily, notably Sister Eliza R. Snow, the poet
and author, and where both President
Young and Sister Eliza died and several
other members of the family. It was in
this house many of Sister Eliza’s poems
were written, and also there the girls and
boys in their youth and childhood had
many parties and private theatricals.
	In that house President Young organized
the Young girls of his own family into an
Association for mutual improvement and
spiritual elevation and advancement in
knowledge and wisdom, and that they
might obtain a more correct understanding
of the principles of life and salvation and a
testimony of the truth of the Gospel. This
association has grown and flourished until
now it numbers many thousands.
WRITTEN FOR THE ANNIVERSARY OF PRESI-
DENT BRIGHAM YOUNG’S BIRTHDAY
JUNE 1, 1897.
	IT is right and proper that children
should honor their parents, and what is
more fitting than to meet together on their
parents natal day, especially after their
death, and talk over the reminiscences of
their childhood and rehearse all the good
and pleasant things they have experienced,
and store their minds with memories rich
and precious to hand down to coming gen-
erations.
	The old Lion House is a monument of
historical reminiscences, some pleasant and
some of a sorrowful nature.
	The childhood days of most of the sons
and daughters of President Young were
spent in the Lion House; some were born
there and some died there; some lost their
mothers, some their children. Life is
made up of a variety of circumstances,
some bringing happiness, others sadness.
Perfect happiness is not to be looked for in
mortal life. None can go through this
imperfect state of existence without making
more or less mistakes, and we ought to
overlook a great deal, which we all certainly
can do, when we consider our own weak
and imperfect natures. If children have
differences they are soon forgotten and
naught but the happy times remembered.
Ties have been formed that will never be
broken. Children grow up, get married
and are in a measure separated, but there
is in each heart a bond of love that draws
them together. A love that has grown
with their lives and will continue to grow
stronger and stronger while time shall
last, and in the eternal worlds it will grow
no less. I do not think another family as
large as President Young’s could be found,
where there was less trouble and difficulty
than among those that dwelt in the Lion
House.
	It is surprising how well plural families
got along together. I am reminded of a
little circumstance that took place soon
after I moved to the Head House (now
owned by my daughter, Mrs. Emily Claw-
son.) It was when Brother Piper had
charge of President Young’s temporal
affairs. He called to see me one day and
said Sister Clara D. Young had requested
him to look after me and see that I did not
lack the comforts of life. He expressed
some surprise that one wife should take


such an interest in another wife in the
same family. Well take them all together,
President Young had about as good a fam-
ily as could be found; and as far as I know
the best of feelings exists among them at
the present time. We have had our ups
and downs. Our trials have been for good,
and the longer we live, and the older we
grow, we can see that we have no cause to
feel sorrowful, but every reason to thank
the Lord for His mercies and kind care
that has been over us; and that He has
deigned to guide us in the right way. Our
paths may not always have been strewn
with thornless roses, but we have been the
recipients of many favors. And as for my-
self, I feel more and more thankful that I
took the course that God marked out for
me, although to look ahead at the time
things looked very dark. And I know that
my children will honor and bless my name
in the far future. I think they realize now
to a certain extent the sacredness of my
marriage with the Prophet Joseph, but
perhaps not as perfectly as they will here-
after. I feel glad that I have been asso-
ciated with the family of President Young.
We have formed ties that will last through
the eternities.
	The last scenes that have been associated
with the old familiar house were of a very
sad and sorrowful nature. The most try-
ing scene of all was when President Young,
the father, the husband and our protector
passed from this world away to a better
land. His family were left like sheep with-
out a shepherd.—Well of what followed I
need not speak, we need no reminder.
Some things are better forgotten if possible.
Times are now changing and we can see
the hand of the Lord in all things.
	May the family continue to be united,
and those who have wandered return and
be reclaimed, is the heartfelt desire of,
			EMILY D. P. YOUNG.

man in many ways. Her husband came

also had in the party a distinguished French

the Text Book Convention and was the

men of notable and exceptionally fine 
qualities and attainments.

was a very fascinating woman. She has
traveled much and kept her eyes open, and
can describe in glowing terms what she has
seen. She is a true philanthropist and a
member of the Red Cross, is a friend of
Clara Barton’s and of the Misses Johnson’s
who visited Salt Lake a year or so ago,
and were entertained by the Press Club, or
rather we should say, entertained the Press
Club most delightfully. Madame Cleophas
has the charming personality peculiar to


the cultured women of France. She is a
brilliant conversationalist and highly en-
tertaining in her descriptions of persons and
places. Madame has had some sorrowful ex-
periences; she has promised the writer a
sketch of her past life which we hope to
have soon, when we may be able to tell
you some thrilling incidents through
which she has passed in her early woman-
hood.
	MRS. CLINTON SMITH was born in Ver-
mont among the Green mountains, and
has all her life been engaged in philan-
thropic work, and has been a contributor to
the “Rural New Yorker,” “Weekly Wit-
ness,” “Demorest’s Magazine,” and other
periodicals. She has used the pen names,
Alica and August Noon, and is now ac-
tively engaged in literary pursuits, though
she has a family of five children and judg-
ing from her conversation one would think
her an excellent housekeeper as well as
wife and mother. Her home was in Mid-
dlebury, Vt., until 1891, when her husband
accepted a Government position in Wash-
ington, D. C. Her father was a pioneer
geologist in his native state. Mrs. Smith
attended the meetings of the Federation of
Clubs held in this city, and made a neat
little speech during the discussion on
Thnrsday morning in which she paid a
high compliment to Senator Frank J. Can-
non who had been most kind and helpful
to the ladies in their work on special com-
mittees with which she had been connected.

Thursday evening at Mrs. Nelden’s, escorted

and certainly she left agreeable impressions

27, commencing at 10.30 on Wednesday
morning, May 26. There was a good
sized audience in the Church and the dec-
orations of flowers and plants were very
fine. It was interesting to note the person-
nel of the women who took part in the pro-
ceedings, for however beautiful any hall
may be, it is not so much of a study as
human nature; and we have often thought
the studies from life in an assembly of this
kind were very valuable. The entire
audience was quite enthusiastic in their
welcome to Mrs. Henrotin, the General
President. The President, Mrs. Jennings,
is a very fine executive officer, and has a
winning manner, and Mrs. Henrotin of
Chicago has a graceful and attractive style
about her, and one knows directly that she
has been much in society.

types of women, the guests from Colorado;
besides the visitors from Ogden, Provo,
Springville and elsewhere. In fact it was
a most delightful collection of women, if
one may be permitted the expression, and
the occasion was inspiring and exhilarating.
There was so much to do and to say and
the enthusiasm never flagged, at least that
is the expression of one member present.

F. Putnam and was earnest and eloquent.
Minutes of last year read and approved.

EVENING SESSION.
	After an organ selection by Professor
Radcliffe, Mrs. Jennings introduced Mrs.
Henrotin, who commenced her address by
stating that she was usually accustomed to
speak only to ladies and that it gave her
additional pleasure to include the gentle-
men among her hearers, as this presence
indicated that the gentlemen of Salt Lake
City were most devoted to the woman’s
cause. She cited three instances that had
come within her personal experience where
prominent public men had made most perti-
nent statements in regard to this cause.
One of them was a remark by Cardinal
Gibbons before a meeting of clergymen in
France that the labor movement and the
movement for the advancement of women
must go on hand in hand, and that as soon as
that fact was recognized the solution of the
two problems would be reached. Another
was a statement by Prince Bismarck that if
the great government of Germany were to
survive, the women of the land must be-
come interested in politics. The speaker
also repeated a public utterance of one of
the great labor leaders of this country to
the effect that now that the women were
learning organization the labor question
would soon be settled. Mrs. Henrotin
then reviewed the history of woman’s or-
ganization, and the incidents that led to
the formation of the present federation.
She said that as women were naturally ad-
apted to the most inaggressive methods, she
chose education as her first step, and that
the development had reached a point now
that there was no woman’s club in America
where education, politics and religion could
not be discussed freely. Soon after the
educational feature was well advanced the
department clubs commenced to be formed
and many instances were cited, particularly
among the department clubs of Chicago and
Minnesota, showing the good they had ac-
complished. Federation, which is the
principle of modern civilization, naturally
followed local and state organization, and
the National Federation which commenced
with 30 clubs, now had a membership of
500 clubs. The speaker declared that the
federation in its liberty and freedom was the
greatest example of organization that the
world had ever seen. The women of Utah
were second only to those of Maine in em-
bracing the federation principle. Mrs.
Henrotin gave several amusing incidents
that had followed the department club work,
among them being the town and village
improvement associations, where the women
of these clubs taught the school children to
become interested in this work. She told
of one little girl who reported to her local
association that she had not been able to ac-
complish much herself but she had induced
her brother to cut the grass in their yard,
and a small boy reported that all he had
been able to do was to stop every man he
met and ask him not to spit on the side-
walks.
	One of the advantages of club life and
federation which Mrs. Henrotin point

[Column 3]
out was that it produced a strong, healthy 
social life and a spirit of true fellowship
which is not to be found in what is or-
dinarily known as society. It produced
a democracy among women which nothing
else could accomplish. The Woman’s
Federation had no one leader, for hundreds
of women were coming into the movement
daily and were giving their hearts to the
work and each would thus lead the other.
Mrs Henrotin closed her address by urging
her hearers to maintain the spirit of organ-
zation at all times.
	Mrs. Thatcher, the president of the Colo- 
rado Federation, then read an address in 
which she extended greetings and congratu-
lations to the women of Utah, and after re-
viewing the work that women had accom-
plished in her own state she extended a
cordial invitation to all present to attend the
coming sessions of the Federation in
Denver.
	Mrs. Gale, the vice-president of the
Colorado Federation, made a most amusing
address in which there were many good-
natured references to the work of the or-
ganization and the persons connected with
it. She apologized for not speaking at
some length by saying that as Mrs. Hen-
rotin had reviewed the work of the national
federation, and Mrs. Thatcher bad exhaust-
ed the subject of the Colorado federation,
and as Mrs. Platt who was to follow her
would talk of everything under the sun,
there was nothing left for her to say. Her
remarks related largely to the work of west-
ern women in general as compared with
that of their sisters east of the Mississippi.
She expressed the greatest pride in the
work accomplished by western women,
particularly those of Colorado and Utah.
	After another organ selection by Profes-
sor Radcliffe, Mrs. Platt, the president of
the Denver Woman’s club, delivered the
closing address of the evening and the num-
ber of bright, amusing stories that she
told in connection with her remarks were
appreciated by the audience. Her speech
was not confined entirely to the subject of
woman, but she had much to say on the
subject of man, among other things that
she wished to see the day come when man
could be taken into women’s clubs, but she
was afraid that there was only one way to
do this and that was like the story of the
New York flats where cruel-hearted land-
lords, no matter how badly they may want
to see their property rented, make the iron-
clad provision that no children shall be ad-
mitted. A little girl whose parents had
made many unsuccessful attempts to rent
a flat and had always been refused on ac- 
count of their child, finally saw another
little girl leaning out of the window of one
of the flats and in her amazement she asked
her how she ever got in there. The other
little girl replied that she was “borned”
in there.
	Mrs. Platt denied the existence of such
a being as the ‘‘new woman” and said
that the only thing new about her was her
club life and organization and the good she 
had accomplished by taking her proper 
place in life. Man need not fear that wo-
man would outstrip him in the race as he
had such a long start before woman com-
menced. Mrs. Platt argued that woman’s
work in the world for the advancement of
her own sex and the relief of distressed hu-
mauity could not destroy her sphere in the
home, and she urged her hearers to regard
home life in the same light that any good
man would regard it.


She dwelt at length on the spirit of
fellowship which club life and organiza-
tion produced among women. Mrs. Platt’s
remarks drew forth continued applause at
the close and her hearers desisted only after
she arose and told them that unless they
did so she would tell them another story.

SISTER ISABELLA PRATT PRICE.
	In, memory of Sister Isabella Pratt Price who
departed this life February 25, 1897 She was
the daughter of George and Margaret Nelson
Pratt and was born in Armbey, Yorkshire, Eng-
land, October 26, 1824. She was baptized and
confirmed a member of the Church of Jesus
Christ of Latter-day Saints, April 8, 1845, at York-
shire, England.
	Sister Price joyfully received the Gospel but
could not understand the principle of gathering,
thought the Lord would save her in England
without the necessity of going to America. After
making the subject a matter of prayer, it was re-
vealed to her in a dream, the plan of salvation
for the Saints in the Latter days; after that she
had no more doubt about the gathering.
	Apostle Erastus Snow on his way to Denmark
on a mission preached in Bradford. She with
some others sought a blessing at his hands, he
said in part that she should go to Mount Zion and
be a savior in her father’s family, all of which has
been fulfilled; a few months after the blessing
was given she left England in the ship James
Pennel, October 3, 1850, to g ther with the Saints
in the gathering place appointed by the Lord.
	She was very faithful in the performance of her
Temple work, doing all she could f r her relatives

[Column 2]
and friends, and she was very anxious to continue
the work had her life been spared.
	She was a devoted mother, teaching her child-
ren the laws of the Gospel from their earliest
youth. She leaves three sons and two daughters
each of these faithful members in the Church,
numerous grand-children, relatives and friends
to mourn her loss.
	She obeyed all the principles of the Gospel as far
as they were revealed to her. She was a mother
to the motherless, performing a noble part. All
through her long illness she was tenderly cared
for by her son, Brigham F. and his wife Sister
Margaret Price
	Our dear sister was a faithful member of the
Relief Society. She was always in attendance
whenever her health would permit. She was a
very fluent, intelligent speaker. She was firm in
her convictions for the advancement of women, a
supporter of the EXPONENT and all good works
in that direction.
	She has finished her work in this probation
and gone to meet those of her loved ones whom
she has officiated for in the Temple of the Lord.
	The funeral services were held at the family
residence, the house was filled to overflowing
with sympathetic relatives and friends. Bishop
J. C. Hanson and Counselors were present; many
consoling remarks were made by the presiding
officers of the ward, each bore testimony to the
faithfulness of Sister Price. Beautiful flowers
covered the casket, the hymn “O my Father,”
was sung by the choir. A large cortege followed

VICTORIA THE GREAT.

POEM BY ALFRED AUSTIN, POET LAURE-
ATE.

	NEW YORK, June 16.—In its forthcom-
ing issue, the Independent, under the head-
line “Victoria the Great,” will publish the
following poem written for that paper by
Alfred Austin, poet laureate of England:

The dew was on the summer lawn,
	The roses bloomed the woods were green,
When forth there came as fresh as dawn.
	A maiden with majestic mien.

They girt a crown about her brow,
	They placed a scepter in her hand,
And loud rang out a nation’s vow,
	“God spare the lady of the land.”

And now the cuckoo calls once more,
	And again June’s roses blow,
And round her throne her people pour.
	Recalling sixty years ago.

And all the goodly days between,
	Glory and sorrow, love and pain,
The wife and mother, widowed queen,
	The loftiest as the longe t reign.

She shared her subjects’ bane and bliss,
	Welcomed the wise, the base withstood,
And taught by her clear life it is
	True greatness to be good.

Yet while for peace she wrought and prayed
	She bore the trident, wore the helm,
And mistress of the main she made
	An empire of her island realm.

So gathering now from near from far,
	From rule where never sets the day,
From Southern Cross a d Northern Star.
	Her people lift their hearts and pray.

Longer and longer may she reign,
	As through a summer night serene,
Whence day doth never wholly wane;
	God spare and bless our empress-queen.


A HEART OF GOLD.

BY CLIO.
	A FAIR young girl stood at the garden
gate at the twilight hour of the 4th day of
July, 1857. She was dressed in holiday
attire, for there had been a celebration and
at that date, no such event ever occurred
without the assistance of Miss Grace Ali-
son.
	The expression on her face was rather

[Column 2]
an anxious one, as she glanced restlessly,
first in one direction, then in another. Sud-
denly her blue eyes brightened, as she
caught sight of a horseman in the distance,
instantly followed by a look of impatience
as she saw he was not alone.
	“Oh, dear!” she sighed, “why will
John associate with those Adams boys.
Everybody knows they are the worst boys
in town, yet he will persist in befriending
them. It puts me out of all patience.”
	By this time the horsemen were nearly
opposite where Grace was standing. They
came along at full gallop, whooping at the
top of their voices, curvetting here and
there and cutting all sorts of antics. They
did not see the girl until they were close
upon her, or it is quite likely they would
have exhibited better behavior. As it was
they drew up their horses sharply, bowed
in a shamefaced manner, and made out as
though they would stop and engage in con-
versation. Grace looked straight at them,
without a sign of recognition, and as they
approached, turned on her heel, and walk-
ed away toward the house.
	“Humph!” exclaimed Joe, the eldest of
the Adams’, “proud as Lucifer, ain’t she?
S’pose she’s ashamed of us. All right
Miss Alison, when I attempt to speak to
you again, it will be a long time from now.
Come Jack lets go down to the Square.”
Away he galloped, followed closely by his
brother, neither noticing, in their headlong
haste, that John Heplar did not follow
them. That young gentleman, complete-
ly sobered by the cool treatment they had 
just received, rode slowly away in an op-
posite direction. Several times he reined
in his horse, on the point of turning back
and seeking an interview with Grace; but
as often he changed his mind, being asham-
ed of his conduct and afraid of her scorn.
	It had been his intention to invite Grace
to the party; but he had fallen in with his
evil genii who had towed him about all
day, until they had made him half drunk
and wholly miserable. He resolved now
to stay at home, and not show himself at
the party at all.
	He flung himself down upon his bed, but
he could not sleep. The demon of jealousy
took possession of him; he felt that he
must know if Grace had gone, and who
had been her escort. If it was that Charlie
Benson,—his blood surged hotly through
his veins at the bare thought, and he felt
as if he should smother. He arose quickly,
put on his hat, and started for the scene of
merry-making.
	He looked eagerly among the crowd
congregated under the great bowery. Yes
sure enough, there she was, and dancing,
though he could not deny that she looked
grave and troubled. She was not dancing
with Charlie Benson, much to his relief,
but with an elder, married brother. He sat
down in a corner to watch, and nurse his
misery, neither occupation proving very
agreeable. He sought later for an oppor-
tunity to speak to Grace, but was not suc-
cessful. She seemed deliberately to shun
him, and put obstacles in the way of a
reconciliation. He went home miserable

[Column 3]
enough, and continued in that condition
for several days. When he thought he
could endure it no longer, he summoned
all his courage, went boldly to her father’s
house, and asked to see her.
	Miss Alison received her guest in the
stateliest and coolest manner compatible
with good breeding. It was quite evident
that poor John had offended deeply the
girl who held the first place in his affec-
tions. Being somewhat prepared for his
present reception from previous experience, 
he was not so abashed as one would ex-
pect and being also courageous in many
ways, he at once plunged headlong into 
the subject which had brought him there.
	“Grace,” he said eagerly, “I am
ashamed of my conduct on the Fourth. I
know I have offended you, and have come 
to ask your pardon.”
	“And if I grant it,” she said, in a cool,
even tone, made so by a great effort,
“What guarantee have I that the same
thing will not be re-enacted on the very
next holiday. You have been ashamed so
many times John Heplar, have offended
and been forgiven so often, that it is like the 
rehearsal of an oft-repeated play, and
amounts to about the same result—
nothing.”
	“You are hard on a fellow,” he mutter- 
ed, “I own I acted foolishly, but I did no
harm, unless to myself.” 
	“No harm,” exclaimed the girl, “do
you consider it is no harm to associate with
those who profane the name of their
Maker; who make demons of that 
which should be second only to the angels;
who break the hearts of their parents, their
sisters and wives; and above all, who set
such a perilous example for my little
brother to follow? For shame, John Hep-
lar.”
	He sat looking at her stupidly as she
poured out this torrent of hot words. It
was an unusual thing for Grace Alison to
express herself so forcibly, being noted for
her equable temper, and the event seemed 
to daze him. When she had finished he
answered quietly.
	“Well Grace, I grant there is truth in
what you say, especially the example part, 
though I own I have never thought of it in 
that light before. I remember I promised
you to forsake my old companions, and I 
have not done so. In relation to the other
matter, I have not been able to see through
it, as you predicted. I have tried your 
plan faithfully, and must say that I am as
much in the dark as ever. You and I
might just as well give up that scheme, 
and try to be happy without so much re-
ligion thrown in."
	“Do you mean to say,” said Grace,
slowly and with an effort, “that you have
thrown aside all hope of being able to see
and embrace the faith of your father and
mother—of your staunch—faithful—father,
and your brave—devoted mother?”
	“I am sorry to say that is my lamentable
condition,” he replied sadly.
	“Then John Heplar,” and her voice
trembled a little, and a mist obscured her
sight, “We may just as well say good bye


[Column 1]
also to our hopes and wishes regarding
each other. You know my belief, and I
cannot—dare not, act contrary to my con-
victions.”
	“Grace, dear Grace, why will you
cleave to these deluded notions? Why in-
sist on making yourself and me unhappy
from a mere whim?”
	“Whim! I hope and pray that you
may some day know the value and force of
the whims which now separate us. Until
that time comes, John Heplar, I will say
goodbye, and when we meet hereafter it
will be as acquaintances only, or as strang-
ers, as you please.”
	Before he could realize it, she fled from the
room and he was left alone. Only as he
left the house did he begin to realize the
depth of the gulf between himself and his
love. Yet even while he cursed the luck
which had prompted him to broach the ill-
fated subject of their marriage to Grace
while in her unusual temper, he was not
without hope of relenting on her part.
Heretofore she had been so kind and chari-
table, even chiding him for his short-
comings in a manner not the least objec-
tionable to him, that he could not believe
she would long hold out in her present
resolution. He was not much of a student
of humankind, and had not yet learned
that the mildest external appearance may
cover a nature deep and determined, especi-
ally when based upon convictions of right.
	John Heplar was of a peculiar disposi-
tion. He had many odd ways for one so
young. Prominent among these was a
habit of talking to himself. What other
people merely thought, he generally spoke
aloud. A rather promising sign, you will
think, indicating an openness of character
incompatible with hypocrisy or deceit. Well
we will see.
	As he walked along the lonely highway,
absorbed in his troubles, he might have
met his dearest friend without any sign of
recognition. He looked straight before
him, at nothing in particular, and thus
soliloquized:
	“Well I’ve done it now, or that’s what
it looks like! I wonder if I ever can get
myself straightened out in her opinion.
May be she’ll get over it if I try to behave
myself in the future.”—Then after a pause,
his feelings change.
	“I wish somebody would tell me why it
is so many good fathers and mothers have
such beastly children. Look at me now,
for instance,” addressing an imaginary
personage beyond the horizon, “my father
and mother walked a thousand miles over
the rough prairies, and toted me along into
the bargain, all for the sake of their
religion. They left good homes and sur-
roundings to come out to this God-forsaken
region, because they thought they ought 
to. To me the religion and the country
has always been detestable. They won’t
even drink tea and coffee because they
think its all wrong, while to me everything
that is forbidden in their code, is just what
most desire.”
	His ruminations were here cut short by
the weight of a hand on his shoulder and a
cheery voice in his ear. He turned quick-
ly with an exclamation of surprise and a
frown on his brow, which changed to the
ghost of a smile when he discovered who it
was.
	“Why, Brother Harper, how you start-
led me.”
	“No wonder, you were in such a brown

[Column 2]
study there was no awakening you except
by extreme measures. But tell me my
boy, what is your trouble? I could not
get by you, so have heard what you have
been telling yourself, for which I ask
pardon.”
	“I will, and may be you can help me to
straighten myself out. I always liked
you; you don’t seem so down on boys like
me as—the bishop—and some others.”
	“I have a little more patience perhaps
having been a sort of ruffian myself in my
young days, but believe me, none has the
interest of the boys more at heart than the
Bishop.”
	“Well may be so, but any how, I feel
more free to talk with you. You see, Brother
Harper, I’m made of wild stuff somehow.
I want to be good and I try as hard as I
can, but once in a while the old nick in me
will come out, and then I get on a tear,
and the dickens is to pay generally.”
	“And you want to know why it is that
the good people in our Church so often
have wild tares in their harvest of children?
Well, I’ll tell you what I think about it,
Brother Heplar. You see these are the
Last Days, that we read so much about in
the Scriptures. Satan is alive to the fact
that his kingdom is tottering to its fall,
that he is on his last legs, in fact, and you
may be assured that he is putting in good
time trying to frustrate the plans of Jesus
Christ, whom he hates. That is his busi-
ness, he is merely paying strict attention
to it, so I don’t know as he is so much to
blame after all. If we would profit by his
example in that, it might be better for us.
Well, you see, he has tried it again and
again on the fathers and mothers. They
have withstood every attack, so he has
turned his attention to the children, who
are not so well grounded in the faith, there-
fore more liable to be deceived. And the
more promising the child, the more deter-
mined are his efforts to conquer him. Oh,
my dear boy, were the veil taken from be-
fore our eyes, we would be appalled by
what is in the unseen world around us.”
	“I believe what you say, Brother Har-
per, for I have felt these influences. But
what can a fellow do? If he has no natur-
al goodness, but is purely mean and con-
temptible, how can he fight against nature
and the influences too?”
	“If he relies upon his own strength, he
will certainly fail. Satan fears no man on
earth, in and of himself. Happily for us
we are in possession of something by which
he can be controlled.”
	“And that is—”
	“ There are three things which he can-
not withstand, God the Father, His Son,
Jesus Christ, and the Holy Priesthood.
This latter we have with us, and if we
honor it, it is our safeguard from the attacks
of evil. Probably I have lectured you
long enough, but I want to give you a
word of advice. If you really want to do
better, and be a good and useful man, go
and ask the Lord to help you. You will
not be disappointed in Him.”
	“That was Grace’s counsel and I have
tried, but Brother Harper, I guess I am
too wicked, for I hav’nt felt any change.”
	“Tut, tut, boy, remember that though
our sins be as scarlet, yet they are made
white in the blood of the Lamb. Try
again my boy, and don’t give up trying; it
will all come right in the end. Goodbye,
and if I can do anything for you at any
time don’t be afraid to ask.” 	And with a

[Column 3]
hearty hand shake the Bishop’s first
Counselor turned in at his own gate.

A PIONEER WOMAN HONORED.

	THE members of the Grantsville Relief
Society gave a surprise picnic party to
Stake President Mary Ann Hunter in their
own new meeting house, Thursday June
17, 1897. There were fifty-seven mem-
bers present. After everything was in
readiness her daughter, Sister Jenus
Anderson, went after her mother. Her
surprise on entering the building is not
easy to describe, as she thought she was
coming to a meeting to talk about fencing
our lot. After the greetings were over
President Rose Hammond called the atten-
tion of the sisters and meeting was opened
by singing, “We thank Thee O God for a
prophet;” prayer by Sister Mary Ann
House; singing “As the dew from heaven
distilling.” President Hammond then de-
livered the speech of welcome.
	President Hunter responded, thanking
the sisters for their kindness, said she was
not aware she had so many friends. Re-
ferred to her travels across the plains with
her child in 1847, while her husband was
in the Mormon Battalion. Said her heart
was too full to express her feelings.
	Sisters Louisa Hale and Mary Ann
House made a few appropriate remarks,
followed by vocal and instrumental music.
The company then sat down to tables
spread with everything eatable that heart
could desire. The evening was spent in
recalling old times, in conversation inter-
spersed with singing and music, all
being well paid for coming together. Bene-
diction by Emily J Anderson.
	MARY ANN HOUSE.

INTERNATIONAL KINDERGARTEN
UNION. 

	THE International Kindergarten Union
met in St. Louis, April 21 22, and 23.
There were about 300 visiting and 300 resi-
dent teachers present making in all an enthu-
isastic gathering. Three sessions a day
were held, this including a reception given
by the St. Louis Kindergartners to visit-
ing teachers.
	The regular session did not take place
until Tuesday, but those fortunate enough
to be in St. Louis on Monday had the
pleasure of hearing the Baroness Bulow
read a paper on Froebel’s principles.—This
was of much value, as the Baroness’ aunt
was often quoted.
	The first day of the session of the I. K.
U. opened Tuesday afternoon and was set
apart for the discussion of the Relation of
the Kindergarten to the Home. Mrs. J.
N. Crouse of the Chicago Kindergarten
College was the first speaker; her talk was
practical and to the point. The necessity
of intelligent co-operation of Kindergarten
and home was urged.
	Miss Putnam of Chicago was to have
had a paper on “Freedom and Law in the
Home,” but was absent, also Miss Fitts of
Brooklyn who was to read a paper on
“Freedom and Law in the Kindergarten.”
	Prof. Louis Soldon, superintendent of
the St. Louis public schools, delivered the
address of welcome Tuesday evening, Miss
Lucy Wheelock President of the I. K. U.
responding. This was followed by a paper
on the ‘‘Relation of the Kindergarten to
Higher Education;” Col. Parker closing
the exercises with one of his enthusiastic
talks on ‘‘The Ideal School.” He said
when a girl or boy is through school we
should not ask how much have you
learned, but how much of a man or a wo-
man are you.
	Wednesday morning was devoted to a
business meeting in which various commit-
tees reported, also delegates were heard
from. There were representatives present
from Japan, Honolulu, New Orleans, New
York, Utah and many other states. It
was decided to hold the next meeting in
Philadelphia the time being changed from
May to February.
	The afternoon was devoted to a Froebel
birthday celebration. A reception had
been arranged by the resident Kindergar-
tners; hand shaking, marches and games,
followed with a paper by the Baroness
Bulow on the life of Froebel, after which
dainty refreshments were served.
	The drawing teachers met with the

[Column 2]
Kindergartners in the evening and were
gratified to hear such speakers as Paterson
Dubois of Philadelphia, editor of the Sun-
day School Times, followed by James L.
Hughes who spoke on Froebel and his
work. Prof. Hughes emphasized the fact
that the child developes as did the race by
slow stages. He said that was often over-
looked by parents and educators and much
injury was done the child; he also dwelt
on the careful training of the instincts of the
child, leading and guiding but not forcing
him in his mental growth. He dwelt
especially on the instinct of activity and
the care with which this should be guided
and led in the right direction.
	Thursday morning was devoted to re-
ports from Committees and the election of
officers, the old officers being re-elected.
Dr. Krohn, of the Child Study Monthly,
gave an address in the afternoon on the
results of child study. Several other
papers were given along this line of work,
Mrs. Hughes of Toronto closing with a
warning to Kindergartners not to take too
much of this work into the Kindergarten
as it kills the spirit.
	St. Louis the mother of the public school
Kindergartens in America has good cause
for her pride in her 68 public school
Kindergartens and her 300 teachers.
	With such a leader and trainer at the
head as Miss Mary McCullough what else
could one expect? Her earnestness and
interest in the work must influence all
who came in contact with her.
	The majority of Kindergartens have two
sessions a day with an enrollment in each
session, ranging in different Kindergartens,
from one hundred to one hundred and fifty.
Each child pays fifty cents a term for ten
weeks for material, and this reduces the
expenses and necessitates only an expen-
diture of room and salary. The salaries
range from twenty-five dollars to seventy
dollars, experienced teachers receiving the
latter. The practical work is good and
shows the result of experienced teachers.
	The only objection to these Kindergar-
tens is that children are not permitted to
enter early enough, the age being limited
to six years, but a movement is on foot to
enter them earlier.
A. C.

OLD FOLKS MEETING.

IN THE TABERNACLE.
	THERE was probably a larger gathering
of the old folks in the Tabernacle this year
than on any previous occasion, and the
picture was a pleasing one. The visitors
occupied the front seats in the centre of the
building, and the delight manifest in their
appearance was in itself compensation to
the Committee. Carnations were distri-
buted by a committee of ladies, and the
fragrance and beauty of the flowers must
have been grateful to the silver haired men
and women. Held’s band was there with
its choice selections, and the “Old Folks
choir” did not neglect to sing “Auld Lang
Syne” and other popular melodies, and
Brother Wm. Dunbar had his bagpipes,
and the Deseret Guitar and Mandolin Club
together with the Tabernacle choir was the
complete musical part of the program.
President George Q. Cannon presided,
Elder George Goddard offered prayer after
singing by the choir; and in remembering
the Old Folks, did not forget Queen Vic-
toria’s Jubilee and the birthday of Bishop
Edward Hunter.
	President Wilford Woodruff was present
in person, and though he was not able, be-
cause of a cold, to speak, had prepared a
written address which was read by Elder
David McKenzie, the speech of welcome
by President George Q. Cannon, was given
in his most gracious and cordial manner wel-
coming the veterans and refering to long
life as a blessing from the Lord to those
who had obeyed His laws and sought to
serve Him..
	We regret lack of space to give his ad-
dress and other interesting facts of the eve-
ning, but we give the address of the vener-
able Pioneer and President in full as it ap-
peared in the Deseret Evening News.
PRESIDENT WOODRUFF’S ADDRESS.
	President Wilford Woodruff, one of the
few who has passed the ninetieth milestone
of life, was on the program for an address,
but being the sufferer from a bad cold, his
address was a prepared one which was read
by Elder David McKenzie. It was as fol-
lows:
	“My Dear Friends—I thank God that I
have lived to see this day. I rejoice to be-
hold the faces of so many of my friends
gathered together in this great Tabernacle.
We who have been upon this earth from
seventy to ninety years and upwards have

[Column 2]
passed through one of the most interesting
generations of the children of men. What
marvelous changes have taken place during
our lifetime! We see things today and
look upon them without wonder that in our
early days would have been considered
miraculous. When I was a small boy we
had just emerged from under the blue laws
of Connecticutt—to greater freedom of
thought and action. The ducking stool
and other forms of punishment akin to it
had been laid aside. Women were no
longer hanged or burned at the stake as
witches. Greater religious liberty was en-
joyed. Women were allowed to kiss their
children, husbands their wives, on the Sab-
bath day without being punished for it. If
a boy took an ear of corn from the field,
although even then he might be punished
for it, he was not in danger of being sold
as a slave, as in former days. The im-
proved condition of things had just been
brought about when I came into the world.
But greater and more wonderful changes
have been accomplished since that time. In
my boyhood every acre of grain was cut
with a sickle in New England. The first
advance made in that direction was when
the hand cradle came into use. The reaper
and the binder were never dreamed of in
those days. If such things had been
brought into the wheatfields then, the peo- 
ple of the land would almost have thought
the millenium had come. And there has
been rapid advancement in almost every
department of life.
	“I need not take up your time in detail-
ing the many changes and improvements
that have been made. We live today amid
the wondrous powers of steam and electric-
ity, by which such marvelous things are
being accomplished. All these things have
been brought about in our day, and I feel
to thank God for what He has done for the
children of men in blessing them as he has
done. Mankind have great cause for
thankfulness to our Father in heaven be-
cause of the many blessings he has bestow-
ed upon them in this day and generation.
	“We who live in Utah have especial cause
to thank and praise His holy name for the
blessings we enjoy. I have spent fifty
years in these valleys of the Rocky moun-
tains and have passed through all the
phases of territorial government until we
have arrived at full statehood, and I feel it
a great privilege to meet with so many of
my friends and citizens of the State of
Utah, and I consider it proper here to
make some reference to our arrival as
pioneers, and the commencement of our
labors in this great American desert. And
I should be recreant in my duty and justly
place myself under strong reproof did I
not give the honor and glory to our great
prophet, seer, revelator and pioneer, Presi-
dent Brigham Young, who under God,
laid the foundation of redeeming this great
American desert from its sterility into the
fruitful and noble State of Utah as we find
it today. President Young was inspired to
come here, and he was inspired to work
after he got here, and when Samuel Bran-
nan, who took the ship Brooklyn from New
York to San Francisco, loaded with Latter-
day Saints, came to this barren country and
met with the pioneers, he looked at the
desolation and barrenness, and tried with
all the power he had to pursuade President
Young not to stop here, but to go on to
California. I heard President Young give
his answer to Samuel Brannan in the fol-

[Column 3]
lowing language, striking his cane into the
soil. “No sir; I am going to stop right
here. I am going to build a city here. I
am going to build a temple here, and I am
going to build a country here.” And by the
help of God he lived to see all this fulfilled.
He dedicated the corner-stone of the Salt
Lake Temple, and dictated the building of
the Temple up to the time of his death.
He directed the building of the St. George
Temple, and he moved a great mountain to
get a place to suit him upon which to erect
the Manti Temple. He pointed out the
place upon which to build the Logan Tem-
ple, and he did a great deal toward its
erection. He built the big Tabernacle in
Salt Lake City, and he dictated to the
architects nearly all that is upon the Tem-
ple block. There is hardly a city or town
in Utah that was not laid out under his
direction.
	“I brought President Young into the val-
ley in my carriage. This was the first
time he had ever seen the place, except in
vision, and as we came around the bend
where we could have a good view of the
valley, he wished me to turn the side of the
carriage so that he could have a better
view, which I did, having a door opening
on the side of the carriage. After viewing
the valley awhile he said: “That will do,
drive on; this is the place. I have seen
this valley before in a vision.” President
Young had been sick for several days on
the Weber river, and several of the Pioneers
had gone ahead and cut the road through a
quaking asp grove, and had formed a camp
and commenced breaking ground, but it
was so very dry that it was difficult to
break it until it was watered. I arrived at
the camp at 11.30. It was a little north-
east of where the Knutsford hotel now
stands. I had in my carriage one bushel of
potatoes, and I covenanted that I would
not eat or drink until the potatoes were
planted in the earth. I fulfilled this coven-
ant, getting some of the brethren to help
me, and soon had my potatoes planted.
	*	*	*	*
	“Although we were on Mexican soil we
planted the American flag soon after our
arrival here. We immediately went to
work and as a protection against Indians
surrounded a ten acre plat on three sides
with a ten foot adobe wall—on the remain-
ing side with log cabins. Immediately we
had to dig ditches and get water to our
crops or nothing would have matured.
With our surveyor’s chain and compass
President Young went to work to lay out
Salt Lake City just as we see it today, with
its broad streets and sidewalks, in the
midst of dry, barren sagebrush, with scarce
a white man’s house within a thousand
miles of us. I assisted in all that work.
Many of you who are before me have also
labored and toiled to build and beautify
this country and make it what it is today.
You know what we have had to pass
through in all this. But God has been
with us. He has crowned our labors with
success. He has blessed the land and
made it fruitful; and we have been able to
surround ourselves with pleasant homes
and many of the comforts of life. There-
fore, I say, we have great cause to be
thankful to our Father for the blessings 
we enjoy.
	“God bless the aged fathers and mothers
of Utah. They have performed a mighty
work in this land. They have worked and
toiled, and sought to do good on the earth,


and they are worthy of respect and honor
in their old age. We ought to do all we
can to make them happy while they live
upon the earth. I hope you who have come
together here, on the old folk’s excursion,
will have a good time while you are here.
I pray God to bless you all, and comfort
your hearts. I do not know how much

THE PIONEER JUBILEE. 

	GREAT preparations are going forward
here and in other adjacent localities for
celebrating the Pioneer Jubilee of a half
century; the Hall of Relics is nearing its
completion and is quite artistic in design,
and presents something of the appearance
in its whiteness of the World’s Fair build-
ings in Chicago, which caused Jackson
Square to be designated as the “White
City.” Were it a permanent building the
city might be proud of it; as it is, one can
only enjoy its beauty for a little season.
	The foundation for the monument in
honor of President Brigham Young is also
in process and the work is being pushed as
rapidly as possible. Other matters that
have been undertaken are not so noticable,
but the people are given to understand that
everything is progressing and it is expected
that there will be the greatest gathering in
this city that has ever taken place here.
	The pioneers themselves, the real veter-
ans should be honored and made to feel
that their labors are appreciated by those
who are reaping in a great measure the
benefits of their toil and endeavors. This
generation born in the valleys of the
mountains know comparatively little of the
hardships and positive suffering of those
who came to this desert and opened up the
land for homes and habitations that they
now enjoy, and made it possible for them
to go on and improve and establish great
industries and great institutions of learn-
ing of which these pioneers laid the earliest
foundations. The greater part of those
valiant men and women have gone behind
the vail, but for those who still remain no
pains should be spared to make the Jubilee
all that it can possibly mean to them.
Something more than mere show and
pageantries, or even brass bands or martial
music or military parade The spirit of
the Lord must permeate the Jubilee pro-
ceedings if it be an acceptable tribute to
those who are expected to be the recipients
of this homage.
	The people who came up here in 1847,
came by inspiration and dedicated this land
to the Lord for a resting place for the weary
saints, and the Lord has wonderfully blest
their efforts, and caused the earth to bring
forth in abundance, and this same spirit
must continue and abide with the people if
prosperity is to crown their labors in the
future as it has in the past. The original

[Column 2]
settlers of these vales were men of God
who relied upon His wisdom to guide them,
and must do so still in order to secure His
blessing and approbation.
	A useless expenditure of time and means
for the sake of show is not in keeping with
the spirit of the pioneers, nor was it to the
Pilgrim fathers and mothers, as history and
tradition abundantly testify. Economy at
the present time is the first consideration,
how to make the wisest and best use of
means and material, and whatever is ex-
pended for the Jubilee should be for that
which will make a lasting impression, or be
a fitting tribute to the memory and ser-
vices of the faithful ones, who labored
more to secure freedom and liberty for
those who should come after them than for
their own individual selves. They were
planning and building for the future, and
deserve commendation from those who have
partaken of the privileges they earned by
the sweat of the brow and wrought out of
the soil.
	Let us all hope that the Jubilee may be
to those veterans all that it promises now
and much more in its spirit of love and
union; that it may teach a valuable les-
son to the younger generation and to the
strangers who may visit Utah on that
occasion.
	The Columbian Exposition taught many
lessons to those who took part in it, and it
is greatly to be desired that the Jubilee
may awaken a spirit of enterprise and of
industry, that will cause capital to be invest-
ed that will give employment to the labor-
er and materially enrich the country.
Utah abounds in raw material of various
kinds, and one of the interests which ought
to have fair representation is the silk in-
dustry. Those who know the great efforts
made by Brigham Young and others
associated with him in securiug mulberry
seed and raising the trees, and finally bring-
ing sericulture to be something of a home
success, realize its importance in furnish-
ing employment, for women and children,
especially, though men not able to do hard
work would find sericulture remunerative,
and much better than being idle,for idleness
is the parent of many vices.
	So much is being said publicly and pri-
vately in these days so near the Jubilee, of
Brigham Young and his wise enterprises, it
is devoutly to be hoped that the silk industry
will be brought prominently forward, for it
was one of the things he emphasized most
strongly, and in which he believed it safe to
invest capital and be sure of quick returns.


A PIONEER WOMAN’S BIRTHDAY.

	Monday, June 14, was the birthday
anniversary of Sister Mary Jane Thompson
a pioneer of 1847, although at that time she
had not entered her teens. Sister Thomp-
son’s parents were veterans in the Church
and she is the cousin of President. Jos. F.
Smith, their mothers were sisters and the
daughters of Joseph Fielding, who with his
family was among the first who embraced
the Gospel in this dispensation.
	Sister Sarah M. Kimball and Sister
Thompson’s son Robert, arranged a sur-
prise for Sister Thompson and invited a
few of her intimate friends and Sister Kim-
ball presented her with an elegant orna-
mented birthday cake with her name on it,
which added to the delicious repast that
had been prepared for the guests, about


twenty-five in number, that they all en-
joyed. Sister Thompson entertained her
friends during the afternoon with reminis-
cences of her father and mother, telling us
of the early days in the valley, and show-
ing us some relics she had kept, old letters
and papers and articles of value, her fath-
er’s epaulettes that are just as he wore
them in Nauvoo, and the little old-fashion-
ed chair in which she sat when a little
girl in Nauvoo and crossing the plains.
She also showed us a pretty little penknife
made shortly after she came to the valley.
	In the evening there was some speaking
in an informal way, President Joseph F.
Smith calling upon those present at Sister
Kimball’s solicitation, and she was the first
one called. She alluded, as did most of
those who spoke, to Kirtland and Nauvoo,
and told us some very interesting things.
Sister Zina D. H. Young and Bathsheba
W. Smith followed in a similar strain and
brought in Missouri as well as Ohio and
Illinois. Sister Margaret P. Young, who
is one of the pioneers of 1847, spoke in the
gift of tongues; interpreted by Sister Zina.
Other sisters who spoke were Sisters Mary
Gray, M. A. Lambert, Diana Reed, E. B.
Wells Ann Y. White, J. L. Smith and
one or two others, also Brother Howard
Corey who is a veteran, and was at one
time clerk for Joseph Smith the prophet in
Nauvoo, Hamilton G. Park and Robert
Taylor, after which Sister Thompson ex-
pressed her gratitude for the good feeling
and wishes manifest in her behalf and al-
luded tenderly to her father and mother,
and to the Prophet Joseph and his brother
Hyrum, and her vivid recollections of their 
affectionate ways with children. President
Smith then offered a few remarks which
was very gratifying to all who had the
pleasure of listening; and he also told us
in an impressive manner some of his re-
membrances of his father and his uncle
Joseph, and a little of his missionary ex-
periences. By request Brother Park dis-
missed with prayer. It was a very pleasant
and enjoyable gathering, there was a
beautiful spirit of peace in the house and
seemingly it prevaded every heart, and
such a gathering of those who love and try
to serve the Lord always leaves its blessing
upon all associated together in the home if
they are susceptible to a divine influence.

SISTER ANN HAMNETT.
	After being afflicted many years our beloved
sister, Ann Hamnett departed this life Feb 18,
1897. She was born April 11, 1824, in Brox-
bottom, England, was baptized the year 1840,
was one of the first to embrace the Gospel in that
land, President John Taylor being the one who
brought her the Gospel; she was baptized by
Orson Pratt, he being the first missionary to visit
that land. She was kept a prisoner, her parents
not being willing for her to associate with the
Latter-day Saints. When an opportunity was
afforded her she would steal away and go and
hear the word of God. For seven years she had
not her liberty to do as the Spirit of God directed
but when she was married she was free to choose
her life and what she would be. She and her
husband set sail for Salt Lake City the 15th of
March, crossed the plains with ox teams and ar-
rived in Salt Lake City on the 6th of October,
being eight months on the toilsome journey.
They resided in Salt Lake City until the Saints
had to leave their homes for Johnson’s army to
pass through the city, then mo ed to Alpine
ward and has made it her home ever since. She
was a faithful worker in the Relief Society, being
a teacher for many years, always willing to labor
in the Gospel  She leaves four children, two
sons and two daughters. May they honor her
memory and follow her example, for she was in-
deed a faithful companion, true to her covenants
and respected by all who knew her. As we
gazed on her beautiful white face, and thought of
her many good deeds, we wondered if all the
good actions were recorded in the tablets of
heaven.

	At 2 p. m. meeting was called and after
the usual devotional exercises the sacra-
ment was administered; during the admin-
istration of the same the choir sang a
sacramental hymn. President Lindsay
spoke a short time giving timely advice
and counsel. Counselor Elizabeth Collins
feebly tottered to the stand and expressed
her thanks to God for sparing her life un-
til this time. Spoke of her travels through
this Stake of Zion and felt that her work
was nearly done. Her only desire was to
be faithful to the end.
	Counselor L. M. Hart spoke of the good
work being done by the Relief Society.
Addressed the young ladies, urged them
to be kind to their parents, especially their
mothers; said “They will not always be
with you, do not wait until after they are
gone before you realize what they have
done for you.”
	Sister Mary Bagley said there was a
great responsibility resting upon the
mothers in Israel, they should be worthy
examples for their children.
	Sister Hodge expressed a desire to do all
she could to further the good work. Presi-
dent Joseph Hart spoke of the liberal


[Column 1]
donation of the Relief Society for the Stake
Academy. Urged a careful education of
the young people in Zion, they should be
examples of purity to the world.
	The statistical and financial report was
then read, after which the minutes of Con-
ference were read and approved. Sister
Amelia Hodge was set apart to assist in
the Relief Society of the Stake and meeting
adjourned by the choir singing an anthem.
Benediction by Bishop Finley.
			ROSE HILLIER, Cor. Sec’y.
SALT LAKE STAKE PRIMARY.
	Minutes of the Primary Conference
of the Salt Lake Stake held June 19, 1897,
in the Tabernacle, Salt Lake City, Presi-
dent Camilla C. Cobb presiding. Singing,
“A happy band of children” prayer by
David Cummings; continued by singing,
“Merry, merry children;” roll called with
twenty-two associations represented; min-
utes of previous conference read and ap-
proved. The following program was
rendered: A quartett, “Song of Welcome”
was sung by the Beesley children from the
22nd Ward. A sketch of the life of Brig-
ham Young was read by Frankie Young
from the 14th Ward. Recitation by Bessie
Fryer, 5th Ward. Lilly Conelly from the
2nd Ward, gave a story from the Book of
Mormon. Birthday song, “The Latter-
day Prophet,” was sung by the children.
Pioneer sketch by Judith Anderson, 13th
Ward. Motion song by a number of child-
ren from Forest Dale. Sentiments from
the sermons of Brigham Young by twelve
children fiom the 19th Ward. “Utah we
love thee” was sung by six girls from the
7th Ward. A motion song was sung by
Irene Tucket from the 11th Ward. Thir-
teen children from the same ward recited
the hymn that President John Taylor sang
to Joseph Smith one hour before his death,
“A poor wayfaring man of grief.” Reci-
tation, “Angels Guide,” by Della Jones of
the 15th Ward. Song by a number of
children from the 21st Ward. Recitation
by Chloe Brown from East Draper. Song
by members from the 1st Ward.
	President Louie B. Felt spoke in a pleas-
ing manner to the children. Said she had
enjoyed listening to the program given in
honor of Brigham Young, was reminded of
the great good he did while he was living;
also of Sister Eliza R. Snow. Related a
story showing the importance of never re-
fusing to do any duty we are called upon
to perform.
	President Cobb said she had listened
with pleasure to the exercises. Felt to
thank the General Board for furnishing the
booklet that had aided us in celebrating
the birthday of President Brigham Young.
Said conference would be adjourned for
three months. Singing Doxology. Bene-
diction by Brother Beesley.
		ELLA W. HYDE, Stake Sec’y.
OAKLEY, IDAHO.
	I will give you a synopsis of a portion of
the celebration of June 1, 1897. Three of
the Primaries met at Oakley Hall, the ex-
ercises were as follows: Opened with sing- 
ing “Hymn for the Latter-day Prophet.”
Prayer by Brother D. P. Curtis, a member
of the Mormon Battalion. Singing by the
Marion Primary association “Let love
abound.” First, Recitation entitled
“Angels Guide,” by Royal Price. Second,
singing “’Neath the old-apple tree,” by
two boys and two girls of the Marion
Association. Third, A short address on the

[Column 2]
life of Brigham Young by President H D.
Haight of this Stake of Zion. Fourth, In-
strumental music by Miss Vilate Bates of
Marion. Fifth, “Twelve reasons why we
honor Brigham Young,” answers by twelve
young ladies, Oakley. Sixth, An essay on
the Biography of Brigham Young by Ruby
Pickett. Seventh, Instrumental music,
Geneva Elison, Oakley. Eighth, Recitation
by Willie Sessions of Marion. Ninth,
Twelve sentiments from the sermons of

of the Mormon Battalion.

EAST MILL CREEK PRIMARY.
	THE Primary of East Mill Creek cele-
brated the birthday of our beloved presi-
dent, Brigham Young, in the following
manner: First was the raising of the
Stars and Stripes, then came the program.
Opening hymn. “Our God we raise to
Thee” by the Primary children. Invoca-
tion by Bishop’s counselor Samuel Oliver.
Continued by the Primary singing “When
many to the Savior’s feet.” The opening
address to the children was given by our
Bishop in a most eloquent and able man-
ner, admonishing the children to read .and
study the life and character of Brigham
Young as one of the most noble and great-
est of men, whose name stands in the front
as a colonizer. “I look back with pleasure
upon different times when I have been in
his company with my father.” And he
then spoke at great length upon the merits
of Brigham Young.
	“Why we honor Brigham Young” was
recited by Leonidas Fisher. Twelve senti-
ments of Brigham Young were given by
twelve little children. A birthday song
for the Latter-day Prophets was sung by
the Primary choir. “Angels Guide” was
recited by Miss Genie Neff; interspersed
with addresses, questions and answers,
Articles of Faith, songs and recitations.
	The program closed by singing “Trust
the children.” Benediction by Brother J
M. Fisher. We then had lunch followed
by dancing for the children, and an evening
party for the adults, making in all a very
 njoyable day and one long to be remem-
bered by both old and young.
			IRENE FISHER, Sec’y.
