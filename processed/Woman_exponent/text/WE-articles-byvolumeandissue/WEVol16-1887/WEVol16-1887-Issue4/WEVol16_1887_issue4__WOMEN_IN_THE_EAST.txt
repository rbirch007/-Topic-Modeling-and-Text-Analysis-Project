WOMEN IN THE EAST.
	“Constantinople,” says Postmaster Leeds, “is
the seat of the Mohammedan religion, but we
must go to the interior to see the customs and
religion of the people in the true light; for the
foreigners in Constantinople have had some, ef-
fect on the manners of the people. Take for
instance the veils worn by the women—for all
women are veiled in that country. In the inte
rior it is of heavy cloth covered with horrid
figures, that look hideous, while in Constantino-
ple it is delightfully thin and tends rather to
improve the looks.	
	The Sultan at the present time has 484
wives. He never marries, but the woman who
bears him the first male child he appoints his
sultana, and upon her confers the ring that
makes her the legitimate heir. When a young
man arrives at the proper age, and his mother
and sisters think that he needs a wife, they
look about to find one for him. At the time
of the ceremony she arrives, closely veiled, and
after the ceremony is performed he lifts the
veil and for the first time sees the face of his
wife. He has eight days in which to decide
if he is satisfied with her, and if not he returns
her to her parents. A divorce is a mere snap
of the finger.—Palladium, New Haven.
