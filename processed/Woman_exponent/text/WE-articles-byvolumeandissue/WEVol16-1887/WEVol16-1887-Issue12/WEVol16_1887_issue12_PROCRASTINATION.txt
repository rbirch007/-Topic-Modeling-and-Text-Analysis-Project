PROCRASTINATION.

“Procrastination is the very thief of time,” is an
old adage and a very true one. “Never put
 off till to-morrow what ought to be done to-
day.” How often we hear that maxim quoted, 
but we do not always practice it.

[Column 2]
	﻿Every day has its duties and cares and we
should not neglect the duties of to-day but
perform each in its time and season, for we
know not what to-morrow will bring. Life is
uncertain. We do not know how long
we will be permited to remain
here. To-morrow for us may never dawn
in mortality. It would make us very unhappy
to be called from our labors to another sphere,
knowing that our work was unfinished, that
we had neglected our duties, and failed to im-
prove opportunities for improvement and for
doing good, that had presented themselves to
us.
	Secretaries should be prompt in their duties
and their books posted and make their reports
in good season, for it takes no more time to
copy a set of minutes, or make a report to-day,
than it will to-morrow and it avoids anxiety and
saves hurry and confusion. We should learn
while young, that there is a time and place for
everything, and learn to have everything in its
place and perform every duty in
its time and season; and not let them accumu-
late on our hands like a stack of unread news-
papers. By reading one paper every day it
is quite easy to keep even but let them accumu-
late for a week and it will take quite an effort,
and considerable time to read them all and get
even again. Let us try to be more prompt, and
persevere a little more and if obstacles meet us
let us find a way to surmount them, and let not
the sly old thief, called procrastination, rob us
of our laurels and let us always remember that
none can wear laurels in our Father's work, ex-
cept those who earn them.
