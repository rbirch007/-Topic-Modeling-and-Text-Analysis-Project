NOTES AND NEWS.

Mrs. Henry Ward Beecher has for many
years edited a department in the Domestic
Monthly.


The Queen of Roumania has just completed
a novel, the scene of which is laid among the
ancient Dacians.
	The Turks are coming forward in History.
Lieut. Col. Talat Bey has written the “Battles
of Plevna.”
	There is a watch in a Swiss museum only
three sixteenths of an inch in diameter, insert-
ed in the top of a pencil-case. Its little dial in-
dicates not only hours, minutes, and seconds,but
also the days of the month.
	It is said that once in happy Finland the
average publication of books was one yearly.
In 1855 the average had increased to ten. But
the world is growing worse and the yearly
yield numbers 1,290 volumes. 
	Mr. Walter Besant, with a collaborateur, has
been rewriting Scribe’s Gringoire, under the
title of “The Ballad Monger” with Mr. Beer-
bohm Tree in the title role. This is not Mr.
Besant’s first essay in dramatic writing. He
with James Rice, early in their double
career; produced “Such a Good Man”—a com-
edy.
The Donnelly Shakespeare incident has
induced Messrs. Funk & Wagnalls to republish
a fac-simile edition of the 1623 folio edition
of Shakespeare, on which the Donnelly cipher
is based. The reprint is made from a photo-
graphic reproduction of the original reduced to
crown octavo size, and is now in press.
	Mrs. Craik was prompted to write her last
book, “An Unknown Country,” which discusses
the condition of the poor in the North of Ire-
land, by overhearing the remark of a laboring
man, who, when rallied upon helping a little
girl across the street, replied, “Ay, but a
’andful of ’elp is worth a cartload of pity.”
