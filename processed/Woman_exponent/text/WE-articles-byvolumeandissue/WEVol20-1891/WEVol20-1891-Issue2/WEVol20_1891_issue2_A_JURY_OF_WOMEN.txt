A JURY OF WOMEN.
	Mrs. Ingersoll, proprietess of the Valley
House, the principal hotel in the city of
Douglas, Wyoming, refused to pay the wages
of two of her girls who quit work without giv-
ing previous notice of their intention of doing
so. They appealed to the law and the case
was brought before Judge Robinson.
	Mrs. Ingersoll demanded a jury. The usual
form of summoning six male citizens was
being proceeded with, when Minnie Jones, one
of the plaintiffs, appealed to the Court. She
said:
	“Mr. Judge, this is a woman suffrage county
and as we are all women in this case, can’t we
have a a woman jury?”
	Judge Robinson consulted the attorneys for
both sides, and as both were willing he direct-
ed the bailiffs to summon women to serve on
the jury.
	This was done, and by the time the jury
reached the court room the unusual proceed-
ings were noised about and the room was
crowded with spectators.
	The testimony of the plaintiffs and defend-
ant was given and the case was argued at
length by the attorneys for both sides.
	The jury listened attentively, and several of
the ladies asked questions as the case was
heard. They retired to the jury room when
the case was concluded and in a business-like
way gave a verdict for the servant girls. The
result was applauded by the spectators and
the jury women were warmly congratulated as
they stepped up to the clerk’s desk to receive
their warrants for the jury duty.—Ex.
