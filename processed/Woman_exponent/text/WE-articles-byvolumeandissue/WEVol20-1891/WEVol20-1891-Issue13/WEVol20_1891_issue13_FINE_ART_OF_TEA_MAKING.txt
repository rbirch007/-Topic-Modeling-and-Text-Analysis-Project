FINE ART OF TEA MAKING.

	Tea making in Japan is a fine art. The
teapot is small and dainty, like those sold for
bric-a-brac at Japanese shops, and the teacups,
often of fine cloissonnes with plain enameled
linings, are each no larger than a giant’s
thimble. With them is a pear-shaped pitcher
for boiling water, and a lacquer containing
choice tea. Among the the rich these appur-
tenances accompany a brand of tea so rare
that none of it is ever exported. The Japanese
host scoops out enough of the precious herb
(with an ivory implement shaped like a large
tea leaf) to loosely fill the little teapot. He
then pours over it hot, not boiling, water, and
in less than a moment the tea maker begins to
pour off a steam of pale yellow tea into cups
which are never filled more than half way up,
and they are at once served to visitors and
the family. It is needless to say that the tea
losing no part of its delicious aroma, is as
fragrant and delicate as any concoction can
possibly be.—Boston Transcript.
