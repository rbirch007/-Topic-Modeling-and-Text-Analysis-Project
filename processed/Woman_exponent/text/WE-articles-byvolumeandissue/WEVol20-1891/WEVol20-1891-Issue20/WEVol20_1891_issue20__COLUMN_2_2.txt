"If you don’t publish mine in May,
	it will be too provoking!”
Half angrily welled up the tears,  
"Ah, do not doubt, dispel your fears,
There lies before you scores of years,
	Take courage little maiden!’’
Years rolled around, one whole decade
Merging the dimpled little maid
into a charming matron staid,
	With children round her growing.
Famous histories unbidden,
Far from mortal sight lay hidden,
'Neath the waves of dark oblivion,
	Sharing fate with this spring poem.
Time’s busy wheel its changes wrought,
Kingdoms arose and fell to naught,
Fashions and customs, turning thought
	Into new channels ever.
This morn, my great grandson exclaimed
"Why that was grandma’s maiden name! —
If is indeed, the very same.
	I do believe she wrote it!"
My pulses gave a sudden beat,
I started from my cushioned seat
And stood upright upon my feet,
	Back in life’s early gloaming.
By chance, as subsequently told,
Searching many volumes musty, old,
A parcel dropped, yellow as gold,
	Upon the floor before him.
Thinking it writing of a sage.
He turned to scan the title page,
And soon in reading was engaged,—
	That long neglected poem.
Then by the Prophet’s beard he swore, 
More fierce than he had done before,
“This shall be over-looked no more,
	I’ve found it just in season.”
Just a turn in the wheel of fate.
O’er the world it flies in state.
Charming and fresh, strange to relate,
As nature's springs returning.
Thro’ agencies unknown before,
’Twas sent throughout the wide world o'er
Echoed in song from shore to shore,
	This treasure of a poem.
Eighteen hundred and twenty-nine,
Date quite correct 'twas surely mine.
What bliss in my old age to find,
	Published; my first spring poem.
No language ever can convey
The joy I feel at this late day,
To read when I am old and gray,
	My very first spring poem.
Grieve not, young aspirants for fame,
Work, wait, not seeking praise or blame,
Leave all, even your much—loved name,
	To time that will restore it.
	LUE.
