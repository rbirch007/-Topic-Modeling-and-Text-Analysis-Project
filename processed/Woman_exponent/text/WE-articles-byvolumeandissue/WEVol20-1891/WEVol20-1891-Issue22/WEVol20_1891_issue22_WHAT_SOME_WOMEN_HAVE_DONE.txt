WHAT SOME WOMEN HAVE DONE

	[Read at Miss Anthony's Birthday Party in
this City.]
	That a new and in some respects a distinct
era has dawned for woman, the world at large
is beginning to admit,—but that there is any
precedent for such a state of things few will ac-
knowledge; yet are we forced to the conviction
when we look backward;—that such conditions
were not general in the strict sense of the term
is well understood,—but the exception proves
the rule—and as this is the age when all things
are to be restored, woman too will be accorded
her proportion in the great drama of the
world.
	The subject I have undertaken, that of wo-
man's work, or what some women have done,
is so vast that I can only present a few facts.
I will not take you back to Eve,or to Deborah,
nor to any of the scripture women, you know
their record; doubtless you are all familiar
too with the history of Zenobia, Queen of
Palmyra, which tells us not only that she was
beautiful and pure, but was also remarkable
for her great courage, high spirit, and literary
acquirements, and spoke Latin, Greek and the
Oriental languages with fluency, and that in
the administration of government she com-
bined prudence, justice and liberality, so that
all the eastern provinces submitted to her
sway.
	It is a matter of history that 320 years B. C.
Martia, Queen of London, first promulgated
the principles of the English Common Law in
her judgments and enactments. Her Martian
Statutes or Code outlived the Roman, Anglo-
Saxon and Norse invasions, and we have it on
good authority that Alfred the Great revived
her Briton laws and enforced them among
Anglo-Saxons and Danes.
