	When Maitland learned that Lady Anna had
not been forbidden by her father's will to
marry him, he felt encouraged; he had not
much fear of his father's interference, as he had
no idea of the cause of estrangement between
the families. Lady Walton, Miss Conway,
and now Dr. Bland were the only ones who
knew the history. Miss Conway realized deep-
ly the peculiar embarrassment Lady Walton
would feel when it was positively known that
Clairville was the son of Sir Edward, but she
had supposed her capable of greater sacrifices
of feeling than she seemed prepared to under-
take. Miss Conway wondered in her own

	Time passed, and young Clair grew every
day more familiar with the scenes among which
his future life was to be spent; and every one
connected with the household seemed attracted
to him, though evidently wishing to keep him
at a distance. He took them by storm irresist-
ably and they looked up to him, as they
had never done to his father, notwithstanding
their belief that gipsy blood flowed in his veins.
Lady Anna depended upon him for amusement,
as well as for an escort, and ere long confided
to him her heart's secrets. His tenderness for
her was like that one feels for a child, and he
determined she should not be thwarted in her
desires.
	"Do you not know Lady Walton?" he in-
quired one day when they were talking con-
fidentially upon the matter. "No, I have only
seen her once when she called on us after the
funeral, and she is so stately and grand, in
appearance, and I know so little of society, I
dare not venture to talk to her of Maitland;
he must do that himself," replied Anna. "But,"
interrupted her brother, "he does not seem to
have much success, and meantime the suspense
is preying upon your mind; let me see this
superb aristocratic fine lady. I will try to
bring about an understanding that will be per-
manent." And so it was agreed after some con-
sultation that if cousin Jane would consent,
Clair should seek an interview with Lady
Walton. Lady Anna was not so sanguine
as Clair himself appeared to be, both of them
knew so little of such matters, and it was
dangerous ground to venture on, yet unsophis-
ticated in the realities of these things they did
not hesitate as wiser and more cautious people
would have done. Before an hour had passed
Jane had been informed of the decision, and
contrary to her usual caution, had said to Clair,
"Go and try to obtain an interview, make some
excuse in regard to the estate, and you may
see her alone. I wish you success though in
such a hazardous enterprise." The fact was
Miss Conway felt it would be a relief to as-
certain more of Lady Walton's real feelings on
the subject and was willing to risk Clair's
visit thinking it could be no worse than waiting
to know all. Dr. Bland had spoken out
plainly and told her what her duty was, and
now perhaps Clair would learn whether she
had followed the Doctor's advice. The same
afternoon, mounted on one of the finest horses
about the place, and looking like a true court-
ier, Clair rode away watched by the admiring
eyes of the two young ladies, who felt he must
win the heart of Lady Walton, inexorable as
she might be, and so it proved.
	When Clair arrived at the entrance of the
grounds leading to Walton Hall the lodgekeeper
surveyed him from head to foot curiously,horse
and all, and then as he dismounted inquired,
"what can I render ye'se? in blunt English.
Clair addressed him in the most suave man-
ner with, "I have a message to Lady Walton
and must see her." "I can see ye'ze a foreign-
er; go up to the lane that winds between the
great elms and ye'll see the porter just there,
your face'll be yer passport." Thus directed
Clair wended his way along the pathway to-
wards the mansion, his only misgiving being
that he had no idea of the proprieties or maniere
usual on such occasions. A surly porter in
the doorway accosted him with, "Your name,
young man, and who do you wish to see, his
Lordship or the young master?" "Neither sir,
my business is with Lady Walton and it is
most important." "Be seated then, and I will
take your message to her valet." Very soon a
Frenchman with a pompous air appeared and
Clair, who spoke the language perfectly,
told his errand in the tongue most familiar to
him, which impressed him much more favor-
ably than if he had spoken in English. The


valet disappeared and in a moment returned
and ushered him into the library. Lady Wal-
ton with all the courage she could command
and querying who the stranger could be, intro-
duced herself by saying, “I understand you
have an important message for me, but having
observed the likeness to Sir Edward, turned
pale as death and added, “your name, young
man, you have not given it?” “I am Monsieur
Clairville Hilliard;” the last name came re-
luctantly. “I thought so,” replied the lady;
“and pray what can be your business with me?”
She shook with fear lest Sir Edward had sent
a message from his deathbed, and yet she had
heard that the Baronet had never acknowl-
edged him. It was not so easy to explain his
errand as Clair had fancied, he had never be-
fore seen such a magnificent, queenly woman,
and his manner was so timid and hesitating as
to cause anxiety on the lady’s part. She
helped him by saying, “What news do you
bring from the young ladies at the castle? Are
they well, and enjoying the fine Spring
weather?” Thus encouraged Clair found voice
to say, “Lady Anna is not well and I have paid
this visit solely on her account, and I beg you
to listen to me, and to promise me you will see
her and have some proper understanding of an
affair that seems now to become more and more
distressingly complicated.”
	“Your ardor in her behalf speaks well for
your affection as a brother, but might possibly
be misinterpreted.” “How could that be. It is
because of my sympathy for her melancholy
condition that I have come to you;surely there
can be no wrong motive attached to that?”
Clair was not skilled in the world’s methods
and it never occurred to his ingenuous soul
that people might think him anxious to get

	‘‘Are you aware that Dr. Bland came here

	“My sister never sent any one to plead her
