	When the Barons wrested Magna Charta
from King John, one of the rights for which
they contended and forced him to grant was
the right of women to a vote in the House of
Lords. He was compelled to summon to that
House all earls, barons, and others who held
lands directly from the King, and he sum-
moned to the very first Parliament the Count-
esses of Pembroke and Essex. In the reign
of Edward I. ten ladies were summoned, en-
titled to seats. There is proof that during the
first three reigns after the existence of Magna
Charta women had a right to a voice in the
English government and exercised it.
	John Stuart Mill said "the list of women
who have been eminent rulers of mankind
swells to a great length when to queens and
empresses are added women regents and wo-
men viceroys of provinces." "It is a curious
consideration," he continues, "that the only
things which the existing laws exclude women
from doing, are the things which they have
proved they are able to do. That women are
not fit for power and cannot take part in civil
government seems to be the cunningly devised
law of men, and has created public sentiment
as the bulwark of the law; and yet it is a fact
that we have as examples, Queen Elizabeth,
Joan of Arc, Isabella of Castile, Maria Theresa,
Catharine of Russia, Margaret of Austria and
in our day the good Queen and Empress
Victoria. To tell in brief what good any one
of these great women has accomplished would
be to fill a volume."
	Women have been distinguished for learn-
ing too in the far distant past, though it must
have gone out of fashion in part, as we are all
aware.
	We know of eminent women in many
countries here and there in the days preceding
the era of our civilization who came into pos-
session of privileges and power; some of their
histories are delightful. For instance Aspasia,
who taught Socrates, is said to have been his
preceptress, the wife of the great Pericles of
Atheus, and the friend of the Greek philoso-
phers; when summoned for trial before the
Greek Areopagus she was charged with
"walking the streets unveiled, sitting at table
with men, disbelieving in the Greek gods, be-
lieving in one sole Creator, and with entertain-
ing original ideas concerning the motions of
the sun and moon." Aspasia was far in ad-
vance of her time, and her contemporaries
could not understand her.
	Hypatia (born between 370 and 380—died
415,) whose pathetic story we read, was another
exceptionally learned woman. Her father was
at the head of the Platonic school at Alex-
andria at the close of the fourth century, the
commentator on Ptolemy and the editor of
Euclid, adding to it here and there a demon-
stration of his own. He imparted to his daughter
all that he knew, and history does not tell us
that she was not capable of acquiring knowl-
edge, or that her brain was inferior to her
father's, but on the contrary Socrates, who was
her enemy, tells us that when she suceeeded
her father in the Platonic school and "ex-
pounded the precepts of philosophy" (in both
Alexandria and Athens) studious persons from
all parts of the country flocked to hear her,
and that she addressed both them and the
magistrates with singular modesty.

	And yet there have ever been some noble-
minded men who were willing to admit wo-
man's equality. In the 16th century there were
books written upon these subjects by men
giving to women in some instances superiority
and also other vigorous writers of the day who
denied to women reason, and considered they
only excelled in iniquity.
	Aristotle and others of his school called
woman "a monster," an "accidental produc-
tion." The Hebrews pronounced her "an
afterthought of the Deity, and the mother of
all evil."
	When the councils of the Medieval church
came together to decide on the instruction
needful to the young, they hastened to count
women out and to declare them unfit for in-
struction, and kind-hearted nuns of the
Catholic church, who in defiance of this de-
cision established schools for girls, were pub-
licly stoned when they were met on the streets.
The early Christian fathers denounced women
as "noxious animals," "painted temptresses,"
"necessary evils," "desirable calamities" and
"domestic perils."
	From the English Heptarchy to the Refor-
mation the law proclaimed the wife to be in
all cases and under all circumstances, "her
husband's creature, servant and slave." Her-
bert Spencer, writing of English laws in his
Sociology says, "Our laws are based on the
all-sufficiency of man's rights, so that society
exists today for woman only as she is in the
keeping of some man." Daniel Defoe in 1696
declared his belief that if men were trained in
the same deplorable ignorance as women they
would be vastly more incompetent and de-
graded.

ment of their true interests." It so wrought

offered ten thousand pounds for the establish-
