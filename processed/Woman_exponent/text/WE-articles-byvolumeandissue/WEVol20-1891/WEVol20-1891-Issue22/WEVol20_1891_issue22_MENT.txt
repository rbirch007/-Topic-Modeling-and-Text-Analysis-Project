	In numberless ways Clair helped the young
people; almost unconsciously he had the most
delicate perception of what was proper and
also of what would be agreeable, and his
plans nearly always succeeded admirably.
	Fishing and boating parties were his chief
delight for pastime, and the doctor was pas-
sionately fond of fishing. On the Hilliard
estate were fine ponds and brooks for these out-
of-door sports, and the young folks were not
slow in availing themselves of these opportun-
ities for out-door exercise. Such excursions
are always favorable for lovers and, now that
Clair knew Maitland Walton, he was generally
one of the party on all such occasions.
	At last Lady Walton consented to be intro-
duced to Clair; she had refused repeatedly, and
it was hard for these young folks to comprehend
why. Jane Conway knew well enough that it
was on account of an old-time fondness for Sir
Edward, but whoever, unless in the secret, could
be made to believe that a lady so refined, deli-
cate and sensitive as Lady Walton could ever
have been in love with such a man, or cherish-
ed through all these years a memory of the past,
that could not be stifled sufficiently to allow
friendly intercourse with his relatives. "To
think"—Lady Walton had said on one occasion
to an intimate friend, "to think that while we all
thought Sir Edward a single man, he had a
wife and child abroad. I can scarcely believe
it is not a hideous nightmare from which I
shall soon awaken to find it a dream."
