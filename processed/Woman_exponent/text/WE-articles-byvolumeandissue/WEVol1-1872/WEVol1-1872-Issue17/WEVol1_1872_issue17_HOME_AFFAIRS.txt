HOME	AFFAIRS.
 				      — 
	There is some excitement in the East con-
cerning Utah, but the people here seem un-
affected by the contagion.	
	A Correspondent in Alta, Little Cotton-
wood, informs us that for several weeks her
bedroom has been perfectly dark, from the win-
dows being under an enormous snow drift.
	Attention is again respectfully called to
the terms of clubs on the last page; with a hope
that the friends of the Exponent will continue
to use their endeavors in behalf of the paper.
	Mrs. Willmarth East, as local Agent
for the EXPONENT, is on hand to receive sub-
scriptions for the same, wherever it may be
more convenient for subscribers to see her
than the Editor or general Agent.
	The latest news from President Smith and
the Palestine party, among whom is Sister
Snow, reports them in Italy enjoying good


health. President Albert Carrington, of the
European Mission, was about to join them.
	Encouragement is offered to the Exponent
in many instances from friends whose judge-
ment and candor, as well as their open hearted-
ness we esteem very highly, and we are happy
in acknowledging our obligations and in tender-
ing our thanks for the same.
	A large number of patrons of the Expo-
nent, who subscribed for six months, have not
renewed their subscriptions, which expired
some time ago. Will they please renew im-
mediately, or we shall be compelled to stop
forwarding their papers?
	The number of restless and bad men, that
are now on the streets of Salt Lake city at
night, render it necessary that ladies should be
careful how they travel the streets after dark,
unless accompanied by a male protector. Time
was it was not so, but changes have occurred.
	That terrible disease, smallpox, has lately
visited a few of the wards of this city and some
of the settlements of the Territory, especially
in Morgan County. In regard to this as to
other diseases which spread infection easily, a
few facts should be more widely known than
they are. Woolen clothes will carry contagion
more readily than cotton, and cotton even
more readily than linen. The harder the
thread of which any fabric is woven and the
closer the texture, it is the, less likely to carry
infection. Cleanliness should characterize
every dwelling at all times, but double care
should be taken where there is danger from
contagious diseases. In such cases disinfect-
ants, such as chloride of lime, should be kept
in the house to be used as occasion might require;
while a little vinegar sprinkled around in the
room where a patient is suffering, will be found
valuable. To these precautions should be added
the avoidance of undue fear or excitement, and
the use of every precautionary measure to
prevent the spread of infection.
