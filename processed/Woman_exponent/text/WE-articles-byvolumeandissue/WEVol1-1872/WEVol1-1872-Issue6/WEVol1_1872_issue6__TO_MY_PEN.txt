TO MY PEN.
				—
And shall I take thee up again, old friend,
And in the cause of truth my weak voice blend; 
Remove from off thy point the gathering rust
And to the weary whisper hope and trust ?
I know the value of a word of cheer,
For strongest hearts grow faint; but God is near,
Guiding our frail barks through the darkest storm,
And while we trust to him we’ro safe from harm.

Methinks I hear thee say again to-day—
“Better to work than rust; this life away;
Better to plant, although it seem in vain;
The seed may reach good soil and grow again.”

The field is broad and long, the workers few
Whose hands are willing and whose hearts are
	true;
And none but such the harvest Master calls
His grain to garner ere the tempest falls—

His storm of wrath, upon the sin-stained world:
Where wrong has gained the power and truth been
	hurled
From her high throne, and crushed and trampled
	down—
Come, all ye brave and help regain her crown.

Long has the cry been sounding in my ear,
Long have I seen the pain, the bitter tear,
Wrung from the suff'ring ones on life’s hard road,
Who onward struggle ’neath sin’s heavy load.

We're weak, my pen, but then Truth's Leader’s
	strong.
And unto her must yield all powers of wrong;
If her defenders be but true and brave,
Truth will not always be a fettered slave.
										H.
