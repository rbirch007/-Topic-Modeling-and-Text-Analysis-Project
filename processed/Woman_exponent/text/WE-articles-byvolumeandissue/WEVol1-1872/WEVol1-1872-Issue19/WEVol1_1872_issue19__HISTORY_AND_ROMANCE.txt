HISTORY AND ROMANCE. 

	A young mother wishes to be informed what
difference it can make whether she reads novels
or history to her children, as either will tend
to inspire thought which is the foundation of
mental greatness? She asks how the acts of
individuals in former generations can make
any difference with the people of to-day? Of
what intrinsic importance it can be to her or
her children whether Henry VIII, of England,
divorced Catharine of Arragon and had Ann
Boleyn beheaded, or otherwise? Whether Na-
poleon was longer remembered and more deeply
mourned by Josephine, or Maria Louise?
Whether Mary Queen of Scots was dethroned 
and imprisoned or not? Whether Hannibal
crossed the Alps, or did not leave Africa? Or
if the city of Thebes had one gate or one hun-
dred?


The facts contained in history should not
possess, of themselves, so great interest for
present or future generations, as the motives
which prompted individuals or kingdoms to
work out such facts. Every deed that is, or
has ever been, accomplished, must, in accord-
ance with the laws of nature, be, or have been,
actuated by some design. The histories of
wise and good men and women, tell us of bright
designs wrought out in noble deeds, worthy of
our imitation; those of ambiguous and repre-
hensible characters show us the tedious and
fraudulent way of walking in darkness with no
pure and exalted ambition to instigate to high
and noble purposes.
	History is solid food, in the shape of facts,
from which intellectual and philosophical sus-
tenance may be drawn; romance is a stimulant,
which excites the mental powers only to leave
them weakened, and stirs up passion to seek
gratification, even by unwise if not by un-
righteous means.
