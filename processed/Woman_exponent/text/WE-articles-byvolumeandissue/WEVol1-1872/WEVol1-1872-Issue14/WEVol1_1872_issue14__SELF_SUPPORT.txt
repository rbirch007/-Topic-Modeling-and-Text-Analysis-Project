SELF SUPPORT.
					—
	THE SUBJECT before us is an important one—
if clearly considered, the one most important
of all subjects, to every reasoning being. One
can do for one’s self that which cannot be done
by another, either for good or bad; and also
that which can not be done for another, be that
other never so near and dear. Self reliance and
self labor are the only true methods by which
one can become intelligent or rich in any sense.
Men and women who have distinguished them-
selves as persons of honor and usefulness, have
carved their own roads up the rugged hill of
science and philosophy. Those who are lifted
from one notch to another and pushed along
continually by the strength and influence of
friends, unless they have used all the power and
force they are themselves possessed of, have no
just claims to wealth and reputation which the
exertions of others may sometimes enable them
to reach.   It is not safe to trust to such posses-
sions, for they will slip away, when the friends
who founded them, weary of proping them up,
or are themselves taken hence.
	No amount of gifts can aford the satisfaction
that well-earned goods will bring. It is better
to obtain less of wealth, or honor, and feel the
gratifying assurance, safety, and that self con-
fidence which inspires to renewed efforts, than
to live in dread of being cast upon one’s own
resources with no knowledge of what may
follow, no self supporting aim to pursue and no
courage to invent means of rising step by step
to higher and more worthy positions. The best
and only safe course to be taken by persons in


such conditions, is to go to work earnestly and
in as short a period as possible, in reason and
honor, work themselves into more reliable and
snbstantial conditions, where self support will
be at once necessary and easy, and suitable.
