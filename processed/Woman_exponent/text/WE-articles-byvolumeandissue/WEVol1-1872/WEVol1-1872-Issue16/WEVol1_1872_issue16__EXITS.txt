EXITS.

	The record of the recent past bears off its page
the names of an unusually large number of dis-
tinguished characters, who have retired behind
the scenes on the grand stage of mortal exist-
ence. The spectacle is one in which almost a
generation of prominent actors seem to have
been represented as passing away, and their
histories alone remain, to remind us that “we
may make our lives sublime.” Eighteen hun- 
dred and seventy-three begins its round of noble 
exits with that of the ex-emperor Louis
Napoleon, who died in exile at Chiselhurst, 
England, on the morning of Thursday, 9th inst.


The career of this man has been so interwoven
with the history of Europe for the last twenty
years, that those who have a knowledge of the
one cannot be ignorant of the other; for he
shaped for years the policy that governed that
continent. His life was an eventful one, full of
light and shade; and in elevation as in exile he
found his truest friend in a woman, she who
shared his imperial splendor and knelt by his
dying bed at Chiselhurst, the empress Eugiene.
But death, when it comes, strikes alike the
highest and the lowliest; and in the tomb,
whether it be a mighty mausoleum or an
humble grave, emperor and peasant are but
equal remnants of mortality.
