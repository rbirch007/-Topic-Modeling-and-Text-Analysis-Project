TASTE.
			   —	
    ﻿That fine, sensitive faculty which enables
us to perceive and enjoy the beautiful, ex-
cellent, or elegant, in nature and art, cannot
be too carefully cultivated. A person who
is entirely unacquainted with Nature, can-
not be a person of good taste, not knowing
whether the production of art resembles
Nature or not; and unless this knowledge is
possessed to some extent, no real satisfac-
tion can be received from the imitative arts.
A lively imagination, clear and distinct ap-
preciation of things, good sense, and the
Iove of virtue, delicacy and correctness are
some of the features to be gained, before
beauty, sublimity, harmony, sweetness and
all those characteristics which speak such
volumes of rapturous delight to the educated
eye and ear, can be received or appreciated.
It is said, and is undoubtedly correct in a
gereral sense, that the toilet of a lady be-
tokens her taste; that to dress simply and
without ostentation is a mark of modesty;
to wear many bright, glaring colors, a flaunt-
ing, red, yellow, or sky-blue hat covered
with ribbons, or as much jewelry as can be
loaded on, gives evidence of an uncultivated
mind and taste. A waist too small for the
natural proportions of the body is styled the
worst possible deformity, and must be the
workings of a narrow, unexpanding mind
and bad taste, if produced by sinning against
the laws of nature by tight lacing.
