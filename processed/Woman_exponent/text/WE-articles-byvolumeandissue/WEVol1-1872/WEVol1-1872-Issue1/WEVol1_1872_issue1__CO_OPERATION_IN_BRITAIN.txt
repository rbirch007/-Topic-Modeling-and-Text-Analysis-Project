CO-OPERATION IN BRITAIN.
			—
  Here is what is being done in co-operation
in England, as appears from the reports of
the late co-operative congress held in Bol-
ton, Lancashire, and presided over by
Thomas Hughes, M. P.  The figures are en-
couraging to advocates of the principle eve-
rywhere. From the minutes of the assem-
bly it appears that 400,000 workmen in the
United Kingdom own 1,500 stores, whose
supplies are bought by their own agents in


the islands and on the continent, and whose
immense profits are suflicient to settle the
question of the feasibility of co-operation.
One association in the north of England sup-
plies 399 societies with goods; has 235 soci-
eties associated with it, and divided a profit
last year of $40,000, its sales amounting to
$3,793,820. One society in Scotland has 97
retail stores and an annual trade of $1,000,-
000.	The capital invested in these societies
is upward of $10,000,000, nine-tenths of it
being in small shares. They do $50,000,000
of trade and make their own profit on them-
selves.
