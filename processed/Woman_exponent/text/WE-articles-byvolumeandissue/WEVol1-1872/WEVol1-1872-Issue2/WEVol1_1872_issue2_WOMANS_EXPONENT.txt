WOMANS- EXPONENT.


﻿WHEN'Mrs. Isabella Beecher Hooker was
pleading before the Willimantic Selectmen
for the admission  of women as voters, she
stated that she believed one of the gentle-
men before her was in sympathy with the
movement (referring to Mr. Lincoln, the
town clerk). Mr. Lincoln asked her for the
reasons for believing so, and she said she
could see it in the twinkle of his eye.  "I
beg your pardon,” said Mr. Lincoln, “but
that eye is a glass eye.”
  MISS CUSHMAN gave an evening’s read-
ing in Chicago, some time ago, for which
she was paid $1,500—very generous remu-
neration for an hour’s labor.  A correspond-
ent of the  "Christian Union” says that “in
spite of the mouthings and mannerisms that
are not good because they are not nat-
ural, it was better than any sermon on do-
mestic bickering to hear her read ‘ Betsey
and I are Out.’ ” He then proceeds to ask
how the church can hope to make a good
fight against the theatre when it has failed
to find a fitting place for this kind of talent,
which is so powerful when used in the
cause of truth and purity.
