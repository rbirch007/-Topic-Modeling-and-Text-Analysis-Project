Lesson I.—Woman.

	‘Oh, woman, woman, know thyself,
	And know thy mission too.
	Thou comest from thy makers hands,
	A mighty work to do.”
						—Eliza R. Snow.

				Woman.
	A. What is she?
		1. Child of God.
		2. Companion of her husband.
	B. Her mission.
		1. Child. Duties of child to parents.
		2. Sister. Duties of a sister to house-
		hold and to mankind.
		3. Wife. “Nevertheless, neither is
		the man without the woman,
		neither the woman without the
		man in the Lord” (I Cor. 11: 11).
		4. Mother. Duty to become a
		mother. “Be fruitful, and multi-
		ply and replenish the earth”
		(Gen. 1: 28).
	C. Her Reward.
		1. Earthly possessions. Husband,
		children. Consciousness of hav-
		ing obeyed commandments of
		God.
		2. Heavenly possessions. Celestial
		inheritance.

Lesson III.—The Baby.
	“I love these little people, and it is not a
a slight thing when they, who are so fresh
from God love us."			Dickens.
	The Baby.
	A. Bath.
		I. Time to bathe. Regularity. Why
		not in health only but in sickness,
		also. Daily bath.
		2. How to bathe. First bath of oil.
		Following every bath with a
		massage of oil. Preparations for
		the bath. How to proceed.
	B. Clothing.
		I. Hygienic dress. How made.
		Loose, light, free from pins. Not
		too long. Why? Bring samples.
		Change of clothing. Material.
		Woolen. Cotton. Which? Why?
	C. Food.
		I. System in feeding. In food and
		drink. Either natural or artificial
		food.
Lesson IV.—Children and their Diseases.
	A. Colic.
		I. Symptoms.
		2. Treatment. Thoroughly warm.
		Apply hot flannels. Rub body
		with oil.
	B. Thrush. Tell cause and how cured.
	C. Diarrhoea.
		I. Cause.
		2. Treatment.
	D. Spasmodic croup.
		I. Cause. Exposure to damp and
		cold, excitement and indigestion.
		2. Cure. Apply warm flannel to
		throat. Give mustard bath or
		foot bath an emetic and a simple
		enema.
		3. How to prevent a return.
Lesson V. —Home.
	“Home’s not merely roof and room
	It needs something to endear it
	Home is where the heart can bloom,
	Where there’s some kind lip to cheer it.”
				Home.
	A. Cleanliness.
		I. Of house and yards. Sanitary and
		moral effects. Why.
		2. Of carpets, floors, walls, nooks
		and corners, bedding, clothing
		and food.
	B. Regularity.
		I. Work.
		2. Study.
		3. Devotional exercises.
		4. Shopping.
	C. Manners at home.
		I. Politeness. The golden rule is the
		embodiment of all true politeness.
		2. Personal habits. Cleanliness of
		person. “A clean mind can not
		dwell in an unclean body.”
Lesson VI.—Home (continued).
	A great many homes are like the frame of
a harp that stands without strings. In form
and outline they suggest music; but no
melody rises from the empty spaces; and
thus it happens that home is unattractive,
dreary and dull.

[Column 2]
A. Amusements.
	I. Books. A library is not a luxury
	but one of the necessaries of life.
	Books of information are the tools
	for life’s daily use. Books of in-
	spiration are the instruments of
	music for life’s loftiest and most
	sacred moments.
	2. Music. Music is not a mere orna-
	ment it is an educative power.
	How? What is its moral effect?
	How does it bring about har-
	mony?
	3. Games. Educational. Avoid
	games that border on familiarity.
	Give substance of Presidents
	Smith’s talk on card playing.
B. Ethics.
	1. Pleasant and attractive surround-
	ings. It is as much the duty of
	parents to beautify their home
	as it is to keep the moral atmos-
	phere of that home pure.
	2. Agreeable associates.
	3. Parents love for child. No person
	has the right to be the parent of
	a child who does not love him.
	4. Obligations of prrents and chil-
	dren are reciprocal. Why? A
	smile is the most useful thing in
	the world iu proportion to its cost.
