HOMESPUN TALK.

	Yes, ma'am, I am Mormon! Mormon to the
backbone. I am no saint, and you won't need to
consult more than two of my numerous house-
hold to find out the truth of this statement.
You want me to tell you what Mormonism
means to me. Well, it means that I have a 
right to wear plain clothes, or rich clothes,
shabby clothes or old-fashioned ones;that I have
a right to put consecrated oil in or on my
children when they are sick, while my neigh-
bors have a right to call in quack doctors; it
means that I have a right to patronize home in-
dustries and co-ops, instead of building up
the men who would see me go to jail like An-
nie Gallifant and only rejoice thereat; I have
the privilege of giving my husband all the
wives he chooses to take, and take comfort
with them, too; that I have a right to teach
my children to respect the dear religion their
fathers suffered for and sometimes died for its
sake; it means freedom, peace, faith, and life, 
full and complete, both here and hereafter; and
it means—God in EVERYTHING!
