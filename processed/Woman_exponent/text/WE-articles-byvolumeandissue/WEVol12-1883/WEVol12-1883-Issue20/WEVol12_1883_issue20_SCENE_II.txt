SCENE II.

	The whole family discovered sitting round a table;
the ladies sewing, Tom looking on.
	Mrs. D. Well, my children, what do you
think of the Mormon Elder?
	Marian. Why, ma, he looks just like other
men.
	Lucy. I like his looks better than that of
some men. He seems so modest in his bear-
ing; so earnest and truthful, that I believe he
means just what he says.
	Tom. I certainly did not see anyhorns or
cloven feet.
	Mrs. D. This evening, my children, was the
first time I ever heard a true gospel sermon—
the same principles that our Savior and His
Apostles taught.
	Tom. But, Aunt Susan, these Mormons be-
lieve in prophets, apostles, and in revelation.
I thought these things were done away.
	Mrs. D. So we have been taught to believe.
I have read the Bible from my childhood, and
it does not inform me that these things were
done away.
	Lucy. Their belief in revelation pleased
me. It is very pleasant to think that the Lord
has not entirely forgotten His children. I
think the Elder showed very plainly that the
Christian ministers have no authority from
God to preach the gospel. There is evidently
something wrong in the teachings we have re-
ceived.
	Tom. What do the clergy care, if they can
please the rich. No matter how corrupt their
congregations are, these ministers, in many
cases, will wink at their vices, if they are well
paid. Well said, my boy. I think, Tom, you
must have studied these things more than I
gave you credit for.
	Lucy. The young man told us that an angel
appeared to their Prophet, Joseph Smith, and
gave unto him the power to preach the gospel,
and that he also gave him some gold plates,
from which he translated the Book of Mor-
mon.
	Marian. He said this book contained the
history of the American Indians, a people who
were once called Nephites and Lamanites. He
also spoke of an angel whose name was Moro-
ni (hesitating). He was a nice looking young
man. Was he not, Tom?
	Tom. Who, Marian? The angel or the
Mormon Elder?
	Lucy. Do be serious, Tom. There's a good
boy. People say the Mormons do not believe
in the Bible. I am sure they do, for he had
one in his hand to-night, and read from it.
	Tom. Yes, and he proved pretty plainly
from it the truth of what he said.
	Mrs. D. I believe he told the simple, hon-
est truth, and I bless him for it.
Mariau. I have been told that the principal
thing these Mormons teach is a plurality of
wives; I am sure I did not hear him say any-
thing about wives to-night.
	Lucy. Indeed he did not! He said he was
an Elder, sent to preach the gospel of Jesus
Christ.
	Marian. And that he preached without
purse and scrip, as did the disciples of old. The
only pay he expected was the blessing of his
Heavenly Father and the approbation of the
Priesthood.
	Tom. Poor fellow! I wonder where he
will stop to-night, (putting on his hat.) Aunt
Susan, I will go and find him.
	Mrs D. Stay, Tom! You would not find
him to-night. You may do so to-morrow.
When you do find him, if he has no home, we

[Column 2]
will make him welcome while he stays in these
parts.
	Marian. Oh, ma! What will the neigh-
bors say if you take in a Mormon? I would
be ashamed to meet Parson Brown.
	Tom. O, bother the neighbors and Parson
Brown! Who cares what they say? (appears
to be thinking.) May be the poor fellow has
had no supper.
	Mrs. D. We wish to serve the Lord and not
our neighbors. As for this young man, we will
leave him in the hands of the Lord to-night.
	Lucy. I wonder if he has a mother and sis-
ters, and if they pray for him now he is away
from them. It must be dreadful to travel and
preach without any money.
	Mrs. D. Our Savior told His disciples to
do so; they were not to take even two coats, for
He said the laborer was worthy of his hire.
	Tom. Now, Aunt Susan, tell me how a fel-
low can live without money. When I see him
I will give him some.
	Mrs. D. I believe this young man to be a
servant of the Lord. He will inspire some
honest soul to see to his wants to-night, just as
He has given you the will to do, Tom.
	Tom. I will go and try to find him the first
thing in the morning. He is just about my
age; what a good time we will have.
	Lucy. Yes; there’s a good cousin Tom; be
sure and try hard to find him.
	Marian. I do not need the blue silk dress,
ma, the gray one will do; you can give the
money to this young man.
	Tom. Bravo, Marian! I see your heart is
in the right place. What a pleasant time we
will have; will we not, girls? Now for bed.
(Exit.)
				Scene III.
Mrs Devenish and danghters seated and conversing.
(Enter Tom with Mormon Elder.)
	Tom. Aunt Susan, I have found the Mor-
mon Elder. Mr. Day, allow me to introduce
you to Mrs. Devenish and the Misses Devenish,
my aunt and cousins.
	Mrs. D. We are pleased to make your ac-
quaintance. Mr. Day, pray take a seat.
	(M. Elder bows and takes a seat.)
	Mrs. D. We heard you preach last night,
and my nephew, Mr. Tom, was anxious to
make your acquaintance and have you visit us.
In fact, we were all interested and wished to
hear more.
	M. E. I shall be most happy, madam, to
impart to you any information that is in my
power to give. Have you not heard much
concerning us as a people?
	Tom. Indeed we have. If we were to take
the papers for a guide, we would think you
a very wicked people.
	M. E. You would, indeed, sir. But the
newspapers say a great many things that are
false. The facts are, those who write these ar-
ticles in the papers know but little about us as
a people, and do not care to inform themselves,
I think sometimes, for fear of knowing the
truth. It has become the fashion to talk and
write against the Latter-day Saints, and our
enemies would die of ennui were these sensa-
tions to cease.
	Tom. Ha-ha. Mr. Day, you have them
there. I think that many in the fashionable
world are becoming very shallow minded.
	Mrs. D. You will pardon me, sir, if I ask,
have you a home? My nephew wishes to ex-
tend to you the privileges of our home and al-
so of his friendship.
	M. E. I am very grateful to you and Mr.
Tom. For the present I have a home among
our people; but I would be very pleased with
the friendship of your nephew, and also of
yourself and daughters, if you will so honor
me.
	Mrs. D. My young friend, you will always
find a welcome at my house.

[Column 3]
Lucy. Pray, sir, have you a mother and sis-
ters?
	M. E. Yes, miss; and the welcome you have
given me reminds me very much of them.
	Lacy, They must be very anxious on your
account, when you are so far away among
strangers.
	M. E. If they consulted their own feelings
they would prefer to have me at home. But
we believe in preaching the gospel to the
world, and the young are better able to go
than the old. Besides, I am in the hands of
the Lord, and my friends have faith that I will
return in safety.
	Mrs. D. It is a great blessing, Mr. Day, to
have the prayers of a kind mother and sisters.
	M. E. Indeed, madam, I realize that every
day. I believe that through their prayers I
find many friends I would not without them.
	Marian. (excited.) O ma, here is Parson
Brown! O dear! O dear! what will we do?
	Tom. Well, what of it, cousin? I expect
we can live through the storm.
	M. E. Madam, perhaps I intrude; allow me
to retire.
	Mrs. D. No, no! I am the mistress of this
house; keep your seat, Mr. Day.
	(Enter Parson Brown.)
	Parson Brown. Good morning, my dear
madam, I hope you are well; also the young la-
dies and Mr. Tom.
	Mrs. D. Quite well, thank you, sir; please
take a seat. (hands him a chair,)
	P. B. My dear lady, I have been very anx-
ious about you, I heard that you went to hear
those dreadful Mormons preach last night. I
have been wrestling with the Lord ever since
daylight, that He might save you from those
wicked people. I have come to warn you a-
gainst them. They are so cunning, they will
throw a spell around you, madam, and be-
witch you. For the sake of your lovely
daughters, my dear lady, he warned in time.
(still standing and wringing his hands.) They
will marry you and your daughters to one
man. Oh, horrible!
	Mrs D. My dear sir, pray be seated and
do not excite yourself. Nothing serious has
happened, I can assure you.
	P. B. (with a groan.) Nothing serious; did
you not go and hear those wicked Mormons?
	Mrs. D. Certainly we went and heard those
dreadful Mormons, as you call them. But I
did not see or hear anything very dreadful. I
saw a good looking young man, and heard him
say many good things.
	P. B. Think, my dear lady, of the poor
dear departed, your worthy husband. With
what sorrowful eyes he must be looking down
at you at the present time. Besides, these
Mormons have a new Bible. They do not be-
lieve in the blessed Jesus. They are a black
spot on creation. 'They are the—the twin rel-
ic.		
	Tom. You make a grand mistake, Mr.
Brown, their Bible is the same as ours. They
not only believe in bur Savior, but they preach
the. same doctrine as He did. (aside.) That
is more than I can say of you, Mr. Long face.
As for the twin relic, I believe they are the
twin relic of the ancient Apostles.
	Lucy. Indeed, they are, Mr. Brown. For
they have prophets and apostles, and believe in
revelation.
	Marian. Yes, sir, they believe in the Bible
and preach from it. Is that very wicked, sir?
	P. B. I see, my child, you are all be-
witched. (turning round he sees the M. E.;
eyes him suspiciously and starts forward.) Ah!
Who have we here?
Tom. (bowing low.) Mr. Brown, allow me
the extreme pleasure of introducing to you Mr.
Day, a Mormon Elder from Salt Lake City.
Mr. Day, our respected minister, Mr. Brown.
	P. B. (in a great rage.) What! a viper in
this very house! Madam, how dare you—


[Column 1]
	Mrs. D. (with dignity.) Sir, you forget
youself. I dare anything in my own house.
	P. B. (cringing.) My dear lady, forgive
me. In my great love for you, I did forget
myself. But it is dreadful to think that my
sheep must be eaten up by these ravenous
wolves.
	Tom. (aside, pointing to M. E.) He is
afraid that this wolf will eat up all his good
dinners.
	Mrs. D. I am quite capable, Sir, of taking
care of my own affairs. As for the wolves, I
think it is vice versa in your case.
	(M. E. seems to be enjoying the scene huge-
ly.)
	P. B. Mrs. Devenish, consider the awful
position you are in. Think of the many years
you have sat under the voice of a servant of
God, (weeping.) your old minister. (holding
out his hands beseechingly to Mrs. D.) Turn
this man from your house; let him not contam-
inate your pure daughters, nor endanger the
morals of Mr. Tom. O, I tremble for your
safety.
	Lucy. Sir, we are not afraid of this young
man. He does not look so very monstrous.
	Marrian. (aside.) I do not think he does.
	P, B. My dear, you do not know them as
well as I do.
	Tom. Do you know them Mr. Brown?
Have you been much in their company?
	P. B. (holding up his hands in horror.) I
know them! much in their company! (with a
groan.) The Lord forbid. This is the first time
I ever saw one. I pray it may be the last.
	Marian. Why, Mr. Brown, I thought you
said you knew them.
	P. B. (confused.) H-m—My child, I have
heard and read so much about them,
	Tom. Pshaw, is that all?
	Mrs. D. Being a minister of the gospel and
a follower of our Savior, you should have
charity, Mr. Brown, and not believe everything
yon read and hear. If you continue your vis-
its here you will surely meet this young man,
for I have given him a cordial welcome to our
house.
	P. B. (in a rage.) I will not be so insult-
ed. I, a minister of the Church of England,
mix with those vile people. Never, never!
(rushes to the door, looks back.) Anathema!
anathema! Exit.
	Tom. Ha-ha, Old boy, you have got your
walking ticket.
	Marian. O dear! I am all of a tremble.
I did not think our good old minister, could
talk like that.
	Tom. So, so, my brave coz., afraid of the
old minister, were you not?
	Mrs. D. Mr. Day, excuse this scene; you
must have been very much amused with our
good minister. I certainly was very much sur-
prised at the part he acted.
	M. E. Yes, madam I was amused, but not
at all surprised. I have met such gentlemen
before.
	Mrs. D. Let us resume our conservation.
	Lucy. If you live in Salt Lake City, Mr.
Day, you are acquainted with Mr. Young,
your leader. Is he anything like our minis-
ter?
	M. E. No, miss, they are as opposite as
light and darkness. I know President Young
well, and some of the Apostles. They are men
of God. I know them to be honest, upright
and virtuous.
	Mrs. D. You are too young to have known
Mr. Smith, your Prophet.
	M. E. Yes, ma'am; but my father knew
him well. I have often heard him speak of
the Prophet Joseph Smith; of his great heart
and noble soul; of his wisdom and intelligence,
and of him being a servant of God, and friend
to all mankind.
	Marian. Do you believe this to be true, Mr.
Day?

[Column 2]
	M. E. I more than believe. I know it to
be true through the many evidences I have
of its truth, and by the revelations of the Ho-
ly Spirit.
	Tom. You are very different from us; we
only believe, hope and pray it is so.
	M. E. That is because you have not been
taught correct principlns by one having au-
thority from God. The Gospel of Jesus Christ
is so plain, that our Lord says, a wayfaring
man, though a fool, may not err.
	Marian. Mr. Day, you do not talk like our
minister. I rather like what you say.
	M. E. I try to deliver the message my
Master gave me in its purity. I am nothing of
myself, and without the Spirit of the Lord I
should be worse than nothing in my present
position. I must now bid you good-day. I
have duties to perform that must not be neg-
lected. Please accept my thanks for the
kind reception you have given me. I shall
deem it a pleasure to call again.
	Mrs. D. My young friend, we shall be most
happy to see you whenever circumstances will
permit you to visit us.
	Tom. Mr. Day, I will accompany you part
of the way.
	M. E. Thanks, Mr. Tom. I should be
pleased with your company, (Both bow to the
ladies and retire.)
