EDUCATION.

	It is a generally accepted idea that women
do not, can not, think as deeply as men. Some
people aver that it is because they have (more
especially a few years past) been educated to
think differently, or rather for their education
to consist of another class of studies.
It is only lately they have been permitted to
attend universities and colleges, they have gen-
erally studied the common branches of educa-
tion, music, language, and some fancy work,
even drawing and painting only being indulged
in by a few; but now that they commonly study
botany, physiology, chemistry and many other
of the higher branches in the high schools,
and so many graduate, it will certainly have a
tendency to make us think more deeply, and
in time we may be credited with scientific dis-


coveries which without this new departure
would undoubtedly belong to those who for so
long have enjoyed these honors.
	It is said that some women are better letter
writers than some men, but that the best lady
writers are only second to the best gentlemen.
Though there are many female novelists  none
enjoy an equal reputation with Dickens, Byron
and Lytton. Mrs. Cross "George Elliot" was
considered the finest lady writer in England.
	In poetry we are again in the shade, and as
artists, historians and composers we see no la-
die's names coupled with those of Landseer,
Macaulay and Handel.
	The business colleges now attended by so
many females are proving very advantageous,
as many clerks and reporters have graduated,
and are employed, and their services equally
valued with those of the men.
	Joan of Arc, Florence Nightingale, Jenny
Lind, Mrs. Hemans, Eliza Cook, Mrs. Cross,
and Jane Taylor, are a few of the names cele-
brated for their natural talents, heroism and
humanity.			ANN FELLOWS.
