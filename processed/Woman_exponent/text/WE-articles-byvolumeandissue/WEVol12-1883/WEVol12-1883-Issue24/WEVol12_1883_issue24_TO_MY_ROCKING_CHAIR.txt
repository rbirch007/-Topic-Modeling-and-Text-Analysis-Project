TO MY ROCKING CHAIR.

	Thou dear, dumb friend! thou dost deserve
Some words in gratitude to thee
For all that thou hast been to me:
	A kindly servant—made to serve.

	When weary with the toils of day
Thine open arms I ever see,
That seem to whisper, "Come to me!"
	And I the loving call obey.

	Reclining in those faithful arms.
Thou seemest to have wonderous power,
To bear me up for one brief hour
	To realms of thought's obsorbing charm

	I fold my arms, I close mine eyes;
The world recedes—I'm into space!
Away in some remembered place,
	Where aught of sorrow it defies.

	I mingle with the loved ones gone,
I visit haunts of early home;
At times in crowds, but most alone
	Ignoring all of sigh or groan.

	I note the sunny spots alone,
The plats where early flowers spring,
The primrose and the violet ring,
	The king cup and the cowslip's home.

	That "Dial yard!" Oh! how my thought
Goes out o'er every inch of ground;
Where peacocks walked so grand around
	The home with birds and bantams fraught.

	And "Wormwood hill!"—how oft we climbed
Its wooded sides—its beauteous crown—
While some the beech nuts showered down,
	And others on the sward reclined.

	Old chair! the transit of the plains;
Yes, we that journey made together,
In sunshine and in gloomy weather,
	In summer draughts and drenching rains.

	But years have rolled, and brought us up
To eighteen hundred eighty-four;
We've plodded on, if slow, yet sure—
	Together we must drain the cup—

	The mingled cup of earthly time,
But to each other let's be true;
No grander chair I choose than you,
	And still within thine arms recline.

	It may be I may pass away
"Beyond the gates" from out thine arms,
This thought, for me, has sweetest charms,
	If near or distant be the day.
								HANNAH T. KING.
May 4th, 1884.
