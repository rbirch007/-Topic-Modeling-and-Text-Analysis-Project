ABOUT EATING.

	The following suggestions from a diet list,
such as each patient under treatment in the
Sanatorium receives from his physician, may be
of service to those who are trying to pursue the
right way at home.
	Take no food whatever—fruit included—
except at meal times.  Eat slowly, and masti-
cate all food thoroughly.
	Ten minutes more at meals may save a fit o
indigestion.  Drink sparingly: never a glass of
very cold or very hot liquid.  A swallow or
two of water, just before leaving the table,
permissible; but neither hot nor cold water
should be drunk freely within a half hour be-
fore or an half hour after meals.
	Good digestion depends largely upon mental
conditions and influences.  It is promoted by
light conversation and gentle exercise, but re-
tarded by severe bodily or mental labor; study
therefore to keep free from all mental or emo-
tional excitement, before, during, and after
meals, and avoid all fatiguing exercise imme-
ditely before or within an hour after eating.
	Choose only pleasant topics for conversation
while at table.The discussion there of diseases
is especially harmful and annoying, to say no-
thing of being inconsistent with good breed-
ing.  Equally ill timed and injurious are all
arguments and discussions at table concerning
the relative value of different articles of food.
	Bear in mind that it is possible to study
your diet too closely, and to do great harm
thereby.  Avoid any tendency to count each
mouthful of food, constantly to study the un-
pleasant effects produced by the different arti-
cles you have eaten, or to let your mind con-
tinually dwell on what you ought to eat and
what you ought not to eat.  Men, as a rule,
eat too much; women too little.
