THE ARTIST CHILD.

"What are you doing, Bosque?"
	Gently he turned his head,
And I saw 'neath his little fingers
	Pencil and paper spread.
"And may I see your picture?"
	Then as he gave it to me
A shade came over the drooping face
	'Twas pitiful to see.

And then a thrill went through me,
	For I that place had seen,
Its walls loom up and seem to blot
	The valley wide and green.
And yet I asked him gently,
	"What do you call this place?"
"Its where they are keeping my father;"
	And turned away his face.

Six years old! But upon his head
	And in his childish breast,
That which would weigh a man's strong heart
	Upon his life doth rest.	
Think of it! While his comrades
	Played at their game of ball,
He, scarce more than a babe, sat alone,
	Sketching a prison wall!

Pictures for memory! Look,
	What will they be for him?
Those gloomy walls will unshaken stand
	When fairer scenes are dim.
Sunk in his soul's deep silence,
	Who can the growth foretell,
When years from now shall this artist paint
	The woes of Israel.

Christendom, reading bibles—
	Boast the advance you've made—
Again in the walls of our masters
	Our children's hearts are laid.
Heroes who left to us freedom!
	Look down on us to-day,
Where tyranny's graft on Liberty's tree
	Wasteth its life away.

Brick for brick as they stand there—
	Tower, and gate, and lock—
Theres a wall in Israel's soul
	That doth the tyrant mock.
Closer they stand together,
	Nor doubt, nor shrink to go
Through waters deep, and fear none but Him
Who will o'erwhelm the foe.
				AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON.
May 22nd, 1883.
