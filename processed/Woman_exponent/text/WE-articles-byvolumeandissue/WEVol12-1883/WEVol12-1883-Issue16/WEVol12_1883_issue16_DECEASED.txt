DECEASED.]

How strong are the ties 'tween the dearest and truest,
	How ruthless is death when he snaps them in twain!
When we sense that some loved one can never come to
	us,
	How crushed is the heart with the parting and pain.
The glorified dead are the fortunated only—
	Misfortune and sorrow no more will they know;
Alas! for the living, the loving and lonely—
	Only those who have suffer'd can fathom their woe.

God grant it, indeed, that these sorrowful mourners
	May prove that for good our afflictions are given;
We're here but "to-day," even transient sojourners;
	To-morrow you'll meet with the lov'd ones in heaven.

To the sweet, tender bud from the tree now trans-
	planted
	We offer this tribute of pity sincere;
Not for thee is the boon that to many is granted,
	The protection and love of a father so dear.

May the angels of mercy be charg'd to attend thee;
	May your sorrow bear fruit of the loveliest kind;
To the love of "the Father of all" we commend thee,
	In whom ev'ry orphan a Parent can find.

While you mourn for the father, whose name you can
	honor,
	His loss to your mother you never will know;
May your love be a light that will shine down upon her,
	And comfort her heart in despite of her woe.

For surely our children can comfort or grieve us
	More than anyone else in the whole world beside;
If of long cherish'd hopes by their deeds they bereave
	us,
	'Twould indeed have been better if early they'd died.

The name that you bear is both honor'd and hoary,
	'Tis known and respected at home and abroad;
Oh! be not the one to diminish its glory,
	Nor turn from the path that your fathers have trod.

For the truth may you seek, yea, and find the refreshing
	Pure waters of life as they flow from above.
May your life and your labors be truly a blessing
	To the Kingdom of God and the friends whom you
