LIFE'S SUMMER IS WANING.

How swift the fleeting years depart—
	Old time's stern hand we trace—
Which leaves, if not within the heart,
	Its impress on the face.
But yester morn to me it seems
	Since life's bright summer's dawn
Shed first on me its radiant beams,
	And now 'tis well nigh gone.

The hair that once was sunny brown
	Is changing fast to gray;
The pearly teeth once whit and sound
	Are falling to decay;
The eyes that once were clear and bright, 
	And sparkled like the dew,
Have now exchanged their lustrous light
	For one of sadder hue.
The face that in life's youthful day
	Was like the apple bloom,
Now plainly says that mortal clay
	Is destined for the tomb.
And with the transient bloom of youth
	That from my brow hath fled,
Lie many of its cherished hopes
	Entombed among the dead.
Yes, many castles bright and fair,
	Which fancy made complete,
Now lie like bubbles of the air
	In ruins at my feet.
And shall I mourn departing youth
	Who's evanescent joy
Hath given place to Gospel Truth,
	So pure, without alloy.
Ah, no! a thousand times more fair
	Is truth's effulgent beams,
A God to hear and answer prayer,
	Than youth's vain, fleeting dreams.
There is a beauty not of earth,
	That fadeth not away,
But for the faithful and the good	
	Increaseth day by day.
The Holy Ghost doth joys impart, 
	Which none but Saints may know; 
A balm it hath for every smart,
	A bliss for every woe;
Its joys end not with mortal life,
	Its peace can never die; 
The righteous still this gift possess
	In realms beyond the sky.
There may we meet, sweet friend of mine,
	When mortal life is through,
Where joys that fade so soon in time
	Shall be forever new.
When earth restored shall be our place,
	All pain and sorrow cease,
When Christ, the Lord, unveils His face
	To bring His Saints release.		M. A. F.
