﻿EARLY MORNING ON THE RIGHI,
			ZURICH, Oct. 16th, 1882.
	The lover of solitude is brought to a sharp
realization of the march of modern improve-
ment if he expects to indulge, a taste for rural
simplicity at the Righi Kulm. Contrasts are
wonderfully striking there. Coming down
from the clouds as far as the hotels, we found
much that was of the earth, earthy. With
our wraps we threw off for the time the en-
chantment which held us spell-bound just
above our then present surroundings. There
on that Alpine height, which only a few years
ago was seldom visited expect by those desir-
ous of performing a pilgrimage, we found
guests from all nations and lands, accommoda-
ted as well as they would expect to be in any
of the cities of our own continent.
	In 1816 a small house to shelter the occa-
sional visitor was for the first time erected
there. In 1848, the first regular inn was built.
To-day we find several handsome and substan-
tial buildings on the mountain, those at the
Kulm alone having comfortable quarters for
about two thousand guests, with plenty of well
trained clerks, porters and waiters to attend to
their wants.
	A long drawn out "table d'hote" dinner is
served soon after the going down of the sun.
We are told there are parties equal to appre-
ciating that exercise even here. Perhaps they
are pilgrims who take it as they would a
penance.
	We found the large salon well lighted and
luxuriously furnished. Gay parties flitted in
and out. A modern piano lent its familiar
tones to the scene, while fun and frolic were
quite as much in order above the clouds as we
had found it in the lower region. It would
seem as though, this mountain peak was one of

	One seldom has a chance to feel absolutely
lonesome even in this far away, land, since at
every turning there is a possibility of meeting

a friend. We have found ours in the bowels
of the earth and on the misty mountain tops,
We often wonder if the journey into that
other land beyond the Sea of Time will be as
full of glad surprises and happy recognitions.
In our own hearts we have a eonviction not
easily reasoned away, that tender greetings
will be waiting for us "beyond the pearly gates
of day," and that loving guides will go with
us into the presence of "Our Father" as soon
as our feet shall be able to tread the wondrous
way, or our eyes have strength to bear the
glory which shall be round about us. Since
we who are still in the flesh are so glad to
clasp the hands of those whom we left on the
other side, to tell them the wonders we have
seen, and show them the treasures we have
been permitted to enjoy, may we not conclude
that our unceasing yearning for the dear ones
who have gone before us will have its most
precious answer when we find that place in the
Father's home which we are assured has been
prepared for all who desire to enter therein?
It very often happens to us now on this side to
meet with some casual friend or acquaintance
whom we have not seen since we left our own
shores, perhaps one whom we seldom met or
scarcely knew there. The sight of such a one
here in this strange land is at once a bond of
union, and we start on in a new sort of ac-
quaintance, finding out so many satisfactory
things about this new friend, or this old friend,
and wondering that we could have been so
blind or so full of other things as not to have
seen deeper before. All time tends towards
eternity. A few years more or less and the
things of this earth will have passed by for us,
then may it not be that the new heaven and
the new earth shall show us our friends again,
revealing more than our earthly eyes could
ever see of sweetness and perfection? Here it
seems certain that we drift on in one sense in
the great current of life. us. There we may hope to find them all.
Here "we see through a glass, darkly;" there,
we will see "face to face." The words which
are spoken or the words which are left un-
spoken in this world will find their true mean-
ing, and we shall understand those about us as
it has been impossible to comprehend them
with our earthly limitations.
	Inside our hotel there was a little world all
by itself. Outside us the evening darkened
into the night. It was cold, damp and cheer-
less. There was not the sense of insecurity
that we felt at Mt. Washington. The clouds
had gathered so protectingly about us during
our ascent that we did not feel how entirely
we were lifted above the standpoint of ordi-
nary mortals. The summit of the Right was
quite a little walk above us, so we felt that we
were quite secure in our moorings. Our sensa-
tions were certainly akin to those we have,
when out at sea. As the moon struggles
through the mists for a few seconds we see a
restless surging ocean beneath us, and uncon-
sciously listen for the warning voice of the fog
whistle, as we did on shipboard when the
clouds close in again. We miss the monoto-
nous, faithful beating of the huge engine,
whose strokes kept pace with all our thoughts,
waking or sleeping, through the many days
that the fair green earth was hidden from our
eyes.
	There is nothing more for us to see outside
the house. We go in, and the clerk brings us
a huge book wherein we are expected to in-
scribe our names. We look it over with now
and then a note or exclamation as we come
upon some well known name. A goodly com-
pany has evidently gone on before us.
	We gathered our forces and went up the
great stone stairs in the rotunda to find our
sleeping rooms, all of which proved comforta-
ble and convenient. We were to be roused


half an hour before sunrise by the reveille,
and it behooved us to get what sleep we could.
The night was not entirely restful, for we were
quite possessed of the idea that the man whose
one duty in this world was to blow that Al-
pine horn, would be the one man in the estab-
lishment who would most enjoy a morning
nap. If we had been the next in order ap-
pointed to fill his place in case of his non ap-
pearance we could not have given ourselves
more concern obout the signs of the times.
We were up and dressed before the time. The
reveille did not sound. Of course the man
was asleep. A suggestion that the clouds
might be at the bottom, or rather the top of
the mystery, met with no favor in our case.
We had seen the sun set the night before en-
tirely regardless of clouds. He might rise in
the same fashion. We went up to the Belve-
dere, but it was of no avail. If the sun did
get up that morning, as we have every reason
to believe he did, his toilet was not made in
public. There was not the faintest suggestion
of a rosy dawn. Nothing but dense, damp,
impenetrable fog. Neither poet nor astrono-
mer could have made anything else out of it.
	We went back to breakfast without having
any occasion to felicitate ourselves on the re-
sult of our "morning watch." There is not
the slightest probability of our becoming a
rival candidate for the honor of blowing the
Alpine horn for the revielle in the Righi. We
have no desire to share the honors with the
rightful owner of that melodious instrument.
	As the morning advanced the clouds re-
ceded. There were great rifts in the mists
below, and many and many an exquisite pic-
ture broke through its silver setting, to have a
mysterious veil flung over it, and be hidden
from our sight again. We would not go down
till we had made sure we had exhausted the
changing panorama.
	When the clouds hemmed us in we turned
our attention to the queer little booths of cu-
riousities arranged actually on the very top of
the mountain. Here sprightly boys and queer
old women drive a brisk trade when the views
are obscured. They remind one of the vend-
ers of ices and lemonade who enliven the the-
atres between the acts, but are utterly forgot-
ten except when the curtain is down.
	We were more than paid for delaying. As
the clouds settled, the snowy tops of the far off
mountains came up above the great ocean of
mist. We never had one continuous view of
all the ranges and diversity of scenery at one
time, but a goodly sight at one point which
would be obscured as another broke into sight
in some other direction. There are days in
which the circuit within view is three hundred
miles.
	As the morning crept on we were apparent-
ly lifted into the sunshine. The clouds rolled
themselves together below our feet, then reced-
ed, and again gathered themselves into solid
masses. As we stood in the golden light of
that beautiful morning we knew that there
were those only a little way below who were
wrapt in shadow. While darkness and dis-
comfort were their portion, the silver lining of
the clouds which were enfolding them was a
vision of beauty to us. Perhaps we shall find
out some day that our standpoints in this
world may affect our vision.
