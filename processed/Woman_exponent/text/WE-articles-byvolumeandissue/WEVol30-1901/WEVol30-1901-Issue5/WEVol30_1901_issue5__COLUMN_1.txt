	Who is that fine looking stranger advanc-

mist arises before her eyes; her heart stands

	"We met, 'twas in a crowd,
	And I thought he would shun me,
	He came, I could not breathe,
	For his eye was upon me.
	He spoke, his words were cold,
	But his tone was unaltered,
	I knew how much he felt,
	For his deep-toned voice faltered.
	He called me by my name,
	As the bride of another."

She looked up trembling. He asked for
a dance.
	"My husband," she faltered.
	"I have your husband's permission," he
said.
	Deferentially prottering his arm, she
leaned upon him as in former days.
	Through the crowd they wended their 
way.
	"Let us promenade," he said, entering
the hall. "I want to talk to you." Look-
ing down upon her, "You have made just
the woman I thought you would," he 
said. "I am not disappointed in you; and
how old is the little one?"
	"A little over a year."
	He spoke kindly now, and fear left the
heart of his companion. He talked on of
things around them, then of the past.
	"I am sorry," he said, "for the unkind
words I spoke, you were not to blame; but
should he ever be unkind to you," clinch-
ing his hand. "Well," he continued, "I 
want you to promise me, should you ever
need a friend and I am anywhere in the
world, you will let me be that friend.
Promise me, will you call on me? I shall
never marry. I loved once, and it was for-
ever, for me there is none henceforth. Will 
you promise?"
	"Yes," was the faint response. "But,
O, you will love again some day; a fair
young girl will love you for your good-
ness."
	"No," he said, "I shall never marry.
Never will I see one like you, never one
that I can love. Thank you for the
promise. You will never be without a
friend while I live. I would come to you
over land or sea. Let us go in to dance."
