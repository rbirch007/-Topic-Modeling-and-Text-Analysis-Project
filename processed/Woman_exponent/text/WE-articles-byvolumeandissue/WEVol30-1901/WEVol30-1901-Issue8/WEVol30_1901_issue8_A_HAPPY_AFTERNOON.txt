A HAPPY AFTERNOON.
	Sister Johanna Nystrom, of the Twen-
tieth Ward, of this city, gave a delightful
dinner party to the aged and infirm widows
of the ward, on Thursday, November 21
1901.
	The day chosen by Sister Nystrom for
the occasion was the fifty-seventh anniver-
sary of her departed husband.
	The presidency of the Relief Society of
the ward, being in attendance, by request
of the hostess, took charge of the social
part of the entertainment. Twenty-three
guests were present, while a few who had
been invited were absent on account of
illness.
	Some of the favorite songs of Zion were
sung, prayer was offered and earnest heart-
felt testimonies were borne by each of the
sisters in turn, telling of many touching
experiences, and how the blessings of the
Lord follow those who trust, and strive
to serve Him.
	Portions of the time were also filled in
with pleasant conversation, and several
interesting recitations were given.
	The oldest one present was Sister Adam-
son, who is in her eighty fifth year.
	At five o’clock the company sat down to a
fine, old-fashioned dinner, a “regular
Thanksgiving dinner,” as the youngest
lady of the party expressed it. A leading
feature of the repast was fat, tender, home
raised chicken, cooked as “mother used to
do it.” And there was an abundant
variety of viands, all following harmon-
iously in the same sensible, healthful line.
	After dinner the impromptu program
was continued. Speeches, recitations and
singing were again called for, and to every
call a cordial response was cheerfully given.
Many grateful thanks and prayers for con-
tinued blessings were warmly expressed to
Sister Nystrom for her generous hospital-
ity, and pleasing memories of Brother
Nystrom’s kindness to the poor during his
lifetime were related by several of the
guests.
	At an early hour the party closed with
benediction and the ladies went home with
feelings of satisfaction and peace not soon
to be forgotten.
