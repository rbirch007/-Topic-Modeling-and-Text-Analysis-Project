THEIR INHERITANCE.

O, far away in a sea-side home
	Two anxious parents knelt;
The prayer that trembled on their lips
	Deep in their hearts was felt.
"O, Father, open Thou the way
	That we our home may leave,
And 'mid Thy people grant we may
	Inheritance receive."
As morning light through darkness breaks,
	He opened out the way;
Sweet was their joy to leave the world
	And parting words to say.
Through toilsome months and dang'rous paths
	In gratitude they still
Kept on their way, and closer strove
	To learn God's holy will.
Ne'er sad complaints nor vain regrets
	Uprose to cloud their light,
His voice came nearer to their souls,
	His purpose to their sight;
Aye! distance shut out worldly ills,
	 And industry made warm
Their hearts with sweet content and closed 
	Their homes 'gainst need and storm.
Fair Zion grew, and 'round their home
	Dear children laughed and played
By purling streams in peaceful streets,
	Or in their orchard's shade.

The Temple's grandeur, over spires--
	O'er all the valley rose,
That House so dear to faithful Saints,
	So envied by their foes.
From east and west the strangers came
	In cars of splendid ease,
Nor knew, nor cared, the journeys old
	That had been borne by these;
Nor thought, nor cared how years of toil
	Part of our being grow;
How gold is powerless to bind
	Our rights, though wielded so—
They only saw the city fair,
	As in its Sabbath dress,
And weighed the stranger's pleasure more
	Than the Saints' faithfulness.

From lip to lip the tidings flew,
	A thousand times retold;
(Aye! woe lurked 'neath the smiling face
	And want beneath the gold;)
"For years of toil and scant returns—
	To ease us in our age,
Here is a fortune in an hour
	For this small heritage."
From mind to mind the impulse spread,
	And hands met over gold
That ne'er had felt each other's touch
	Had TRUTH not wealth been told.
And, looking out from open doors,
	How soon the fact was met—
With lonely sense—a stranger's face,
	With cold aversion set. 
On fever's bed a darling lies,
	And pleads with homesick pain,
"I want my own dear little room
	And playmates back again;
I want to see our orchard trees
	And play around our door,
And go to school and Sunday School
	Just where I did before.
O, buy it back, I want to see
	The Temple.  Ere I pray,
I look and miss it every hour—
	Why did we come away?
I passed the home where I was born,
	Bessie and Johnnie, too,
A great, proud man was standing there,
	And caught me looking through;
So stern he looked and spoke to me,
	I hurried quick away.
O, pa, that man might drive the Saints
	If trouble comes, some day.
Please buy it back, the roses hang
	Right at my window now,
I know the birds have come to build
	In ev'ry apple bough;
My swing is by the cherry tree,
	And where my trap I set

[Column 2]
For busy sparrows, they are thick—
	Pa, try and buy it yet?
The grass ain't thick here like 'tis there,
	Calo and us can't play
Half good; Oh, everything's so strange—
	Why did we come away?
Pa, when I am a man I'll work
	And you shall work no more.
Pa, you forgot; the home was ours,
	You said so long before;
We'd not have sold the home from you,
	When you were weak and old—
You let him have it from us all—
	For just—a bag—of gold!"

Vain tears fell on the dear one's face,
	They sobbed, "O, cruel fate
That brings its penance though we feel
	Repentance all too late"

Years hurried on, and changes great 
	Had made the world like new;
Chaos and avarice had fled,
	And men were proven true.
A noble city reared its spires
	Above its foliage green,
And shaded walks with silver streams
	Showed here and there between.

Day after day before its King
	In humble rev'rence came
His subjects, each and every one
	With dearest hope and claim;
The gift of an inheritance
	In Zion the redeemed;
How sweet the hope! and trials past
	Of trivial moment seemed.
O, wondrous were the blessed words
	Unto the faithful told,
Each was repaid with justice true
	For loss, a thousand fold.
But others! angels pitying turned,
	And hearts turned faint away,
Who but received the fruit of seed
	Sown in a bygone day.
Faded and tattered wanderers
	From Truth, they had come back,
And saw the harvest of their days
	Barren as desert track.
"Inheritance? Not masters now,
	Servants ye are enrolled—
Inheritance that once was  yours
	Ye parted with for gold!"
