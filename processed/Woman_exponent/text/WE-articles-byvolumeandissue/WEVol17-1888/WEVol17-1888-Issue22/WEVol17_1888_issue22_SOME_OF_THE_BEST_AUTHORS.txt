	SOME OF THE BEST AUTHORS.

II
	In my last I made a couple of selections
from Mrs. Hemans, a poet of whom the Eng-
lish people are justly proud; nor do I find in
rambling among other poets of her sex (and
indeed, but few of the opposite sex) many who
desire to be ranked above her. She is the
sweetest, truest, purest expositor of the guile-
less human heart; her own we may say uncon-
scious, interpreter of a beautiful sinless life.
While her elegance of diction is unexcelled;
and the purity of her conceptions unrivaled.
She is not sentimental, but exhibits the strength
and beauty of inherent truth. She may be-
going out of fashion in these fast and practical
times, but so are nearly all the old poets; and
as true poetry is beauty, and beauty is truth,
so we will some day come back to our lost love
for "truth will prevail."
	We shall grow tired of our superficalities,
and find that true happiness, that bright gem
for which philosophers of all ages have delved,
lies not in fashion, but in the beautiful Nature
which God hath wrought. Nowhere is nature
more ideal and true, than in the pure depths
of a guileless heart, and nowhere shall we
find this beauty more perfectly interpreted
than in some of the poets of the olden school.
	Mrs. Francis S. Osgood occurs to our mind
as an American poet of genius, who was born
in Boston in 1812. Who has not been cap-
tivated by her sweet poem Labor ?

	"Labor is rest—from the sorrows that greet us,
	Rest from all petty vexations that meet us;
	Rest from the sin promptings that ever entreat us;
	Rest from world sirens that lure us to ill;
		Work—and pure slumbers shall wait on thy pillow.
		Work—thou shalt ride over Care's coming billows;
		Lie down wearied 'neath Woe's weeping willow,
	Work with a stout heart and resolute will!

	Labor is wealth; in the sea the pearl groweth,
	Rich the Queen's robe from the frail cacoon floweth.

	From the fine acorn the sfrong forest bloweth,
		Temple and statue the marble blocks hide.

	Work for some good, be it ever so slowly;
	Cherish some flower, be it ever so lowly.		
	Labor! all labor is noble and holy,
		Let thy great deeds be thy prayer to thy God."

	Where can we find a poem more truly poeti- 
cal than that written by her entitled "To The
Spirit of Poetry." Let us cull a few of its
lines:

	Thou that dost veil the fairest flower with glory,
		Spirit of light, and lovliness and truth!
	Thou that did'st tell me a sweet fairy story.
		Of the dim future, in my wistful youth.

	Thou that came'st to me in my dreaming childhood,
		Shaping the changeful clouds to pagents rare;
	Peopling the smiling vale, and shaded wildwood
		With airy beings, faint, yet strangely fair;
	Telling me all the sea-born breeze was saying
		While it went whispering through the willing leaves.
	Bidding me listen to the light rain, playing
		Its pleasant tune about the household, eve's.

	Tell me not Time, whose wing my brow hath shaded,
		Has withered spring's sweet bloom within my heart;
	Ah no ! the rose of love is yet unfaded,
		Though hope and joy, its sister flowers, depart."

	But Mrs. Osgood has already gone "out of
style," although a comparatively recent
writer; though the writer of several volumes, I
have never yet seen one of her works adver-
tised. She died in 1850.
	Adelaide Procter, the daughter of a poet,
was herself a poet of renown. She was born
in London in 1825, and died in 1864. She did
not write voluminously, but many of her
pieces are true and pretty. Perhaps none is
better known now than "Rise! For the day is
Passing." "One by One" is very sweet and
well known.
	Her genius was not so great as many others,
but she was a true and noble womau; who de-
voted her whole life to doing good. Her
poetry is well written and displays the work-
ings of a benevolent high toned mind.
