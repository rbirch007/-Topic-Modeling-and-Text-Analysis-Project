GOLDEN WEDDING.
"Hear the mellow wedding bells—
Golden bells.”

Ring soft and sweet—O golden bell—
Ring pure and strong—O golden bell,
Now rise, now fall, the chorus swell,
The precious story tell, ah, tell,
How fifty years have flown away,
Since Pallas’ happy wedding day!
Adown the years, far, far away—
A sweet girl-bride, and grown so gray,
see them plainly, as you say,
				O golden bell!

A home so cosy, snug and neat,
I see, I see,
I hear a patter of childish feet,
And baby voices calling sweet,
And full of glee.
The happy chorus rises, swells,
To bursting with the joy it tells,
O, golden bells.

As your swaying tongue goes to and fro,
I watch the years, they come and go—
They bring their joy and bring their woe;
For all must know the ebb and flow
Of grief and pleasure, here below.
Like mists upon the mountains height,
A rain of tears obscures the sight;
'Twas here a precious hope, saw blight,
The veil hung dark as inky night.
Where mists of tears their hopes bewail
Ring visions of the “holy Grail,”
That brings redemption, rends the veil,
Ring Christ’s promise, that will not fail,
O, golden bell.

Now as you back and forward swing.
Sweet bells, what visions pure you bring.
Upon her hand, I see a ring
Worn to a tiny golden string;
Two heads so white with winter’s rime.
Two hearts, true, as in life’s spring time;
Such love doth mark their lives sublime,
And well deserves a gladsome chime 
Of golden bells.

Swing soft and low, ye bells of gold
The story ever new, the old.
The dear sweet story, is almost told,
How true love never shall grow cold
Though mortal heart be dust and mold.
Immortal born its home’s above
For God is love, yes, God is love
Ring happy bells!


Lovingly inscribed to my Aunt and Uncle on the 50th
anniversary of their wedding day.
	Woodina, Feb. 21, 1889.


[For the EXPONENT.]
