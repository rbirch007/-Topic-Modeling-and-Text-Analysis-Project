	SOME OF THE BEST AUTHORS.

	The universality with which the best authors
are conceded their position in English litera-
ture, should at once quell any controversy
in the matter, and yet opinions, tastes and cul-
ture differ, and a writer thoroughly loved and
admired by one reader, may be considered in-
sipid, sickly sentimental, by another. Truly
one is led to exclaim, “Oh, poetry! what license
has been taken in thy name,” when reading
some of the trash that floats around in our
literary age. If quantity were a standard of
poetry and literature, we could not fail to be- 
come thoroughly well read, but, thank fate, it
is not quantity, but quality that makes the
poet. It is to be feared that the former has
the precedence at present. Let us hope that the
strength and originality of the older poets is not
forever lost. But I did not intend to berate our
age, our customs, or our effeminacy, our self-con-
ceit, or the glory of our wonderful nineteenth
century; “they who live in homes of glass should
never throw a stone,” and it suddenly occurred
to us that ours was very thin and brittle !
	About two years ago one of our best Ameri-
can writers, Mr. Maurice Thompson, of Ohio,
had the temerity to publish an article on the
subject of Shakespeare, somewhat deprecia-
tory of the exalted throne upon which the
much applauded dramatist was seated. Actu-
ally depreciatory, but, to the idea of this
unqualified reader, full of good, independent,
common sense. Mr. Thompson received what
he might have expected, a sound scoring by
one of your Shakespearian students, who re-
ceived it as a personal affront that his little
god was not held in the same estimation by
each individual of this little world as by him-
self. Mr. Thompson seemed to think that his
"glasshouse” was completely broken, and
actually thanked the kind friend who had
performed for him the task of giving him the
“giftie” to “see himsel as ithers” saw him.
This would have been all right, but somehow
we could never get over the idea that Maurice
Thompson had taken hold of the right handle.
We never could see any reason for being
ashamed to confess to a depreciative feeling
for the monarch of the drama.
	God speed the day when at least many of
the sayings of Shakespeare, the great English
dramatist, will be forever lost in the light of
modern civilization ! It is surely, at least, one-
sided to study Shakespeare, quote Shakespeare,
act and worship Shakespeare to the exclusion
of everything else, But, there ! we confess to
being a very incompetent critic. Perhaps our
little world is so remote from the sun as to re-
ceive its light but as a faint star.
	Let us, then, descend from the discussion of
a subject too remote and high for our under-
standing, for truly the erudite give us some
interpretations of their favorite poet, that are
too intricate for the understanding of mere
ordinary minds.
