SOME WOMEN'S LIVES.

	THERE is a strange hunger in the average
female heart to be the one and only love of
some other heart. A God given instinct
no doubt, to make the monopoly of mar-
riage dear and desirable, but fatal if the
man be—as is often the case—selfish, need-
ing daily and systematic discipline;
tyrannical, requiring a little wise suppression
and deposing now and then; extravagant
needing constant lessons in economy, vain,
needing an occasional lesson in humility;
and the woman one of those poor, feeble
souls who meekly accept whatever is offered
and who will bear anything and everything
without a murmur rather than be found
fault with. A tender, sympathetic, self-
denying woman who deals with his follies
and faults gently and patiently, as she vain-
ly struggles to forget all the fond unrealized
dreams of her girlhood now so far behind
her; and feeling hopelessly in her inmost
heart that so far as her husband is concerned
the future holds naught of love or happi-
ness for her; feels with an icy pang a chill
sense of desolation and neglect, a
disappointment black as the murky clouds
which hide the blue of Heaven on a stormy
day; knows that her husband who has his
business acquaintances, his friends whom
he meets at the club and elsewhere is surely
growing away from her; that while she has
remained at home busy with household
duties and had grown rusty both in looks
and ideas, he has acquired the superficial
polish of the man of the world and has be-
come even more selfish, more egotistical
until he finally convinces her by continued
coldness and neglect that he is both weary
and ashamed of her. Oh! romantic idealist
with an impossible hero standing as the
type of all that lover and husband ought to
be in that bright fancy land which you

who fulfills all her household duties faith-

her husband's side in public, live, the life
that honorable woman leads in this en-
lightened century, many and many a wife
looks back with a secret bitter smile to
those other days when she thought her
lord and master a hero, a noble champion,
who was always to be her lover and now!
	Many a neglected wife feels that same icy
chill; and such a pang is only the first of
many pangs which go on increasing until
they finally lose their power to sting so 
keenly; and the wife that meant to be so
patient, so loving and true becomes indiffer-
ent as to whether her husband spends his
evenings with her or elsewhere; oftentimes
finding herself glad that he is with her so
seldom. A strong-minded woman said to
me once: "Some women make so much of
trifles. Why, my husband is always out; 
and I'm sure I never fret over such a trifle,
and as for cards, provided he divides his
winnings with me I never think of object-
ing." And yet those very trifles are the
rocks on which many a poor woman's life
is wrecked.

	Memory brings before me to night, a fair,
young face, soft, confiding blue eyes and a
wealth of golden hair; now hidden under
a coffin lid; white their owner sleeps the
last sleep, the world forgotten, by the world
forgot. I saw her in her bridal robes when
she fondly imagined life held nothing but
sweets; that life, her life, would be one
long summer day; and I saw her agin
when she had proven the fallacy of it all-
had quaffed deep draughts of the bitter
waters of Marah, was again arrayed in her
bridal robes, but this time, she was the
bride of death. I well remember when
the first consciousness of her husband's cold-
ness and neglect began to dawn upon her.
She said no word uttered no complaint; but
the sweet face grew thin and pale, the
sweet blue eyes inexpressibly sad. Night
upon night she would sit alone watching
and waiting. Carriage after carriage would
roll by; sometimes one would stop and
then she would listen with intense eager-
ness expecting ever moment to hear his
step in the hall and then to see him enter
the room with his usual careless grace.
