TO SARAH.

THIS fern, a little beauty, on the rugged moun-
	tain grew;
And gets its pretty lustre from storms it has
	passed through,
It grew where great old boulders for years have
	reared their heads;
To mark the lonely beaten path,the weary travel-
	er treads;
Where rain has poured in torrents, where fiercest
	winds have blown
By Nature’s God protected, my tiny fern has
	grown.

The rays of scorching summer sun’s might well
	have dried its leaves,
Or roaming beast have nipped it from the soil to
	which it cleaves;
The pouring rains have washed its roots and left
	them bare and dry
For coming winds to twirl about and scatter in
	the sky.
But lo! a giant tree is there to temper wind and
	rain—
Protection from the burning rays the sun sheds
	there in vain.

And thus the little plant has grown in beauty 
	wondrous, grand,
Until, to fill a higher sphere, ‘twas plucked by
	human hand
And pressed and dried, and now is sent to one
	across the sea—
A token of undying love which fills my heart for
	thee;
Although a thing of little worth, it yet may serve
	this end
And, till once more I clasp thy hand, remind thee 
	of a friend.

And cannot we compare our lives unto the little
	fern?
Do giant rocks not lift their heads at every nook
	and turn?
Do trials not like pouring rain descend upon our
	cot?
Our faults, are they not like the wind exposed
	with little thought?
Do sickness, want and sorrow come, like wither-
	ing summer heat?
And does not Father ever guard our humble, low
	retreat?

And when death takes us from the place we long
	have known and loved,
And we to fill a holier sphere, from trying scenes
	are moved,
For all the sorrows we have passed, the trials
	we’ve endured,
Shall we not shine the brighter in the joy we have
	secured?
O, let the fern a lesson teach of guileless trust
	and love,
That we may serve our Maker’s plan and dwell
	with Him above.
							ALOFA.
Samoa, Oct, 18th, 1892.
