daughter of Col. Ferry was the next
speaker and told much of the miner’s life
and hardships in a pathetic way; she also
gave clearly the condition as it now exists
in mining camps. Mrs. M. Isabella Horne
who came to Utah in 1847, and knows
perhaps as much of this country as any
woman living, made some strong points on
home industries and our being self sustain-
ing, thought the people had done well in
keeping calm during the time of excite-
ment around us. A solo by Miss Jennings
“Tis our last plea for silver" etc., was
exquisitely rendered, also a song by Mrs.
Woodrow, “A miner’s plea for silver,” was
given in excellent taste and clear deep tones.
	A severe storm coming up caused
some of the speeches that had been pre-
pared to be passed over which was very
much regretted.  Mrs. Ryan and Mrs.
Nelden both ladies of special talent had
been expected to speak.  Dr. Ellen B.
Ferguson gave a synopsis of her speech
which was a sort of review of the silver
question and history of trade and commerce
that would have been valuable to give in
full had there been time.
