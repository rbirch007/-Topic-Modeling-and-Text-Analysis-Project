	How often have we heard women re-
gretting that their school days were over,
feeling that something tangible had gone
out of their lives, that they are losing, that
they are not advancing, in fact even those
who are happily married always speak with
a marked affectionate earnestness of the
school or class association, this lack is great-
ly obviated in club life—these clube are
sometimes spoken of as “married wo-
en’s colleges”—they aim to fill a place left
vacant, as woman grows away from the
friends of her girlhood, often called, to a
new home and surroundings, away from
all that was familiar, perchance to great
responsibilities for which she may be
totally unfitted either by birth education or
training; what outlet has she for the active
forces within, that would respond to some
call for expression, some ventilation of views
or opinions that might be a positive relief
to an overburdened heart, an antidote for
loneliness, and even uplift those around
her as well as her individual self. Often
the tired mother would be glad to converse
intelligently and sympathetically on equal
terms with some sister woman, but calls are
formal, dinners and teas are stupid affairs
usually, but the club comes in where all
meet on an equal footing, not to gossip, or
to talk scandal, but for mutual benefit and
that greater good to the larger number.
	If twenty-five or thirty women, or more
or less, unite to meet regularly for mutual
improvement, even then it may happen,
that a subject is sprung that is new to them
or to the world generally, for new ideas are
generated, or new phases of thought, as
well as new discoveries made. In such cases
certainly secure for the club the most able
exponent of the subject, to lecture or speak
upon it, that the whole club may get the
best that can be brought to bear upon the 
question.
	In all clubs there are some exceptionally
bright people who enthuse the majority,
carry them forward (so to speak), yet people
brought into close communication soon get
to know each other’s capacity and to compre-
hend what each one is equal to—and one
has to be diligent as well as brilliant not to
grow commonplace in the eyes of those
who meet them regularly. The saying “a
prophet is not without honor save in his
own country and in his own house, ”is a very
true one, think of it as you will; we all
know with what enthusiasm we go to hear
some new orator, or lecturer, even though
he may not be intellectually above those
with whom we are daily associated, yet it is
different, and stimulates our energies and
faculties. A change is wholesome, the great
world of nature teems with variety; and
there is nothing more disadvantageous to
the growth of the soul than to get into a 
rut, either in individual life, or in club
life, “variety is the spice of life,” and the
club needs new wine every now and then
to infuse and effusive, giving vigor, fresh-
ness and animation; people grow weary of
sameness, and pull contrariwise sometimes,
not because they are disagreeable but they
need uplifting. A club may and often
does tire of general work planned for them
even though they have consented to it,
those who take part may not be as brilliant
in dealing with questions as had been
anticipated, and in such cases it would seem
wise and expedient to have some outside
talent brought in; no one person knows it

more, there may not be one who could tell
of actual experiences in Africa, or travels
up the Nile, or who had visited the Holy
Land, or been in East India, or a thousand
and one other things equally entertaining
and instructive. We are all of us likely to
tire of familiar themes and a new element
introduced occasionally increases the vigor
and power of development, brightens the
dull edge of wit, and sharpens the intellect.
In fact whenever a club talent introduced just
as you would send for a doctor in case of
sickness, sometimes two or three doctors
are needed; tone up the club with some-
thing strong, just as you would a patient,
apply a remedy that will be effectual, if
help can be procured.
No particular method can be laid down;
it seems to me these things must be left to
the discretion of the management, or a
committee wisely chosen, but certainly a 
club should take advantage of experts
along any line of thought, or of work in
which it is desirable to get the best and
most reliable information.
	All knowledge does not come from books,
there is the Great Teacher who inspires us all
in a greater or less degree, and none of us
know what may yet be developed in this
progressive age of the world, and whether
this new truth may be given to a sister wo-
man, or brother man, we may find our-
selves wiser, and better by living in a con-
dition to receive it. In summing up the
matter I would say outside talent may per-
haps depend too upon the financial condi-
tion of the club, that sometimes controls
larger interests in the community than club
life. To some people thoughts came pour-
ing in like great rivers, to others in little
streamlets clearer and purer perhaps, the
club may be the outlet and get the benefit
of the overflow of the great river, which
may refresh the parched soil, and the lit-
tle streamlet may, winding about here and
there, gladden many hearts with purer in-
spirations and be more desirable than the
great river.
	One great advantage of woman’s clubs is
the awakening of thought that bears fruit
in ideas, often very original ones, and per-
haps crude, but if one would not hesitate to
present these to the club, they might be
polished up and have a most brilliant set-
ting; only be sure and preserve the kernel,
or the essence of that which first came to
the individual, the seedling, dont lose the
positive thought through some sophistry of
expression. The world is moving onward
and the good time is surely coming when
we may grasp the ideal as well as the real,
instead of letting it soar away from us in
the dim distance, when we who have al-
ways longed to scale the heights inaccessi-
ble, may be uplifted above the clouds and
reaching down, help others to climb to a
place of safety above the storm and tempest,
but while we journey onward and upward,
let us clasp hands as sisters in sweet assur-
ance of helping each other to cross the
desert of ignorance and the dark river
of tradition, where the little boats of error
and superstition glide in and out alluring
the unwory. We will watch for the
beacon light of truth that shines from afar
over the Elysian fields of knowledge and
understanding. And while we pursue
our pioneer march, let us not forget the in-
numerable company that follow in our
wake, and leave for them shining waymarks,
hopeful harbingers of success in attaining
the goal of our—ambitions,
