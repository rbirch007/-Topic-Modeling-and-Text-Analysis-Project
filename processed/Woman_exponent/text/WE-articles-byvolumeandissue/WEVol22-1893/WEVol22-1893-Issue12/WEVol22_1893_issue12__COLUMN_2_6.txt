	In 1851 the company was re-organized
under Bishop Raleigh as president, and
named the “Deseret Dramatic Association.”
In 1852 the historic “Social Hall” was
built and opened by the association, with
the performance of Buhver’s classical play
of the “Lady of Lyons.” A splendid or-
chestra under the direction of Professor
Ballo contributed to the eclat of the oc-
casion. The association now numbered
among its members, John T. Caine, David
McKenzie, D. O. Calder, W. C. Dunbar,
Henry Maiben and others; and with this
added strength were enabled to cast first
class plays, but after a few years the Utah
war broke up for a time both the theatrical
performances and the Dramatic Association
itself.

	It was just at the outbreak of the civil war
in America that the theatrical history pro-
per of our city commenced.
	The Utah war was a memory of the past,
Camp Floyd was evacuated, all in Zion
was peace, for though the nation was en-
gaged in war neither Utah nor California
had the honor of taking part in it. In 1861,
our citizens saw a colossal building in pro-
cess of erection and it was known that
Brigham Young designed to give the Mor-
mons a great theater, one of the best in
America, and one that in its appointments
and accessories should bear favorable com-
parison with the theaters of the East
	The Salt Lake Theater was opened to the
public on March 6th 1862, and stands a
monument of our Rocky Mountain civili-
zation, emulating in many respects its
prototype, the Old Globe Theater of Lon-
don which under Shakespeare’s manage-
ment, voiced the dramatic genius of a new
epoch. 
	The ceremony of the dedication of the
new theater, was the remarkable event of
the opening, and nothing in the history of
the English and American stage is so
unique in its object and sentiment. Re-
served seats were placed before the curtain
for the First Presidency and officials of the
Church, and at the appointed hour Brigham
Young as president of the Dramatic As-
sociation called the house to order. The
choir sang the hymn “Lo, on the mountain
tops appearing,” and President Daniel H.
Wells offered the dedication prayer in which
he imploud the blessing of the most High
upon the building and all its appointments,
and upon the actors and actresses and all
who perform in it, and dedicating the whole
to virtue, sobriety, and excellence, for the
glory of God and the prosperity and good
of this people. After the prayer the or-
chestra and choir performed the “Star
Spangled Banner” and the President gave
an impressive address on the true mission of
the drama. Other addresses followed inter-
spersed with songs and music.
	Not in the whole history of the stage,
ancient or modern, was ever a theater thus
endowed as a sacred dramatic temple for the
people. Probably Brigham Young at that
time had never heard the text of the play
of Hamlet, yet he described the object of
the drama as it was designed by him for the
Salt Lake Theater very much like the spirit
and exposition of Hamlet to the players;
“The purpose of playing, whose end, both
at the first, and now, was, and is, to hold
as ’twere the mirror up to nature; to show
virtue her own feature, scorn her own im-
age, and the very age and body of the
time, his form and pressure,” and it may
be regarded as one of the strange things of
dramatic history that Brigham Young, a
man of no art culture but what was self
evolved, the high priest of a despised
church, should have lifted the theater to the
conception of the great high priests of
dramatic art, and given it its proper place
alongside the church, as one of the great
teachers of humanity, as powerful in its in-
fluence on the intellectual, as the church is
on the spiritual development of society.

	Before the opening of the second season, 
the veteran actor Mr. T. A. Lyne was sent
for by his former pupil, Manager Clawson,
and engaged as tutor to the company. Dur-
ing this season the following fine plays were
given. "The Honeymoon" with John T.
Caine as Duke Aranza, Phil Margetts as
the Mock Duke; "Virginus" with Bernard
Snow in the title role; "Damon and
Pythias" with Lyne as Damon, and "Pizarro"
with John T. Caine, Lyne, J. F. Simmons
Mrs. Marian Bowring in the cast. Later
on, Lyne played Virginius and again Da-
mon, in which he has been acknowledged
to have had no equal in America, excepting
Forrest himself, then as Shylock in the
"Merchant of Venice" gave a fine exposi-
tion of "the jew that Shakespeare drew"
in which Edmund Kean, won the scepter of
the English stage. In the third season
1863-4 the Irwins reigned and gave a great
variety of plays, being well supported by
the home company. In the "Colleen
Bawn" Dave McKenzie scored a triumph
as Danny Mann; and his impersonation has
never been excelled on the Salt Lake stage.
	In 1864 Mr. George Pauncefort, an ac-
complished English actor, with Mrs.
Florence Bell, came to Salt Lake City, and
alternated his light classics against T. A.
Lyne's grander, statelier parts of the old 
school, giving to the public a pleasing 
variety of the old legitimate and new legi-
timate class of plays. This refined and
finished artist gave as his opening play
"The Romance of a Poor Young Man" in 
which he wrought out one of the most ac-
complished and natural works of dramatic
art. Lyne followed the next night with
the "Merchant of Venice" and the two 
artists held the boards alternately during
the season of 1864-5. Among the plays
given during this engagement were "Mac-
beth," "Hamlet," "Don Cesar de Bazan,"
"Belphegor," "Damon and Pythias," and
under the stimulus afforded by such finish-
ed performances, the stock company at-
tained to much greater excellence and were
capable of executing star plays of the 
second class. The queen of the Salt Lake
stage was undoubtedly Julia Dean Hayne,
who made her debut in Salt Lake City in
1865 in the play of "Camille" a represen-
tation that stirred the heart of the Theater
going public of the city to profound hom-
age to her genius and art. So cordially did
she reciprocate this kind feeling, that she
remained here nearly two years, swaying
the scepter over the local company and
educating them by her brilliant example.
Her repertoire included "Medea" "Lucretia
Borgia" "Lady Macbeth" "School for 
Scandal" "Parthenia" in "Ingomar" "Lady
of Lyons" "Evadne" and other plays of a 
similar class, every one of which in her
hands were works of the highest dramatic
art.
