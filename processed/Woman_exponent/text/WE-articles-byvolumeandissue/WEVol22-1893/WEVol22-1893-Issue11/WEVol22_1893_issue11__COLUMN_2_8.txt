	It has been said that the Civil War of
America was more extended in area and
more destructive of life and property, than
any other recorded in history. It is
estimated that one million men were
sacrificed, that the burden of the nation in
loss and debt was $6,000,000,000, and that
it gave freedom to about 4,000,000 slaves.
After the terrible convulsions produced by
the war, the powers of the government
took steps to bring about a reorganization,
political, social and industrial.
	It was a critical period of the nation’s
history, and required a master mind to
stand at the head. It was not long before the
people became convinced that those neces-
sary elements of character to bring about
the results desired were not possessed by
President Johnson; indeed his administra-
tion forms a lamentable page in our nation’s
history. Almost immediately after taking
the reins of government he took issue with
Congress, usurped its authority, and showed
conclusively that though he had been
elected Vice-President by the Republican
party, his sympathies were with the South,
and he worked and ruled in the interests of
the Confederates. Early in the year 1865,
Congress adopted the XIII amendment to
the Constitution, which was as follows:
“Neither slavery nor involuntary servitude,
except as a punishment for crime, whereof
the party shall have been duly convicted,
shall exist within the United States, or any
place subject to their jurisdiction,”
The President opposed this, his plan being
to restore to the Confederate States their
former position in the Union, without any
provision for securing to the freedmen the
right to the exercise of citizenship, and he
labored secretly and underhandedly for
that object. It was well for the nation that
there were many loyal, honest and de-
termined men in the Congress who would
stand firm to their convictions of justice
and right, notwithstanding the President’s
opposition. Measures had been going on
for perfecting peaceful relations among the
whole people of the Republic, but it
made very unsatisfactory progress, as the
President would veto nearly every bill
passed by Congress, and they would then
reconsider it and pass it over his veto, by a
two-thirds majority. Matters even went so
far that there was a convention held at
Philadelphia on the 14th of August, 1866, 
of the insurrectionists, their object being to
form a new party, with President Johnson
as their leader; but there was such a discor-
dant element there that the scheme utterly
failed. Soon after that another convention
was held composed of loyal men from the


South and representative men from the
north. These men supported the measure
adopted by Congress, and succeeded in un-
doing much of the mischief done by the
President and former convention. In the
spring of 1868 the President’s conduct be-
came so disgraceful that he was finally im-
peached of high crimes and misdemeanors,
and he was ordered to answer the impeach-
ment. On the 30th of March 1868, the
trial was begun, and owing to many wise
men of the Senate counseling moderation,
the President was acquitted by one vote.
	The forgoing items have been gleaned from
histories written by northern men, and pos-
sibly they have exaggerated Prest. Johnson’s
actions, he being born a southerner, had
naturally greater sympathy with the
southern party than Congress, which was
composed of northern men, could have.
	In 1867, the peninsula of Alaska was
purchased from Russia for $7,200,000.
The value of General Grant’s military
services in behalf of the Republic and his
patriotism in assisting to defeat the designs
of President Johnson at the Capital had
won the confidence of the loyal people, and
in June 1868, the Republican National Con-
vention at Chicago, nominated him for
President of the United States, with
Schuyler Colfax as Vice Presideut; and they
were elected by an overwhelming majority
in November.
