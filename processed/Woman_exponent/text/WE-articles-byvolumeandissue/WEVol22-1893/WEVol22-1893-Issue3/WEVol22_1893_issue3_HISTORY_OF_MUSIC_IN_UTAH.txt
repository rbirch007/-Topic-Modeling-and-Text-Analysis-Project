HISTORY OF MUSIC IN UTAH.
		DR. ELLEN B. FERGUSON.
	IN THE growth of civilization among
nations, as well as in the settlement and
opening up of new countries and territories,
music is the first outgrowth of the arts,
taking precedence in the unfolding of social
development of  every genius but that of
poetry. The peculiar form of expression
in music is also an index of national char-
acter. The pioneer settlers who traversed
the plains and crossed the Rocky Moun-
tains to make homes in the valley of the
Great Salt Lake, were certain in the early
stage of their peculiar civilization to mani-
fest the genius of music. Being so emi-
nently religious in their tone of character,
music would naturally form a very impor-
tant part of their worship, and the hosts of
Israel beguiled many an hour of their
weary march across the Continent by sing-
ing the songs of Zion.*
	The first musical organization formed in
Utah, was that of the Nauvoo Brass Band,
a company of fifteen musicians under the
leadership of Captain William Pitt, who
starting from Nauvoo with the first com-
pany of the pioneers, traveled as far as
Grand River, where [---] [--
stances compelled them to remain and earn
sufficient means to enable them to continue
their journey to the mountains.
	In 1850, most of the members of the
band having reached Salt Lake City, they
once more re-organized under Captain Pitt,
and around them cluster the first musical
reminiscences of the Mormon people. For
two years the band discoursed sweet melo-
dies on every anniversary and local cele-
bration, gave concerts, and assisted the
first dramatic association in its representa-
tions. In 1851, Dominico Ballo, an Italian,
highly endowed with the musical genius of
his race, came to Salt Lake City, having
been for a number of years previously
bandmaster at West Point. He was one
of the best clarionetists in the United
States, a fine composer and leader, and his
name is still held in reverence by many of
the old pioneers, who will tell you with a
sigh and shake of the head, that “we have
never had any music out here since Ballo
died.”
	In tone, style and execution, he was
unrivalled, and of all the excellent clarionet
soloists who have visited this city with
Gilmore’s band, and other military combi-
nations, not one has been able to produce
the tone that Ballo could draw from his
instrument. Soon after his arrival he or-
ganized Ballo’s band of twenty instru-
ments, viz, seven B flat clarionets, one E
flat played by himself, two piccolos, two
first cornet, two second cornets, one oph-
icleide, the bass horns, one tenor trombone
and drums. This combination created
great excitement on its first appearance and
continued to gain power and renown by
its skilful rendition of some of the most
difficult compositions of the day. Later
he assumed the directorship of the theatre
orchestra in the Social Hall, composing or
[Column 2]
arranging all the music used by the com-
pany for several years. In 1855, Ballo’s
Hall was erected by the band for practice
and concert purposes, and is still one of the
old landmarks of the city. In 1861 Ballo’s
health failed, having played the clarionet
for forty years, he had passed beyond the
limit usually alloted to the lives of per-
formers on wind instruments, and on Sep-
tember 8th, he was found dead in the
yard, having instantly expired from apo-
plexy or heart disease. The band he had
labored so hard to perfect, played at his
funeral, but did not long remain together
after his death. But few of his composi-
tions are extant. The best known were,
an arrangement of the duet, “Hear me
Norma,” for flute and clarionet, with
orchestra accompaniment;Governor Young’s
Grand March; Governor Young’s Quick-
step; General Wells’ Grand March, and
the orchestral parts to the old hymn, “O
my Father.”
	In 1853, David O. Calder, the pioneer
class teacher of vocal music came to Salt
Lake City and settled over Jordan, where 
he taught the first singing school in the
Territory. In 1861 he organized two
classes of two hundred members each, for
instruction in Curven tonic sol-fa method,
[---] the first introduction of the
system in America. He compiled, arranged,
and printed the class books used. In 1862
Mr. Calder organized the Deseret Musical
Association, composed of two hundred
picked singers, from the different classes
under his tuition. This society gave sev-
eral concerts in the theater and Tabernacle
with marked success, and during its exis-
tence stimulated musical culture among
the people of Utah. Mr. Calder subse-
quently established the first musical busi-
ness in Utah, and in connection with
Professor Careless, imported a large num-
ber of pianos, organs, brass and string
instruments, and a due supply of the best
music published in Europe and America.
	In 1862, Professor Charles J. Thomas,
who had for years been associated with
some of the principal theatre orchestras in
London, came to Salt Lake City and at
once took charge of the orchestra in the
new Salt Lake Theater, then just opened,
and under his leadership it maintained for
several years a high standard of excellence. 
	As conductor of the Tabernacle Choir,
Professor Thomas did some excellent work, 
and he long held a ruling musical position.
Professor John Tullidge also deserves men-
tion at this time, as fine tenor singer and
composer. His name will be remembered
as long as his anthem “How beautiful upon
the Mountains’’ delights the ears of a Salt
Lake audience.
	The man who did most for the early
musical progress of Salt Lake City, and the
establishment of the legitimate profession
is undoubtedly Professor George Careless.
Born in London, and trained with some of
the greatest instrumentalists of the day,
under the batons of such masters as Sir
Michael Costa, Sir Jules Bendict and other
famous conductors. Mr. Careless brought
to Salt Lake City in 1865, such musical


[Column 1]
genius, brilliant execution and talent for
leadership, as produced a complete revolu-
tion in musical circles, and marks an epoch
in the development of the divine art that
led to the attainment of the present high
standard of musical taste and culture in
this community.
	During the years that he held the leader-
ship of the theater orchestra, he produced a
mumber of musical plays, including Mac-
beth, The Brigand, Aladdin and Cinder-
alla—for the two latter he composed the
entire music, consisting of solos, duets,
choruses and dramatic interludes.
	During this engagement he conducted
the first opera given in Utah, the “Grand
Duchess,” by the Howson Troupe. In
1875 the great musical event in the city,
was the performance of the oratorio of the
Messiah, by the Philharmonic,Society,under
the training and conductorship of Professor
Careless, with over two hundred performers
and a full orchestra. The performance was
a great musical triumph, and was pronoun-
ced by the critics of the day, a presentation
far superior both in its vocal and orchestral
excellence to the performance of the same
oratorio in San Francisco, with Madam
Anna Bishop and other vocal celebrities in
the principal parts. In the instrumenta-
tion, the first violins, and the cornet obliga-
to of Mr. Mark Croxall, were particularly
fine, while among the vocalists Mrs. and
Miss Haydon, Messrs. Williams, Black and
Hollister, deserve particular mention. But
the palm of excellence belonged without
any question to Mrs. Careless, the wife of
Professor Careless, whose rendition of “I
know that my Redeemer liveth” was sim-
ply perfection. Her pure sweet bell-like
tones, and her exquisite delivery, intense
with feeling [illegible line do to page fold] selection to
the exalted pitch of epic song. The fact
that this oratorio could be executed in such
perfection by a local association, and call
out a high class audience fully capable of
appreciating such music proves that, as has
been often affirmed, Salt Lake City even
then, was one of the greatest musical cities
of the world,a pre-eminence that her subse-
quent history proudly maintains.
	Professor Careless also conducted the
celebrated Parepa Rosa concerts in 1868,
the Anna Bishop concert in the Tabernacle,
and the grand Wilhelm concerts in the
theater in 1880, and received from the great
virtuoso the highest praise and many marks
of personal esteem.
	In 1879, Professor Careless gave Sullivan’s
opera of “Pinafore” and later “The Mi-
kado,’’both brilliant successes,and rendered
exclusively by home talent. In 1885, he
succeeded in organizing the largest local
orchestra ever brought together in this city,
consisting of forty-five members. During
his partnership in the musical business with 
Mr. David O. Calder, the firm published
the “Salt Lake Musical Times.” the first
musical publication in the Rocky Moun-
tains, and under his leadership of fourteen
years, the musical services by the Taber-
nacle choir reached their crowning excel-
lence. To Mrs. Lavinia Careless, the lam-
ented wife of Professor George Careless,
belongs by right of her rare genius, the
highest niche of fame among our musical
stars. With a voice of phenomenal purity
and resonance, she so educated Salt Lake
City audiences to quality and sublimity of
music, that only a Patti could sing here
without being adversely compared with
her. Her rendition of oratorio solos was
simply exquisite; and no heart could listen

[Column 2]
unmoved to the glorious strains of that
angelic voice, too early called from earth to
heaven. Her sudden demise in 1885, left
a vacant place in our galaxy of stars that
has not yet been filled. Among our local
musicians who have done much to develop
musical taste both by education and talent,
may be mentioned Professor Jos. J. Daynes
who has presided at the Tabernacle organ
since 1867, with the exception of a few
months spent in New York under the
tuition of Mr. G. W. Morgan, also Orson
Pratt, an able teacher of the piano, organ,
harmony and counterpoint.
	In giving the musical history of Salt
Lake City, we must not omit reference to
the grand organ in the Tabernacle, the
building of which was commenced in 1866,
when all the materials necessary had to be
obtained from the east and hauled across
the plains in wagons. The architect and
designer of this magnificent instrument
was Mr. Joseph Ridges, who worked on it
for several years, but to Mr. Johnson
belongs the credit of bringing its present
perfect completion. It has now four mam-
als and pedal, fifty-seven stops, and two
thousand six hundred and forty-eight
pipes, supplied with wind by three large
bellows, operated by two hydraulic motors.
In its completed form, situated in one of
the most perfect acoustic buildings in the
world, it is justly an object of pride to our
city, and the admiration of tourists. Al-
most equal to it is the organ in the new
Congregational Church, and beside these
the pipe organ in St. Mark’s Cathedral, and
and in the Assembly Hall, are very fine
instruments and above the average.
	When Professor Careless resigned the
conductorship of the Philharmonic Society,
Professor Radcliffe came to Salt Lake City
and took the vacant position, and a year
later gave the Creation in the theatre with
marked success. Professor Radcliffe is a
great organist, and his recitals given on the
Tabernacle organ prove his complete mas-
tership of that noble instrument.
	Among our noted violin solists, Mr.
Willard Weihe stands in the front rank. A
protegee of Ole Bull, for whom he played
at the early age of ten years, he had by
force of his genius and perseverance worked
himself up to a very high position, and
under the tuition of the celebrated Vieux-
temps, developed a marvelous technique
that has been rarely equaled except by a
virtuoso. A long list of performers on
piano, organ, violin and other instrnments
might be given, which together with a
number of vocalists of unusual excellence,
justify the claim that we are in Salt Lake
especially a music loving and appreciative
people, and that no Territory, and but few
States in our land can equal Utah in the
progress made in the divine art. Especially
is this the truth with regard to choral
music. Salt Lake leads with her Choral
Society three hundred in number; the Tab-
ernacle Choir four hundred and fifty in
number—the largest probably in America,
heads the list of thirty choirs belonging to
[-]he various churches in the city, average
not less than twenty voices each, making a
grand total of six hundred choristers. Add
to these the two hundred and fifty choirs in
the various towns and settlements of the
Territory, and it shows at least five thous-
and choristers taking part in divine worship
every Sunday, and if the Sunday School
children, who all take part in the song
worship of the day be counted, they will
swell the number to thirty-five thousand.

	To Professor Evan Stephens, a practical
musician and composer of considerable
native genius as well as professional study
and training is due the success of the
general movement in class teaching in the
Sunday Schools of the Territory possessed
of rare personal magnetism, a born musical
leader, with perfect choral control, he
sways the hundreds of children on the stage
with a few simple movements of his baton,
maintaining a perfect tempo with the most
exquisite variation of forte and piano. The
National Concert given in the Tabernacle
on the 22nd, Feb. 1893 was one of his
most unique efforts, and demonstrated the
possibility of a high standard of musical
training for children, thus educating a
generation of musicians who will in future
years maintain Utah’s Musical supremacy
in the Rocky Mountains.
	In the public schools, the leading day
schools, academies and colleges as well as
the University of Utah, music forms a part
of the regular curriculum of study, and
scholars of all ages enjoy in Salt Lake City
musical advantages equal to those afforded
in Boston, Chicago, or Cincinnati, the only
difference being in the number of lessons
given and not in the quality.
	There are few homes in Utah without a
piano, or organ, and brass bands are num-
erous throughout the territory, while guitar
and mandolin clubs delight the young
people and male glee clubs and quartetts
are being organized extensively. Proud
as we my justly be of our past musical
achievements and present standing, our
future possibilities are far more glorious and
with perseverance in well doing and a more
united effort among musicians and lovers of
music, it is possible for Utah to become
world famed for her unequaled musical
excellence and progress.
