			ALPINE STAKE.
	The quarterly conference of the Relief So-
ciety of the Alpine stake was held in the
2nd ward chapel American Fork, Friday,
January 29th, 1909. Stake Counselor Annie
C. Hindley presiding[-] Meeting opened at
10 a. m. with the usual exercises.
	Counselor Hindley greeted the Sisters.
Thought it the largest gathering since the
stake was organized. Extended the greet-
ings of the season in behalf of President
Standring, who had been called away to at-
tend two funerals of very dear friends. The
first time our president has been absent from
conference since the stake was organized.
Sister Hindley thought the sisters had done
well this year and the president was well
satisfied with their labors, and desired her
love to them. Invoked our Father’s blessings
would rest upon us, and that by common
consent we would hold a testimony meeting
this morning, and all who desired could take
part.
	Song, “Count your blessings,” by the con-
gregation.
	Counselor Harriet Jacobs bore her testi-
mony, and urged the sisters to not spend all
their time in temporal affairs. We must
feed our spirits and seek to overcome our
weaknesses. Set a good example to our
children and make a determination that we
will serve the Lord.
	Twenty-two sisters bore faithful, spirited
testimonies, interspersed with singing and
prayer from the congregation. The spirit
of the Lord worked so powerfully among the
sisters they could not keep their seats but
were on their feet two at a time, and it
seemed quite difficult to bring the meeting
to a close.
	Benediction by Sister E. D. Clarke.
	(Lunch was served during the noon recess.
300 sisters were present.)
	Afternoon Session: Sister Annie C. Hind-
ley presiding.
	After the opening exercises Sister Kate
Allred reported Lehi 2nd ward in good con-
dition. Have testimony meetings and les-
son meetings alternately. Have 850 bushels
of wheat on hand. Gave a report of the
Kirmess, given by the Relief Society for one
week, and had cleared something over four-
teen hundred dollars to finish paying off the
debt on the Tabernacle.
	Sister Louisa Halliday reported Pleasant
Grove. “We work unitedly and have quite
a few poor and elderly people, who feel
grateful for the help the Society gives them.
Have a new cement granary, with iron roof
and door, and feel that our grain will be safe
from mice and insects.
	Mrs. Maycock of the Agricultural College
of Logan was introduced to the sisters and
gave a lecture. Traced the growth of do-
mestic science and home economics, proving
that the educational system has been at
fault, and that professional training of girls
should be home making. They should
choose their profession early in life and all


their education should tend toward that of
home making, as ninety-nine girls out of
every hundred come to that some time in
their lives. The day is coming when we will 
realize the benefit of proper home making
even in the question of diet, if the mother
wishes to raise the standard of her family,
the community, the state and the nation,
she must look well to the diet in her home.
The boys and girls must be taught to be
home makers and home keepers. Teach
them how to cope with problems they will
meet in the homes they expect to make.
Girls should be taught to save time and
energy by knowing how to keep house prop-
erly, but to do so we must be alive, be
awake and progressive. The business man
knows that he must get out and converse
with people, read along his line of work and
be a wide awake, progressive man. The
home maker should do the same in order to
profitably use her time and energy.
	It is the duty of the mother to see that
the daughter is in full practice in all the du-
ties of home making, before she sacrifices
her time and energy to let the daughter take
so much text book and cultural work. More
household duties would be of more health-
ful benefit to the daughter and at the same
time prepare her for wife and motherhood,
and we would have fewer nervous wrecks.
Some mothers think that the daughter is not
strong and too delicate to perform the duties
of home, and so does that herself when nine
times out of ten the mother is the most un-
able, and it would be the best thing for the
daughter to drop some of her mental work
and perform more physical labor. Instead
of education preparing one for life, we should
be living the actual practical life while get-
ting this education.
	President S. L. Chipman enjoyed the spirit
of the meeting and also the ideas advanced
today. There is nothing that will bring more
comfort in the home than a little regularity
and arrangement. This is a fact in business
and it is in the home.
	We are living in an age of progress and
advancement. Our schools have been mak-
ing a mistake in training the head and not
the heart nor the hand. I hope the time is
not far distant when the training will be di-
vided between the head, heart and hand,
and make the education one of practical
work, so that our boys and girls will be
turned out practical men and women.
	Let us have our ideals and ambitions high
and work to that and reach it.
	We do not find such another organization
as the Relief Society. It is a labor of love;
so is that of the bishop. Stand by them and
do not allow any one to say anything against
them in your presence.
	Meeting closed by singing and the benedic-
tion was by President James H. Clarke.
	Conference adjourned for three months.
			EMMA J. T. ROBERTS,
							Cor. Sec.
