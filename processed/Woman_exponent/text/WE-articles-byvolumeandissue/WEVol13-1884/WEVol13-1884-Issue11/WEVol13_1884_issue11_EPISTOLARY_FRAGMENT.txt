	EPISTOLARY FRAGMENT

	I feel there is fine material in you, and there
fore my interest and sympathy is called out be-
cause, permit me to say, it will require wisdom
to properly lay it out for your own comfort
and exaltation in the Church and Kingdom of
God. Mormonism, so called, is so entirely dif-
ferent to any thing else we have met in our
former life, that we are apt to think we are in
a clear path, where soon we find we have to
walk cautiously or before we are the least
aware we are entangled in a mesh not easily to 
be disentangled. Pardon me, if you think my
words gratuitous, I have the best and most ap-
preciative feelings for you, but I am older than
you, both in age and in the Church, and I
simply give you portions of my experience and
that which I have found to answer my highest
ambition and expectation. All conquerors
have had to "stoop to conquer" in some way or
other; they have had tact, which is more than
talent; they have been close observers of their
surroundings, and deep thinkers; they have
walked cautiously, calmly, humbly, it is only
the great who can stoop, the opposite have no-
thing to stoop from, they have no platform;
people of large heart and brain have a natural
platform born with them--they are a constitu-
tion and an institution in themselves. Ponder
my words before you dissent or decide; then I
think you will realize truth in them to your
entire satisfaction.
    Let me tell you I read in you a character of
[Column 2]
no mean order--but for that very reason it will
have to be developed, slowly, cautiously, unless
some unforeseen event were to call you out. I
consider the way in which you have begun
your Mormon life in Utah is good, and you are
now sowing seed that will form a foundation
for a grand superstructure. Foundations are
always constructed out of rough, unpolished
materials, such as are powerful to endure and
bear up the superstructure. Therefore do not
be discouraged if you find these materials su-
perabundant in your present path of life; keep
your eye upon the grand edifice yet to be re-
vealed.
    Woman's sphere is extending and expand-
ing all over the world, even in obscure coun-
tries there is the same movement, but it must
needs be of slow growth because of its long
inertia and because of its mighty sequel. The
curse so heavily laid upon woman and so pas-
sively borne until a second nature has been
engendered and she believes it is to be--but
there has long been a class of women upon
whom the spirit of restoration has been work-
ing silently, unknown even to themselves in
the first movement, but nature and circum-
stances (generally severe ones) brought their
minds to a certain focus not before arrived at
by women, and they became almost impercep-
tibly leaders for their sisters in a more expan-
sive state of things; by their agency the yoke
became lighter--lighter, and now the move-
ment has become universal. Good, noble,
strong-minded men are assisting "strong-mind-
ed," but womanly women to gently but un-
sleepingly throw off the yoke of servile bondage
of long and dark ages, in which woman has
been but a "chattel" in her husband's house;
and an era of light, liberty and happiness is
dawning upon that half of God's creation
who have borne the yoke and the suffering
even to martyrdom--martyrdom of maternity
added to the list--all these long, dark centur-
ies, and she will awake one fine morning and
rise up an unfettered being, bound only by the
law of God and her own pure nature; those
awful words and their still more awful mean-
ing: "Thy desire shall be to thy husband and
hs shall rule over thee," will have been can-
celled, because the curse is fulfilled and the
judge opens the door and bids the captive go
free, and she walks forth a free, unfettered being
as in her primal advent in the garden of Eden
--she will then feel her dignified responsibility
and will lay aside the frivolity that has so often
disfigured her, and which has generally arisen
from the very feeling that somehow she was but
an outlaw in her Father's house, where He
had intended her to live as the free, unfettered
co-partner of her brother, man. In a great
measure she is to be her own agent in this re-
form movement, always seeking and having the
Spirit of her God to guide and direct her.
There are, and there have always been women
who have led out in this effort of restoration of
God's daughters to their primal position on
His earth--their names are famed in history's
page, though probably they understood not the
spirit then agitating their minds, but they were
loosening the bands setting "gates ajar," till in
our own era we joyfully note names that would
fill a catalogue--to numerous to mention,
and no need to name them--all leading minds
know them as household words, all these have
been helpers in the restoration of woman to
her primeval grandeur of character and posi-
