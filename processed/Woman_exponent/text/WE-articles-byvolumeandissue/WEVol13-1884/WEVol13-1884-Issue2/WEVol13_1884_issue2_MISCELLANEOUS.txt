MISCELLANEOUS.

	Henry Carey, a cousin to Queen Elizabeth,
after having enjoyed her majesty’s favor for
several years, lost it in this manner: As he was
walking in the garden of the palace, under the
queen’s window, she asked him, in a jocular
manner, "What does a man think when he is
thinking of nothing?" The answer was a very
brief one. "Upon a woman’s promise," he re-
plied. "Well done, cousin," said Elizabeth,
"excellent!" Some time after he solicited the
honor of a peerage, and reminded the queen
that she had promised it to him. "True," said
her majesty, "but that was a woman’s prom-
ise."—Ex.

	Maud—Isn't it dreadful, Julia, about the
banks, and Wall Street, and stocks, and every-
thing of that sort? Julia—Just shocking!
Politics are such dreadful things. Maud—And
don’t you know I have been worried to death
about pa! Julia—Why? Does your pa own
a bank? Maud—No: but he’s got lots to do
with mortgages and speculations and that sort
of things, and I just know that when I ask him
to get a new gold-plated set of harness for
Kitty, he’s going to look up with that awfully
solemn look and say, "My daughter, do you
read the papers?"—Washington Hatchet.
