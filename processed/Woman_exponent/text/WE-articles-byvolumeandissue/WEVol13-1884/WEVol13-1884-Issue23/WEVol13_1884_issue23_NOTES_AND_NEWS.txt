NOTES AND NEWS.

	Mrs. Mason, once the wife of Charles Sum-
mer, has taken up a permanent residence in
Rome.
	The Empress Eugenie has written a book up-
on the various recollections of her life, which
will be issued immediately.
	Mrs. Helen M. Gougar will contribute an
article to the New Era for May, descriptive
of her recent journeyings in Cuba.
	A son of Charles Dickens is a captain in the
Canadian mounted police, and is in command
of a post threatened by Riel's rebel forces.
	The "Woman's Realm" department of the
Pittsburg, Commercial Gazette, ably edited by
Matilda Hindman, is widely quoted with friend-
ly comments.
	Mrs. Elizabeth Cady Stanton and Bishop
Spalding debate in the May number of the
North American Review the question "Has
Christianity Benefited Woman?"
	Mrs. Rosa H. Thorpe, the author of the popu-
lar "Curfew Must Not Ring To-Night," resides
at San Antonio. Texas, and talks of going to
California to live.
	Jenny Lind has made a conditional promise
to give a concert next summer in Norwich, Eng-
land, in aid of the Children's Infirmary there,
of which she is the founder.
	The Ohio Hospital for Women and Chil-
dren at Cincinnati was established three years
ago, through the efforts of two women physi-
cians, Ella N. Kirk and Martha May Howells.
	"In 1882 there was a majority of five against
the woman suffrage bill in the New York As-
sembly; in 1884, a majority of four. This year
there was a majority of one in its favor, but
it did not receive the 64 votes necessary to pass
any bill."
