GRAHAM BREAD.
			GUNNISON, 1884.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	The reason why Graham bread is not more extensive-
ly used, is perhaps because so few of us know how to
make it taste so good that we can let it take the place
of white or fine flour bread or at least a place on our ta-
bles.  While I do not presume to be able to tell my sis-
ters the beneficial effect of graham bread on the systems
of our bodies, twenty years of its constant use in our
family may perhaps entitle the following recipe to a
trial among our enterprising housekeepers, the readers
of the EXPONENT:
	Mix in warm water, cold in summer, with yeast the
first time (after which always says about a teacupful of
the dough, which is an infallible rising, and does not
sour the bread). Do not bake immediately after the
dough is up.  In working to put it up in the drippers do
not use one speck of dry flour.  Put on the dough a
teacupful or more of water, cut the dough loose from
the sides of the pan and work it until it does not ad-
here to the hands or pan.  Do not try to mould it into
loaves, but turn it in one bulk from the pan into the
dripper; put immediately into a hot oven and bake for an
hour and a half;  then let the oven gradually cool down
for one hour longer, when it may be taken out.  It is
very essential that it should be baked in this way, as ab-
solutely nothing else can impart such a fine flavor to
the bread.			HELENA MADSEN.
