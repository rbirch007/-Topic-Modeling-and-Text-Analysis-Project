IN MEMORIAM.

	On Sunday morning, at seven o’clock, Nov. 16, 1884,
after two days’ illness, of membranous croup, Lewis
Burnham, son of James P. and Mary A. Freeze passed
quietly to his welcome rest, aged five years, five months
and seven days.
	“Little Lou” was an unusually bright, beautiful and
gifted child, around whom the fondest hopes and expec-
tations were centred; he had suffered much from lung
complaint the past three years, during which time he
had received in vision glimpses of a better and brighter
life, which seemed to have robbed him of the desire to.
remain in this; and he said he wanted to die and be an
angel. His loss makes a deep void in hearts which,
though resigned to say “Thy will, not mine,” can never
be filled till the resurrection morn gives back its jewels
—but
“We shall shortly know that lengthened breath
	Is not the sweetest gift God sends His friend;
And that sometimes the sable pall of death
	Conceals the fairest bloom His love can send.
And we shall see that while we frown and sigh,
	God’s plans go on as best for yon and me;
How, when we called he heeded not our cry,
	Because His wisdom to the end can see."	L
