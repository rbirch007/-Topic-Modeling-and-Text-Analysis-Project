THE CHRISTMAS GIFT.

The Christmas had come and was over,
And ev'ry one, great and small,
Had rejoiced in that full, glad household
From morn till the twilight fall.
And the children had said in the evening
"There wasn't another thing
To fill up their measure of gladness
Old Santa himself could bring."

So, in the sweetest of "good night" kisses,
Just after the prayers were said,
They laid aside ever treasure
And hastened away to bed;
And soon every light-hearted darling
Was so far away in dreams,
They never came back till the sunshine
Wakened them all with his beams.

And while still dimly recalling
The joys of the day before,
Auntie came in with the tidings
Of one precious treasure more.
"Now rise and come quickly to welcome
the Sweet one that's waiting here,
For rarest and best of all presents,
This new little brother dear."

They whispered a moment together,
When Auntie had shut the door,
Framing a grave "resolution"
Against the glad tidings she bore.
Then they hurriedly dressed, and the whispers,
Grave winks and shakes of the head—
That not even cook could interpret—
Or gather a word that they said—

With three year old Johnnie between them,
Each holding his dimpled hand,
And he looking wisely in league with
What he could not understand,
They started, paused, whispered, proceeded,
Then Mabel herself made bold
To knock at the door, which nurse opened,
Strange enough sight to behold.

Fresh, tidy and sweet, not a smile, though,
Illuminated either dear one's cheek;
But all frowned straight at Auntie,
Waiting for Mabel to speak;
And, hand in hand, all three together,
Set nurse's head in a whirl;
"We're not going to kiss the baby,
Because it isn't a girl!"

And then, while the embassy waited,
Grandma to the rescue came,
To treat with the stern deputation
The message they had proclaimed.
"What! not when the dear little baby
Was sent to us Christmas night,
On the birthday of our dear Savior,
To meet with so cruel a slight?"

Then over the sweet little faces
A wav'ring expression came;
As their glances met, there followed
The rose red color of shame.
"I guess," said the little Mabel, then,
"I guess it wouldn't be right,
For it might be His little brother
That was sent to us last night.

"And oh!  just to think, his birthday's
The happiest day of all!
But he came too late for us to hang
His stocking upon the wall."
"And who'll divide," said grandma, "With
The baby Santa Claus missed,
That came in the stormy night, and then
Was not to be even kissed?"

Then swift to be first to answer her,
Each repentant darling rushed
And shouted, "I;" and they clasped her so
Her ribbons and kerchief were crushed.

Ah, sweetest of stories that e'er were told!
"The Babe of Bethlehem" lives
And lights in ev'ry heart, the love
That pities and shields and forgives.
	AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON.
December, 1884.
