TIN WEDDING.

	A few of the especial friends of Mrs. Reba
B. Pratt secured some tin valuables and a
hamper each of the good things which maketh
the heart glad and took to their buggies and
rode out on Sunday to the farm of the well-
known stock man, Mr. Alma Pratt.
	Mrs. Pratt has been an invalid for years,
and unable to visit and enjoy the society she
is so well capable, mentally, of adorning. She
was President of the Y. L. M. I. A. of Farm-
er's Ward until her health enforced her resigna-
tion. After the arrival of the guests, and
wishing her "plenty of tin forever," she repaid
us by reading a touching poem she had lately
written, portraying her appreciation of her life
on earth, and also most beautifully her resigna-
tion to await the Father's call, and the joy of
the kiss of the loved ones on the other bright
shore. A fear of sympathy glistened the
eye of every listener, and each heart felt
strengthened and edified in the contemplation
of the beauty and grandeur of her "blessed
hope of the future."
	Dinner was announced, and all did ample
justice, after the ride through the sharp, frosty
air, to the many inviting and palatable dishes.
After dinner we again surrounded the center
of social interest and sympathy, the invalid,
when she was the favored recipient of many
precious blessings and promises out of the ful-
ness of some great hearts who have borne the
heat of the day and the brunt of the battle
faithfully, and whom the Lord delights to own
and bless and answer their prayers.
	The sinking sun admonished us to hasten
to our warm home firesides before "jack frost"
became absolute sovereign of our frozen valley.
When we kissed our pale friend "good bye"
she said, "I am so glad you all came, for it has
made me feel better;" and our hearts answered,
"'tis sweet to comfort the sick."
