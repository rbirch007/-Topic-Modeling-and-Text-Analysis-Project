SEEDTIME AND HARVEST.

In springtime a dearly loved friend and I
Planted seeds in hope of the blooms by and bye;
We planted and planned day after day,
Not in joy of the work, but in hope alway.
Ere the summer came my dear friend was gone
Forever from earth, and I, alone,
Tended and watched for the flowers to bloom— 
I gathered and laid them upon her tomb.

A mansion was builded, strong and fair,
And finished and furnished with greatest care;
A child lit a match—a fire was made,
And the grand new house in ashes laid.
The man, in building, spent all his wealth,
And the work and worry ruined his health—
By a child's little act so much undone,
The work of years in a moment gone.

I noticed a neighbor over the way,
So hopefully busy day after day,
Fashioning garments dainty and neat,
A smile on her face tender and sweet.
One morning I looked—crape on the door
Told me I'd watch the worker no more;
White crape and black—child and mother gone,
The dainty, neat garments would never be worn.

Two little birds in an old apple tree
Builded a nest just as nice as could be,
What became of the nest no one could tell,
Nor what cruel fate to the birds befell;
So blithesome and happy, the nest was begun,
Singing so cheerful till it was done,
Then when ,twas ready, all was o'er,
The little birds came to the nest no more.

So it hath been and will ever be,
None can tell the future for you or me;
Our flowers of hope so often bloom,
And are gathered only to deck a tomb.
Let's joy in improving life while we may,
Not worry and work for a future day;
Our future is with a Higher Power,
Though we build, or make, or but plant a flower.
		E. S. D.
Wyoming, O.
