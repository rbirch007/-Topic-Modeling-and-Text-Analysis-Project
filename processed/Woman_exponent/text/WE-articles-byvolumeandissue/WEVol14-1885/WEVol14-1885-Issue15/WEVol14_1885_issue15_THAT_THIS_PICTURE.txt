THAT (THIS) PICTURE.
	The sun is setting in a mass of dark clouds
that threaten to pour down, drenching us and
spoiling the sugar cane now over-ripe in the
fields.
	The little valley is filled with rice fields, tended
by dirty Chinamen. The fruit trees may be never
so heavily loaded, they bear nothing for me.
	The grass huts may look picturesque now, but
one doesn't hanker to go inside, for they look un-
tidy. From the white cottage at my feet, a woman
comes out with blowsy head and her one article of
clothing, her dress, tucked clear to her waist.
	The grass is the shelter and home of thousands
of big centipedes.
	As the sun lowers, the frisky mosquito sails
hummingly round my head. I go out for a breath
of salt air, and admire the sea in the distance,
wishing the distance was a deal less, for it is too
far for me to walk down there.
	The natves go home, shouting to each other in
their harsh loud unmusical voices, making the
smooth vowels of their language sound like the
gutterals of the German.
	They commence their everlasting practice over
at the Mission House, and I am glad to go in to
get away from the darkness and noise.
	Inside, I step very carefully, for fear of disturb-
ing a wandering scorpion or centipede.
	If I feel at all lonely, I have plenty of company
in mice, rats, scorpions, centipedes, cockroaches,
fleas, mosquitoes, lizards, and millions of ants.
	All these are not visitors, but co-residents of my
little 12 foot square room. The neighbors' chil-
dren whine sleepily, the dogs bark lustily, the lamp
burns dimly, and thus commences another evening
away from home and Utah.
