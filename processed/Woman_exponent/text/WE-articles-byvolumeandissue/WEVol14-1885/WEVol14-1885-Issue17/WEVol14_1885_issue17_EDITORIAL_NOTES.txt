EDITORIAL NOTES.

	The Territorial Legislature is meeting, as ex-
pected, with determined hostility on the part of
the Governor. He has vetoed two very impor-
tant measures which passed both houses, and in
one of his messages took occasion to add insult to
injury. The legislators, however, are calm and un-
disturbed and so far treat his slanders with digni-
fied silence.
	The new Edmnnds Bill which will be found on
another page, was introduced Dec. 8, 1885 and
passed the Senate on the 8th of January, 1886 by
a vote of 38 to 7. The latter were Senators Blair
of New Hampshire, Call of Florida, Gibson of
Louisiana, Hampton of South Carolina, Morgan
of Alabama, Vance of North Carolina and Hoar
of Massachusetts. Mr. Hoar opposed the infa-
mous measure because of the section depriving the
women of Utah of the suffrage; outside of that
portion he was in favor of the bill. The other
six whom we have named opposed it unquali-
fiedly and would under no consideration give
sanction to so palpable a scheme of robbery and
oppression.
	The following letter to the Editor of the EX-
PONENT, written Jan. 2, by Lillie Devereux Blake,
Prest. of the New York State Woman Suffrage
Association, we publish, for the present, without
comment: "At a meeting of citizens, held under
the auspices of the New York State Woman Suf-
frage Association, at No. 54, Union Place, on
Saturday, Jan. 2, the following resolution was
unanimously passed:
	Resolved; That while approving of the main
object of the bill to suppress polygamy, introduced
in the Senate of the United States by Senator
Edmunds, of Vt., we earnestly protest against the
clause which proposes to disfranchise all the
women of the Territory, whether innocent or
guilty of the crime of plural marriage, as an un-
just discrimination against the class most op-
pressed by this cruel institution, and call upon
Senators Blair of N. H., and Palmer of Ind.,
and other fair-minded men to oppose the passage
of this clause of the bill."
