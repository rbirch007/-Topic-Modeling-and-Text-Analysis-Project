	MISSIONARY EXPERIENCES.
			(Continued.)
	Another instance was that of a young sis-
ter who was afflicted with a white swelling
on her knee, about the size of an English
loaf. The Elders asked me to go to her and
give her a blessing, which I did, after the ad-
ministration. She was quite alarmed as she

[Column 2]
could not stand or walk. On visiting her
the next day, I found her well, walking
around. She was then a faithful Saint; af-
terwards she was married out of the Church,
relying on his promise that he would not in-
terfere with her in her religion. At once
after her marriage he forbade her to go to
the meetings or recognize the elders when
she met them. The result is she has left the
Church.
	Before leaving for London, I dreamed
one night that I was ill, I went home; but as
soon as I reached there I was well. Then I
grieved that I had not remained longer, and
resolved to return and finish my mission. So
I was busy packing my trunk, my two
daughters assisting me. As we eagerly
discussed where this thing should be placed,
and that, our hands touched as we laid them
in. Then I awoke, and found myself alone
in my little room in Sittingbourne. And
how glad I was that I was there!
	Then a letter came from Aunt Em. “Do
not get discouraged, Lydia,” she wrote;
“lady missionaries are an experiment now,
and I want you to adapt yourself to condi-
tions; do not get discouraged and come
home.”
	“O Aunt Em,” I thought, “if you could
only see some of the conditions!” Neverthe-
less I was grateful for remembrance and
encouragement. I enjoyed walking through
the fields and green lanes of England, as
we went to meeting or visited the Saints.
Often as we walked we sang, “Ye who are
called to labor,” and “Though deepening
trials throng your way, Press on, press on ye
Saints of God.” How beautiful the hymn
sounded, echoing and re-echoing, near and
far the songs of Zion always inspiring,
seemed doubly so now.
	London once more, and a most refreshing
conference. We met friends as well as
Elders from Zion whom we were rejoiced to
meet again. Some of the missionaries were
transferred to other fields, some re-appoint-
ed. I was among the later. I was glad for 
I had made friends where I stayed. It was
not that the dear woman (a Catholic) had
not been subjected to disagreeable things,
neighbor even going to her husband, telling
him of what was said about the Mormons.
But they withstood it all, and allowed me to
remain, rejoicing when I came back to them.
I had only just gotten down to work again
when disquieting letters began to arrive
from home. My eldest son was ill, his baby
also. Other letters stated things were quite
critical, and I decided to go to London, so
as to get the letters earlier, or if it should
be deemed advisable, start for home. One
of the Elders accompanied me.
