The interview was in every way satisfactory,
and Jane really felt much more at ease than
if the cloud that hung over the domestic
department had not burst.
	Two or three days sufficed to settle Mr.
Burke's business, and off he set to see after
the final arrangements for the house in town.
Sir Edward had never kept a house in Lon-
don always taking handsome apartments
suited to a single gentleman who only
wanted to mingle with his own "set."
	Lady Anna was all excitement and her
cousin feared nervous prostration but the
Doctor was watchful and gave her strict
orders and also warned Maitland in regard
to the matter, telling him that she was so
frail he would need to avoid everything
calculated to trouble or to weary her.
Young men don't comprehend such mat-
ters very well, and young Walton was very
ignorant about girls having never had any
sisters and his ideas about women were all
formed by his own mother, whose natnral
constitution was excellent.
	Lady Anna was almost ill by the time
they were ready to go and her cousin felt
reluctant about leaving home, remember-
ing the last journey and the terrible fatality,
but the Doctor consented to travel with his
ward and see her safe to her journey's end,
and that reconciled Miss Conway to leaving
home. The young girl improved as they
went by easy stages, the cold bracing air
gave her an appetite and when they arrived
she looked more like herself and Dr. Bland
felt no reluctance in leaving her in her
cousin's care.
