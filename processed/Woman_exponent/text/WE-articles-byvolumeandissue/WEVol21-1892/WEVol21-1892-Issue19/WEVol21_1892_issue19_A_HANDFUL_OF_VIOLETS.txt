A HANDFUL OF VIOLETS.

'Twas a morning in midwinter,
	Cold but clear as joyous May,
Over dale and lonely woodland,
	A snow-white mantle lay.
The delicate hoar frost decking
	Tree and shrub with strange device;
And a filmy mist was rising
	From the river's broken ice.
The sun shone out with a brightness
	That dazzled the watching eye;
The smoke curled lazily upward
	Towards the clear azure sky.
The wind rose high and sadly moaned
	Through the boughs of leafless trees,
And whirled the white snow into drifts,
	Joined the ice-clad river's breeze.
Thus it was when they came to me, 
	Still with dewy fragrance wet,
Plucked in the warm and sunny south,
	A "handful of violets."
And in fancy I saw the hands
	That gathered the blossoms rare;
A sad-faced mother, somber clad,
	And fatherless boy so fair.
So tenderly, aye reverently,
	She stooped the flowers to part,
With thoughts of their hoped-for mission
	Fast filling her aching heart.
The great brown eyes gaze wond'ringly
	At his mother's falling tears,
"Poor mamma," the sweet lips whisper,
	Words of comfort low she hears.
Two weeks, with water daily changed,
	They bloomed nor their beauty lost,
'Till sunshine stole the snow away,
	And spring rains destroyed the frost.
Long before their fragrance perished,
	Over his couch they were strewn;
In the lone, beautiful city,
	With its peaceful dead entomed.
Wooed to life by balmy breezes,
	With their wishes unexpressed,
Far from the sunny south they came,
	On a northern grave to rest.
Only one lived, and that—ah, well,
	I have told you where it rests;
How the stilled heart beneath must love,
	That "handful of violets."
May 3rd, 1893. HYACINTH.
