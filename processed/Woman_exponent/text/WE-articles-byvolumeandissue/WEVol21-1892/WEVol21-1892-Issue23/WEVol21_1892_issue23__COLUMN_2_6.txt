Miss Jessie Miller, the “California Child
Cornetist,” was very wonderful. The
singers, both men and women, had fine
voices and it was really the singing we
liked best. Miss Laura Lucile Harmy,
soprano, was perhaps the best, though Miss
Belle Chamberlain has a very rich and pure
mezzo-soprano voice. Miss Jennie O’Neill
Potter after announcing that she would
give as an encore, “How Salvator Won
the Race, ” recited pathetically, "Swinging
in the Grapevine Swing,’’ and then, true
to her promise, gave the other in a truly
exciting style which elicited loud applause.
Mrs. Albert Barker, styled “The Bird
Songstress,” created quite a furore with
her extraordinary mimicry of bird notes,
which she does not produce with her lips,
the sounds being of the thorax it is a re-
markable vocalization. She gave as an
encore, ‘‘The wind and the moon,’’which is
more extraordinary still. One might
imagine the whistling roaring wind as it
comes in shrill and piercing blasts from the
canyons of the Rocky Mountains, and yet
she does it in the most natural manner,
without apparent effort. We must not for-
get Miss Helen Mar, the humorous reader
and raconteur. Mr. W. P. Mc'Henry,
baritone, has a superb voice,and. Ashfon D.
Goodrich, improvisator, played, the piano,
and’ whistled bird imitations with much
ability.‘‘Southern Melodies” by the
Virginia Students was vociferously ap-
plauded. All the music and other artists
were good but one cannot go into details,
there is no time save for generalities.
