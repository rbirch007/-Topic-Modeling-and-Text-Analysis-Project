	The writer is free to confess she knows
not where to begin or what to see first;
however, making a bold dash to accomplish
some little in the way of sight-seeing, we
began with one place seemingly more in
our own line of work—viz., “The Wo-
man’s Building.” We had attended one,
two, three receptions there, one of these
the unveiling of the statue of Harriet
Beecher Stowe, with ceremonies or exercises
in the beautiful and artistically decorated
library; in fact had been chaperoned
through some parts of it by one of the
Lady Managers, and yet felt in view of its
exquisite beauty how comparatively little
we know; and so as we say in the story,
this was our starting point.  Like many
other buildings it contains many unpacked,
unopened boxes, mysteries yet to be revealed.
There is more even now than one can 
readily study or describe, and the fact seems
always uppermost how little we poor mor-
tals know of the great, wide, wide world in
its richness and abundance—how small and
insignificant we are in and of ourselves.
	As all our readers are doubtless aware, the
architect was Miss Sophia S. Hayden of
Boston (how glad I am ’twas a Boston wo-
man.)  The building is two stories high
and the rotunda is sixty-five by seventy
feet reaching through the height of the 
building and covered with a skylight.  On
the roofs of the pavilions are open areas.
One serves as a cafe and one as a tea
garden.
	At the extreme ends of the rotunda are
two immense paintings, containing many
figures, and scenes in the most delicate
colors, and the effect upon the observer is
almost entrancing; it lifts one into a region
of highly cultivated art and leaves the im-
pression of the picture engrafted upon the
imagination. In the midst of the beauty
and delight of these crowning efforts of
woman's genius, displayed in the building
and its adornments, it is difficult to define
the emotions that possess the human soul,
at least the soul of a sympathetic, sensitive
woman.  It seemed we were lifted out of
this every day, practical age in which we
are living, into a land of beauty, of lights
and shadows that blended in the most at-
tractive harmony of color and of effect, and
all around above and below were things of
beauty of the finest order.
	Mrs. Candace Wheeler, who has had
charge of the decorative work and the
blending of harmonious colors, and objects,
is herself the personification of exquisite
taste in dress and appearance, and her
sweet unaffected ways win all hearts.  One
sees at a glance how well the lady man-
agers have chosen in making the selection
they did for this important but onerous
position.  All the pictures, needlework,
lace, embroidery, and tapestry here have
been done by woman’s hands and skill,
wrought out of her ingenious brain.  The
pictures she has painted, the books she has
written, the papers she has edited and pub-
lished, the silk she has manufactured, the
spinning, weaving and knitting, with all 
and every sort of work or of invention, all
these are here a splendid testimonial of
woman’s power and force in the great liv-
