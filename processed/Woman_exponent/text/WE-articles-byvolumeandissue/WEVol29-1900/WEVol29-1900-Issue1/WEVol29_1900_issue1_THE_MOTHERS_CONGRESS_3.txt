THE MOTHERS’ CONGRESS.
			(Concluded.)
	Educators for years have realized the im-
portance of this strength of union, but
its worth has never until the last few years
seemed to arouse the women to action.
Goethe tells us “A talent may be perfected
in solitude, a character only in the world.”
It is certainly characters we want and
character can only come to woman and man
alike, and that way is through service for
the children of God.
	God’s greatest gift to mankind was love;
man’s greatest gift to his fellow is love; we
know thoughts are things, and as we think
of people we are helping them to grow into
ideals, for our thoughts generally rule our
acts.
	Poor though we may be, without influ-
ence, without ability, yet can we help
humanity to attain to the best, the truest,
the highest. So I would say organize, if
the spirit of progression pervades you will
have crowded meetings, for there are
women and mothers who hunger for knowl-
edge, as they feel their inability more and
more each day, to battle with the responsi-
bilities which surround them.

	The evening session of the Mothers’
Congress was the “fathers’ evening,” and
was presided over by Professor Byron
Cummings. Rev. Clarence T. Brown
opened with prayer and Professor Cum-
mings followed with remarks that expressed
the recognition of the noble and worthy
aims of the women’s congress.
