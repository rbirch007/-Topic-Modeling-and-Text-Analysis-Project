NOTES AND NEWS.

	MARK TWAIN has come home again after
a journey around the world. He is in good
health and fine spirits at the age of sixty-
five years.
	THE death of Charles Dudley Warner,
which occurred October 1, has taken away
one of the foremost literary men of the age,
but besides his loss to the world of letters,
he was a man of highly developed social
quality, much more genial than is usual in
men of such rare gifts and scholastic attain-
ments.
	The Suffrage Bazaar in Madison Square,
New York City, will be one of the unique
exhibits of the closing year of the century.
A distinguishing feature will consist of
booths and tables in honor of the most emi-
nent women in the suffrage movement.
Mrs. F. S. Richards, president of the Utah
Woman’s Council made the collection in
Utah for the Bazaar display, and forwarded
the articles to New York. Mrs. Gilmer
will be in charge of the Utah Booth or
Table. Governor Wells, of Utah, sent a
beautiful doll handsomely dressed, and the
ladies of the Council furnished an elegant
Navajo blanket. Other articles of fancy
work and pictures by home artists were
donated, also some books by home authors.
