	The president has been married twice.
His first wife only lived a short time.
Several years after he married a niece of his
first wife, by whom he has had sixteen chil-
dren, seven are still living.
	Allen Sangru in the Ainslee says:
	If Paul Kruger died today the Boer nation
would pass into history tomorrow; no man nor
combination of men could replace him. He alone
welds together the chain of humanity that is strug-
gling for liberty in South Africa. It is as though one
mighty individual were fighting the British Em-
pire. He is Atlas, supporting the world. He has
no counterpart on earth Enemies may ridicule his
religion, slur his principles and accuse him of hy-
procrisy, but he stands a figure as adamantine as
it is animated which no one believes will ever be
cast down. He is a Sphinx surcharged with wis- 
dom and energy to whom 40,600 Burghers look up
as their savior.
	Twenty-seven years ago Cecil John
Rhodes, then a tall, thin lad, was advised
by his physician to go to South Africa if he
wished to live more than three years. He
and his brother sailed and reached Natal
while the diamond mines of Kimberly were
in full blast. Cecil remained there for
several years and finally went back to Ox-
ford to complete his education. Returning
to South Africa with the Rothschild money
he began to be a factor in politics, mining
and government and in 1875 he was in the
zenith of his glory. He was many times a
millionaire, the head of one of the greatest
enterprises, director of the affairs of the
dominion that occupies one tenth of a conti-
nent, almost the sole owner of Rhodesia.
His power was nearly absolute over a ter-
ritory that extended from the Cape of Good
Hope into Central Africa, then eastward to
within a few miles of Indian Ocean. But
