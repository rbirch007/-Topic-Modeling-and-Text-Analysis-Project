HOUSE OF LIFE.

My Lord built a beautiful house for me,
	With arch and pillar and wall,
With many a chamber fair to see,
And corridor, closet and hall.
The carpets and curtains were rich and fine,
	And every convenience was there;
With workmanship cunning, this dwelling of mine
	Was fashioned and furnished with care.

And I was mistress and owner of all,
	This opulence, beauty and worth,
Like Eve in her garden whom God did install, 
To dress it, the gem of the earth.
I looked from my window and lo, I beheld
	More palaces lovely and grand,
Eclipsing in splendor my own, and dispelled
	Was my joy in both palace and land.

No more did I polish each casement and stair,
	And brush every dust-flake away,
Nor open its windows to woo the sweet air,
	Nor to deck it with garlands so gay,
Its fair rooms grew tarnished and laden with dust,
	And cobwebs hung heavy and gray,
Unloved and abandoned to moth and to rust,
	Its beauty began to decay.

A footstep draws near! my Lord—He is here!
	He gazes in pain and amaze—
"This wreck is the Temple for you I did rear,"
	For shame I could not raise my eyes.
"O, foolish one, what didst thou lack, but the will?
	If these were too narrow for thee,
More stately apartments to plan and to build?
	Who loves not my gift, loves not me."

He passed, and I dared not beseech Him to stay—
	My opened eyes saw clearly now,
How foolish and blind was my envy; straightway
	I made with repentance a vow—
To honor my gift, to fill it with light,
	Embellish and keep it with care,
That henceforth it be in my Lord's kingly sight
	A dwelling place lovely and fair.

When next His dear footsteps draw near to my
		door,
	With gladness I'll usher Him in;
No mildew or rust shall He see as before;—
To restore it at once I’ll begin.
I'll love my dear home for the Giver's sake,
	Nor sight if more stately ones be,
For God, the dear Giver, can make no mistake;
	He knows what is best for me.
