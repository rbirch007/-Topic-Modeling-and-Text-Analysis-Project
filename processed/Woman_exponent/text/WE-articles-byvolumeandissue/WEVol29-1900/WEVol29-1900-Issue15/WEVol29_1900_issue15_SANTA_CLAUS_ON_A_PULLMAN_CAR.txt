SANTA CLAUS ON A PULLMAN CAR.

It is New Year’s Eve, but let me tell
	What happened, strange but true,
To travelers on a flying train
	As they sped the darkness through.

It was Christmas Eve upon the train
	As well as in crowded town,
And through the windows we could see
	The drenching rain come down.
And while we sped as a bird might fly,
	Safe there in the warmth and light,
An old man spoke. “I think the rain
	Will change into snow tonight;
And ’twill be more like Christmas time
	If we get there covered with snow.”
And the old man laughed and a smile went
		round 
	For the heart still all aglow.

There sat another, but stern of look,
	You could tell no thought had he
Of a home ahead, or a happy past
	Around the Christmas tree;
For he scanned and scowled o’er columns long
	Of the market’s fall and rise,
With now and then impatient sniffs,
	Or deep drawn, troubled sighs.

Before him sat a mother pale
	And a restless boy of four,
Who turned in his seat and upon his knees
	Looked the stern man o’er and o’er
In silence, but so deep and long
	The man looked up to see
A chubby face and wondering eyes,
	Grave, earnest as, eyes could be.

“Has you any boys like me?” he asked.
	“No!” he answered, surlily.
“Maybe you’ve got little girls, I guess?”
	“No boy, don’t bother me.”
But he could not read, for the restless boy
	Whispered out, in his mother’s ear—
“I’m bein’ still—but that poor man—”
	“Sit down and don’t talk, my dear.”

He sat awhile, then his head turned back
	As a bird in its cage will do;
Gazed earnestly, slipped around, leaned o’er:
	“I am so sorry for you;
We’re going to grandpa’s to live;
	Papa is dead, and you see
I’m all mama’s got; aint you sorry,
	Mister, for mama and me?”

But we never knew what answer
	The stern man might have made,
For some one called—“Just look outside!”
	All the earth in white was laid.
The pale young mother rose with her boy
	It was time for him to sleep,
But I knew she longed to be alone
	Her burning tears to weep.

But ’twas not long ere the little hands
	Stole gently, and there outside—
To the curtain’s fringes, in open view,
	His little stocking he tied.
And O, what a look and startled thought
	Shone out in every face;
Till some cynic said “A Pullman car
	For that’s a poor kind of place.”

But a woman answered: “I don’t know—”
	And she opened her satchel wide,
Candies and cakes and a pretty knife—
	She laid them side by side;
And as the impulse ran through the car
	Each took a sudden joy
In giving out from the gifts they bore
	To the widowed mother’s boy.

The stern man folded a golden coin
	In paper and softly said:
“God bless the child, he has waked to life
	Memories that seemed dead.
I must hear his little voice again,
	I would like to know his name,
For long ago, I have known such eyes—
	Their color, and look, the same.”

The little stocking never could hold
	The gifts of women and men;
But silken handkerchiefs came forth
	And did a duty then;
The word had passed all through the train
	And passengers came in,
With smiles and gifts, to see the sight,

Why was it that each one would pause
	To list the sleeper’s breath—
The little one around whose lot
	Love rose in shades of death?
And if a sigh, or oft a tear,
	Before that curtain fell,
’Twere love’s and pity’s offering,
	Sweeter than words could tell,

The early morn waked the little lad,
	And who were there but smiled—
To see the happiness love had wrought
	For the soldier’s stranger-child!
And the stern man when he asked his name
	Found sudden joy and tears,
For the little one was his brother’s child,
	Brothers, parted for years.

And he clasped him then in his strong arms,
	And the stern look fled away,
And he cried: “This is my Christmas gift!
	I’m going to grandpa’s today!”
She looked at the gifts and paler grew—
	“O, Teddy, why did you so!”
“Why Santa Claus didn’t know where I’d gone,
	And I wanted him to know.”

“Goodbye!” spoke many a smiling one,
	As they parted from the train;
But grandpa’s house was filled with joy
	For the dear ones home again.
