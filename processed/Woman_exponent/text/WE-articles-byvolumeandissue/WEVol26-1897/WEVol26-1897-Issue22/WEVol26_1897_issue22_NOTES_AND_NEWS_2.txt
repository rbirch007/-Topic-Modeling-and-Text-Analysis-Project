NOTES AND NEWS.

	SINCE the statement about the poet sing-
er, James G. Clark, was made in the Tri-
bune of September 25, he has passed to the
land whose glories seemed very real to him
when he composed the immortal melody
for the hymn “When the Mists Have Rolled
Away.”						—Ex.
	THE District Woman Suffrage Associa-
tion has elected its ex-president, Mrs. Ellen
Powell Thompson, again as its leader, and
this insures an active and strong society for
this very important season when the great
celebration is to be held here and much im-
portant work is to be done.		Ex.—
	MRS MARIA FRINK, of Valparaiso, Ind.,
who writes under the nom de plume of
“Grace Gold,” thinks that the social re-
demption of the world wil be obtained not
by destroying the criminals and incapables,
but by putting out of existence the causes
which make them what they are—saloons,
gambling dens, immoral dives, obscene
pictures, impure literature—every thing
which tends to drag humanity downward.
	JUST before leaving the country last
month, Mr. W. L. Stead, the London edi-
tor, who some years ago exposed crimes
committed against young girls in his city,
was interviewed by a New York World re-
porter. Mr. Stead has reached the conclu-
sion that English women receive less of the
“small change of outward courtesy” than
do American women, but are given much
more solid and serious respect. In support
of his opinions he said:
	The English are not ashamed to place a wo-
man high above all their politicians, to keep her
there for sixty years, and to give her a direct and
potent voice in settling all the great affairs of
state, from dealing with which in the American
Republic women are debarred. You may say
the Queen is only a figurehead. If so she is
much the liveliest and most potent figurehead in
history. The chance of being such a figurehead
is denied by your Constitution and customs to
the ablest person of the states if she should chance
to be born a woman.				—Ex.
