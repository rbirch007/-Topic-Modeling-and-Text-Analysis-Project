THE NATIONAL UNIVERSITY.

	WASHINGTON, NOV 16.—The agitated
question of establishing in the city of
Washington a national university on the
lines suggested by President Washington,
has taken more definite form than in the
past, and representative American women
have taken hold of the project with a de- 
termination to push it to a successful con-
clusion if possible. They have started out
in a practical manner by seeking to raise
the first $250,000 necessary for the erection 
of an administration building to form the
nucleus of the university and hope to lay
the corner stone on Feb. 22, 1899. Their
purpose in the interim is to urge the matter
continuously on the attention of women all
over the country. They intend likewise to
interest the school children in the work.
As a first step for awakening interest in the
undertaking they will assemble in conven-
tion in Washington on Dec. 14, to decide
on ways and means for arousing public
sentiment.
	Among those who have initiated the
movement to fulfill Washington’s wish are
Mrs. Phebe Hearst of this city, Mrs. Ellen
A. Richardson of Boston, Mrs. Clara A.
Anthony, Mrs. Rachel Howland, Mrs.
Louise Tincker and Mrs. Edmond Anthony
Jr. of Massachusetts, Mrs. L. Boyd of 
Georgia, Mrs. W. A. Roebling of New
Jersey, Mrs. John K. Goodloe, of Kentucky,
Mrs. H. H. Adams of Connecticut, Mrs. C.
S. Brice of Ohio, Mrs. Eugene Hall, Mrs.
David Starr Jordan, Dr. Charlotte Bleak
Brown, Mrs. E. S. Hubbard and Mrs. 
Caroline Jackson of California.
	Mrs. Ellen A. Richardson of Boston,
chief organizer of the movement writing to
Mr. George H. Harris of this city of the
plan thus far formed, says:
	“The patriotic ladies of America are to build
the administration building and they propose to
make the 22nd of February, 1898 the nation’s first
offering day for this purpose, asking no large co-
tribution, begging nothing. America’s 70,000,000
people must build this great monument. If the
13 000,000 children in the public schools in 
America would give an offering of a penny each 
on Washington’s birthday they would have suffi-
cient to erect the building and endow it. Let us
then join hands and have an American university.
American sciences, American ideas for American
citizens.”
	Mrs. Richardson further stated that she
has arranged with the proper authorities
that the bureau of engraving and printing 
shall produce certificates which shall be
given to those who contribute.

THE MEETING IN SALT LAKE CITY.
	Mrs Margaret Blaine Salisbury has been
appointed by the president, Mrs. Richard-
son for Utah, to take charge of the work of
this State.
	The meeting held at her residence on
Saturday afternoon Nov. 27, was for the
purpose of organizing.a committee for this
enterprise. Letters were sent out before-
hand not only to women in this city but in
Ogden, Logan, Provo and Park City. A
number of ladies responded to the call, and
at the time appointed Mrs. Salisbury stated
the object of the meeting, and the ladies
assembled then and there elected Mrs. Salis-
bury permanently to fill the office to which
she had been appointed by the central or-
ganization. She was authorized by vote to
appoint a secretary and such other officers
as were needed to assist in the work. Fol-
lowing this suggestion the chairman ap-
pointed Mrs, C. E. Allen, vice-chairman
and representative of the First Congressional
district of Utah; Miss Hester Harkness, secre-
tary; Mrs. F. J. Fabian, treasurer, and Mrs. E.
B. Wells, press representative.
	Mrs. Salisbury read an address which
stated all the important facts concerning
the movement, and read from the will of
George Washington that part relating to the
founding of such an institution, and also
how the funds left by him for that purpose
had been lost; the chairman also read the
Senate bill upon the subject, therefore the
ladies present were informed of the object
in view and the needs of such a magnificent
building as would be required for the pur-
pose named.
	Mrs Allen spoke upon the necessity of
the people of this State proving their inter-
est in the movement, bringing out clearly
the idea of American patriotism.
	Mrs. Fabian spoke briefly upon the
great need of a university where such
degrees could be given as were obtained in
foreign institutions and avoid the neces-
sity of so many thousands of young people
going abroad to study. Mrs. Kinney made
some practical suggestions as to how the
money should be raised.
	A committee on resolutions was appointed
and later an important resolution submitted
and passed unanimously to aid the chair-
man in matters to be considered in the
meeting to which she is called as delegate
from Utah. The meeting is to be held in
Washington on December 14th, and Mrs.
Salisbury expects to leave in a few days for
the capital.
	The money which the women are prepar-
ing to raise at the present time or in the
near future is to build an administration
building as a nucleus of the university, and
is to be entirely woman’s work or a memor-
ial from the women of this country to the
immortal Washington.
	The cost of the building is estimated at
$250,000, and the proportion for Utah is ex-
pected to be $1,000. It is anticipated that
the corner stone will be laid Feb. 22, 1899.
A part of woman’s work will be to arouse
an interest in the school children of the
country to aid in the enterprise, and the
committee of women propose to make the
first offering for this building on Feb. 22,
1898.
	The committee of ladies already appoint-
ed at the meeting held are, besides the
officers whose names are: already given,
Mrs. Frank J. Cannon and Mrs. Whalon of
Ogden, Mrs. Hoyt of Park City, Mrs.
Coray of Provo, Mrs. Luther Foster and
