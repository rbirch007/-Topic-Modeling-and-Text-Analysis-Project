RESTORATION OF MOUNT VERNON.

	IT will be gratifying news to many to
learn that Mount Vernon is to undergo
restoration to as near its condition at the
time of its occupancy by our first President
as is practicable.
	It was recently discovered that decay had
set in in some of the timbers used in the
erection of the mansion and work has al-
ready begun under the direction of the
Mount Vernon Ladies’ association for their
replacement by materials of most lasting
quality. At the same time the house will
be given over to T. Mellon Rogers of Phila-
delphia, a colonial architect of note, for
restoration as far as is possible to its origin-
al beauty of decoration and arrangement of
furniture. It is not well known that in
the years that have intervened since the
death of its illustrious tenant that the inte-
rior beauty of the house has been sadly mar-
red by careless and ignorant renovation.
On his examination Mr. Rogers found that
exquisite carved work had been partly obli-
terated by numerous coats of paint, as many
as ten being found on some of the wood-
work; that delicately tinted walls had been
covered by several layers of wall paper, in
which no intelligent care had been taken to
preserve the original scheme of decoration.
This extraneous matter will be carefully re-
moved and as far as possible original color-
ings and decorations be restored. New
cornice wlll be put in place of that impair-
ed by time and misusage. The plain col-
umns now supporting the upper floor will
be replaced by colonial fluted columns; the
staircase will be repaired and a blackwal-
nut effect imparted to it, and the furniture
of the mansion will be rearranged in con-
formity with its placing in Washington’s
time.
	The expense of this radical restoration
will be assumed by the respective state
representatives of the Mount Vernon
Ladies’ association.—Home Magazine.
