A WORD TO THE PRIMARIES

Dear Sister Wells:
	KNOWING that you are interested in the
welfare of all the borders of Zion, we
thought perhaps you would not turn away 
a few words from its center Stake.
	Since our reorganization, which occured
Sep. 12 of last year, shortly after the death
of our beloved president Sister Ellen C. 
Clawson, we have endeavored to visit every
ward in the Stake. If we have omitted any
it has been because the opportunity has not
been tendered us. We can visit the city
wards at will but the country have our
visits in their own hands, because we have
to wait not only for invitation but for trans-
portation from the car lines. Where we
have visited we have been treated right
royally, but some of our city visits have
been like the angels spoken of anciently,
unawares. But the sisters take it all in
good part for they know that we have but
one aim, and that to benefit them and those
whom they are called to teach.
	As we have visited we have generally
graded, and have received excellent reports
of its results. The need of grading is easily 
seen; for instance in addressing a Primary
association pupils will very often range
from three to sixteen years; such an ad-
dress will be like a handful of promiscuous
shot, it will hit where it does not miss, or

[Column 2]
part of it will be thrown over the heads of
the little tots, or larger ones will be left un-
fed or uninterested. Whereas by assorting
the children and placing each grade apart
we can appeal to each grade according to
their degree of intelligence. Grading is
quite a little task, but once accomplished it
more than pays. We take the tots from
three to six years, give them in charge of a
counselor or aid, then pick out three, say
from seven to ten and eleven, interest and
instruct them according to their capacity.
Then comes the grade from eleven to
twelve or fifteen; now this grade feel their
importance immediately, they feel that they
are no longer classed with babies and can
do a higher grade of work and are proud of
it, and from this grade we take our aids
who help in time of need, and from this
class we can take our secretaries. Each
class prepares its own minutes and program
but hands them in as a whole to the secre-
tary of the association at the close of the
meeting.
	We have great pleasure in our work and
our colaborers are a willing band of sisters.
Since our reorganization the attendance at
the officers meeting, which occurs on the
first Saturday in every month, has increased
greatly, yes to an overflow and even to
utilizing the window sills. Some wards
that we visit are doing so well that we are
delighted, while in some wards there is a
lack of energy or ability which makes us
feel sad to think that the darlings must go
unfed spiritually or intellectually. Those
who do put their soul and energy into their
work are deserving great praise and will re-
ceive a great reward, for we know they
must drop their own affairs no matter how
trying this may be, and many times drag
their weary bodies to the place where the
little ones can be taught the ways of the
Lord.
	We must not forget to tell you of a
delightful sociable that we had lately. It
was a reunion of the officers of the Salt
Lake Stake Primary association including the
general board and the boards of other asso-
ciations of the Church. As your dear self
could not meet with us as per invitation,
we want to tell you that it was a pretty
sight to see the happy faces of our sisters
and some of our brethren on that occasion.
It was decided there and then by our guests
and your correspondent that this delightful
affair must be repeated at least once a year.
Please pardon us if we have trespassed on
your valuable space.
				MARY L. MORRIS.
	In behalf of the Board.
