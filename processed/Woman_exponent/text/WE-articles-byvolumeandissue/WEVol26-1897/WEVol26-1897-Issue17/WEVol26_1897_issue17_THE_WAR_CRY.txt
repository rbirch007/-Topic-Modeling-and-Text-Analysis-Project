THE WAR CRY.

	FOR some time past a war cry has been
in the air all over this country and in
other lands and to many war has seemed in-
evitable. The telegraphic news has been
conflicting yet on the whole it has sound-
ed threatening, to put the matter mildly.
The explosion of the Maine has formed
the topic for weeks, and now that the
Court of Inquiry has rendered its verdict,
there seems little change in the general
commotion. Preparations for war in the
army and navy are going forward and the
outlook is not a happy one to say the
least.
	At last President McKinley has spoken;
his message on the subject including the
findings of the court, were presented to the
Congress of the United States Monday,
March 28, and was received with intense
interest. Congress took deliberate and dig-
nified action—the message was referred to
Committees.
	The times are war-like in aspect and
many “wise men of Gotham” anticipate
war in the near future. The City of
Havana is reported to be a veritable vol-
cano. War ships and torpedo boats seem
to be engaging the attention of men, and
if one listens to the war talk in public
places, one might easily assume that peace
was indeed taken from the earth.
	However, one fact is apparent, there is
much suffering and destitution. America


is acting nobly in relieving the distressed
and the Red Cross Society are on hand to
take charge of supplies and manage these
affairs economically.
	Spain would show wisdom if she would
consent to the measures proposed by the
American government, for surely she will
be made to yield ignominisously if war is
entered upon. Those who desire an ami-
cable settlement of affairs continue hopeful
and perhaps the cry of war may pass by
this time without involving nations as
now seems not unlikely; but eventually
the prophecies concerning these things
must be fulfilled however much people
who love peace may regret the necessity of
war.
