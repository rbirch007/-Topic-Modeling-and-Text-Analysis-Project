A SAD OCCURRENCE.

Dear Sister Wells:
	WE are called upon to record a terrible
calamity that has befallen our little colony
in the sudden and dreadful death of our be-
loved sister, Agnes A. McDonald, wife of
Patriarch Alexander F. McDonald, who was
ruthlessly murdered by Mexicans on the
23rd of February last.
	Quite a number of settlers had gone to
Colonia Juarez, a distance of forty miles, to
attend the quarterly conference. She was
the post mistress of our colony and also
kept a small store, and it is supposed this
was what induced the wretches to commit
the horrible deed. She was a great deal of
the time the only occupant of the house,
though her son James lived just across the
street and other neighbors lived near.
	From the appearance of things the
wretches must have been watching her un-
til she retired, when crushing the window
in with an ax they leaped in, and from the
position she was found in it is supposed on
hearing the crash she sprang for the gun
(which she always kept hanging by the
bed) when the villians seizing her, threw
her back upon the bed and cruelly choked
her to death, then placed a  pillow over her
face and proceeded to go through every-
thing in the house and store room, taking
bolts of cloth, ready made shirts, overalls,
jumpers etc., and finding the box in which
she kept the cash, it being locked they took
it with them. In this position she was
found the next morning by her daughter in-
law who called her name repeatedly, and
received no answer. She went a little way
from the house to the cellar where they kept
vegetables, then returned and called again;
receiving no answer she tried the door, it
yielded. On looking in she saw everything
in confusion in the entry. She then passed
into the bedroom where the contents of
trunks, boxes and drawers lay strewed pro-
miscuously over the floor and glass all over
the room. She then summoned up all her
courage and went to the bed, where she
found the poor unfortunate woman with her
feet hanging from the bed and the cover
thrown over her body, and the pillow over
her face. To say that poor Ettie was para-
lyzed for the moment would be poorly ex-
pressing it. She then ran and gave the
alarm.
	The most active of the villians is sup-
posed to be a Mexican who a short time ago
had been working for them, for he knew
well the situation of the house and sur-
roundings, and just where she kept every-
thing. It seems that the deed was commited
in the early part of the night, as they seemed
to have taken their time in ransacking the
house and then rode leisurely away.
	Brother Peas who was hauling lumber
and was camping about three miles north of
here, heard a disturbance among his animals
and looking up to learn the cause saw four
horsemen riding by. It being very dark he
could not tell who they were, but supposed
they were settlers from the Colonies. In
the morning on looking for his harness he
missed a very valuable large horse, but
found the hobbles in the road where they
seemed to have been dropped.

the murderers and learning of the dreadful
deed Brother Peas very naturally concluded
that they were the thieves and he too joined
