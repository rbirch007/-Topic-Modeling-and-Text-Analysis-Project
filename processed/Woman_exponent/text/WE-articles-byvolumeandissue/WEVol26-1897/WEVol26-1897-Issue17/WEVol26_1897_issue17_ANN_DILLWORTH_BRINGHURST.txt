ANN DILLWORTH BRINGHURST.

Dear Sister Wells:
	IT has been the will of our Heavenly
Father to call home our beloved sister,
Ann Dillworth Bringhurst.
	She was born on Feb. 12, 1812, in Ches-
ter county, Penn., and came to Utah with
John Taylor’s company in 1847; moved to
Springville with her husband, ex-Bishop
William Bringhurst, in 1860, where she
has lived ever since. She was the mother
of seven children, five of whom are still
living.
	Coming to Utah with the pioneers the
deceased endured all the hardships and
privations necessary to the opening of a
new country, and then moving south she
experienced many difficulties in building up
the home in which she spent so many hap-
py days. She was chosen president of the
Relief Society and held the position for
years. She was always ready to assist the
poor and needy, and many are the good
deeds recorded to her.
	Sister Bringhurst left this earthly exis-
tence for a higher sphere at 3 o’clock Tues-
day morning Jan. 25, 1898. By her death
we lose one of our beloved members, but 
we feel to say, she has gone to meet kind
friends on the other side and will await our
coming.
	Relief Society of 2nd ward, Springville.

	A JUDGE of the Bombay High Council,
who is pompous in manner and never for-
gets that he is a judge, was walking up and
down the platform of a small railway sta-
tion just before taking his seat in the train.
At that moment a perspiring Englishman
rushed onto the platform and said to the
judge, “Is this the Bombay train?” The
judge coldly remarked, “I am not the sta-
tion master.” The other man at once re-
torted, “Then confound you sir, why do
you swagger about as if you were?”
