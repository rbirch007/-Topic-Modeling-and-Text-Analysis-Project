WRITTEN FOR THE JUBILEE PRIZE COMPETITION. 
LOVED Utah! the eyes of the nations are on
		thee;
	In gladness, in glory arise, and appear;
Thy beautiful garments and jewels now don thee, 
	Salute with the name of thy brave Pioneer,
	Thy Jubilee Birthday, thy Fiftieth Year. 
Thy children come forward to honor and bless
		thee, 
Thy guests gather ’round thee, to hear and to
		see, 
What tales sha l be to’d by the friends who ad-
		dress thee, 
	What relics and gifts shall be brought unto 
		thee,
	To crown thy fair Statehood and show thou 
		art free. 
To listening thousands, thy story beginning,
	Recounts thy good lineage, birth undefiled; 
Thine infancy healthy, well-favored and winning—
	Then rich illustration portrays thee a child
	Full of vigorous spirits, courageous and wild. 
In Orient phrase, thou wast “salted and
		swaddled;”
	Developing early, precocious and strong; 
From pattern unique thou wast carefully
		modeled,
	As told in the voice of thy cradle-bed song; 
	And louder in strains which thy school-days
		prolong.
The Masters—thy Founders, were zealous in 
		holding 
	To courses and plans which were just, in their 
		sight;
Until in thy wealth and thy beauty unfolding, 
	The world is beginning to see they were right;
	To thy Nation’s great heart thou hast grown a
		delight.
Thy Temples unequa led; and man a dwelling
	With peace, love and innocence blissfully
		crowned; 
In flowers, fruits, grains and good works oft ex-
		celling; 
	Thy sweet singers rank with the world’s most 
		renowned; 
	With joy and thanksgiving thy soul may
		abound. 
Touch lightly, if need be, of sorrows and dangers, 
	Thy hardships and trials with shadows o’er-
		cast;
Today let thy children, thy friends and thy 
		strangers, 
	Rejoice in thy blessings, far reaching and vast; 
	Thy Golden Day dawning, the past be the past.
Yet tell of thy great ones, like Spa tan or Stoic, 
	How Brigham and Heber and Wil ard arose; 
Jedediah and Daniel and George A. heroic; 
	Hyde, Taylor and Woodruff, the Pratts and the
		Snows;
	And others, yet scarcely less valiant than those
Of the Pioneer men with their faithful com-
		panions, 
	Their wives and their little ones, dauntless and 
		bo'd, 
Who traced the wild plains, rugged mountains 
		and canyons, 
	No shelter to find, from the heat or the cold, 
	Too much, dearest Utah, can never be told. 
No luxur’ous couch, and no rest giving chariot, 
	Were shown as inducements that journey to
		take:
With Clara and Ellen and brave “Mother Har-
		riet,”
	It was for their religion and love’s holy sake; 
	And thousands have cheerfully followed their
		wake.
