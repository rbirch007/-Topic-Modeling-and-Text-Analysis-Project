VICTORIA.

I.
HAIL, all hail Victoria! England’s royal Queen,
The noblest and the best the world has ever
	seen;
All hail thy Jubilee, honored woman, mother, 
	wife,—
Thy charms grow even sweeter and rarer with
	thy life.
Nations arise and hail the auspicous day,
For peace o’er England’s land holds universal
	sway.
Her shores round all the earth by many ships
	are found,
And the radiant sun sets not, beyond her imperi-
	al bound.
Great is England! and of her Sovereign proud,
	And rousing cheers of greeting arise from many
	a crowd,—
Long live Victoria! a woman brave yet meek,
Who mercy grants to all deserving, who her pre-
	sence seek.
Women of En land! speak on this glorious day,
Sing to all the world this glad and joyful lay!
The scepter held in a woman’s tender hand,
Has quelled the flame of War that maddened
	winds have fanned.
O, England’s royal Queen! so dear to every
	heart—
Such tender thoughts awaken, that cause the
	tears to start,
Thy sacred vow fulfilling—“My people shall be
	free,”—
They to thy yearning bosom are closely held by
	thee.
They are thy thought, and they thy slumbers
	wake,
Just and kindly laws made f r thy subject’s sake,
Endear thee to thy people, who loving homage
	pay,
And all implore their God, the tyrant’s hand to
	stay.
Faith in thy father’s God through all thy length-
	ened years,
Thrills through the thinking world, and Nations
	move to tears.
II.
A girl so young and fair, and by the world un-
	known—
Yes in thy youth, wast called to sit upon a throne;
Yet splendor, envy, nor one besetting sin,
Has ever made thy crown or jewels shine more
	dim;
No thought of wealth nor greatness, or power
	came to thee,
But heavy on thy heart, the great responsibility,
Brought out thy woman’s nature even in the day,
When England’s maids and matrons frittered time
	away;
Shadowed in thy mind and hov’ring round thy
	heart,
Were omens of the sadness, that of thy life formed
	part;
And though a girl, not thoughtless, but serious
	for thy years,
Came glimmering o’er thy future, the mist that
	falls in tears.
III.
A wife! gaze all the world upon this precious
	gem,
That sparkles brightest in her royal diadem!
Loving, faithful, ever mourning her Albert dead,
Elects alone, the varied paths of life to tread,
Her heart cry, “I love those who long loved 
	thee,
And where they are I often long to be;
When to the grave they’re slowly borne away,
I too will follow, if perchance I may.
Albert I am true to thee, my love, my love,
Most precious thought, all earthly things above;
I wait the summons that long delayed shall come,
’Twill call me hence away to my unknown

[Column 2]
	home.—
Then we shall meet, again clasp loving hands,
And wander by the sea, upon the golden sands,
Again the love of youth, the music and the joy,
For which my heart has yearned, unmixed with
	earth’s alloy,
Will crown our happy lives, that Death can part
	no more.
For life is everlasting on that Elysian shore.”
IV.
A mother! all the world looks up in pride to 
	thee;
And loving speaks of thy sweet simplicity;
Thy wisdom, meekness, pure and holy thought,
To England and to children a precious boon has
	brought.
Victoria! would that every mother, over every
	land.
Their children in obedience so strictly would
	command.
Thy counsel “Though Princes born to such a
	mighty realm,
Serve God, for of thy lives He is at the helm;
Love Him, honor him, thy mother ne’er forsake,
And for the world live not alone—then of His
	peace partake.”
Thy children’s children, even generations three,
Thou hast caressd and fondled on thy mother
	knee,
Thy words so full of wisdom given in youthful
	days,
Have turned their lives in faith, to walk in Virtue’s
	ways.
These children too, are jewels that sparkle in thy
	crown
Thy mother’s crown so glorious and grand in
	world’s renown—
There may they shine forever, until the perfect
	day,
And be thy precious jewels when thy crown is
	given away.
All things must fade, and change comes to us all!
The mist comes down in rain drops, even plea-
	sures pall;
The Monarch on his throne, prone in the dust
	must lie
While Winter’s wind or Summer’s breeze ever
	wakes a sigh.
Proud Ambition too must fall, totter to the earth,
Locked close within her circling arms—she who
	gave us birth.
We leave behind to others the cottage or the
	throne,
And lost on changing breezes falls our last sad
	moan.
V.
This day all hail to England! Mistress of the seas!
Whose flag so proudly floats on every ocean’s
	breeze;
All the world is gazing, they all the glories see,
And in future ages behold thy grandest destiny.
Thy poets sing thy greatness, and thy statesmen 
	too,
Tell of thy future prowess wherever skies are
	blue;
Thy ships sail the ocean, thine armies tent the
	land,
And “Live, Long live Victoria” is played by
	every band.
					LYDIA D. ALDER.
	Written for the Utah Woman’s Press Club for
the Queen’s Jubilee, June, 1897.
