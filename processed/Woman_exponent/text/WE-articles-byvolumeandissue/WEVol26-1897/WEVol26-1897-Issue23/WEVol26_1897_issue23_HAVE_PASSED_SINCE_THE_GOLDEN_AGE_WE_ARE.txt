	If we look for some especially beautiful
piece of sculpture, we will be refered to
the work of some Greek artist. If we hear
of some remarkably beautiful woman,
we will perchance hear that she has Grecian
features. If we listen to a scientific sermon
or lecture, we will hear of the greatness of
some Greek poet or philosopher whose
voice is still heard in the halls of modern
learning.
	Greece is truly "the home of the poet
and the sculptor,” and her lamp of learn-
ing has indeed kindled an exalted admira-
tion in the hearts of the sons and daughters
of all civilized nations, for the heroes who
have left to their descendants names that
were “not born to die,” but to live on in
the memories of those that may follow on
for generations to come. For it was there,
in the land of Greece, through the bravery
and intelligence of those same heroes, that
political freedom for man was attained for
the first time in the history of the world.
	The glories of Greece never waned until
her government was wrested from her and
placed in the power of a nation whose every
thought and act was contrary to her own,
then the cloud of sorrow and despair set-
tled over her people, robbing them of the
power to maintain the standard of civiliza-
tion their fathers had established.
	The Greeks for hundreds of years have
believed that when a Constantine should
once more be united in marriage to a
daughter of Hellas by the name of Sophia,
it would be a day of rejoicing for Greece,
for she would then be in possession of Con-
stantinople once more and Prince Constan-
tine, Commander in Chief of the Greek
forces, being married to Sophia, a true
daughter of Hellas at the present time,
might, or might not have had some effect
upon the enthusiasm of the irregular band
of impatient but patriotic Greeks who
unfortunately precipitated the Greco-
Turkish war of 1897, before the Greek
Government was prepared for the struggle.
	In 1822 the Greeks rebelled against the
Turkish rule and after a fierce war in
which the Greek leaders made themselves
famous for all time by the brave and fear-
less manner in which they led their little
army against every conceivable disadvan-
tage on to victory, they recaptured cer-
tain parts of Grecian territory which are now
known as Modern Greece, whose literature
is still in its infancy and whose people have
been slow to regain their natural inherit-
ance of independence, as well as being
dilatory in trying to better their condition,
on account of their not having thoroughly
rallied from the depressing effect of Turk-
ish rule to which they were so long sub-
jected.
	Naturally, the Greek people were Platon-
ic in their love affairs and are sensitive in
the extreme, possessing the capacity to en-

dure great suffering or great joy. They
have very weird imaginations and are
easily discouraged, and just as easily in-
spired by the din of battle and are willing
to follow a brave leader in whom they have
confidence into the very jaws of death.
When the news of the certainty of a
Greco-Turkish war reached this country
there was both fear and hope in every
home in the land where Greek history was
understood. Each heart trembled for fear
the Mother Land of political freedom
would go down in defeat on account of
her comparatively defenceless condition in
not being prepared for a great military con-
flict.
	The one hope common to every liberty
loving heart was that Greece, the one
country that had taught all other nations
their first lessons in intellectual develop-
ment, might conquer the people whose his-
tory is one of massacre and bloodshed,
whose very name is a disgrace to the world. 
	Had King George been of strong mili-
tary ability himself and possessed less con-
fidence in some of his personal friends and
in his son, Prince Constantine, whose
courage seemed to have vanished ere the
war began, there would have been a dif-
ferent story for the Greek sympathizers to
tell; but whatever the other faults of King
George might have been, his greatest
enemies must of a necessity give him credit
for the prompt manner in which he re-
sponded to the cry for help that came from
distressed Cretes when the brutal Turks
began their massacre on the island.
	Though King George was deficient in
the ability to command a great army, he
deserves commendation for the stand he
took in behalf of humanity; for when the
Powers continually threatened Greece with
coercion if he did not withdraw his troops
from Crete, he answered, “Greece knows
she must be victorious or disappear; we
know that all the Powers are more or less
against us, but a great wrong has been
committed against right and humanity in
the Cretan question, and the chastisement
has commenced.”
	The thoughtful lovers of justice will al-
ways honor the King of Greece for defend-
ing his countrymen and fellow Christians
as he did, by refusing to withdraw his
troops from Crete and leave the field in
possession of the Turk for he knew full
well if he did so the people would be
massacred, the island destroyed and turned
over to anarchy and ruin.
	It cannot be said of the Greek soldiers
that they were not good or willing fighters,
for even the Turks have given them that
credit, and when they have been ordered to
retreat on account of the bad management
of the officers in command, they have fallen
back with tears of vexation in their eyes
and curses upon their lips.

any more than the ready manner in which
they gave up their money, their homes and
their loved ones in this and other countries
