Where once they rose in air
The Pagan tomtoms blare,
And in the place of Latin prayer and song,
Are heard the heathen cymbal and the gong.

Thy halls are filled with spectres shadowy gray
By night and day.
The Jesuit Father General, sweet and sad,
The deathless poet of the Lusiad,
Bishops and brothers, princes, padres, peers,
Soldiers in armor, merchants, buccaneers
Throng every spot
Contrasting what has been with what is not.

Yet, O Cathedral, though thy days are fled
And thou art but a phantom from the dead
Thy work shall live until the final day 
And grow alway,
In wiser measures then the past has wrought,
In better manners, nobler laws and thought.
The years to come thine epitaph shall find
In the new race of Godlike human kind.
—From the Chicago "Inter-Ocean," Dec. 23, 1894.
