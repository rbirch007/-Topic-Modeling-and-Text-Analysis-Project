	IN RURAL ENGLAND.
			XLVI.
    ALTHOUGH Clair Hilliard’s health was
delicate, he was a  very interesting acquisi-
tion to the family at  ROSEWOOD for so Sid-
ney Barrington had named the residence of
the Harrison’s and it passed current; Sidney
and Clair were like two  brothers  who had
met again after   a   long  separation.    The
children were  a never-failing  source  of
pleasure and delight, and  the  family were
united  in  anticipating  the  future    of the
children. Anna’s children  were  always
mentioned in connection with those at home
and in all the numerous plans Edward and
Anna Walton were included.    Conway was
always spoken  of  as a  prospective  Doctor,
and all  his studies   had  been  sort  of  pre-
paratory.
    Clair's  health improved  in   the  genial
atmosphere  and  he undertook of  his own
accord to teach the children music, and the
winters  he  passed  with  his  cousin  and
family were very happy  ones  for them all.
Indeed Jane who had been much depressed
when Clair first came,  grew quite  hopeful
of his ultimate recovery.    He had  told her
at  first  that  he  should   never  return  to
England, and that he wished his boy trained
