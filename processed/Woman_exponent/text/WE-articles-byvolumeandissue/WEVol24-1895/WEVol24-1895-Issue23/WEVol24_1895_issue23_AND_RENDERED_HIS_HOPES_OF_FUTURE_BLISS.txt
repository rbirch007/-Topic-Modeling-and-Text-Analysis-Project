And rendered his hopes of future bliss,
	But vain dreams unrealized.

[Column 2]
And she? A night of utter darkness 
	Took the place of sunny day.
When the gloom of that unkind story,
	Threw its shadow ’cross her way.

How often affliction’s hand must, turn
	The course of a loving heart,
And bridge the gulf of indifference.
	That holds it from love apart.

At last the star that should light her way,
	Has risen in clouds of despair;
Its dim rays fall on her lonely path,
	Like spectres so grim and bare.

Often to him her yearning heart longed
	Sweet words of hope to impart;
But his wounded pride forbade him speak,
	And they drifted still apart.

Alas, for the life that weary waits,
	For the joys that never come;
For the soul that starves for love’s caress,
	Whose lips are forever dumb.

A gloomy shroud of deathlike silence,
	Chased the light of glor’ous day,
Without a sorrow-laden chamber,
	Where she lonely dying lay.

She had waited long and patiently,
	For the words that never came—
Only a symbol that proved to her,
	That he still remained the same.
 
Sweet hope that once shone o’er her pathway,
	Now lies dead within her breast;
While oft the pale lips plantive murmur,
 	“Love, why thus my heart oppress?’’

But no answ’ring echo reached her ear,
	Her anguished heart to console.
Like a tender flower, storm-tossed and bruised,
	She has reached the heav’nly goal.

Softly the tread of a footstep falls,
	Without the death-chamber door,
He has hopeful come, his love to tell,
	And to count their parting o’er.

His passionate cry meets no response,
	As he kneels beside the cot;
Unheeded now his agonized words,
	“Come back, dear heart, leave me not."

Too late he has come with tales of love,
	To that still heart pleads in vain;
Too late he knows, that his selfish pride,
	His life’s dearest hope has slain.
April 30, 1896.	H.
