LETTER TO U. W. P. C.

	WILLOW BEND, July, 30th, 1895.
Dear Friends:
	LIFE seems one endless contradiction of
itself, its pendulum swings continuously from
one extreme to another.
	Thus when I wrote you last I was in the
far north, now I am drifted to southern
climes, so far, in fact, that I find myself at
this writing, unable to join my little band
at our regular gathering.
	This is a beautiful little oasis, a green
spot in a wilderness, as it were, the regions
round about being of red clay, even many


of the hills in this vicinity are of the same
color and have a barren appearance, with-
out vegetation, but as we find good in
everything here are located important Salt
Mines.
	Traveling southward we are at once
struck with the artistic outline and color-
ing of the surrounding mountains. It
seems to me any one raised in Utah has a
natural or instinctive love for the dear old
mountains, there is a peculiar charm about
those in the vicinity of my early home to
me they seem as old friends ever faithful
and true, constant and unchanging, and
when in a new country my eyes enjoy these
rugged formations. I study their physical
arrangement, their history and origin as I
am wont to study the characteristics of new
acquaintances and friends I make in life.
	But I imagine you, my dear friends, at
the present time more absorbed with the
political phase of woman’s career than with
literature, poetry, or art, and I trust that in
the near future the great problem will be
solved to your individual satisfaction and
for our mutual benefit.
	In the meantime please push on the in-
terests of the club and find in it joy, satis-
faction and continued progress.
	With kind love to all I am as ever yours,
