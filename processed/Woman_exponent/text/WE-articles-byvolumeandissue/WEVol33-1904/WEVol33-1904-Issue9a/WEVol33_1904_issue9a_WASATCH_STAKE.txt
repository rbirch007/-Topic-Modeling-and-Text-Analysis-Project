WASATCH STAKE.

Lesson XIII.—Charity.

Bear ye one another's burdens
And so fulfill the law of Christ.
	a. Teach children to sacrifice some of
their pleasure for the happiness of others.
b. Pure religion and undefiled before God
and the Father is this: “To visit the father-
less and the widow in their affliction, and to
keep himself unspotted from the world.”
Ref. Oct. Era, 1903. Nov. Y. L. Journal,
1904.

Lesson XIV.—Economy.
	a. Money, its use and abuse. b. Self-
denial. c. Avoid debt, danger of borrow-
ing. “Neither a borrower or a lender be,
for loan oft loses both itself and friend and
borrowing dulls the edge of husbandry.”
d. Economy of time.

Lesson XV.—Selfishness.
	a. Parental and family selfishness. b.
Christ's example is pure unselfishness. Ref.
Nov. Y. L. Journal, 1904.

Lesson XVI.—Self-Control.
	a. Temptation. b. Temperance. c
Courage. Self-control is the virtue which
regulates behavior by reason. Ref. Jan.
Era, 1904. Y. L. Journal, May, 1900.

Lesson XVII.—Cheerfulness.
	a. Cheerfulness and its effects, fault-find-
ing and its effects. b. Our duty is to be
happy and to make others happy. Ref. Y.
L. Journal, Aug. 1900.

Lesson XVIII.—Reverence for Sacred
	Things.
	a. Reverence for Deity. b. Reverence
for the house of worship. c. Reverence
for sacrament vessels. Irreverence comes
mostly from thoughtlessness and not from
desire. Ref. May Era, 1903. Journal, Oct.
1900.

A great meeting was held in London on
March 14 by the National Union, a federa-
tion of twenty-seven Woman Suffrage As-
sociations. Queen’s Hall was packed, and
there was much enthusiasm. About eighty
members of Parliament were present. Mr.
Leonard Courtney presided, and there were
addresses by Sir Wilfrid Lawson, Sir Chas.
M’Laren and six other members of Parlia-
ment, and by the Countess of Aberdeen and
Mrs. Millicent Garrett Fawcett. John Mor-
ley sent a letter, in which he said: “It is
absurd to pretend either that women are
incapable of political interest and capacity
or that voting must be injurious to their
womanhood.’’ Our American “Antis” al-
ways try to make out that municipal suf-
frage in England is limited to a few rich
women. At this meeting Mr. Bamford
Slack, M. P., quoted some interesting Lan-
cashire figures. In Bolton, he said, there
were 5234 women voters on the municipal
register, of whom 4752 were working wo-
men. In a typical ward in Nelson the pro-
portion of working women was 95 per cent.
