THANKSGIVING. 
	Thanksgiving is now considered by 
young and old to be one of the greatest 
and most glorious events of the entire year. 
It is a day on which we all seem to open 
our shut hearts freely, and try to make 
our homes cheerful and bright. As we all
know;

	“The heart has many passages 
Through which the feelings roam,
	But its middle aisle is sacred 
To the old, old home.”

	At the call of our beloved President with 
gladness and gratitude and in conformity 
with the beautiful custom of our national 
life, we pay the tithe of thankfulness to our 
Universal Father for His great mercy and
goodness.
	Custom has broadened the horizon of the 
day, and made it more than one of mere 
formal thanksgiving. We are all coming 
to see that gratitude to God does not con-
sist merely in prayer and psalm and song,
however beautiful and sincere they may be, 
but rather in that ministry to men out of 
the store of all which comes to bless our 
life, because we are the children of God. It 
is fit and proper that we should remember 
the mercies of the year. The great tide of 
goodness that has overflowed the shores of 
our humble life, the abundant prosperity 
that has rested upon all the land, the bless-
ing of home and friends and simple faith 
and love, should make an indelible impres-
sion upon every heart. Of course, our 
own lives with their perils and needs, their 
problems and burdens, their mercies and 
gentle providences, are our first thought, 
and should rightly be. Our homes should 
be shelters where life’s highest inspirations 
are given, a school where true training 
is imparted, that will develop men and
women; filling them with self-knowledge, 
reverence and self-control.
