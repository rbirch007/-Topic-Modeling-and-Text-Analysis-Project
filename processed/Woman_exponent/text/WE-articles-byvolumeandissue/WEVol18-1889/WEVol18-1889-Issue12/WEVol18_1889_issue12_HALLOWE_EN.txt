HALLOWE'EN.

Only a picture from my boyhood’s past—
A picture that on memory’s walls shall last

An Autumn sunset bathed the farm-house old
With deepening tints of ruby and of gold,
And leaves of yellow, purple, brown and red,
Like jewels, everywhere a lustre shed.
Within the merry shouts of boys and girls—
A happy mother’s cluster of fair pearls;
And grandma, with her knitting, rocking slow,
Upon her dear, old face a peaceful glow. 
She thinks of other eves that she has seen,
And other hearts that played on Hallowe’en.
Cuddled in craddle, bady coos with glee,
And rafters ring with mirth and melody,
And round and round, with clear, sweet voices sing:

	“Russet or bough or bitter-sweet,
		Greening	or golden	pippin;
	Happy are they that do the feat
		While their heads they dip in.
	Bob, bob, bibbety bob,
		You take one breath between;
	Oh. this is the way we children play
			On Hallowe’en!

	“Russet or bough or bitter-sweet,
		Greening	or golden	pippin;
	Only the lazy find defeat
		While their heads they dip in.
	Bob, bob, bibbety bob,
		You take one breath between;
	And this is the way we children play
			On Hallowe’en!"

Now mystic spells are hovering in the air,
And grandma tells us from her rocking chair,
Of long ago when she, a little child.
Joined in these revels, and the fun grew wild.
“Ah dears," quoth she, “with salt held in my hand,
Before the glass one night I chanced to stand;
For I was told that one who loved me best
Would o’er my shoulder peep before I’d rest.
Then o’er my shoulder I the salt would throw;
All this was many and many a year ago,"
"And did you see a face?” with wonder-eyes
Some one would ask. Grandma would look so wise.
“I did, my dears; the charm ne’er failed with me,
I looked, and o’er my shoulder I could see 
The face of one who loved as loves no other—
The gentle face of my own sweet, sweet mother!’
		—GEORGE COOPER in The Golden Days.
