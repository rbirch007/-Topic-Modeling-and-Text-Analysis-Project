	Long lessons both bitter and sweet;
And here are they clustered, and I, for awhile,
	Must feed them, as Peter “My sheep.”
They ask me for bread, shall I give them a stone?
Dare I trifle with them who are God's very own—
		His precious own girls and boys?

With scale seldom erring, my lightest word
	They fearlessly, gravely weigh;
By fear or by favor quite undeterred,
	They judge me from day to day;
I dare not be slothful, untrue or weak,
Their bright eyes such strong condemnation speak;—
		Oh, terrible girls and boys!

These eager, young spirits are hurrying on,
	Athirst for the tumult and strife;
All crowding and panting in haste to be gone,
	To join in the battle of life,
They know not the ambuscades laid in the way,
	Nor the wounds and groans in the heat of the fray;--
		Oh, pity the girls and boys!

Then who is to arm and give strength to their hands,
	And point out the snares in the way?
That safely they pass thro’ the foe's routed bands,
	And fall not, the slain in the fray?
O skill, and O wisdom, attend and give aid;
The task is so mighty it makes me afraid!
		My beautiful girls and boys!

What if through my weakness one soul be lost?
	One pair of fleet wandered astray?
Of the heavenly jewel who knoweth the cost?
	And what to my Lord should I say?
Could I join in the songs of the blest without tears
While the cries of the lost ever rang in my ears?
		 The cries of my girls and boys!

Dear Lord, give me wisdom to guide as I ought,
	A few days, these dear ones along;
To warmly encourage each high; worthy thought,
	And tenderly weed out the wrong.
If I be found worthy the mansions above,
Oh, grant that my welcome be kisses and love
	From these very girls and boys!
							Lu Dalton.
Manti, U.T.
