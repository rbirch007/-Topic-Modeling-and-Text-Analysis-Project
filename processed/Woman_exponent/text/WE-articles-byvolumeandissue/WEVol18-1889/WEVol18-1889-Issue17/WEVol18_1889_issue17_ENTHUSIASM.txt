ENTHUSIASM.

	Another pleasant morning is refreshing after
the storm of rain and enthusiasm which came so
profusely last evening. 
	It was quite enjoyable to see the earnest and
persevering zeal manifest, not heeding the rain
storm which came quickly down, the wind having
ceased the torches burned brightly, better than I
should have supposed while rain was pouring
down. Yet true and loyal hearts were beating
beneath the wet jackets.
	Thus we are proven from time to time to test
our integrity and prove our energy for our
Father's cause. Not being enthusiastic myself
I wondered how I could drink into the spirit of it
so deeply; believing as I do that honest en-
deavors with sincere and devoted hearts will
wear well; and in a case of emergency will be
all one can ask for or need to win a good cause.
	But at the present time we are driven to what
shall I say: desperation, yes if I may use the term.
Only think to be looked upon as mean, low-lived,
the off-scouring of our Father's footstool; which
we know that we are not, but quite the opposite.
Our principles will not admit of such a character
because they are the best set of rules ever given
to man.
	Do you ask by whom made? I answer, they
were framed by the King of heaven when he
lived upon this earth, and the only rules which
will fit us to come into His Kingdom. Should
not we then be up and doing, earnest and sincere?
Have not we proven them to be the best for over
forty years? Does not reason and common
sense teach the same?
	Yet we are so easily satisfied with this world,
that we would not take time to weigh the subject
as we ought, or give it its true value. Accord-
ingly the opposite spirit must be permitted to
awaken us to duty. Only think we are not ac-
counted fit to become citizens of the United
States, or make our homes on public lands. Well
if we are not I should like to know who is?
							M. E. K.
Jan. 26, 1890.
