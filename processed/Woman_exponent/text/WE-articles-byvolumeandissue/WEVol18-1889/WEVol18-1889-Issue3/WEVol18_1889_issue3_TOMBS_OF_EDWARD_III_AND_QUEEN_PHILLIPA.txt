TOMBS OF EDWARD III AND QUEEN PHILLIPA.

	These are fine tombs, brass effigies on top,
which are said to be very correct likenesses.
The queen bore her husband many children, a
number of whom died before their parents;
among them the promising Black Prince.
When near her end, the good queen called her
husband, who tenderly loved her, to her bed-
side and implored him to continue the same
truly regal life, after her death, which he had
hitherto led. She also requested that he might
be buried beside her. As an evidence of the
unfaithfulness of man it is reported that at a
tournament in Smithfield the royal widower
paraded with a mistress, forgetting the injunc-
tions of his dying wife. Under the Edwards
England was great; unity having been brought
about by conquest. Beyond is the simple altar
tomb of Edward I (Longshanks). At one
time there was some doubt as to whether his
remains were really desposited there; but not
long since, all conjecture was removed by an
examination which was made; when, to the
great amazement of those who looked,the body,
which had been embalmed, was found in a
state of excellent preservation; measuring more
than six feet in length showing that Edward
well merited the appellation, “Longshanks.”
On the tomb are brass effigies, representing
Edward I and Queen Eleanor, which are said
to be correct likenesses. She was a most ex-
cellent princess; and at her death Edward
mourned her loss. To show his devotion he
erected nine crosses to her memory at the towns
where the body rested during the journey to
London. Charing Cross was one of them.
That great king subjugated Scotland and
Wales. Edward possessed vehement energy
and a strong, conquering spirit. He wished
his body to be carried before the army until
Scotland should be completely subdued.
	The tombs of Richard II and his queen are
also in this enclosure.
