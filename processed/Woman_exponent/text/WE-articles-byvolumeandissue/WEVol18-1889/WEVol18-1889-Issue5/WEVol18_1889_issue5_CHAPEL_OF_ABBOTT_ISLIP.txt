CHAPEL OF ABBOTT ISLIP.
	The date of this chapel is 1532. The abbott
laid the foundation stone of Henry IV's
chapel. The screen is of exceptional beauty,
the carving being an exquisite piece of work.
	We have now come to the descriptions of
what appear to be rather strange objects,
namely, the wax figures. They are found
over the chapel of Saint Islip, and were car-
ried at royal funerals. In early times it was
the custom to bear the bodies of the deceased
kings and queens exposed upon a bier, as is
represented in Shakespeare's play of Richard
III, at the funeral of young Prince Edward.
But at a later date wax effigies were substituted
in their stead. That of Queen Elizabeth is of
importance, as it was probably the first one
made, and as there was great mourning at her
funeral. The one of Charles II is next in
order. Once it stood over his grave in Henry
VII's chapel. There is a figure of General
Monk. William and Mary are together, and
near them Queen Anne. Earl Chatham and
Lord Nelson are there also. The one of Lord
Nelson is of especial importance, as it is
dressed in his garments. All the figures are
attired in rich and appropriate costumes. We
wonder if those specimens were the origin of
modern wax work.
