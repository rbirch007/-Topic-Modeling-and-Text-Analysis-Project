NORTH TRANSEPT.
	After leaving the chapel of Henry VII we
wended our way to the north transept. It is
directly opposite the poet's corner, and is
devoted to the monuments of statesmen, as the
other section is of poets. A prominent and
pleasing statue is the one to the memory of
Sir Robert Peel, with date, 1850. A states-
man worthy of honor! The service which he
rendered toward the passage of the reform
bill, with other excellent patriotic work, make
this monument of special note. But by far
the most satisfactory monument in this sec-

[Column 3]

tion is that to the great Earl Chatham. This
is one of the unusual instances where every-
thing favorable has combined to make a satis-
factory result. In the first place, the work of
the sculptor is superior, and for once he em-
ployed his power upon a worthy object. It is
by Bacon. The monument is large, being
thirty feet in height, and cost six thousand
pounds. There is a pyramidal shaft, and the
figures of Britannia, Prudence, and Forti-
tude are represented. The slender figure of
the great earl is seen, in a peaceful attitude,
near the top. The monument is located close
to a door, and attracts the eye in an imposing
manner as one enters. There is likewise a
monument to Henry Grattan, the Irish cham-
pion, with date, 1820. The eye is attracted
to the statue of Wilberforce, the Abolitionist,
the man by whose instrumentality the slaves
in Jamaica were emancipated, which act was
the forerunner of  breaking the shackles of the
slaves in the United States.
