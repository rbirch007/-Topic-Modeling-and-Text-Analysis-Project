		WESTMINSTER HALL.
	This famous place had its location near the
houses of parliament.  Perhaps no place in
England is more replete in historical remi-
niscence than is Westminster hall.  Many im-
portant state trials as well as banquets took
place there.  To take the former in order, we
have first the famous one of Charles I.  Then
come the trial and acquittal of the seven
bishops, in the reign of James II.  In this
place Lord Lovat, the wicked Lord Byron, in
1765, and Warren Hastings, in 1768, were
tried.  The trial of Warren Hastings is known
to everybody in the brilliant pages of Macaulay.
In this grand hall coronation banquets took
place, from the time of William Rufus, for
whom it was named, to that of George IV. For
several centuries it was the seat of the courts,
and until recently, when a building of great
extent was erected for the purpose, near Temple
Bar.
	It will be remembered that a few years ago
Westminster hall was destroyed by dynamite.
Outside of this hall hung the decapitated heads
of Cromwell, Ireton, and Bradshaw, after the
restoration.  Hare informs us that the head of
Cromwell is still in existence, and is in the
possession of a person by the name of William-
son, at Seven Oaks, Kent.  This is the only
instance where an embalmed head has been
decapitated.  The story concerning it is as
follows:  One wintry night, as the sentry was
pacing backward and forward in front of the
building, the head fell down before him.  He
picked it up, hid it under his coat, and after-
ward sold it.  On his death-bed he confessed
what he had done.  After several shiftings
about, it reached the gentleman above men-
tioned.  And this is the head of that great man
who made the world tremble.

To what base uses we may return, Horatio!
Imperial Caesar, dead and turned to clay,
Might stop a hole to keep the wind away.

	Thus we come to the end of that long
chapter, Westminster abbey.
