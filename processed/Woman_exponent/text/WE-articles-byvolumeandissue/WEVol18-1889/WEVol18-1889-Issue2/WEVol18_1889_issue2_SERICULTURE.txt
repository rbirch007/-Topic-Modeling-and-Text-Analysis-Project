SERICULTURE.
	Several young girls were taught the reeling of
silk in Provo at the late Sister Beebee’s residence
and we sent quite a number of the skeins East to
be worked up. The Silk Association of Utah
Stake emigrated a woman, a silk weaver from
England to Provo; who wove dress goods, and
100 Handkerchiefs were made by her and another
sister in this settlement, and we donated 30 yards
of silk goods to the Manti Temple as furnishings.
We sent to the Chamber of Commerce a silk
handkerchief and reeled and spun silk which went
East in the Exposition Car.
	We are now having 50 pounds of Cocoons spun
which we intend making into dress goods. There
is a great need felt for the proper machinery for
manufacturing the silk, but the sisters of this
County do not feel like letting the silk industry
die out, though we realize all new enterprises
have to encounter many disadvantages, but we
still have faith in President Brigham Young's as-
surances on the silk question.
				M. A. TILL, Prest.
				JANE P. JONES, Secy.
	Brother Graves wishes made public in the
columns of the Exponent that Congress last year
donated $20,000 to the silk industry but confined
the reeling of silk to the District of Columbia as
experimental.
	He received this spring from the Department at
Washington, a number of Official Envelopes and
Circulars for distribution, also schedules to be
answered; enquiring the number of trees raised
for feeding silk worms, what experience and suc-
cess heretofore in raising cocoons, and the amount
of eggs wanted which would be furnished gra-
tuitously.
	Brother Graves made it known through the
papers and received applications for the same
from as far north as Cache Co., and to St. George
in Washington Co. Several persons in Provo
have taken eggs it being fully understood that the
market price will be given for all cocoons raised,
or if preferred they will be worked up on shares.
						M. A. TILL,
	President Utah Co., Silk Assocation.
Provo, June 5th, 1889.
