							FLORA.


IT is not well for us to cherish the habit
of dwelling much on the faults and short-
comings of those with whom we live. It
makes us more critical than generous. It
effects the cordiality of our manner toward
them. It insensibly lessens our confidence.
It interferes with the delicious ease and free-
dom of our intercourse with them. It col-
ors the remarks that we make about them.
It is said of the virtuous woman, whose
price is above rubies, that the law of kind-
ness is in her tongue. But in order to be in
the tongue it must first be in the heart, and
the habit of dwelling much on the imper-
fections of our friends and associates will
soon drive it from us.
