FLOWERS.

	HOW many troubles might mankind be
spared if they would only wait to hear each
other's explanations!
	SOME persons are above our anger, and
some below it. To contend with our superiors
is indiscreti on and with our inferiors is in-
dignity.
	EVERY person has two educations—one
which he receives from others, and one,
more important, which he gives himself.—
Gibbon.
	Do not complain of life's trials. Through
these you may gain incomparably higher
good than through indulgence and ease.
	EVERY kind word and feeling, every good
deed and thought, every noble action and
impulse, is like the ark-sent dove, and re-
turns from the troubled waters of life bear-
ing a green olive-branch to the soul.
	CONVEY thy love to thy friend as an ar-
row to the mark, to stick there, not as a ball
against the wall, to rebound back to you.
That friendship will not continue to the end
that is begun for an end.—Enchiridion.
	THERE can be no friendship where there
is no freedom. Friendship loves a free air,
and will not be penned in straight and nar-
row inclosures. It will speak freely, and act
so too, and take no ill where no ill is meant;
nay, where it is, it will easily forgive, and
forget, too, on small acknowledgements.
	THE son of Emir had red hair, of which
he was ashamed, and wished to dye it.
But his father said, "Nay, my son, rather
behave in such a manner that all fathers
should wish their sons had red hair."
	GET close to your children—find out what
they are thinking—their aims and purposes,
what traits of character they admire, and
which they are most disposed to imitate.
Be kind, genial and sympathizing. Make
goodness attractive to them, Don’t be
afraid of nearness.
