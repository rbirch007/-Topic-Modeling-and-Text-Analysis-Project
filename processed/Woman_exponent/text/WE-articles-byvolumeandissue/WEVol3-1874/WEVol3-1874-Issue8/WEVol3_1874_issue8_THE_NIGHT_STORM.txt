		THE NIGHT STORM.

The sun has set behind the western hills,
	And lurid clouds hang o’er the sky;
While sighing winds in murmurs strangely sweet,
	Are whispering that a storm is nigh.

The fast approaching clouds still swifter fly,
	And shroud-like hide the stars from sight;
The moon-beams struggle to be seen a while,
	But soon are hid in deepest night.

Hark! now the distant thunders roll along,
	As peal on peal they nearer sound;
And flashes quicker in succession come,
	And light the darkness all around.

The clouds pour forth on earth their watery stores,
	And vivid tongues of flame still flash;
While thunders, louder, quicker than before,
	Come down with deaf'ning, frightful crash.

Ah! now the leaden clouds fly fast away,
	As if pursuing some retreating foe;
The angry tumult of the storm is past,
	And silence reigns above, below.

The sobbing wind at last is hushed to sleep;
	The moon's pale rays more radiant seem;
The stars are peeping through the rifted clouds,
	And earth and flowers with frangrance teem.

O! Would that thus the storms of life could pass;
	Each dark'ning cloud roll from my sky,
And leave my soul in brighter glow to shine,
	And make me fitter, after each, to die.
									J. E. D.
Salt Lake City, Aug. 29, 1874.
