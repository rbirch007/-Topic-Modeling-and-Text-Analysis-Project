	MOST RESPECTFULLY TO QUE
		(EXPONENT, Jan. 15th, 1875.)

Dear Queery: 'Tis wrong,—that I freely admit,—
	To judge from a standard too low;
And that I did, was not that the bright opposite
	I am not so blest as to know.
God bless him! who knows and is free to affirm
	Women's lot has been low for her worth; 
Who holds her his equal while in her soul burn
	Thoughts and aims of as heavenly birth.
Who holds that no sex 'mong the passions is known,
	Love and virtue as holy in all,
That daughter and son God will equally own
	When made pure and redeemed from the fall
Yet, this Ideal, noble, unselfish and grand,
	Still winning, and tender, and sweet,
The lovely, the Christlike example of man,
	Deny not, 'Tis rarely we meet.
When beloved by such, whom we proudly revere,
	We are not quite at ease even then—
From fair heights of joy we look down in pale fear
	Lest our sisters fall victims to men
Who know not, who care not if Love be divine
	And woman beloved of God,
Who scorn her as being "a frail, clinging vine"
	And who'd trample her down to the sod.
Who think that God made her a slave to man's will;
	If not, HE will will make her so yet,
Who will win her affections with cunningest skill
	And lead her to lifelong regret,
I wish her to learn to be  wary and wise
That by such she may never be won,
That poor, sightless Love may be furnished with eyes
	And never cool judgment outrun.
That without losing faith in Humanitys worth,
	She may set a just value on each,
And judge righty between the things hoped for on earth
	And those that are now within reach.
When her own Priesthood quickens her spiritual sight
	'Til she know whence her love would return,
'Twill then be quite safe to give Love a free flight,
	And—as safe just to wait till he come.
									LU DALTON.
Beaver, Jan. 23, 1875.
