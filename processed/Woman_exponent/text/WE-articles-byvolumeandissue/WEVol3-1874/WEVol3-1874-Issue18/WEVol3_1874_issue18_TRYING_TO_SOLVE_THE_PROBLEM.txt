TRYING TO SOLVE THE PROBLEM.

	A lady visiting Salt Lake City, chanced
to fall in company with a Mormon lady,
when the following conversation ensued.
	Stranger. How long have you resided in
the City?
	Mormon. Nineteen years.
	S. Are you Mormon?
	M. I am.
	S. I suppose you are acquainted with
President Young and family?
	M. I am.
	S. How many wives has he got?
	M. I do not know, but those I am acquain-
ted with are noble women.
	S. What do you think of Brigham Young?
	M. I think his history proves him to be
one of the greatest benefactors of the pre-
sent age.
	S. In what do you consider him a pub-
lic benefactor?
	M. He has not only gathered the poor
from the nations of the earth by preaching
the gospel, but reclaimed the desert, which
has become a fruitful field and peaceful
home for the oppressed of all nations.
	S. Have you received evidences of the
truth of Mormonism?
	M. I have.
	S. What are they?
	M. When I investigated the doctrines
taught by the missionaries I found them to
be the same as those contained in the Bible,
and prayed earnestly to God to make known
to me if He had revealed Himself by mani-
festations of His power as he did anciently; I
received a testimony that I cannot doubt,
and this is the reason that I am here.
	S. Do you believe in polygamy?
	M. I do.
	S. What evidences have you that polyga-
my is acknowledged of God?
	M. I have history of the ancient Patri-
archs and the testimony that God has given
me, that it is accepted of Him.
	S. Has your husband another wife?
	M. He has.
	S. And did you give your consent?
	M. I did.
	S. Are you the first wife?
	M. I am. 
	S. How long did you live with your husband
before he took another?
	M. Fifteen years.
	S. How long have you lived in polygamy?
	M. Ten years.
	S. How did you feel when your husband
took another wife?
	M. I suppose I felt something like Abra-
ham did when he went to offer up Isaac. 
	S. Are you satisfied with your position?
	M. I am thankful that I have had the
privilege of living in polygamy.
	S. Are you happy now?
	M. As happy as I can be.
	S. I cannot comprehend you.
	M. I do not suppose you can. I will ex-
plain a little. Nowhere in the revelations

[Column 2]
of God to man do we find salvation and
exaltation promised to any individual who
has received a knowledge of God, only
through proving his or her integrity by sac-
rifice. 
	S. Do all the Mormon ladies take that
view of the subject?
	M. They accept polygamy as a revelation
from God, believing it to be a command-
ment. 
	S. Do you think it has a tendency to re-
generate posterty?
	M. I do think if the physiological and
paternal laws that should govern maternity
were understood and carried out, posterity
would be greatly improved. 
	S. Do you think Mormon women will
continue to sustain plurality under the
present circumstances?
	M. I believe every true woman will de-
fend it under all circumstances, when she's
convinced that it is true.
	S. If we admit that it is true, it comes so
in contact with my feelings, I never could
bear it.
	M. A noble woman will not consult her
feelings when she is fully persuaded that a
whole people is to be benefitted. There was
Josephine, the wife of Napoleon; and also
Sarah, Abraham's wife who were called to
make such sacrifices, and whose names are
held in honorable remembrance.
	S. I admit your reasoning is philosophi-
cal, and yet I cannot see how they can be
happy.
	M. Be happy in doing right and trying to
elevate and save woman from the prostitu-
tion and disgrace which curses the whole
nation with degeneracy and darkness.
	S. So you think polygamy is calculate to
elevate woman do you?
	M. I think that was the design of our
Creator in revealing it.
	S. I would rather have one Isaac than to
have a dozen lackeys. I do not see how a
man can take care of so many children.
	M. God takes care of those who keep His
commandments. If you were going to form
a colony, or fight a battle, who would you
call upon; the man who could command
twenty sons or the man with five?
	S. If you have a Napoleon perhaps he
can command five thousand.
	M. The revelations of God teach us that
it is a blessing for man to perpetuate his
name upon the earth, and a curse if he
loses his identity.
	S. I have been very much interested in
the conversation, and regret that I have to
go on the train at four o'clock.
	M. We, as a people, take pleasure in dis-
abusing the minds of strangers when they
visit us, as we are aware that we are greatly
misrepresented abroad.
	S. That's very true, for I could not have
believed what I have seen and heard had I
not stopped in the city.
	M. I hope you will not forget to correct
any misrepresentations you may hear con-
cerning us—
	S. I certainly will not. I am surprised
and interested in finding this people so dif-
ferent from what I expected, and I thank
you for the information you have given me
and regret so soon to say goodby.
							  W. E.
