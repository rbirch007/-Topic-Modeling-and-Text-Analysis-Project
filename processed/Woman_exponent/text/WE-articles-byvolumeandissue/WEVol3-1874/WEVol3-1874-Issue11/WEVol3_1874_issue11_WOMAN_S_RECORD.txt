WOMAN'S RECORD.

	LADY Marian Alford has laid the foun-
dation stone of St. John the Evangelist,
in Holborn England.
	MISS Mary Gray Retray has left a legacy
of 300l. to the Charity Organization So-
ciety of Great Britian.
	QUEEN Victoria has asked for an allow-
ance of $75,000 a year for her youngest son,
Prince Leopold, and after a little snarl the
House of Commons granted it.
	THE New York daily papers announce the
death of Mary Talcott, aged 77 years, who
is described as having been for sixty years a
most faithful servant of the late Governor
Van Ness.
	MISS Mitchell, Professor of Astronomy in
Vassar College, is going to give a course of
lectures on her favorite science, at Gothic
Hall Boarding School in Stanford Ct., dur-
ing the coming school year.
	MISS Mary Ellis, formerly Acting Princi-
pal of Mt. Holyoke Seminary, who has
been touring in Greece, Palestine and
Egypt since her connection with Mrs.
Gould's school at Rome, takes the position
of Lady Principal in Iowa College.
	THE famous Mrs. Ann Hutchinson, whose
religious notions and teachings caused such
consternation and excitement in the infant
settlement of Massachusetts in 1638, became
first known, trusted and warmly loved there,
because of her freely rendered offices of care
and skill to the sick of her own sex. 
	MISS Carrie J. Dresser, a teacher of ex-
perience, has achieved a success in the con-
servative town of Fitchburg. The charge
of the grammer school there, hitherto pre-
sided over exclusively by gentlemen, has
been given her, and she is to receive equal
remuneration for equal service. 
	MRS. Scott, of Hope College, Michigan,
wanted a dose of citrate of potash, and took
strychnine by mistake. As soon as she be-
came aware of the mistake she swallowed a
glass of milk and some mustard, and sent
for a physician, who administered powerful
emetics, and saved her life. 
	FORTY of the Rev. George Wheeler's
parishioners at Concord, Mass., both men
and women, cut, cured and got in fifteen
acres of his grass in one day, recently, and
the champion of the crowd was Miss Emma
Wheeler of Providence R.I., who mowed a
wide swath twenty-five rods long, which
handsomely beat every man present.
	THE young Empress of Japan has visited
the boys' and girls' colleges and schools
which her empire contains. She has been
present at the lessons and distributed re-
wards to the pupils. It seems also that she
has refused to shave her eye-brows and to
blacken her teeth, a duty which is expected
of every married lady in Japan. She also
wears English shoes, drives in coupes, &c. 
	IN the western corner of the old burial
ground in Charlston is a solid and well-
wrought slate monument, erected more than
a century and a half ago, to the memory of
a notable woman "who was ordained and
commissioned by the Lord Bishop of Lon-
don to act as mid-wife in His Majesty's Pro-
vince of Massachusetts Bay." The original
inscription probably credited her with
"1300" babies as the trophies of her skill;
but some rougish hand hid in the grass has
interpolated two ciphers in the broad spread
characters of the inscription, so that it reads
"130,000 children."


﻿Names of countries.—Europe signifies
a country of white complexions; so named
because the inhabitants there were of light-
er complexion than those of either Asia or
Africa. Africa signifies the land of corn, or
ears. It was celebrated for its abundance of
corn and all kinds of grain. Spain, a coun-
try of rabbits, or conies. This country was
once so infested with these animals that the
inhabitants petitioned Augustus for an
army to destroy them. Italy, a country of
pitch; from its yielding great quantities of
pitch. Gaul, modern France, signifies yel-
low-haired; as yellow hair characterized its
first inhabitants. Britain, the country of
tin, as there were great quantities of lead
and tin found on the adjacent islands. The
Greeks called it Albion, which signifies
either white or high mountains, from the
whiteness of its shores, or the high rocks on
the western shores.
