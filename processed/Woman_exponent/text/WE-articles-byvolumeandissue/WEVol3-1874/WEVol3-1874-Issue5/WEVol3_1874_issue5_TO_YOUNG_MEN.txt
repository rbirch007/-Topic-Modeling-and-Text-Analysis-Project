TO YOUNG MEN.

	Young men have it, as an excuse for leav-
ing the farm, that educated, well-bred
young women turn up their noses at and
will not associate with nor marry farmers;
and they fling out that the women of the
day are too refined and delicate, too lazy for
work and working men; but they are mis-
taken. It is not labor, but coarseness and
vulgarity that they turn up their noses at;
it is not the sun-browned, toil-hardened
hand, but the dirt, that they refuse; it is
not health-giving, soul-elevating work, but
slavish, degenerating drudgery and repul-
sive surroundings that they shun. No wo-
man who is worthy the love and respect of
a true man would shirk the labor and re-
sponsibilities of his calling, whatever it
might be; and there is no sensible one that
would not prefer an educated, well-bred,
robust farmer, to a pale, sallow, nervous
professional man.
	Cultivated, unselfish manliness is more
desirable in a husband than effeminacy and
gentlemanly leisure; and because a man
toils need he be a niggard?—becase he
plows and hoes need he be a boor?—because
he tills the soil and cleans the stables need
he forever go about and sit at his fireside
with the filth and the smell clinging to him?
—because his life is a life of labor, need he
spend his rainy days and winter evenings
in lolling and dozing, and live and die in 
ignorance?
