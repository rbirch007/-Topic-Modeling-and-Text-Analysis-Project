THE PERAMBULATOR.

	Do not be without one. Nothing is better for
the baby or its mother than a walk out, in the
frest, pure air and merry sunshine. But there
is something much better for the mother, when
for a few brief moments she gets release from
the many cares and duties ever existing inside
the threshold of home, than carrying in her
arms a great, bouncing baby that wants to be
twisting about this way and that, to stare in
wonder at every object they pass, getting the
wind in its face and the wrappings off its feet.
And that something is the trundling of a per-
ambulator. We do not believe in unnecessary


household expenses, neither do we think that
unnecessary waste of strength is ever profita-
ble when the final result is proven. Therefore,
we would advise every mother who has a baby
to carry about to get a little carriage for the pur-
pose. If your husbands tell you their mothers
carried them without carriages until they were
able to walk, and they think you may do the
same with your children, remind them this
is an age of improvement. Tell them you
want to enjoy better health and raise stronger
and more perfect children than their mothers
and your mothers have done; and for this rea-
son you should be provided with every labor
saving, health promoting piece of machinery
that your circumstances will in any wise justi-
fy. Do without some other things that can
better be dispensed with, but as baby must be
cared for, let it be in the right way. Let moth-
ers take proper care of themselves, and the ba-
bies will not need half the attention that they
otherwise claim.
	Mr. Henry Dinwoodey of this city will fur-
nish you with any sort of a "baby carriage"
you wish to procure, at prices from thirteen to
twenty dollars. Mothers (or fathers) in the coun-
try, should make their babies rich enough to
ride in carriages, even if they cannot indulge
in such luxuries themselves.
