		A VOICE FROM CUMORAH.

Though not so awful grand as Sinai
In thunder, fire and clouds; yet, thy mild voice
Low whispering from the dust, hath more of God
Than Sinai's awful scene! And there is heard
In its weird depths, the chant of Prophecy
More startling than Sinai's awful thunders.
Thou art the key of keys, sent to unlock
The consummation of all precious things
Spoken by Prophets since fair Eden's morn.
Art and Science quick followed at they voice.
For if prior thou wert not, they both were vain.
By them thou goest to nations, kings and tongues,
On fierce winged lightning made for thy dispatch.
And they and thou art come to bless mankind,
And drop Millennial dew o'er all the earth,
Fresh and smiling with never-fading peace-
	Under the music of thine enchanting voice,
The roseate hue of endless summer comes
To unborn millions soon to people earth.
While Sinai's startling notes would fail to tell
Half of thy deep and grand significance.
Like a king of terrors thou dost prostrate thrones
To herald in th' eternal-Prince of Peace.
And kindling now thou art the effulgent dawn
of the Millennium a swiftly coming day,
So fraught with peace and bliss ineffable:
A long and calm and sunny morn 'twill be:
Let never-failing harps chant its glad-praise.

To God, allo'er the heavenly naradise.
Let Seraphim and Cherubim all hail
Each other at remembrance of that scene.
Let angels stop their fiery chariots
In heaven to chant back the glad refrain,
As they gaze earthward at this spectacle
Of heaven-born sublimity and awe.
And in their new-born bliss forget all else
As they echo, "Peace on earth'good will towards man;"
While Sinai bows her head in meckest awe,
With hand upon her lips and boasts no more.
