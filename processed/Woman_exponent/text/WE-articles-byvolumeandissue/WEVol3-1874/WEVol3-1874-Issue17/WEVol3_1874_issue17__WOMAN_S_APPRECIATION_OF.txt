WOMAN'S APPRECIATION OF
	WOMAN’S BEAUTY.
	*	*	*	*	*	Men—even the
wisest of them—are very easily duped by
little artifices of dress and adornment by
which women seek to beautify themselves
(for the masculine eye, shall I say?) and a
woman thus "gotten up" would rather face
a regiment of men than the scrutinizing
eye of one keen-eyed woman. For she
knows full well that her abundant locks,
her soft pearly complexion, with just the
suspicion of bloom on the cheeks, the round
waist and well developed bust, will no more
deceive that woman than a coarse clay
model will be mistaken for Parian by the
true artist.
	No, if one woman excites the admiration
of another, by her beauty, the male critic
may generally make up his mind that the
beauty under contemplation is genuine,
and no sham.
	In the street cars, on the ferry boat, in
the street, and at church, the face of a
lovely woman will excite more genuine ad-
miration in one of her own sex than in that
of the opposite; for to the true artist mind
a beautiful human being is like a flower, a
lovely landscape, a rare gem, a painting or
a statue, a subject for sincere admiration
and not one to engender envy or jealous
feelings. It is not true, then, that women
as a rule are envious of each other's beauty,
the assertion of man to the contrary not-
withstanding.—"Golden Dawn."
