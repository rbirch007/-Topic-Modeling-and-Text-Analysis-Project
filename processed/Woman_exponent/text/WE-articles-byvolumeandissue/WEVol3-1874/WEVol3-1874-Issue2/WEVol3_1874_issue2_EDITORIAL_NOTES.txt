EDITORIAL NOTES.

	ONE STRANGE fact in relation to literature
is, that a number of English writers have
gained reputations in America before re-
ceiving any attention at home.

	NATURALISTS HAVE lately decided that
crocodiles are voiceless. The young, it has
been ascertained, are endowed with a bleat-
ing voice, but the adults are utterly power-
less, so far as utterance is concerned. They
may be tortured to death without giving
forth a sound. This phenomenon is sup-
posed to be due to the gradual hardening of
the ligaments of the larynx and of the
whole internal structure of the mouth as
they advance in age. A good remedy for
the afflictions of some men who find such
great annoyances in the tongues of women,
and think they should not speak, would be
to reside a while with a family of croco-
diles.

	A LITTLE four year old girl, of Indiana,
named Rosa Mansfield Eversole, is reported
to be a musical prodigy which eclipses all
others ever known. Several meritorious
pieces of music from her compositions have
already been published in musical papers;
and after once hearing an operatic selection
on the piano she is able to repeat it. Will
she be wisely induced by her friends to let
music alone as much as possible, until she is
older and stronger, when she may become a
brilliant and powerful attraction to the mu-
sical world; or will the little heart and brain
be encouraged to hoard and bear up load
after load of melodious treasures, until they
sink and break down under too great a pres-
sure? Most likely the latter. It is usually
so.
