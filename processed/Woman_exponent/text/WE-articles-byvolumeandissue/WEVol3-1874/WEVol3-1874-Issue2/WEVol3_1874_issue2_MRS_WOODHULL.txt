		MRS. WOODHULL.

     She is most decidedly a splendid speaker.
Listening to her lecture of May 12th, given
in the Salt Lake Theatre, was to me a grand
treat, so far as eloquence was concerned.
Then, she evidently speaks what she has to
say in all sincerity, believing that her state-
ments ALL, as many of them most certainly
are, ponderous truths. Her correct showing
of the general ignorance of woman, coming
as it does from a woman, and spoken to
women, should assist in rousing, many to a
sense of the importance of their making
earnest endeavors to become woman worthy
of the names of wives and mothers.
     The face of Mrs. W. as seen through an
opera glass, tells that she is a women of sor-
row, and acquainted with grief. And listen-
ing to her appealing tones and touching ex-
planations, after she has thrown aside her
notes, and begins to speak upon the impulse
of the moment, one can feel that at some
time in her life she has known deep wrongs
which have embittered what must have
been a beautiful soul. Then arises before
the mind's eye a picture of French women
when they gather in mobs for vengance;
and the thought occurs, “if they had such a
woman to lead them, what works of ruin
would they leave behind them!” And if
the American nation is in the state she rep-
resents it, a vast body of corruption, who
can tell when the seething mass may arise
and devastate this whole nation? Reigns of
terror may not be confined to France.
