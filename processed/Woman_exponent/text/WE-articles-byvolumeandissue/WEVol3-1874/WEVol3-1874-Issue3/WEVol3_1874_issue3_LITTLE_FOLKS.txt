LITTLE FOLKS.

	JOHNNY was telling his ma how he was
going to dress and show off when he was a
man. His ma asked “Johnny, how do
you expect to get your living when you are
a man?” “Well,” said Johnny, “I’ll get
married, and live with my wife's pa."
	"WON'T you ever tell?" whispered a girl
to a group of playmates,
	“No, no; only my mother,” answered a
third.
	“But you must not tell her,”
	“I will not hear what my mother may
not know,” she replied, and withdrew. And
that girl was safe.
	COL. Tom Scot was hailed on the street,
lately by a little bootblack with; "Boss, have
yer boots shined?" The Col. pleasantly shook
his finger at him, saying. "My boy, I'm no
boss." The little waif swung his box over
his shoulder, and eyeing him from head to
foot, replied: "You'r boss of yer boots, ain't
you?"
	HOW touchingly beautiful this is! Three
little New Hampshire girls, not long since,
lost their mother by death. Hearing that a
lady friend of the family was dying, these
three little children went to their grand-
mother, called her aside, and whispered in
her ear: "Mrs. — is dying, and we want
you to go and tell her to give our best love
to our mother.
	A SCHOOL girl of one of the rural dis-
tricts of Ohio, was heard trying to convince
a school boy that she liked him better than
she did some other urchin of whom he seem-
ed jealous. "Of course I like you better than
I do Bill," said she, "for don't I miss words
in my spelling lesson on purpose, so as to
be down to the foot of the class where you
are?"
	AT a school examination, one of the visi-
tors made a brief address to the pupils on
the necessity of obeying their teachers and
growing up loyal and useful citizens. To
give emphasis to his remarks, he pointed
to a large national flag on one side of the
room and enquired, "Boys, what is that
flag for?" A little urchin who understood
the condition of the house better than the
speaker, promptly answered, "To cover up
the dirt, sir."
