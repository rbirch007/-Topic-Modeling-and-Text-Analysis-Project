MOTHERS, TEACH YOUR DAUGHTERS.

	I am inclined to think we are apt to un-
der-estimate the abilities of the girls of the
present time. If they could have a little
better chance and much more credit for
what they do, we should soon see a great
improvement. There are few, especially
farmer's daughters, but know how to per-
form various kinds of household duties.  If
they do not, the fault is not all their own.
I think girls now-a-days just as ambitious
as they were in our grandmothers' days—
grandmothers' stories to the contrary not-
withstanding.  People are so very forgetful.
There is a time when every child is anxious
to learn about everything, especially what
mother does, and they are too frequently
put off until some other time.
	It requires much patience and not a little
skill to teach new beginners, but it must be
done or they must remain in ignorance, and
when they are grown up bear the blame.
They can't learn just by seeing mother do
the work day after day; they must have
experience to do a piece of work well. If
mother would teach them how to do, then
leave the care to them, let them feel that the
result depended on them, they would make
use of what they had learned and at the
same time take an interest in the work. We
should not expect them to do as well as an
old, experienced hand at first; but praise
them all you can and pass the mistakes by
lightly as possible. It will give them cour-
age to try again. And soon they will take
much of the care off mother's mind, and
be a constant delight to their parents. They
would feel better contented at home, and
save their parents pain and mortification in 
after years when they leave the paternal
roof for other homes, where no mother will


be near to superintend the work or teach
them how to begin.  Domestic economy
must begin at the commencement in order
to prove effectual in all our ways.
			A YOUNG FARMER'S WIFE.
