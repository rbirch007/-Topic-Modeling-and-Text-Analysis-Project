PRESERVE YOUR PAPERS.

	A few weeks since, we happened to be present
when a Swedish brother was making inqui
ries of a gentleman concerning words and fig-
ures on a paper which he had received from
the tax collector. The paper was to inform
him that his taxes for the present year were
due and that he had been delinquent in last
year's payment. How could it mean that, the
man wanted to know, when he had another
paper which had been given him and which
he produced to show that last year his taxes
were all paid. Sure enough the mistake had
been made by the assessor and collector, who
must have failed to credit the man in his book,
for payment of which he had given a receipt
But the Swedishman had wisely preserved
the receipt, the presentation of which would
be sufficient to save him the necessity of pay-
ing his taxes twice for one year.
	Lately an assertion was officially made
against a gentleman who is a native of Great
Britain, in which he was said to be not a citi-
zen of the United States. But being able to
produce the recorded certificate of his naturali-
zation, the matter was put at rest.
	Some persons in this Territory have not
proved so lucky, from the simple fact of their
not having learned in years past, the impor-
tance of preserving papers, which might
then have seemed of little consequence, but
would now be of great value could they be
produced.
