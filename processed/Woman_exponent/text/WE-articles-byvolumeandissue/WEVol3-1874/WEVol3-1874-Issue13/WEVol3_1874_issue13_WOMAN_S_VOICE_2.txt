WOMAN'S VOICE.
			BEAVER, DEC. 8th, 1874.
MRS. L. GREENE RICHARDS,
	DEAR FRIEND:—Allow me thus to ad-
dress you, although I have not had the pleas-
ure of personally knowing you.
	We had a pleasant time last evening at
Sister Tyler’s, although she is quite ill.
While I was making a call there, your
father and mother came in on their way to
St. George. We had the privilege of re-
ceiving our Patriarchal Blessings from your
father, which to me, is no small thing. We
do not often meet with any of—“The good
old Stand Bys”—I call them, in these times 
when there is such a mixture of Gentile and
Jew, those who think they are Mormons and
those who know they are not, but manage
to keep a “coat of many colors” which may
be turned and re-turned just to suit conve-
nience and cheat the wear of public opinion.
Brother Tyler and my husband are Patri-
archs also, and we had a blessing meeting
last evening in which we truly rejoiced.
Sister Tyler was full of faith and we are in
hopes she will reap much benefit from the
visit. My heart beats with warmest feelings
of respect and love forthose staunch and true
spirits—the bold Pioneers of this Church.
They are slowly passing away from our
midst, and I feel as I think of it to-day,
what a great privilege it would be to gather
all who are now living, in some comfortable,
commodious place, and have a feast once
more, a feast of fat things, a feast of the
beauties of the Kingdom, a feast of thought
and experience brought forward to cheer
and instruct, ere many last farewels should
be said.
	The people of Beaver are striving to do
as near right as possible, though oftentime
it seems against fearful odds, those who de-
light in troubling the Saints being numer-
ous about here at present.
	With many kind wishes, I am sincerely
yours,					A.	B.	C.
