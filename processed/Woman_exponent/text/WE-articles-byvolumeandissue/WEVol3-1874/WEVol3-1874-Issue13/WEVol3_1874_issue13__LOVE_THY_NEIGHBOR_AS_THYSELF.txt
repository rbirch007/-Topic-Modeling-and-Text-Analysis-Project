LOVE THY NEIGHBOR AS THYSELF.

	All day long this commandment of our
Savior has been sounding in may ear, as
though He were repeating again the words
spoken so many hundred years ago; and
wherever I go I seem to see them writ-
tea in letters bold and indelible.
	Many, many times I have asked, what
did He mean when He. said “Love thy
Neighbor as thyself,” and left the com-
mandment to be handed down to all future
generations?
	Did He know how strongly developed is
the love of self, how it deafens the ear to
the cries of our fellow creatures, how it
blinds the eye to their pain and wants, and
chills or crushes out the purest and most
noble impulses of the heart? Did He real-
ize the weakness of human nature and still
speak those words to us, and expect obedi-
ence? Would He tell us to do that which is
impossible, or did he speak in jest or mock-
ery?
	It is a state of' perfection we must attain
unto before we are prepared for His pres-
ence and glory, and if so what are its re-
quirements?
	Does it merely imply a willingness, or
even a desire to share with others the bless-
ings, the plenty and happiness that we en-
joy, retaining still our portions? Or is it to
stand by and see some object for which we
have lived, have hoped and prayed through-
out long days and years, given to another,
and with words of joy on the lips, and joy
in the heart—such joy as we had felt had
the prize been ours—thank God, and be con-
tent and happy in anothers joy?
	The subject is worthy of our deepest
thought and most careful study, and of ear-
nest endeavor to think less of ourselves and
more of the suffering humanity around us;
