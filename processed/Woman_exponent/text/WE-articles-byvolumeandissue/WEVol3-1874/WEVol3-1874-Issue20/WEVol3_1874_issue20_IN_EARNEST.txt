IN EARNEST.

	Fortunes, both of time and money, may be
spent in selecting and collecting books. The
works of renowned historians, scientists, doc-
tors, naturalists, philosophers and theologians,
and of all the great artists and poets may be
gathered, and a most complete library formed.
But, to what will all that amount?
	What will it profit one to be the owner of a
thousand precious volumes, who cares not to
familiarize and enrich the mind with the truths
which they contain? Not so much as the pos-
session of a single volume will the careful stu-
dent who becomes thoroughly acquainted with
it.
	Or even if the mind should be stored with
great treasures of wisdom, what benefit can be
realized therefrom, what reward met for all the
time spent, and pains taken, except the infor-
mation gained is applied to practical use?
	Laws and notes pertaining to physiology, hy-
giene and longevity may be studied for years
without the slightest beneficial result, if the
knowledge received be not utilized. While a
week’s reading, if put to proper account, may
accomplish a vast deal of good.
	The same rule applies to all other acquire-
ments. Wealth may be amassed, but if not
used to adorn and beautify, to enliven and make
happy, it were as well that it be scattered
broadcast as placed in an unserviceable, grace-
less heap.
	For many years the Latter-day Saints have
had the privilege of being instructed in the
ways of eternal life and salvation. The honest,
the pure, the sincere among them, those who
have been earnest in seeking to learn, realize
that their labors have in no wise been in vain.
They have treasured up wisdom for practical
purposes.


And still it is necessary that they become
more and more in earnest in striving to do the
will of the Lord on earth as it is done in heav
en, that truth may triumph and the enemies o
righteousness may be o'erthrown.
