TWO SECRETARYSHIPS TO BE FILLED.
	Miss Kate N. Gordon asks us to announce
that she will not be a candidate for re-elec-
tion as corresponding secretary of the Na-
tional American Suffrage Association at the
coming National Convention in Seattle. Her
local work in New Orleans is constantly
making greater demands upon her, and she
feels that she can no longergive the needed
amount of time and strength to the national
correspoding secretaryship. This will be
much regretted; but Miss Gordon gives us
to understand that her decision is final.
	Miss Alice Stone Blackwell also gives no-
tice that she will not stand for re-election as
recording secretary.				Ex.

	Harvard has always thought it beneath its
dignity to confer a degree on any woman,
no matter how distinguished. Brown has
already honored four women in this way.
Sarah Elizabeth Doyle was given the degree
of A. M. in 1894; Julia Josephine Irvine,
president of Wellesley College, was made a
Litt. D. in 1895; Adelia Thompson Hillman
was giyen an A. M. in 1898, and Caroline
Hazard, president of Wellesley, was made a
Litt. D. in 1899.—Ex.
