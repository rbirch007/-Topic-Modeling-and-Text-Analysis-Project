L. L. GREENE RICHARDS.


I think women capable of a great deal
more work than they have been accustomed
to in times past. If overwork sometimes
leads them to disease, it is morally more
wholesome to work into it than to lounge
into it.—Sir Spencer Wells.
	Mrs. Meynell, the poet, declines to draw
a mental line between men and women.
“It is not so much a question of men and
women,” she says, “as of individuals.
Many women are wofully lacking in obser-
vation and decidedly dull in perception
while, on the other hand, many men fail
miserably in reasoning power, and many
women excel in it.” Mrs. Meynell is a
suffragist.
	Lady Huggins is as gifted an astronomer
as was Caroline Herschel or Maria Mitchel.
It was her profound and self-acquired
knowledge of this science that attracted to
the unassuming Irish lady the brilliant
astronomer, Sir William Huggins. Lady
Huggins has not sought favor for herself
but is content to be her husband’s assistant,
and every night she works in their observa-
tory by his side. To her is due a share of
the honor acheived by Sir William for his
discovery by means of the spectroscope, of
the composition of the heavenly bodies.
