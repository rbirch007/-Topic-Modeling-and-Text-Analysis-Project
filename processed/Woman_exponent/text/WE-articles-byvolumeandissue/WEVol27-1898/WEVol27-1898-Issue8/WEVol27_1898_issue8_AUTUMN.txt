AUTUMN.

Dismal, doleful katydid
	Sad is thy song this autumn night,
It speaks of death to the budding rose,
	It tells of the swallow’s homeward flight—
Yes every bud will droop and die,
And every bird will southward fly.

The bees that in the forest rove,
	Have gathered in their winter’s store,
And soon will hide themselves away,
	Where we shall hear their songs no more—
Happy, busy little bee,
Sadly do we part with thee.

The meadow brook must speed away
	And join the river deep and wide,
The squirrels must cease their merry play,
	And in the giant oak tree hide—
Frisking squirrel and babbling brook,
We give to both a last, long look.

Waving trees with golden garb,
	Softly sigh of dreary days,
And beyond the hill crest high
	See the veil of gathering haze—
Hark! I hear a mournful bell,
	’Tis sweet summer’s funeral knell.

Summertime with beauty rare,
	Decked with flowers rich and gay,
Strewing sunshine everywhere,
	Why—Oh! why canst thou not stay?
An echo through the mountain rings,
And sorrowfully this answer brings:

What we prize so quickly fades, 
	All we love must someday die,
Even thou must leave us soon,
	And soar above yon azure sky,
There summertime with garlands fair
A crown will make which thou shalt wear.
