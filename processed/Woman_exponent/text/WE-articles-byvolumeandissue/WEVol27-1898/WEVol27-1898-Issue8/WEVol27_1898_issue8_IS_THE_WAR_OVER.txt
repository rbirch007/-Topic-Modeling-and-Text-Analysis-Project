IS THE WAR OVER?

They say that peace has been proclaimed
	And the dreadful war is o’er,
And the chime of bells the glad news tells
	As it wafts from shore to shore.
The shouts of acclamation rise
	From every heart and tongue,
Of the deeds of valor of our men
	And the noble work they’ve done.

They have counted up the men that fell
	On each conflicting side;
And as results are brought to light
	They smile with National pride— 
As figures do not make mistakes
	But counteth all things plain, 
So we find but few of our boys in blue,
	Were numbered with the slain.

And from the facts as claimed above
	Expressions have been free, 
That from the first no land could claim, 
Such wondrous victory,
Success at[-]ended every move, 
And not by cha[-]ce they say,
But Providence did intervene
	And lead them on their way.

But bear with me and I will dare
	A protest to main[-]ain,
And think it far too soon to count
	The number of the slain,
As there are still, and yet may be
	More added to the dead,
Such is the news that greets our ears
	As the papers o’er are read. 
Oh! piteous sight on those cots laid low
	Those once brave, stalwart forms,
Who faced the dangers day and night
	Through scorching heat and storms.
Seventy hours ’tis said they fought, 
Almost unceasingly,
With purely true, ueselfish aim
	In the cause of liberty.

Oh! do not shout aloud too soon
	Nor count the victory won; 
Till each mother’s boy is in her arms
	That death don’t claim its own;
For there are weapons worse by far 
Than swords that hew men down,
And aggravate their sufferings more 
As fever, chills and wounds. 
LETITIA WILLIAMS.
October 14, 1898.
