RED CROSS.

	Clara Barton, president of the National
Red Cross; George Kennan, first vice-
president and D. L. Cobb counsel, appeared
before the Executive board of the American
National Red Cross Relief committee
yesterday afternoon in the Chamber of
Commerce to answer questions concerning
the standing of the organization they rep-
resent. The meeting was behind closed doors.
	Doubts heretofore expressed by members
of the committee, notably by Vice-Chairman
E. Orr, as to the exact status of the Red
Cross, were according to Secretary John P.
Faure, completely dissipated by Miss Barton
and her associates. Mr. Orr was not pres-
ent, but William G. Low appeared as his
proxy.
	A week ago two members of the National
Red Cross asserted that the members of the
association were ten in number. Miss Barton
quoted it as a society of over thirty, whose
meetings were merely for work in the field,
"entirely unhampered by elective commit-
tees." This hitherto regarded as an ob-
jection was apparently sanctioned by the
Relief committee.
	"Miss Barton's telling address," said Mr.
Faure, "descriptive of Red Cross work car-
ried every man off his feet. Many wept."
	Mr. Kennan spoke especially of the busi-
ness machinery of the Red Cross—its care-
fulness of distribution, economy of adminis-
tration and completeness of record of its
doings.
	The committee was entirely satisfied and
commended the methods and practice
of the Red Cross as described by Miss Bar-
ton and the others. The assertion that
Miss Barton had appeared before the Relief
Committee to vindicate the Red Cross is in-
dignantly scouted, and the further announce-
ment that a special staff would be appointed
from its ranks to accompany her on her re-
turn journey southward was also denied.
