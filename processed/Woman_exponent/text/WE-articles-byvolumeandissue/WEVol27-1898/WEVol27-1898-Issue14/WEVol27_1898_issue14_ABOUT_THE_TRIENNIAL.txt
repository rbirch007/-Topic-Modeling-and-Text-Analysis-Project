ABOUT THE TRIENNIAL.

	The ladies who attended the sessions of
the Triennial of the National Council of Wo-
men of the United States in Washington, D.
C., in 1899, will probably never forget the
impression it has made and the benefits re-
ceived. To those who have never been pres-
ent the general uplifting cannot be too greatly
emphasized. The banding together of more
than a million women to influence the ac-
complishment of great purposes must neces-
sarily be a power in any given direction. It
is not so much individual as collective
power and the individual speeches made,
however brilliant, are not the predominating
influence. It is the united force of the whole
that gives such tremendous weight to the en-
tire effect. The propagation of the ideas em-
bodied in the platform of the Council express
forcibly in a few words what is expected, and
one can judge from that what the future will
