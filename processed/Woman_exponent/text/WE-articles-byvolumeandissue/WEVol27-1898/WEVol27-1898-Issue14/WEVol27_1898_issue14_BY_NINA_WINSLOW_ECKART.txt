BY NINA WINSLOW ECKART.
The snow lies on the frozen ground,
	The night is dark and still,
Save now and then the winds low moan
	Across some distant hill.
And i[-] a r[-]om a young man sits, 
His head is bowed in prayer,
He longs to be at home again
	Where life is free from care.

Around the room are tokens dear—
	A pic[-]ure on the wall,
Recalls to him his college days,
	His friends and classmates all. 
The room where four glad years were spent,
	In thought he sees once more,
His books and chair await him there,
	He sees the open door.

And thinks “Oh would that I could stand
	Upon that threshold bright,
And have those dear, familiar scenes
	To cheer me up tonight.”
And then like mi[-]t before his eyes
	Another vision sweeps,
And swiftly as the life blood flows, 
	Around his heart it creeps.

He hears again his mother’s voice,
	And on his fevered brow
Her gentle hand again is placed,
	(Oh, were she with him now!)
The tears unbidden fill his eyes, 
	Then dashing them away
He rises to his feet again,
	And now I hear him say:

“Ah, yes, my life is lonely here
	But I will not give way,
The sun behind the cloud is bright,
	Although the sky is gray. 
And when a fortune I have made,
	They’ll be so proud to know,
How I have worked and won my way,
	In all this world of woe.”

Yes, hopeful be, my friend, for Fate
	Is good to those who try,
Beneath the cold snow of despair,
	The Crocus blossoms lie,
And they will bud and bloom for you,
	Ere many days are done,
The clouds will pass from out your way,
	And the behold the sun.
