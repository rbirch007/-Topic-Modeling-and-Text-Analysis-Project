	COMPETENCE AND THRIFT.
 	(6) Every individual should be an asset
to the community in which he resides and it
is our duty to do all that is possible toward
teaching the able-bodied who are not “mak-
ing good” how to become at least self-sup-
porting. Teach that every soul should per-
form a just portion of labor. Let us en-
courage the habit of depositing a little in a
savings bank. If all would learn and prac-
tice saving, the state would be without
paupers. Discourage greed, graft, slothful-
ness, wastefulness and extravagance.. En-
courage industry and thrift and the habit of
tithing. Remember what President John
Henry Smith said to us last Conference:
“An idler, man or woman, is a menace to the
community in which he or she resides.”

		TREE PLANTING.
	(7) The poet wrote, “He who plants a
tree lives forever.” We would impress upon
you the necessity for the planting of trees,
for grove making and for forest building.
Plant the Lombardy poplar for fuel, six
trees, after a few years growth, furnish a
home with wood. The locust is even better
for fence posts than cedar. There are so
many unused spots, whole lots and corners,
upon which groves of locusts could be raised
for fencing. The English walnut and al-
mond for nut growing may now become a 
reality. The walnut furnishes a beautiful
material for cabinet work. Any and all
hardwood trees, the maple, ash, oak, mahog-
any and walnut are good to plant, for they
rapidly enhance the value of land; in fact,
every year’s growth adds greatly to its
money value. The hardwood varieties are
ideal for shade and what is more charming
than the cherry and plum and apple in blos-
som? Then the fruit trees—remember we
have set a high standard here and there can
be no turning back. We must study the best
varieties adapted to the localities in which
they shall be planted.
