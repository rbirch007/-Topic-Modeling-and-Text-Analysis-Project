PROGRESSIVE CATHOLICS.
	Catholic women have always distin-
guished themselves in church and charitable
work. In these latter days they are gradu-
ally coming into greater prominence in edu-
cation and public service.
	The Cathoilc girls’ high school in Phila-
delphia for the first time was this year ad-
dressed by a woman, Miss Agnes Repplier,
the Lateare Medallist of 1911 of the Univer-
sit[-] of Notre Dame.
	One of the six graduates from the law de-
partment of St. John's, Toledo, Ohio, was a
woman.
	It is reported that the Catholic Univer-
sity of America in Washington. D. C., is to
become co-educational in a measure.
Courses have been arranged for nuns. It is
expected that in the next few years the
work will grow and that the university will
make a special point of educating women for
orders. Sisters from Detroit, Mich., have
entered the university for the summer, and
it is expected they will pass the next two
years there working for the degree of B. A.
	Announcement is made that Sister An-
tonio Mercedes of St. Xaviers Convent,
Beatt[-]. Pa., has received the degree of doc-
tor of letters from Cardinal Gibbons. She is
the first American nun to win such a dis-
tinction. Sister Antonio. who is of Spanish
descent, belongs to the Sisters of Mercy.
For many years she has written articles on
religion under the pen name of Reverend
Richard W. Alexander.
	The "Catholic Women's Suffrage Soci-
ety," an efficient and active organization.
marched in the great suffrage procession in
London. A pamphlet which this society
takes pride in circulating is entitled "The
Views of Cardinal Moran and the Late Car-
dinal Vaughan on Woman Suffrage.”—
Woman’s Journal.
