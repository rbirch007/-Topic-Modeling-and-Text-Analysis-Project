NOTES AND NEWS.
	Mrs. Adelaide Johnson, the sculptor, has
just returned from Rome.
	It is now said to be an established fact
that women have the right to vote at Parlia-
mentary elections in Portugal.
	It has been officially announced that an
International Men’s Union to further the
cause of woman suffrage has just been
formed.
	Polish women of the City of Chicago
have organized a woman suffrage society
to be known as the Polish Political League
of America.
	During the annual Episcopal Diocesan
convention in Connecticut a few days ago,
a motion was made by Rev. Edward Living-
ston Wells, to give women an equal voice
with men in parish administration. Mr.
Wells added that in many parishes women
do a great deal, if not most of the work.
	It is estimated that from 40,000 to 60,000
people marched in the Votes for Women
procession in London, June 17. Lady Stout
headed the New Zealand section, while Mrs.
Fisher, wife of the Premier, led the Austra-
lian division, and Mrs. McGowan, wife of
the Premier of New South Wales, walked
with the delegation from that state.
	Sunday-school Teacher: “I told you last
Sunday that I wished each one of you
would try to make at least one person happy
during the week. Did you?” Boy: “Yes,
miss; I made grandma happy.” “That’s
noble. How did you do it?” “I went to
visit her, and she says she is always happy
when she sees I’ve a good appetite.”
	A school teacher told a pupil to buy a
grammar. The next day she received this
note from the child’s mother:
	“I do not desire for Lulu shall ingage in
grammar, as I prefer her to ingage in use-
ful studise and can learn her how to spoke
and write properly myself. I have went
through two grammars myself and I cant
say they did me no good, I prefer her in-
gage in german and drawing and vocal
music on the piano.”
