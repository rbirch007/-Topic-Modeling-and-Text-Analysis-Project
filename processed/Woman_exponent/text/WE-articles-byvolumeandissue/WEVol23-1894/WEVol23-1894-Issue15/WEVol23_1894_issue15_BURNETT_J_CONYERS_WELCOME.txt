BURNETT J. CONYERS WELCOME. 
	“To say that I am proud of this privilege,” said
he, “but feebly expresses the happiness I feel in
addressing this convention and in giving you the
welcome of our city, which more than counter-
balances the awkwardness of its presentation.”
(Applause.) The grace of greeting that char-
acterized the welcome of the young speaker was
profoundly appreciated by the convention. He
assured the delegates that the south was worthy
of them as they were worthy of the south. The
section had suffered from misunderstanding, but
the light of a new era was dawning upon her hills
and a morning flush was on her brow, betokening
a future of prosperity and progress. The speech
of Mr. Conyers was greeted with the wildest en-
thusiasm. He conceded that the south was con-
servative but he dared any one to say that it was
the conservatism of cowardice. She had never
repelled the logic of truth and she welcomed
this convention because it entered her gates in the
name of liberty for which she had sprinkled with
her bravest blood the battlefields of the revolu-
tion. He encouraged the delegates by giving
them his confident assurance that nothing
could prevent the ultimate triumph of the cause to
which they were devoted.
