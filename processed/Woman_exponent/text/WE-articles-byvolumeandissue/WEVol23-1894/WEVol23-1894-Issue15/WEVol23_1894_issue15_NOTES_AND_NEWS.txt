NOTES AND NEWS.

	MISS MARY E. WILKINS is busy at her
home in Randolph, Mass. on a new story.
	A CONSTITUTIONAL amendment extend-
ing full suffrage to women has passed both
branches of the Oregon Legislature.
	MRS. FRANCES KLOCK has introduced a
bill in the Colorado House providing for an
industrial school for girls and appropriating
$15,000 for its establishment and mainten-
ance.
	DR. CARRIE LIEBIG, of Hope, Idaho,
has been appointed division surgeon upon
the Northern Pacific railroad. It is said
that this is the only instance of such dis-
tinction to a lady known in the United
States.
	The Georgia W. S. A. and the Atlanta
E. S. A. will tender a reception to the
delegates and visitors to the National Con-
ention, on Monday evening, Feb. 4.  
	MISS FRANCES E. WILLARD is the third
woman to have the right to write doctor of
laws after her name. Maria Mitchell, the
astronomer, and Amelia B. Edwards, the
Egyptologist, were the others.
	HON. GEORGE FRISBEE HOAR has been
re-elected U. S. Senator for six years by the
Massachusetts Legislature, and Hon. Wm.
P. Frye was the same day re-elected U S.
Senator by the Legislature of Maine.
Suffragists offer their congratulations.—Ex.
	MRS. ELLA B. CARTER lately read be-
fore the Ray Palmer Woman’s Literary
Club of Newark, N. J., an interesting
paper on the Scriptural aspect of the wo-
man question. It is published in full in
the Newark Advertiser of Feb. 6. Mrs.
Carter argues well in behalf of equal rights,
and brings forward a host of illustrations
from the Old and New Testaments.
	The establishment of full woman suff-
rage in South Australia is in some respects
the greatest triumph ever gained for the
cause. It establishes woman’s freedom
over nine hundred and sixteen thousand
square miles of territory—a region larger
than all the United States east of the Mis-
sissippi River. It is a country destined to
become densely populated by the Anglo-
Saxon race, and to dominate the Southern
hemispheres.
