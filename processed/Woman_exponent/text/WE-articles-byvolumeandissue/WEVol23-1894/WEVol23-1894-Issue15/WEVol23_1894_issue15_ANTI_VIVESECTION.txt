ANTI-VIVESECTION.
	ONE of the latest acquisitions to the
membership of the National Council of
Women is the women of The American
Anti-Vivisection Society, who, through a
Committee of women, of which Mrs.
Caroline Earle White is Chairman, have
entered the National Council and will have


their work represented in its Triennial at
Washington, D. C., February 17th to
March 2nd.
	As this organization is so new in member-
ship, it may interest thouse who are already
in touch with the work of the National
Council of women, to know something of
the history of the Anti-Vivisection Society.
It seems particularly appropriate that these
women should have their work represented
in the Council, since the Society itself had
its origin in the thought and work of wo-
men.  It is an outgrowth from the Wo-
men’s Branch of the Society for the Pre-
vention of Cruelty to Animals. Soon after
the the formation of the latter organization,
its members interested themselves very
much in the effort to put a stop to cruel ex-
perimentation upon animals, and once or
twice some of them went to Harrisburg
with a bill restricting vivisection, which
they tried, but unsuccessfully, to have pas-
sed by the State Legislature. At length,
stimulated to increased action by the argu-
ments of Miss Frances Power Cobbe, who
declared it a disgrace to America that no
Anti-Vivisection Society existed in our
Country, a few of the most ardent among
the Women’s Branch met together and with
the aid of some outside, effected such an
organization.  This was about ten years 
ago.  The infant Society had great dis-
couragement and opposition to work against,
and at first, its progress was very slow,
but of late years a marked change is visible
and there is every reason to think that the
subject is beginning to agitate the com-
munity to a hopeful extent.
