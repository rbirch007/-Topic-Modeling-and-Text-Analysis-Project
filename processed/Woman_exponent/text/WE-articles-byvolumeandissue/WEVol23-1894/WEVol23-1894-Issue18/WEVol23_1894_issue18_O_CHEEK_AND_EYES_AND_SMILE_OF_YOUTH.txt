O, cheek and eyes and smile of youth,
	I read without your speaking:

[Column 2]
“It is not here, has passed away
	Years back, beyond your seeking.”

For bloom of health and hope of youth
	Graced e’en the simplest dressing;
What now in place of childhood’s wreath
	And parents’ fond carressing?
What do we win for what we lose
	While we are growing older,
Have we soul-jewels for bright cheeks,
	Has life grown bright, or colder?

But I forget—I take your time;
	Why should I still look vainly,
For your fair face beside my own
	In mirror answers plainly;
There is no use to argue with
	Facts all too plain and truthful,
Your dainty goods and all their grace
	Belong to faces youthful.

Just pick me out the plainest hat—
	I think at last we’ve found it;
As kindly moss, hides storm-scarred bough
	Just wreathe a veil around it,
And have it large enough, my dear,
	My face to shield and cover
So I might pass unknown beside
	My truest old-time lover.

Can’t you slip in a little tint
	To light it just the slightest,
Some where just as through clouds at night
	One star shines out the brightest?
Or, as in ashes that seem dead
	There hideth still the embers,
That wake to touch like some past joy,
	The heart oftimes remembers?

Ah, lovely milliner, how well
	You know your task and do it,
They could not tell as swift I pass,
	Who sees so plainly through it.
Bright be thy life as in thy smile
	And blessings rest upon it,
And chase forever far ahead
	As I could wish in sonnet—
The time, when you no more can wear
	The loveliest new bonnet.
			AUGUSTA JOYCE CROCHERON.
Bountiful, April, 1895.
