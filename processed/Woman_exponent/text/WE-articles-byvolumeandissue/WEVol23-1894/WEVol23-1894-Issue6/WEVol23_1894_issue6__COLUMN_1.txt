	Jane Bland promised to consider, which
to say the least was decidedly philosophical
for a woman who was actually in love, but
it was in keeping with her whole life. Mr.
Harrison felt assured from her manner that
he was to be accepted and let the matter
pass; in his heart he commended her course,
for she had Conway’s future to think of
which was more to her than her own life.
	Some days went by full of pleasant in-
tercourse, long rambles, and drives, the
weather was fine, and out-door companion-
ship suited well the mood of love-making.
During one of these excursions, Mrs. Bland
inadvertently admitted to Mr. Barrington
that her future would be a blank unless
associated in some way with his uncle.
Sidney lost no time in repeating it, and the
matter was soon settled. Meantime Con-
way had become so endeared to Mr. Harri-
son that he was like his own son, and he
seemed to take the matter for granted. The
parties were so independently situated that
the matter was in their own hands, and Sid-
ney Barrington performed the marriage cere-
mony in the primitive little meeting house
where he had preached from the time of
settling in the colony. Only the few people
who had known them best were present
and there was no gaiety or wedding festivi-
ities whatever. A quiet winter followed,
and during the time letters had been inter-
changed by them with Clair and Maitland,
and as early as the weather would admit
the party set sail for England, to transact
some necessary business and to see Anna’s
children. Lady Walton was very desirous
of seeing Mrs. Harrison, and all things
considered it was a great satisfaction to
Mrs. H. herself, to visit Hilliard Castle and
take with her the little son, who had been
born on the estate. There were many
pleasant things connected with this visit to
her beautiful English home—many mem-
ories recalled, and visions of the past
awakened, that were sweet but sad. Some-
times she trembled lest the happiness she
experienced in being the wife of one she
loved so intensely should be only transient.
	Lady Walton had once reminded her how
passionately her husband had in his youth
loved Lady Hilliard, and added ‘‘it drove
him almost distracted when she married
Sir Edward, and to think after that he
could ever be in love, and with some one
so unlike I can scarcely credit my senses
when I see him with you, indeed it is
paralyzing, but he is a charming friend,
and so clever and brilliant, and withal his
eccentricities so fond of children. And Mr.
Barrington how I should like to hear him

	“And why then do you not try to look
into the Scriptures and comprehend the say-
ings of the Savior, there is comfort and
strength in his words. Mr. Barrington
would help you if you would listen, he

	“It is too late for me to accept a new
faith, I must still cling to my own, though
I do like the simple way these dissenters
have of feeding the people the word of life
and salvation.”
	After much more conversation about re-
ligion Lady Walton surprised Mrs. Harri-
son by saying she was sure her days were
numbered, and she begged her to obtain
Maitland’s consent to take the children
back to America, where they might have
the simple home training she knew Mrs.
Harrison would give them. It was exactly
what Jane Harrison was wishing and long-
ing for, and when she talked it over with
her husband, he began interceding almost
immediately, and Maitland needed very
little persuading.
	Mrs. Harrison had been much at the
castle but she had also been in Devonshire
to see the old home of her husband who
took much delight in showing Sidney as
well as his wife over the places he had
been familiar with in his boyhood and in
early manhood ere his life had been soured
by melancholy. Only a few remained of
those he remembered though many there
were who knew him and were glad to see
him among them again and urged him to
remain, but no inducement could be offered
that would have tempted either of the
three to settle down in England after hav-
ing lived only for so short a period in the
“new world,” and yet for the time being
the green fields the beautiful parks, the
verdant meadows, the luxuriant hedges,
the grand old oaks, had a charm that awaken-
ed emotions that touched the tenderest
chords of affection for their native land.
	After having spent the lovely summer
reveling in the beauties of the country,
Mr. and Mrs. Harrison began preparations
for their return, Clair Hilliard was to go
with them on a visit, and the children Ed-
ward and Anna Walton were to be com-
mitted to their care for a time; no definite
compact was made; Maitland assured Mrs.
Harrison he would come for them soon, be-
fore they had forgotten him. Edward was
a fine stalwart fellow and little Anna was
beautiful as a dream.
	Lady Walton was really much affected
when the time came for parting with them
and affirmed over and over again that she
should never see them more; her whole
manner of life was changed she had become
deeply religious and though she still clung
to her own established church yet she en-
joyed much more the simple faith of Sid-
ney Barrington.
	Mrs. Harrison’s leave-taking of the old
castle, (though there had been all sorts of
repairs and many changes made) was al-
most as sorrowful as on first leaving it to
embark for America. She went over and
through it with the children describing the
rooms and relating what had transpired of
interest to them, trying to impress upon
them the grandeur and significance of the
ancestral home of their mother and grand-
parents. There was a great change in the
surroundings even more than in the house
