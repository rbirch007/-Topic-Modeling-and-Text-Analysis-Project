“THOU SHALT NOT BEAR FALSE
			WITNESS.”
	In the decalogue there is, perhaps, no
command more binding than the foregoing
no one more loosely carried or more often
broken. Binding, because throughout the
scriptures it is repeated oft and oft again;
twice in Exodus, once in Deuteronomy,


twice in Matthew, twice in Psalms, five
times in Proverbs, once in Zecharias, three
times in Matthew, once in Mark, twice in
Corinthians, once in Galatians, twice in Pet-
er, and once in John. Surely an offense
which calls forth so much of warning from
the prophets is in itself an heinous deed.
Let those beware who commit it, for the
harm will fall again upon their own heads.
“Who steals my purse steals trash, but he
who filches from me my good name takes
that which not enriches him and makes me
poor indeed.” The thief, the liar, the crim-
inal can be hunted out and brought to just
punishment; but for the smooth bearer of
false witness who achieves his own ag-
grandizment, by his neighbor’s injury, who
can tell the penalty that awaits! Woe to
him! for to his inner conscience some day
will come back the sorrow and heart ache he
has caused. The law of compensation is a
natural law, and divine; and the piercing
thorns will return and prick the conscience
of the falsifier—will encircle his brow until
in agony, he cries for that mercy which he
did not show, and which he has small reason
to expect.—A. W. C.
