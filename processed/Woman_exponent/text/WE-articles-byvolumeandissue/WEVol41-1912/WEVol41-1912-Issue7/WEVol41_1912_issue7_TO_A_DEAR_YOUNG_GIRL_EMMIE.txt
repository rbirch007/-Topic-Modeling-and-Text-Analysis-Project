TO A DEAR YOUNG GIRL, EMMIE. 
Slowly with the changing seasons
	In loneliness the years had passed,
Heated summers, autumns somber,
	And the winter ’  s chilling blast;
Dear ones sleeping past earth ’ s waking,
	Dear ones toiling far away;
And my heart still listening, waiting,
	For their coming, day by day. 

Far away the salt lake glistened;
	Fair days, when its waves were blue;
But when white, rude storms descended
	And isolation sterner grew. 
Mountain ranges, gray clad stretches
	Where no streams ran, nor green trees
Broke the monotone of landscape
	With the wild birds ’  melodies. 

Thus the years passed, pioneering,
	Hoping time and toil would bring
Round our dwelling bloom and beauty,
	And the valley yet would ring
With the sounds of busy labor,
	Shine with streams from depths below,
And the desert gray yield harvests,
	As foretold so long ago. 

So the heart fed on its mem ’ ries
	Of dear friends and days gone by;
Ah, how many have passed from us,
	Happier now than you or I. 
Then, at last, almost a stranger,
	Came I to old haunts of mine;
Scenes may change, but true selves ever
	Changeless keep their countersign. 

“Sister Crocheron, you ’ re remembered,”
	 ’ Twas a girl ’ s sweet voice that spoke;
Ah, the long years ’  shadows vanished
	And glad feelings, long hushed, woke. 
Snow was still upon the mountains,
	But the spring, with flower and bird,
Filled my being with its joyance
	As her welcoming I heard. 

Grace and sweetness were thy dower,
	Never may their beauties fade;
Ne ’ er across thy happy pathway,
	Shadows cold, or thorns be laid. 
May the sunshine and the music
	Of thy face and voice endure;
As thou gav ’ st, to thee be given,
	Heart gifts true and sweet and pure.
