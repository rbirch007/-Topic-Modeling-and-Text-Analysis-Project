A NATIONAL ACADEMY OF LITERA-
	TURE AND ART FOR WOMEN.
	It is with great satisfaction that we note
the wonderful advance in thought among
great people concerning the status of women
in art and literature. While some of the
greatest writers have been women, it is still
a fact that public acknowledgment of wom-
en’s talents has seldom if ever been given,
save perhaps to those of histrionic ability;
now, however, an American has taken the
initiative and the word has gone forth, and
another great step has been taken in human
progress. Professor Brander Matthews of
Columbia University, president of the men’s
national institute, in an address before the
academy proposed a national institute for
women.
	Professor Matthews said that women had
never been given an opportunity to become
members of the national institute. He said
the subject had never come up, it has never
been considered. It is quite proper, how-
ever, that women should have an institute
and academy of their own. If such writers
as Mary Wilkins Freeman, Edith Wharton,
Margaret Deland, and Ida M. Tarbell, and
such painters as Martha Caisatt and Mary
Beilitt, would join to initiate such a move-
ment the national institute would be glad to
co-operate with them in any way possible in
the formation of the women’s organization.
	The gold medal of the academy was pre-
sented this year to Augustus Thomas as a
dramatist. Previous awards have been in
sculpture to Augustus St. Gaudens, in his-
tory to James Ford Rhodes; in poetry, to
James Whitcomb Riley, and in architecture,
to William B. Mead. Why have a separate
institute for women? Rather let these noted
women, mentioned together with others,
unite in persuading Professor Matthews to
interest himself in the admission of women
to membership in the academy and institute.
