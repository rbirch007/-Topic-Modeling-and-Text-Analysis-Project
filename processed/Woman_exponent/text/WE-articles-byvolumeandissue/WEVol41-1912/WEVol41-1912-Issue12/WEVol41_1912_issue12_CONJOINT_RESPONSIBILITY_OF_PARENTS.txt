CONJOINT RESPONSIBILITY OF PARENTS.
	Much has been said about the responsi-
bility of motherhood. We have it sung into
our ears from pulpit and press. I would like
to impress upon those present, if possible,
the unity of the father and mother in the
rearing of a family of children. Neither
parent, no matter how zealous, can alone
make a perfect home. It takes the united
wisdom and effort of both to successfully
cope with the difficulties that arise. How
often we see fathers who seem to take no
farther interest in their child, than to see
that it is well housed, well fed, well clothed.
When mother says the boy, or the girl,
should go away to college, he stands ready
to meet all accounts for board, etc., that
accrue therefrom. He leaves all the train-
ing—physically, mentally, spiritually and
morally—to the mother, teacher, or any one
who will assume the responsibility. Now,
all this I hold to be wrong. The father owes
it to himself as well as the child, to be its
companion and confidant. I have not much
fear for the future of a child whose father
nightly calls the family together and, in a
few well-chosen words, tells them of life,
and the temptations they will meet, and then
with a short earnest prayer, sends them to
bed with a heart filled with gratitude for
God’s goodness. Long after that father has
answered the summons from above, will his
humble prayer be a strong factor for good,
in the memory and life of that child. I be-
lieve we should live more with our children
and not so much for them, and I believe we
will profit equally with them in the com-
panionship. One writer thus speaks of chil-
dren:

“These truants from home and from heaven
	They have made me more manly and mild,
And I know now how Jesus could liken,
	The kingdom of God to a child.”

God help us parents all to live aright,
And may our homes all Love and Truth enfold,
Since life for us no loftier aim can hold,
Than leading little children to the light.
