AN OLD-FASHIONED PARTY.
	On one of the cold, frosty nights in De-
cember the sumptuous home of Mrs. A. W.
McCune was the scene of a most delightful
old time party. While without the air was
brisk and cold, within everything was bright
and merry, as old friends met in pleasant
greeting. The message from the hostess,
“Come in old-fashioned dress,” was readily
responded to, and the result was a scene
of different eras, as passing from room to
room, one greeted first a colonial dame with
powdered hair and flowered silks, then per-
haps a southern belle with filmy flounces and
jewels rare, and turning noted a bride of
some forty years in a gown of ivory satin
—such a mixture of old lace and lavender
bringing back the scent of roses from some
old fashioned garden or the strains of a
waltz from the long ago.
	The most charming figure in all this
quaint array was the hostess herself, as she
greeted the incoming guests and so solicit-
ously looked after their entertainment. She
wore a handsome gown of chantilly lace,


flounced over crinoline, a costume seen per-
haps in some old lady’s magazine, but almost
beyond the memory of this generation, with
jewels and laces of the same period. The
guests enjoyed to the extreme the inter-
change of memories and laughed heartily at
the sight of the queer bonnets and hats. The
ball-room presented a quaint and interesting
picture and many advanced in years seemed
young again while engaging in the minuet
and quadrille of their youth. To give such
pleasure to one’s friends is a delight in itself
and this old-fashioned party is another beau-
tiful page in memory’s book for both hostess
and guest.
