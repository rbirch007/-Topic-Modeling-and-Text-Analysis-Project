UTAH TERRITORIAL FAIR;

	On Monday October 3d, at 10 o'clock the
Fair formally opened and Hon. George Q.
Cannon delivered an appropriate address. He
spoke upon the importance of proper represen-
tation such as was there represented to encour-
age the growth of home ability and manufac-
ture, and promote industry which lay at the
foundation of success in life. The people had
been urged from the beginning to exert them-
selves to make within the Territory all those
things which their necessities required for
home consumption, as well as articles for ex-
portation. The speaker felt pleased at the in-
terest now manifest upon various matters per-
taining to the development of the resources of
the country and thought all who sought to
create labor for the people should be stimulated
by the result which had already been reached
to make still greater efforts in the future.
Pres. John Taylor made a few brief remorks
referred to the benefits of united or cooperative
effort in all the branches of manufacture.

cellent quality, good butter and cheese. The
display of furniture was quite elaborate and
showed well for this branch of industry. Soap,
leather, brushes and marble work were very
creditabley represented. Woolen dress goods,
and heavy cloth of all kinds, blankets flannels
yarn of all colors and varieties making this dis-

	The ladies department was greatly admired
by all, quilts, and rugs in all the varieties of
patterns, embroidered cushions and in fact
fancy wool work of all kinds, and many sorts
of needle work, knitting, crochet, etc.
	The homemade silk was perhaps more at-
tractive than anything else to strangers
especially ladies who were profuse in their ex-
pressions of admiration at the energy and in-
dustry of those ladies who had been persevering
enough to accomplish so much by their own
labor from the very foundation—the silk worm
eggs. One show case contained reeled silk
manufactured by hand, one pound of which
was made by Miss Nellie Pyper of this city
aged 13, the reel used by her was also on ex-
hibition and was hastily constructed from
pieces of a broken chair and the spoke of a
wheel. Miss Nellie's silk took the prize and so
did the reel. Among some of the most notable
articles manufactured from the home spun silk
was a handsome netted shawl made by Mrs. S.
Bergman from silk raised and reeled by the late
Mrs. Ann B. Dunyon. It was elegant enough

[Column 2]

for the shoulders of any lady in the land.
There was a child's hood all of silk, a very ex-
quisite affair, made by Mrs. O. Evans of Nephi.
A handsome netted scarf made by Mrs. R.
Wareham of Manti. There were specimens of
silk lace manufactured in a variety of patterns
by the famous lace maker Mrs. Lizzie Ursen-
bach Compton of Morgan. There were many
other specimens and articles of home-made silk,
collars, neckties, purses, etc. There were also on
exhibition two kinds of dress goods, 70 yards, of
home spun silk which was reeled by the late
Mrs. Josephine Schettler, one plain the other
brocade. The first silk dress goods ever manu-
factured in Utah was made by Mrs. Laurinda
Robinson of Farmington, who raised the co-
coons from silk worm eggs, and reeled the silk
and did all the work herself.
	The articles for sale manufactured from the
home-spun silk can be obtained at Mrs Betsy
Long's Fancy Store on First South Street near
the Theatre. Many of the articles on exhibi-
tion were manufactured from cocoons raised by
Mesdames Ann B. Dunyon, Susan A. String-
am and Mary Ann Rockwood, at the Forest
Farm Cocoonery.
	The Primary Department in the Fair at-
tracted considerable attention because all the
articles were the work of the "little people."
Perhaps the most wonderful was the picture of
a child by a girl Clara Bradford 9 years old.
A copy of a photograph made by the child her-
self and painted very artistically. That child
is certainly a genius and ought to have an op-
portunity for art cultivation. A handsome
breakfast shawl knitted by Georgina Toone, 12
years old, took the prize, and there were several
other articles very ingeniously made; the indus-
try of the little folks is very commendable.
Handsome wax flowers made by Louie Sloan,
and many other fancy articles by little girls.
Some excellent cocoons were on exhibition
raised by a small boy, which were of superior
quality, and another lot raised by a girl 13
years old which took the prize.
	The picture gallery or art department re-
cieved a good share of attention and elicited
considerable admiration. Many of the paint-
ings by home artists were very fine, especially
scenes from nature. The Mormon Battalion
was seemingly true to life, and quite touching
to those who knew its history. The painting
of the Savior and the woman in sorrow was a
beautiful illustration of the tenderness of the
divine Master and the depths of love for hu-
manity which abounded in his nature. It was a
grand picture and a glorious subject. "He
wipeth the tear from every eye." None but a
true artist could design it.
	Master Samuel Barratt's pictures show tal-
ent of no ordinary character in one so young
and he may justly be proud of them.
	Miss Jennings' paintings were very fine, one
or two real gems.
	Some specimens of crayon drawings were ex-
cellent and displayed real talent. There were
many other choice specimens of work in this
department which were really meritorious.
	There are many very beautiful and roman-
tic places in Utah that form excellent material
for subjects for the artist to delineate upon canvas.
