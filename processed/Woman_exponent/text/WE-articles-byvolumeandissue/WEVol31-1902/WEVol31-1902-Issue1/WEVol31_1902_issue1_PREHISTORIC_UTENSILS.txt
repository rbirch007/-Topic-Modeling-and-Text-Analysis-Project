	PREHISTORIC UTENSILS.

	The utensils found at the spot consist of
a bailer for a boat, a carved ivory rowlock,
four stone knives, a bone knife and a skin-
ning knife. This skinning knife is a curi-
osity. It might be said to be in the shape
of a crude, old-fashioned drawing knife,
such as is used by carpenters, minus the
handles. It is about the length of a draw-
ing knife, but the stone blade, which is
fitted in the center, is about four inches in
breadth by three or four inches deep. The
boat bailer is made from the ivory of a
mastodon tusk. It is shaped somewhat
like an old-fashioned gourd dipper and will
hold about one pint of water. The bone
knife resembles a corn knife or machete
such as was used by the Cubans. A pecu-
liarity of this knife is an etching of a pre-
historic animal on the blade. The image
appears to be a rude caricature of a rhino-
ceros. There is a probability that the knife
may have been made from the bone of the
animal depicted on the blade.
	The ivory rowlock is also etched. The
work on this relic is much more delicately
and artistically done than that on the knife.
The etchings are of fish and sea monsters
and there are five or six of them.
	An effort will be made to bring the bones
of the house frame down from Alaska and
set them up in the Smithsonian institution
just as they were found in the cliff. The
fact that there were thirty feet of gravel

and earth above the house, and that all the
utensils were of stone and bone of masto-
don’s tusks, warrants the conclusion that
