FROM CACHE VALLEY.

		SMITHFIELD, March 4th, 1874.
Dear Exponent:—
	I have just returned from Bishop Wm.
Hyde's funeral; how comforting when we
are called upon to part with friends, to rea-
lize that, "What is our loss is their gain."
	I have known him since June, 1874. He
was born in Sept., 1818; embraced the gos-
pel April 7th, 1834, under the hands of Bro.
John Murdock; and has been a true and
faithful servant of the Lord Jesus Christ,
ever since. He may truely be said to have
worn his life out in the service of God. He
has traveled much to preach the gospel to
the unbelieving world; was one of the 500
who volunteered to lay down his life to save
his friends, and marched across the plains
and deserts to the seat of war in Mexico.
He has finished his work upon the earth, he
has kept the faith and now has gone to reap
his reward; a crown of righteousness, redemp-
tion from sin, andeternal lives in the king-
dom of our God. Well did the Poet say,
"The chamber where the good man meets
his fate, is privileged beyond the common
walk, 	*	*	*quite in the verge of hea-
ven."


﻿There was a very lage congregation out at
the funeral notwithstanding the cold inclem-
ent time. The speakers were Prest. B.
Young Jr., Bishops Wm. B. Preston, M. W.
Merrill and Elders E. M. Greene, Moses
Thatcher and J. Hatch: They dwelt mostly
upon the subject of the "passing away" of
those who had learned from the Prophet
Joseph, and have been faithful to the present.
Bro. Hyde was buried with military honors.
	Colds, coughs, and a kind of scarlet fever
are quite prevalent here, mostly among the
children: The snow is from eighteen inches
to 2 feet deep, and more is coming nearly
every day. The weather is very cold: The
Utah Northern is blockaded with snow from
four miles below Hampton's Station, so
there is no passing by rail into this valley.
								M.
