			JANUARY.

The fair South Wind, with the magical key
	To unlock Earth's cold fetters of ice,
Came singing of Southland about us to-day
	With tenderly tremulous voice.

She played with old Winter,s long icicled hair,
	With blandishments soothed him to sleep,
Then sprinkled with raindrops his carpet so fair,
	Which melted in rills 'round his feet.

She breathed on the diamonds which studed his crown—
	His rare—lustered jewels of frost
They melted, and trickling his wrinkled cheeks down,
	Plashed into the rills and were lost.

Then she mimicked and flouted the doting old man,
	And dabbled her feet in the rills,
Till bubbling with laughter the giddy brooks ran,
	But weeping were all the white hills.

But the stern old King woke from his slumber at last,
	And quickly perceiving her jeers,
He shook his wet locks, in his terrible wrath,
	And his glance froze the hills' tender tears.

He sent for fresh gems from his treasures untold,
	His rolling cloud-banners unfurled;
And called up the North Wind so cruel and bold
	To hunt the pert Miss through the world.

He came with his legions in frost-armor mailed,
	and with needle-keen darts poised in air—
They shrieked and they shouted and fiercely they wailed
	Like demons escaped from their lair.

The maid, at their coming, in trembling affright,
	Fled back to her innermost cell,
While furious North Wind, with torches alight,
	Obeyed stern old Winter King well.

He searched every dwelling with ruthless intent,
	He shouted through each lonely dell,
He shrieked down ravines 'mid the mountain-tops pent
	And all night his hoarse voice rose and fell.

His power supreme none might dare to deny,
	So, though quaking in terror and dread,
The meek driven snow, with a shivering sigh,
	Once more for a carpet was spread.

At last dawned the morning, stark. lifeless and cold,
	Enshrouded in vestments of white;
And though South Wind was safe 'mong her flowers of
		gold,
	All her rills had been slain in the night.
