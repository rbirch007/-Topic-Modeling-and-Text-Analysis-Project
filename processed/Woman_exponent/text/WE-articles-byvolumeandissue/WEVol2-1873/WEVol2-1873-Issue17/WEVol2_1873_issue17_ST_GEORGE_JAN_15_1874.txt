			ST. GEORGE, JAN. 15, 1874.
	DEAR EXPONENT;
	St. George is now enjoying a sweet, gen-
tle rain. Since we have been here, we have
had but one-half day’s rain, until to-day, to
hinder the workmen on the Temple. I
never before saw the roads about St. George
so good as they have been this winter.
There are sixty men at work on the founda-
tion of the temple, and a hundred and fifty
in the quarries, hauling rock, lime, sand and
other material. The ground where the tem-
ple is being built is so tinctured with min-
eral that it will dissolve sand stone and
limestone in three years, for this reason the
foundation and basement are built of black
volcanic rock so very hard that the mineral
does not effect it. The boulders from which
it is quarried resemble cast iron, and it
weighs thirty-four lbs. more per cubic foot
than sandstone. These stones are hauled off
the mountain west of St. George about two
and a half miles. We expect the whole of
the black rock will be laid by the first of
February. About one hundred and eighty
cords were yet necessary to finish the work
when we came here. Large quantities of
red sandstone are already on the ground.
It has to be hauled about two miles; and fif-
teen hundred cords of this beautiful rock
will be required to complete the temple.
The quarry from which this sandstone is
obtained is worth visiting.
	The Saints here seem healthy and pros-
perous; their meetings are well attended.
I met with the Relief Society on Tuesday
last, had a large attendance and a splendid
meeting. 		 BATHSEHBA W. SMITH.
