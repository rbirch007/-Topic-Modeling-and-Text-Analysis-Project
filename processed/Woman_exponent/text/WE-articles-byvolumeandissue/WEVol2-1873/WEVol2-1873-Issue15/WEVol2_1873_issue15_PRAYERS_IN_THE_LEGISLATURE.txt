PRAYERS IN THE LEGISLATURE.

	The "Stockton Leader', says of the sit-
ting of the Legislature of California:
	The most interesting event in the Senate
was the debate upon the motion to elect a
Chaplain. Lane, of Santa Clara, did him-
self credit by opposing the system of pay-
ing for prayers, but "declared himself a be-
liever in the efficacy of prayer, and would 
gladly join any Senator in asking a blessing
from Almighty God. He believed that the
prayers of pious men and women which
went up in closets were of more efficacy than
prayers paid for at $5 a day." Of course
such old-fashioned Christianity as that was
too unpopular with our meek-minded Legis-
lators, and a Chaplain was duly elected at a
salary of $5 per diem.
`	We regret this, for the experiment was
well worth trying; to dispense with paid
prayers and invite the benevolent clergymen
of Sacramento, or the State at large to sup-
plicate the Throne of Grace in behalf of our
Representatives without hope of pecuniary
reward. It would have tested the zeal of
ministers who profess anxious solicitude for
the safety of the unsanctified; and beside it
might not be a waste of time—judging from
the record of Legislators in general—if the
prayers of two or three, or even a dozen holy
men should ascend daily in their behalf.
