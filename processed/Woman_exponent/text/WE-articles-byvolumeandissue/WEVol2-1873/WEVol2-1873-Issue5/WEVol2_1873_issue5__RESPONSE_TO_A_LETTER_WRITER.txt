RESPONSE TO A LETTER WRITER.

	A letter dated Fountain, Indiana, July 14,
1873, and addressed "To any lady of good
standing in the Mormon Church" having
reached the EXPONENT post office box,
through the courtesy of the postmaster of
this city, demands notice. The writer
claims to be a physician, expresses his de-
sire to emigrate to Utah and become "a
member of the Mormon Church;" says he is
"a strong advocate of the Mormon doctrine
and principals;" but he is very anxious to
know whether, if he were to come to Utah,
"engage in practice, and goin the Mormon
Church" the Mormons would lend him "a
helping hand" and assist him in getting
a practice by giving him "their patronage
and influence!" Evidently the writer's
devotion to"the Mormon doctrine and prin-
cipals" is to be materially influenced by the
amount of patronage he would receive in
his "specialty of doctrine the eye." Were
his pretentions of belief sincere and honest
he would accept the truth for the truth's
sake, independent of all considerations of
"patronage and influence;" were he an
honest gentleman he would have addressed
his letter to "Any gentleman in good stand-
ing in the Mormon Church" instead of to
"any lady;" for although he says he has
written to several gentlemen in Salt Lake
City and received no answer, we are satisfied
he has not written to any of the authorities
of the Church, nor to any leading Latter-day
Saint in the Territory. We notice the letter
as a specimen of what some men can
attempt. This man has a small family, yet
proposes to leave them behind in Indiana;
and endeavors to open a correspondence
with some "Mormon lady;" asking advice 
of her as to what course he should pursue.
His pretentions are too flimsy. To our
mind he is neither honest, sincere nor a
good man, but one who would try to work
evil under a thin covering of religious pre-
tention.


A man named David Nesbit, in Lindsay
Ontario, proved his unlimited devotion to a
lady, Miss Mary A. Hopwood, the other
day, by shooting her dead—not by accident.
