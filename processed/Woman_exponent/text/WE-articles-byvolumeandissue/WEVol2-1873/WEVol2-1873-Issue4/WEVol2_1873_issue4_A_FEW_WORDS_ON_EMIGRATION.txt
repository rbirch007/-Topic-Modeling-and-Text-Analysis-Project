A FEW WORDS ON EMIGRATION.

 Through the medium of our friend and
companion, (the Exponent,) I would say a
few words to my sisters on a subject that
should interest all; and that is the emigra-
tion of the poor Saints from Europe.  The
hand of God has gathered us to these beauti-
ful vales, where we can enjoy peace from
the mobocrat, and freedom from the op-
pressor; and has poured blessings out upon
us without stint.  And shall we in return,
refuse to help those who are in want and
distress, depending upon us for aid?  Or
shall we stretch forth our hand in the
direction God has pointed out by His
servant, our beloved Leader, and enjoy the
sweet consolation of having done what God
required.  Inasmuch as our Father has
blessed us with much of this world's goods,
will He not hold us accountable for the use
we make of it?  And does He not require
us to share with those less favored than
ourselves. The way is now open, let us
walk in it ere it is closed.
	
	"If thou hast passed an aching heart,
	Turn back a little way;
	Let not thy giving be a part
	To act another day;
	Give while the weary eye is dim,
	And if a tear should fall,
	'Twould be in gratitude to Him,
	Who heard the mourner's call." 

	There is another consideration; and that
is, laying up treasure in Heaven, the only
place where treasure never fades. We
would all like to have something there to
our credit.  And when the time comes for
our actions to be weighed in the balances,
we shall want every good deed to weigh as
heavily as possible.  If we should lay  aside
some of our real or imaginary wants and
impart to the real necessities of others, I do
not see where the harm would be. And
this I do know, that “sacrifice brings forth
the blessings of heaven.”

	“When thy to-morrows all have passed,
	Kind acts will then appear
	Like angels waiting by thy side 
	To bless thee and to cheer.”
								M.L.M.

A DAUGHTER is almost always right when
she endeavors to imitate her mother; but it
is doubtful wheather the mother is equally
right when, at a certain period of life, she
tries all she can to imitate her daughter.
	THE Indians in Plumas County begin to
show signs of adopting the white man’s civi-
lization.  The Quincy “National” says that
the red men who reside in the vicinity of
Greenville have subscribed $200 for the
erection of a school house, and engaged Mr.
Summerland to manage the enterprise for
them.  It states also that the Indians on
the north side of the Plumas valley have
raised $300, to be devoted to a like purpose.
Now, here is a worthy object that the whites
ought everywhere to encourage.  It is bet-
ter to educate the Indians, teach them the
arts of husbandry, and have them become
lawabiding residents, than to be under a
constant state of apprehension from their
proximity as idlers and vagabonds, at any
time liable to get into difficulties with their
white neighbors.
