		TO HIS WIFE.

Come darling put your sewing by,
	And chat with me awhile.
I want to hear the glad, free tone,
	And see the old sweet smile:
The tone in which you spoke to me,
	The smile you used to wear
When we were courting on the Ranch,
	When love was young and fair.
If then we loved each other well,
	As little as we knew
Of all the wrangling, jangling things
	That throng life's pathway through—
If then we loved and trusted well,
	Just starting on our way
When near a dozen years have passed,
	How should it be to-day?
We never got discouraged then,
	And harsh words never came;
But since that time we neither one
	Have always been the same:
We never got too tired then,
	To walk and take delight,
After a hard day's work was done—
	How would it seem to-night?
Let's be again the lovers true
	We were before we wed;
Nor think so much of "getting on,"
	So we have clothes and bread:
For supper and for breakfast too,
	Hot meals let us discard;
I'd rather eat but once a day
	Than have you work so hard.
I think that if we courted still
	Just as we used to do
Our children would be healthier,
	And better natured too.
Our home would be as cheery bright,
	And we should be again
As happy in each others love,
	And blest as we were then.
Now when the men have left their work,
	And quit the field and shop,
And I come in to read or rest,
	It's time for you to stop;
So let's put this machine aside,
	And cease its tedious hum:
The moon is bright, the air is mild—
	Then come, my darling, come.
								LULU.
