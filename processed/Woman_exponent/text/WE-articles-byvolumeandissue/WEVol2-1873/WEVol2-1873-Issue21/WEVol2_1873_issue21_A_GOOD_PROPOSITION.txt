A GOOD PROPOSITION.

	Utah produces yearly an abundance of fruit
of various kinds, still thousands of dollars are
annually expended by the people for canned
fruits, jellies etc., which are imported from
California and other places. This is poor econ-
omy. But besides this, by the great quantities
of surplus fruit which is left to rot on the
ground or dry and shrivel on the trees and
bushes, the atmosphere of cities is infected with
impure and unwholesome effluvia, which, as is
well known, engenders disease.
	Miss Eliza R. Snow offers a suggestion, to
the effect that the women might take the mat-
ter in hand, and by co-operating, find measures
for preserving the fruit at home—thus prevent-
ing the waste and curtailing expenses.
	We doubt not, the hint will meet with general
favor, and be productive of good results. If
such a plan were adopted on a large enough
scale, it would follow as a natural consequence
that more pains would be taken and better
qualities of fruit raised. And it would also
cause a necessity for the starting of some use-
ful branches of industry in our midst, which
are now unknown in Utah.
