		REFLECTIONS.

	Under the above head, thoughts on all
things may be circumscribed with which
we have to do. The education of the young,
chiefly occupies my mind. Some teachers
contend strongly that what they call gym-
nastics are of the greatest benefit to develop
and strengthen the nerves and muscles of
the young And this is certainly of use
where no better employment can be had.
But I think if there could be a piece of
land properly fenced in with each school-
house, and instead of playing marbles, gym-
nastics or any other useless amusement,
the boys and girls were allowed to have
each a light hoe or spade with which to cul-
tivate the earth, it might prove very advan-
tageous. Each having a piece of ground
allotted according to the age, to cultivate
anything he or she might choose. Choice
should be made of vegetables, plants, or
flowers which will mature in one season.
Such a plan might be pursued in a way to
encourage a love of being useful. Where-
as, jumping the rope, running races wrest-
ling and all like games are often injurious.
	And again, I believe that too many studies
are not beneficial but hurtful. Reading
writing and arithmetic are all sufficient, till
these branches are well understood. Then,
geography, grammar and elocution; and
after these, the higher branches of educa-
tion. In this way the student may become
a good scholar in a shorter time than if crowd-
ed with all these branches at once. I am
sorry to say that I have seen some boys re-
quired to study six or eight different
branches in one day. In this way only a
smattering of each is obtained and none of
them perfected.
		ELIZABETH T. HEWARD.
