SLEEP.

	The necessity of sleep, it may be admit-
ted, is a disagreeable necessity. To turn
aside from all the pleasures of life, from the
sweet consciousness of existence, to give
over thoughts and love and memory and
hope—all plans and pursuits—and go down
to unforgetfulness or unconsciousness, is,
or seems to be, an unspeakable loss. It is a
death, temporary, but imperious and ever
recurring. Yet it is so universal, so gradual,
so natural, that we yield to it not only with-
out dread, but with delight. In the silent
splendor of star-lit nights, which seem to
put us on a footing with the whole universe,
in the blackness of nights that knew no star,
when we seem to be standing alone in the
solitude of eternity, life is too fascinating,
and we begrudge a moment lost; but even
then, without will, against will, the heavy
eyelids droop, and, all unhindered, the shy
soul slips away into some remote recess of
the brain to lie in ambush for the rising
dawn and the strong new world.
