KNOWLEDGE OF COMMON THINGS.—Car-
lyle laments that when he was a boy he had
no teacher to instruct him as to the nature
and qualities of the things about him, such
as trees, rocks, grasses, water — to talk
to him about the stars, and show him
about the constellations, etc. That lamenta-
tion is one very many have to make. In-
deed, we sometimes think that education
ought to begin somewhat at the other end
of it. That is, that we should be taught
earlier of the things that are nearest to us.
Here is the ground we tread on, and the
dust we kick up when we walk. What
child, yes, or what man, can tell us of what
it consists, where it comes from, or how
made? The grass we walk over, the stones
which boys throw at the geese, what do they
know of the substance of these stones? These
are the last things we learn. Ought not
this method to be reversed? Should we
not learn, first of all, about the things which
are right under our eyes, and under our
feet?
