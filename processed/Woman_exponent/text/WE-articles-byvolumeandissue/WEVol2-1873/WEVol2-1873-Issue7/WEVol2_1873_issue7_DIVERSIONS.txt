DIVERSIONS.

	The Shortest Day.––The day before
that on which you have a bill becoming due,
which you are unable to meet.
	An American editor cautions his readers
against kissing short girls, because this habit
has made him round shouldered.
	“Jem, you’ve been drinking.”—“ No, I
haven’t; I’ve been looking at another man
drinking, and it was too much for me.”
	Slow Work— Making an auger hole with
a gimlet, eating soup with a fork, and run-
ning to fires on crutches.
	What are you writing such a big hand
for, Pat?” “Why, you see, my grand-
mother’s dafe, and I’m writing a loud letter
to her!
	To kiss a rosy-cheeked girl and find your
mouth filled with Venetian red, and the
girl grown suddenly pale, is truly awful.
	Refinement. — A servant-maid, in a
small town in Herefordshire, last week left
her situation in consequence of her being
incapable of "reconciling her feelings to the
very bad grammar spoken by her mistress.”
—Country Paper.
	Anecdote of Northcote.—One day Sir
W. Knighton called upon the veteran and
asked him, "What do you know of the
Prince Regent?" “Nothing," said North-
cote; "what should I?" “Why, he knows
you very well," said Knighton. “Who
says so?” "Himself” "Poo!” said
Northcote; “it is only his brag."
	Somewhere in the West, a sable knight
of the lather and brush was performing the
operation of shaving a hoosier with a dull
razor. “Stop!" said the hoosier, “that
won’t do.” "What’s the matta, boss?”
“That razor pulls.” “ Well, no matta for
dat, sah. If de handle of de razor don’t
break, de beard’s bound to cum off!”—
Pittsburg Mercury.
