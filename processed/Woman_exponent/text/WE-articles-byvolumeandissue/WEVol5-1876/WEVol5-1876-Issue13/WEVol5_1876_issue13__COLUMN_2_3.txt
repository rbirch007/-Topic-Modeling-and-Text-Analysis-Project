[COLUMN 2]

	At the meeting it was suggested that a com-
mittee be appointed to visit the men in the
ward to see what they would do to help us;
the suggestion was carried and a good report
brought in. The majority of the brethren en-
tered into the spirit of the mission, and either
donated means or labor.
	Some of the brethren felt quite humbled to
think the women should undertake what the
men had failed to do; but I feel to offer a word
of encouragement to all such. The words of
Prest. Heber C. Kimball, which come to my
recollection, I think should suffice, wherein he
prophesied that the time would come when
the sisters would store up the grain and pota-
toes. I do not think that any good woman
would take the praise to herself, or think she is
better than her husband, on account of this
move that has been made. I feel that we
should work together, for the man is not with-
out the woman in the Lord, nor the woman
without the man; which is according to Scrip-
ture.
	The Society of Farmington have some grain
on hand and means to buy more, and do not
think of slackening up their energies in the fu-
ture.
	I wish to speak a word in favor of the Young
Ladies Retrenchment Association in this place.
They have a faithful president in Mrs. Lucy A.
Clark, and hold their meetings once a week;
they also have a paper of their own composi-
tion, called the "Index" which is both instruc-
tive and interesting. They also read selected
pieces in their meetings. The younger portion
of the members are quite zealous, so much so,
that they can hardly be induced to stay away
from meeting under any consideration.
	Excuse me for being so lengthy.
	Yours &c.
