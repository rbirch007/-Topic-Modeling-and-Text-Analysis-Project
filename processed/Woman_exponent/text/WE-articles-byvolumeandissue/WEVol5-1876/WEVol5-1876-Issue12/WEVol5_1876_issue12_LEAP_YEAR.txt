LEAP YEAR.

	Remember, O, maiden fair! two months
more and leap year is gone!—for you, per-
chance, forever-gone! How inexpressibly
sad—how inconceivably heart-touching—
the thought! But within two months,
brief though the period be, much may be
accomplished. Pick out judiciously, and
with an especial eye to his total abstinence
from those patent air-brakes to the aspiring
youth, beer and tobacco, the lordly leal and
liquor-contemning young man upon whom
you would lavish your gushing affections.
Where is the girl, whether fluttering in the
brocade of a princess or wearing the calico
polonaise of the laborer's daughter, who
would be told that for her three months are
not time enough for the wooing and the
winning of as good a husband as may be
wooed and won by any other girl with no
better faculties for the business? It is Shaks-
peare who somewhere says—and it is un-
questionably Shakspeare who knows—that,
"that man who hath a tongue, I say, is no
man, if with that tongue he cannot win a


woman." This is scarcely less true, perhaps,
than gospel. If it be true that any man
who hath a tongue can win a woman, it
would seem to follow, from a purity of reas-
oning, as the cheap disputants of some of
the religious newspapers have it, that the
woman who hath a tongue of her own—and
there be few of the sweet, delicious sex to
whom this gift is denied—must be very 
nearly akin to no woman if she cannot win
a man—and win him in less than two
months, too, and be ready with the assur-
ance in the end that she won him and
didn't half try. But enough.
	"Canst thou love me gentle stranger?"
		Blushing like a rose she stood:
	And the youth at once acknowledged
		That he rather thought he could.
			Selected.
