ALFRED TENNYSON.

			Alfred Tennyson is to-day the
monarch, the sovereign of English verse.

	It began a new era in English verse when
Alfred came caroling out of Lincolnshire.
and since [-]e has devoted his life exclu-
sively to his calling. Having once discov-
ered the divine gift in his wallet he has
never neglected or slighted his charge.
Tennyson as a poet adds industry to inspir-
ation. Not running to publishers with
quickly created verses, he leaves them out
until he has given them the virtue of the
fullest posssible excellence. His motto has
ever been "Perfection or Silence."
	With some writers poetry is an employ-
ment, with Tennyson it is an existence.
Tennyson's knowledge is extensive and
great. In science he keeps fully abreast of
the times; he is a master of philosophy as
well as poesy; and in botany he is almost
without a peer. His knowledge is exact.
As well as Huxley he is the very genius of
verification. It is Tennyson's custom af-
ter writing a poem, to have it put into type,
carefully corrected and then put away for
years, until the time comes when the au-
thor sees fit to have it published. His
egotism has often been condemned but it is
magnificent. There is nothing small or
mean about it. He is always altering his
verses, but ever for the better. Willis
once said in that habitually felicitous man-
ner of his, that a literary reputation could
only be built up, like the walls of Jerusa-
lem, with a trowel in one hand to plaster
in friends, and a sword in the other to
smite down foes. But Tennyson has been
as a rule, supremely scornful and regardless
of all his critics.—"The Pulpit of the Day."
