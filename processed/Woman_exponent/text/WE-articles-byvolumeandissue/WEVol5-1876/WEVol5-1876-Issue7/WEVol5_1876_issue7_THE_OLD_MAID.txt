			THE "OLD MAID".

	I looked for you at the Normal Institute,
for I was there—were you? If you an-
swer me that you were really present at
those very instructive meetings, I believe
I may pronounce myself satisfied that I
know your name now, and should recognize
you if good fortune should ever chance to
bring us face to face.
	Please tell me if you were not greatly de-
lighted with Mr. Ottinger's lectures. I
thought them remarkable for clearness, and
besides drawing is a subject in which I am
much interested. I thought your face gave
evidence of interest during all the lectures,
but especially when something particularly
amusing was going on. I feel sure that you
were there, and that I made no mistake in
the one I singled out for you.
	You have not read Nasby's "Hannah
Jane?" You will find it in Hill's Manual,
which is an excellent book for young teach-
ers.
	You would like to see me, "just for fun"
would you? I am sorry to say that I am
not at all funny. But as funny people and
sedate people generally agree well together,
I hope to hear from you again very soon;
and
	If human forms were formed to soar,
		Like human fancy light and free,
	Here would I calmly wait no more,
		On hope's bright wings I'd fly to thee.
	But since we're doomed to dwell apart,
		And 'tis the common fate of men—
			(And women, too,)
	Thank heaven for this goodly art,
		Communication with the pen.—
			Fair maid, adieu.


THE most inquisitive of ancient divini-
ties—Tellus.
