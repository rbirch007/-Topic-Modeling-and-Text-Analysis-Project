	     FEARS OF FAMINE ENTERTAINED. 

	The condition of affairs among the poor peo-
ple of the coal regions is assuming an alarm-
ing aspect. Thousands, who have neither work
nor bread, nor the prospect of obtaining any
for themselves and families, are on the verge
of starvation and do not know what to do or
which way to turn. The destitution is wide-
spread and distressing and is not confined to
the laboring classes alone, as there are num-
bers of skilled mechanics who have been idle
for many months and whose little store has
long since been exhausted. There are hun-
dreds of families in and about Scranton, and
not in this city alone, but in every settlement
throughout the coal regions, who do not know
where to get to-day's dinner, and who would
be shivering by cheerless firesides were it not
for the heaps of coal dust that lie scattered
along the valley and from which they draw
their supply of fuel, such as it is. So many
poor people, or such intense downright despair-
ing distress has not been seen in this section
of the country for many years. It is actually
painful to witness the want and sorrow extant.
