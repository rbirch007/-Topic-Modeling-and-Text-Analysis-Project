		OXFORD, ONEIDA Co, IDAHO TER.
							Jan. 17, 1877.

	We are organized into a branch of the
Relief Society as follows: Mrs. Mary Ann
Boice President; Mrs. Mary Black, Mrs.
Martha R. Cooper, Counselors; Mrs. Louisa
Croshaw, Secretary; Mrs. Ann Hepworth,
Treasurer. Our settlement is small conse-
quently our numbers are few, yet we wish
to be acknowledged by our beloved sisters
as co-workers with them in the cause of
Zion. Our little girls are classed off in
their labors under the tuition of Mrs. Lucy
Clements. The products of our labor
amounts to forty dollars since July last, con
sisting of carpet, quilts and wheat.
	I wish to mingle my voice with the
daughters of Zion. My ideas have been
aroused with regard to the high positions
that women are considered worthy of fill-
ing, aside from that of housewifery; espec-
ially in the Grain Movement given by our
beloved President. I exclaim in my aston-
ishment, can we become Saviors like Joseph
who was sold into Egypt, who by the sav-
ing of grain preserved his kindred from
the devastation of a famine, or is this com-
mandment given us that our brethren will
be more diligent in this direction or is it
given us so that we will retrench from ask-
ing our husbands or fathers for unecessary
gewgaws. My opinion is that it requires
all this to accomplish the one purpose of
storing grain; consequently I esteem it a
great privilege to be an active worker with
my associates in this great and wise under-
taking, and I thank my Father in Heaven
that I live and have a standing in a proph-
etic church. Although young, I can say
I have a husband constant and true, and
bright and promising little children, and
with him I desire to rear them in the way
of righteousness. This desire is with me
when I labor, when I eat, and even in my
night visions the care of my little ones is
with me, for in my surroundings is my
hope founded and in the cause of Zion is
my faith predicated.
		Your Sister etc.,
