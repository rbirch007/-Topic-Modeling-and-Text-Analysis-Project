STORING GRAIN.

	In an article upon storing grain, published
in the Deseret News of Jan. 17, written by
O. B. Shaw, we find some items in regard
to the subject which as we are seriously in-
terested in the matter, we desire to lay be-
fore our readers.
	"There are three great agencies operat-
ing in all nature in the destruction as well
as in the organization of all vegetable
and animal life forms, viz. heat, moisture
and light. To the operation of these subtle
forces is due nearly all the great changes
that recur from season to season. Modify
or control these forces and you have the
key of the whole subject. Construct gran-
aries impenetrable to moisture and unaf-
fected by the heat of the sun or surround-
ing air, cool, dry, and dark, and no in-
sect life can flourish in them, no parasitic
life will grow, and the conditions thus se-


cured, together with the conditions of dry
soil for the land where the various grains
grew and mixed copiously, completes the
natural law or conditions for their preserva-
tion through long periods of time. This
soil mixing among the grain does no injury,
as it all escapes in the milling process.
	Dig deeply into the alluvials of the earth
the world over, and expose the soils from
those depths to heat, moisture, and light,
and in nearly all cases, a vegetation essen-
tially different from that found on the sur-
face springs np.
	Joseph in Egypt, directed the storing of
grain with the dry soils of the lands, thus
showing himself to be well acquainted
with the natural facts before mentioned.
	Granaries constructed of good concrete of
lime and gravel are perhaps as good mate-
rial as any; they must be cool, dry, and
dark, and care be taken that the building
and contents, wheat and soil, are as dry as
possible.
