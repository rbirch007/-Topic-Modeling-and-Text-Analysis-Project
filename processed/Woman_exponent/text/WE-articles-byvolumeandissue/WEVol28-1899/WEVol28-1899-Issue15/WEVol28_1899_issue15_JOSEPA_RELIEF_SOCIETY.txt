	JOSEPA RELIEF SOCIETY.

   As some of our readers will perhaps re-
member, there was an article in the June
number of the EXPONENT, stating that we
had planted an acre of watermelons with a
view to selling them and using the pro-
ceeds to buy wheat and store up.
   Our melon patch did well and we sold
one hundred and six dollars worth of water-
melons, which you will see will greatly in-
crease our wheat.
   Then we made an experiment in seri-

[Column 2]
culture, raising twenty-three pounds of
cocoons of average quality, and have had
one of our young ladies learn the reeling,
so that we will be better prepared lor the
work another year.
   The Hawaiian sisters are very faithful in
the performance of any trust placed upon
them, and perform their duties in the Re-
lief Society promptly and cheerfully,
whether going out into the field to hoe or
water the melons, or to glean wheat, or
whatsoever they may be doing, sowing or
quilting or waiting on the sick. They al-
ways meet in time to sing a hymn and
then unite in prayer, asking the blessing of
the Lord upon the labor of their hands; and
the Lord does bless them and gives them
success in many ways, and I often think if
we were all more childlike and depended
more on the promised blessings than in our
own wisdom, we would be more happy and
contented.
   I will just mention that we said to the
boys, “I do not think there is one boy in
Josepa so mean that he would steal the
Relief Society melons,’’ and so far as we
know, not one has been stolen. For their
good behavior we gave a watermelon feast
to the whole colony, which was very much
appreciated by old and young.
   We are enjoying our usual good health, 
and a spirit of contentment prevails.
   With “Aloha” to all of our colabors, we
remain.
	Your sisters,
		EMILY G. CLUFF, Pres.,
