RESULTS OF THE REVOLUTION.
	(Read at a meeting of the Daughters of the Re-
volution.)
	The revolution in 1776 was one of the
most important events in human history.
It related not only to the freedom from
tyranny of the people who took the initia-
tive and carried it to a successful issue, but
to the liberties ot the human race as a
whole. The history of the world from then
to the present proves this, if it proves any-
thing.
	This momentous occurrence produced
the most perfect basis upon which to build
a beneficient governmental superstructure—
the American Constitution. It has no
counterpart in human affairs. It provides
to the individual citizen the fullest possible
freedom, the most ample personal protec-
tion and the complete security of legal
property possession. It is the basis guar-
anty of exact equality before the law,
without classified distinctions. Hence the
American nation is composed of the most
independent and, therefore, the most strong-
ly individualized race on the face of the
earth today, with a record for progress that
has no parallel.
	The leaven of the American Revolution
and its results have not been combined
within the borders of this nation, but have
stimulated emulation in every civilized
country, and a degree of its genius has

[Column 2]
even been carried into nations hitherto en-
veloped in the shroud of absolute barbarism.
	But perhaps the most striking beneficient
effects have been produced upon the nation
from which the American colonies revolted
—the kingdom of Great Britain. This
nation is now but a monarchy in name.
The estimable lady who nominally occu-
pies the throne of the British empire is
practically subject to the will of the minis-
terial government, her prerogatives of
sovereignty being mostly mere matters of
form.
	Similar changes in a more modified de-
gree have been developed in all of the
great nations, even among those which
were heretofore unqualifiedly despotic.
The central course of this remarkable ten-
dency toward popularization of rule is com-
prised in the American Revolution; and its
results—the production of the constitution,
the development of institutions of govern-
ment in harmony with that magnificent
basis, and the progressive effects upon the
nation.
	The history of American institutions
shows that every impediment to the devel-
opment of human liberty, as embodied in
our national system, must, in the process of
Divine adjustment of human affairs, sooner
or later be swept out of existence—hence
the civil war of 1861. Its first result was
the preservation of the Union; its second,
the abolition of slavery. The removal of 
that blot upon our escutcheon made the
great Republic consistent within itself, and
placed it in a position to champion, when
necessary, the cause of the oppressed out-
side of its own border, as well as within the
circle of its own domain.
	The nation may pass through many nar-
row places in the future. Trial is a de-
veloper of national as well as Individual
character, but every barrier to her progress
will be surmounted.
	Her past shows that her destiny is to
continue to be the central light of good
government to all the world.
