FRANK B. STEVENS.

	Frank B. Stevens quoted at some length
from an oration on “Peace” by Senator
Charles Sumner, delivered on Boston Com-
mon, July 4, 1845, in which the great
statesman urged upon the nation its duty to
take the lead in a movement in the inter-
ests of universal peace. “All men are
more or less combative in disposition,” he
said, “and if we must have peace we must
learn to overcome this spirit. Until, more-
over, we can learn to revere the man of
peace and place him on the same pinnacle
of public esteem that we place the man of
war, the day of universal peace will be de-
layed. But the best omen of the success of
the project now is that it has been so en-
thusiastically taken up by the women all
over the world. If women had been as far
advanced years ago as they now are, we
should not have had to wait until now for
the era of universal peace the world over.”
	Miss Maud May Babcock was then intro-
duced by Governor Wells. She read an
original poem by Mrs. Lydia D. Alder, en-
titled "The Day of Peace.’’
