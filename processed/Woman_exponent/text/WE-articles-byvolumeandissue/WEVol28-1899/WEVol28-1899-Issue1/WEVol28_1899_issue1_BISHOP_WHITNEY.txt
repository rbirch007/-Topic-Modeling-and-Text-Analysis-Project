BISHOP WHITNEY.

	Bishop Whitney, who followed, started
with a quotation from Tennyson, contain-
ing a prediction of universal peace. He
said the poet was not the first to make such
a prophecy. The prophets of Israel, he de-
clared, foresaw and proclaimed such an ad-
vent in lofty and sublime language. Bishop
Whitney said the church militant had
something to answer for. The most
Christianized natives had rushed against
each other for war and conquest. The
civil war was cited as another example.
	The speaker thought peace could purify
as well as war, and that there must come a
time when all will sing with Richelieu:
‘‘Take away the sword; states can be saved
without it.” Continuing he said:
	“One may be pardoned for thinking, for
hoping that that time has already come,
when he hears that the Czar of all the Rus-
sias has submitted to the powers of the
earth a proposition for universal disarma-
ment. I care not what motive may be be-
hind it, whether it be the shrewd political
maneuvering of an ambitious autocrat,
seeking to gain time in order to better sub-
serve some scheme for future conquest and
expansion, or whether it be, as I fain
would believe, the pure desire of a Christ-
ian ruler, the head of the Greek church, to
realize the sacred prediction and anticipa-
tions of centuries. Be that as it may, it is

[Column 2]
a move in the right direction. All’s one, if
God be in it for man’s salvation.” 
	A quintette consisting of Miss Lennie
Savage, Miss Edna Dwyer, George Pyper,
Professor Goddard and Mr. Peterson then
sang Rudyard Kipling’s “Recessional."
