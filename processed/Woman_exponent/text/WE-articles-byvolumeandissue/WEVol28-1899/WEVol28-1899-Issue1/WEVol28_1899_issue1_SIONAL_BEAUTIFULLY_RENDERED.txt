		SIONAL BEAUTIFULLY RENDERED.

	“Peace on earth, good will to men” was
the keynote of the Woman’s Peace conven-
tion held at the Assembly Hall on the eve-
ning of May 15. An audience gathered
that filled the structure to about two-thirds
of its capacity, and although there was lit-
tle demonstration or enthusiasm shown,
there was a deep undercurrent of intense
feeling through all the addresses of the
evening, which sank into the heart of
everyone there. Governor Wells presided
over the meeting.
	George A. Smith opened the meeting
with a prayer, in which he called down the
blessing of the Almighty on the peace
movement. He asked that those who sat
in high places in the councils of the nations
and who had the power to plunge the lands
under their control into war, might be led
to desire for peace and not for war. He
spoke of Utah’s sons now battling in the
Philippines, and asked that those to whom
our boys were opposed might be lead to
realize that the American flag meant to
freedom and peace, and not the yoke of
slavery.
	Governor Wells then read the call for
the meeting and introduced L. E. Hall,
who read the letter of the Czar of Russia
in which he summoned the powers to meet
in a peace convention at The Hague on
May 18.
