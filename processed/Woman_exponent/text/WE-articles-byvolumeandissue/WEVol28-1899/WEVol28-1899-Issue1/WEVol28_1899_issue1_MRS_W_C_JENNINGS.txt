		MRS. W. C. JENNINGS.

	Mrs. William C. Jennings said that it
was too much to hope that universal peace
would follow immediately after the peace
conference. “The meeting itself will have
no definite results,” she said, “but all
great movements have been preceded by
the spread of a sentiment and a desire for


the result to be attained that in the end led
to the accomplishment of that result. Dec-
ades before the abolition of slavery the senti-
ment was spreading abroad against it, and
at length the time came, and behold ! slav-
ery was at an end. I hope the spirit in
favor of peace may spread, and continue
spreading until at length the desired end
comes. It has been said that the move-
ment is premature; that the time for it is
not ripe; but with this I cannot agree. I
feel with the old gentleman who arrived
ten minutes before his train left, “There’s
not a mite too much.’ ”
	Held’s band gave a selection, and then
Dr. E.V. Silver read a set of resolutions pre-
pared by the Woman’s International Peace
Committee, endorsing the peace movement,
and, on motion of Mrs. E. B.
Wells, the resolutions were adopted by
a unanimous rising vote.
