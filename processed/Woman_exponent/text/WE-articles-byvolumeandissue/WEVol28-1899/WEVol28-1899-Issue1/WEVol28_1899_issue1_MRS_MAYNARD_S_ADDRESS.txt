		MRS. MAYNARD’S ADDRESS.

	Mrs. Mila Tupper Maynard was the first
speaker. “The proposal for a peace con-
vention,” she said, “was received with de-
light the world over. It was welcomed
gladly by those in high official positions in
all lands. Yet among the people, among
every class, it ought to be more welcome
than it has seemed to be. All over the
land peace meetings are being held this
evening. Yes, all over the civilized world.
And the number in attendance here and
the number at each one of these meetings
all around the globe will be cabled abroad,
and our peace commissioners will know of
what the people they represent are doing.
If all the people cared to see the dream of
universal peace realized, war wouldn’t be
possible. War appeal to our young men,
and why? Because it seems to them the
only way to win honor and glory. We
honor our heroes of war, we point to them
as models for our children, we revere them

[Column 2]
and celebrate their birthdays. They may
have been just as heroic in their works in
times of peace, but it is the war side that
we think of as the heroic side. And so we
must set up for our children heroes of
peace. We must make them realize that
the heroes of science and statesmanship and
literature are as great as those of war, and
then they will not so greatly long for the
glory of war, but rather for that of peace.”
