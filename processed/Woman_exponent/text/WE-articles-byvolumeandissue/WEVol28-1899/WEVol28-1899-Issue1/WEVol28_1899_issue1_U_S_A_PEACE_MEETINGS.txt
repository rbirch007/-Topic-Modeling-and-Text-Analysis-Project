U. S. A. PEACE MEETINGS.

	Reports of demonstrations of women in
behalf of peace and arbitration are still com-
ing in to Mrs. May Wright Sewall, chair-
man, of the American branch of the Inter-
national Committee. Mrs. Sewall reports
up to date news from seventy-one meetings,
at which 30,482 women were actually pres-
ent. Some of the meetings were of a dele-
gate character, the delegates representing
and actual voting constituency sufficient to
bring the number endorsing the resolutions
to over ninety thousand.
	As the initiative of this movement was
taken up so late as April 16th this response
was very gratifying.
	The states in which meetings were held
are as follows: California, Utah, Minneso-
ta, Iowa, Kansas, Illinois, Michigan,
Indiana, Ohio, Georgia Pennsylvania,
Rhode Island, New York, Maine and the
District of Columbia.
	Among the meetings those held by col-
lege women at the University of Michigan
and the University of Chicago are among
the most interesting reported. The former was addressed by President Angel, who
gave his enthusiastic support to this wo-
man’s movement.
	In addition to the women’s meetings re-
ports have come to Mrs. Sewall from hun-
dreds of congregations whose pastors
preached on the subject on Sunday, May
14, and took the votes of their congrega-
tions afterwards. In New York City,
where Mrs. Mary Lowe Dickinson has had
charge of the demonstrations, very thorough
work is being done in meetings of the
individual organizations, where the resolu-
tions are endorsed after discussion. Twenty
six organizations in New York City have
thus endorsed the resolutions.
	The response indicated so much latent
interest and sympathy that Mrs. Sewall
has communicated to the Central Commit-
tee at Berlin a proposition for continuing
these meetings at intervals during the ses-
sion of the Congress at The Hague.
