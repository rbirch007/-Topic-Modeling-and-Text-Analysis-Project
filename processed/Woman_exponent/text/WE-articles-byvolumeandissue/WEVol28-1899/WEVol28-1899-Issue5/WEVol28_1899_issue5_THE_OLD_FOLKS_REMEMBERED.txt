THE OLD FOLKS REMEMBERED.
	The first old folks’ day in Bingham Stake
was celebrated in an agreeable manner
at Brother Joseph Rich’s grove, he having
kindly offered the same for the occasion.
There not being many very old people here,
the age was put at forty-five, and every one
of that age, or over, was invited. Consider-
ing that it was harvest time, and a very
busy time, and near the Twenty-fourth
celebration, there was a pretty good atten-
dance.
	We were entertained with plenty of good
music from the Willow Creek band at the
commencement; then followed a hymn and
prayer, speeches, songs and recitations.
	Bishop Jardine in his remarks alluded to
the vast difference in our number now and
when he came here, the Saints at that time
being few and far between. When the
first ward was organized the first tithing
paid amounted to two dollars; now it
amounts to one thousand dollars.
	In the afternoon we had a short program,
after which they danced; and though I
know little about dancing, I very much en-
joyed seeing them, especially the dance
they call “Grand French Four.” Sister
Clark, I believe, received the prize for the
best step dancer.
	After the dance Brother Bybee inquired
who was the oldest brother on the platform,
and we found that Brother Williamson was.
He was awarded a cane, and the oldest sis-
ter, Abigail Campbell, received a rocking-
chair. Brother Bybee then asked the oldest
sister who had never had her ears pierced
to come forward. She was Sister Stokes,
and was promised a breast-pin. Then
Brother Bybee inquired who had given
birth to the most children. Sister M. L.
Bybee was the lady, having had fifteen chil-
dren. I believe her prize was a lamp.
	Every one went home looking happy.
We separated hoping to meet again next
year, and that none of the dear faces will be
missing.
