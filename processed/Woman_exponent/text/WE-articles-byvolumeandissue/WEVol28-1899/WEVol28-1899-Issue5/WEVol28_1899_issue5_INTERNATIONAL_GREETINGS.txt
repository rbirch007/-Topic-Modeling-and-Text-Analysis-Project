	INTERNATIONAL GREETINGS.

	Then came a long series of speeches from
the delegates. Mrs. May Wright Sewall
gave a bright account of the rise of the
movement which began to take shape at
the Chicago congress of women. Mrs.
Sewall put in a plea for tolerance and for-
bearance. Baroness Gripenberg, who
spoke (as indeed all the foreign delegates
did, with the exception of Madame Deflon
and Madame Shen) in very good English,
referred to the present state of her country
(Finland) and to the “local difficulties”
which were in the way. Miss Susan B.
Anthony spoke of the States, and Mrs.
Boomer for Canada. Lady Battersea and
Lady Hampden followed, the latter saying
that women led the movement for the federa-
tion of the Australian colonies. An interpre-
ter from the Chinese Embassy said that
Madame Shen was sent to the conference
by special orders from the Emperor. He
controverted the usual opinion that women
in China were not good for anything.
That was an entirely erroneous impression, 
he said, amid laughter.
	Among the other speeches was an ani-
mated one from Madame Finkelstein 
Mountford, who, to show her cosmopolitan
claims, said she was a Russian born in
Turkey and married to an Englishman.
		 —Daily Chronicle.
