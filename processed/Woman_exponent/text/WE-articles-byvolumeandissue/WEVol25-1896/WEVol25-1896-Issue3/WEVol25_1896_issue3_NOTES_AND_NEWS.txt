NOTES AND NEWS.

	THE W. C. T. U. of New Jersey, is to
give a “Welcome Home” reception to Miss
Willard and Miss Gordon on their arrival
to their native land, about October 15th.
	AT the Kuntucky Chautauqua, held at
Lexington, the W. C. T. U. School of
Methods was in charge of Mrs. S. Fesseden,
President of the W. C. T. U. of Massa-
chusetts. One hour a day was given to
various lines of the work, and on Temper-
ance day the address was given by John G.
Woolley. There was a fine audience des-
pite the fact that the weather was not
favorable, and the multitude hung spell-
bound upon his impassioned eloquent words.
There were about two hundred white rib-
boners present during the entire assembly,
but a much larger attendance upon the day
of Mr. Woolley’s address. Mrs. Frances
E. Beauchamp, Kuntucky’s brilliant young
president is a firm believer in the value of
Chautauqua Assemblies as a means of
popular education, and believes also that a
W. C. T. U. School of Methods could be
made a most valuable adjunct to these
eighty odd Assemblies throughout the en-
tire nation. There is already a good
beginning in this direction since the W. C.
T. U. will be represented in the majority
of the leading Chautauquas during the sea-
son of 1896. The original Chautauquas
will head the list with August 5th. as its
grand W. C. T. U. day, when Miss Slack,
Mrs. Burt, Miss Ackerman and John G.
Woolley will be the speakers. There will
also be five conferences in the Hall of Phi-
losophy under the charge of Mrs. Steven-
son, where the various phases of white
ribbon work will be presented.
