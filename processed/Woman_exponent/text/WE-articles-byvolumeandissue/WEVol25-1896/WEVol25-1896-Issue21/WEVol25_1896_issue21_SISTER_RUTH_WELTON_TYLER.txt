SISTER RUTH WELTON TYLER
	WHO departed this life April 14, 1897,
after an illness of only three days.
	She was the daughter of Asa and Clarissa
Norton Welton; was born in Spafford,
Onondaga Co., New York, February 25,
1820, and was baptized into the Church of
Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints in the 
same place, January 1832, by Elder Jared
Carter. She was the first of her family to
embrace the Gospel. She moved to Kirt-
land, Ohio with her parents in 1834 or
1835. She was married to her life-long 
companion, Daniel Tyler, September 11,
1836, and emigrated to Far West, Caldwell
Co., Mo., arriving August 3, 1837; was 
expelled from that place under the extermi-
nating order of Governor Lilburn W.
Boggs, February 13, 1839. She went to
Illinois where her daughter, the late Parin-
tha O. Oakden, was born June 4, of the 
same year. Moved to Nauvoo about 1842
where she became a member of the first Re-
lief Society organized by Joseph Smith,
the Prophet. With her little family she
was expelled from Nauvoo in the spring of 
1846. Afterwards left on the road in an
Indian country while her husband enlisted
in the Mormon Battalion and went to fight
the battles of his country. Another child
was born to her in that place. After the
Battalion was disbanded her husband re-
turned for her, and they arrived in Salt
Lake Valley November 18, 1848. She
was called with her husband to Dixie in
1862, where they suffered all the privations
and sickness from chills and fever incident
to settling that country. They were re-
leased and called to Beaver in the spring
of 1866, where she resided until her death. 
	Brother Tyler has filled various missions
during which Sister Tyler mainly support-
ed her family by her own labor. She
joined the Relief Society when it was first
organized in Beaver. Soon after she be-
came a teacher, subsequently counselor to
two presidents. June 16, 1876, she was
chosen president, which office she held for
several years. Again December 6, 1886, 
she was chosen president and faithfully ful-
filled the duties of that office until she was
released to take the position of President of
the Relief Society of Beaver Stake, March
5, 1897.
