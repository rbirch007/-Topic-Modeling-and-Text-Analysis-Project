LETTER FROM BUNKERVILLE, NEV.

Editor Woman's Exponent:
	I THOUGHT I would address a few lines
to you from this far away place. We are
about sixty miles from St. George but be-
long to that Stake of Zion. We have a
good Sunday school here and in good run-
ning order, also a Relief Society and
Primary Association. We have Mutual
Improvement meetings with both young
men and young ladies; the young people as
well as the old take a great interest in at-
tending to their duties. There is no out-
side influence here to contend with, all are
our own people.
	I am a subscriber for the WOMAN'S EX-
PONENT and I think it is a very nice, use-
ful paper, and I peruse it with care and in-
terest. When I received the Exponent
which contained the selection from the
“Times and Seasons” by President Joseph
Smith, although I have never seen that
volume of the “Times and Seasons,” but I
sent the poem in full that I took from the
Deseret News that was printed May 14,
1876. I want to keep up my subscription
for the paper and will send the money just
as soon as I can get it.
	Your sister and co-laborer in the Gospel
H. M. EARL.
	April 4, 1897.


DR. HENRY M. HOWE, the distinguished
son of a distinguished mother, Mrs. Julia
Ward Howe, has been elected professor of
metallurgy at Columbia University. Dr.
Howe will give a course of lectures in the
School of Mines, beginning on April 19,
treating of the properties of steel, crucible
steel, cementation and the Harvey process.
Dr. Howe received the Bessemer medal
from the British Iron and Steel Institute
for his work on the “Metallurgy of Steel,”
and he is the ex-president of the American
Institute of Mining Engineers.
	THE White Ribbon (New Zealand) states
that two Maori ladies, Mrs. Hirani and
Mrs. Taranaki, were members of a deputa-
tion which recently waited on the premier
of New Zealand in regard to native land
disputes.
