				D. E. DUDLEY.


FROM a recent monograph of the United
States Census Bureau is taken statistics
that show how the new woman developed
in the United States between the years 1870
and 1890. The figures relate to the num-
ber of women in other than domestic em-
ployments, and are of great interest. In
1870 there were 693 actresses, in 1890 there
were 3,948. The solitary woman architect
of 1870 twenty years later had 21 rivals in
her profession. The number of artists and
teachers of art has increased from 412 to
10,810. Under the head of authors, liter-
ary and scientific persons the number in-
creased from 159 to 2,725. There were no
women chemists, assayers or metallurgists
in 1870, but twenty years later there were
46. Women preachers increased in num-
ber from 67 to 1,235. The success of 24
dentists inspired 337 new women to take
up the trade. Designers, draughtsmen and
inventors grew from 13 to 206. There was
not a known woman civil engineer or sur-
veyor twenty-five years ago, yet five years
ago there were 127 mistresses of the transit,
theodolite and chain. A nucleus of 35
journalists grew to an army of 888 in two
decades. Musicians and teachers of music
increased nearly seven fold, or from 5,753
to 34,519. In the field of politics, that is,
Government officials, State, Federal and
local, the number had grown in twenty
years from 414 to 4,875. Physicians and
surgeons of the fair sex were rapidly ad-
mitted to practice, and the ranks grew from
527 to 4,555. By 1870 the competency of
women teachers in public and private
schools was generally recognized, and
84,047 were so employed, but twenty years
later the birch was wielded by 245,965
qualified women, some of whom held chairs
in famous colleges. The 100 theatrical
managers and showmen in 1870 must have
succeeded, for the year 1890 found 634 of
them “in the business.” Two women in
1890 had taken up the profession of veter-
inary surgeons. The army of bookkeepers,
accountants and clerks grew from 8,016 to
91,825. A most extraordinary statement is
that in 1870 there were but 7 women sten-
ographers and typewriters, and they were
the pioneers of the 21,185 that came later,
a figure which at the present time must be


increased by 10,000 at least. Saleswomen
could hardly be said to be established in
1870, as the census takers could find but
2,775. When they went around twenty
year later they discovered 58,499 young
women calling for “Ca-sh.” There is no
question but that the employment of wo-
men on all the lines indicated has increased
enormously since the census was taken.
