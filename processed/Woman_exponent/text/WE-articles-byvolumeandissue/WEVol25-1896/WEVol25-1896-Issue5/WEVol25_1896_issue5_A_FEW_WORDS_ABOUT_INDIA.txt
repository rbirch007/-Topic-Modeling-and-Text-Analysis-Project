A FEW WORDS ABOUT INDIA.

	As a rule I think readers are more inter-
ested in details of a foreign country when
given by one who has really visited it, and
personally witnessed the various scenes
depicted. As many years of my life were
spent in India, I shall have great pleasure-
if I can interest you with a few details re-
garding it, its inhabitants and my exper
ences while there.

	I arrived in Calcutta in December 1860,
after a four months’ passage on board a

[Column 2]
sailing vessel, of which my uncle was the
captain. 
	The river Hugli by which Calcutta is
reached is a most dangerous river on ac-
count of its shifting sands, consequently
the pilot who takes charge of the vessel
at the Sand Heads, that is, where the
mouth of the river is entered from the Bay
of Bengal, has men sounding and shouting
out every five minutes the depth of water.
It is always a most exciting time when
entering a foreign port, and Eastern ports
are more especially interesting to those
who have never seen anything but the
Western hemisphere surroundings. The
banks with their strange trees of cocoanut
and palms have a great charm; the houses
with their strange forms and colors, fill the
stranger with curiosity to know what the
interior of such remarkable buildings must 
be, and what the inhabitants are like.
	First we pass Tangir Island where
nobody can live as it is infested with the
largest species of Bengal tigers, and where
many Europeans every year lose their
lives in tiger hunting.
	On sailing up the river the next sight
that interests one in nearing the city is the
residence of the late King of Oude. When
the British took possession of his territory
he was brought to Calcutta and a large
tract of land, together with a handsome
pension was granted to him. The reason
of the forfeiture of his territory I will ex-
plain later on. He has many houses in his
grounds, all of different colors and designs,
where he has an immense harem. He also
keeps thousands of pigeons, and it is most in-
teresting to see them flying about in flocks.
The old man is never allowed to leave Cal-
cutta, and is frequently seen taking a drive
in an old-fashioned carriage attended by
about 20 troopers, natives of course. They are
dirty, badly dressed, badly mounted, are
poor riders and most unsoldierly in appear-
ance. The attendants of a third class circus
in a procession are quite respectable com-
pared with these royal body guards; it is
indeed a relic of departed grandeur. To
give a clear idea of Calcutta to those who
have never seen it is not easy. The city may
be roughly divided into two parts—the
European and the native; though in the
native part some poor Europeans live, and
in the European part some rich natives re-
side. Here are the fine houses that give
Calcutta the name of “The City of
Palaces.”
	They are built of brick and plastered
white, with verandahs and venetians paint-
ed bright green; when clean and the paint
fresh, a Calcutta house is very pretty. The
roofs are mostly flat, and in the evenings
we sit there and chat, dance and generally
enjoy ourselves, for however hot the day
may have been there is nearly always a
pleasant cool breeze from the sea that comes up
the river in the evening, and I assure you it is
enjoyable. There is a fine drive called the
Strand where in the evening may be seen
every imaginable conveyance, as everybody
who can get one takes a drive to see and
be seen—others walk and enjoy the sight
Of the gay dresses, the flirtations, etc., that are


[Column 1]
going on. All the scandal of Calcutta is
discussed on the Strand. There is a small
park with a band stand, and whilst the
band is playing, every item of news is dis-
cussed. If Miss So and So is engaged it is
made known there as she is seen driving
with Mr. So and So for the first time alone,
and Mrs. Grundy is sure it must be so! A
curious feature of Calcutta is the native
part of the town, especially China bazaar,
a row of native shops. When the shops
are open about 10 o’clock, the business of
the day commences; and a lively scene
it is. A stranger is pestered as he walks 
along or his carriage is stopped by the tout
who declares you are certain to get all you
want at his shop, and further that at his
shop you will have honest men to deal with
whilst at all his rivals there are only liars
and rogues. At times they seem as if they
were going to fight, but you need not fear
they are mere words and they have no idea 
of giving offence; in fact if you look back 
after refusing to deal with any of them, you
will see they are having a good laugh to-
gether at your expense. In China bazaars
you can meet with almost everything you
can require, but you will have great diffi-
culty in finding it, and when found, in get- 
ing it at anything like a reasonable price,
especially if you cannot speak the language
and do not understand the customs of the
people. They usually ask about double
what they will take. There is an old say-
ing amongst the Europeans, “Multiply by
2, divide by 4, and then give them a half of
that.” The Balameal Gardens are the
next place I visited. I saw the wonderful
Banyan Tree which is said to be large
enough to shelter 10,000 men. The new
branches of the tree run down until they
touch the ground where they take root and
become supporters and feeders of the older
parts of the tree. There is no reason why
this tree should not in the course of years
spread so that it will cover miles. I have
frequently spent a pleasant day’s picnic un-
der this tree where we have put up curtains
and had a cook-room, kitchen, parlor with
cane chairs and couches, dining-room etc.,
and have had our servants there with every
necessity for the tables which have been
spread on the ground, to satisfy ourselves
very thoroughly, in dancing, racing,
courting, playing hide and seek, and on one
occasion we went down the river in native
boats to the residence of Sir Barnes Peacock,
about forty of us. We were told to be
sure to take to our boats again before 7
o’clock, as we could not turn a certain
point after 8 o'clock as the current in the
Hugli River was very stormy four hours’
in and eight out. However some of the
girls and boys did not attend to the beating
of the gong, and the consequence was
when we reached the point it was found to
be impossible to round it, and there we
had to stay tied up to the bank all night
in open boats, eaten up by mosquitoes
and frightened to death almost by hearing
the owls shriek, the hyenas laugh and
the jackals howl. But we were well se-
cured by strong ropes about 100 yards from
the bank; and I can assure you we were
very glad when the tide turned at 4 o’clock
in the morning and we reached Calcutta in
about an hour and a half, very hungry, as
we had demolished everything before we left
for the boat. In one of the baskets, one of
the gentlemen who was desired to see if
there was anything eatable in them, found a
piece of cheese and a cucumber, which were

[Column 2]
speedily devoured, but no bread or biscuits
or even water; the river water was scarcely
palatable as it is a common thing to see
dead bodies float up and down the river
with great vultures, or adjutants as they
are called, picking at them, and sometimes
the sight is too dreadful to describe.
	Another place of interest on the banks of
the Hugli is the burning ghaul of Kale 
ghaul, as it is called, where the natives
burn their dead. I have frequently sat on
board the ship in the Hugli River and
seen the natives build their pyre and when
ready pour the fat on it and set fire to
it. When hot enough they bring the corpse
down on a charpoi (or bamboo couch made
of strirg and bamboo) and turn it over,
throwing the corpse into the middle of the
burning mass; then begin the howling and
shouting such as only the native Indians
can make. At one time it was the custom
to burn the widow with her husband, but
now it is forbidden by the English law though
doubtless it is done secretly. Until the
law interfered many a child of tender years
who had been married when perhaps four
or five years of age and whose husband
might have been twenty or thirty, and had
died, was thrown into the fire with her hus-
band and burned to death. The cries of
the victim were drowned by the howling
and shouting of the mad fanatics.
Child marriage is still very common; some
girls are married about as soon as born,
where there is some advantage to be derived
from the union.
	Plural marriage is also very general, but
among the poorer natives it is seldom until
the first wife is about 40 when the man takes
another about 15 and the first then becomes
usually a slave to the second, and is very
cruelly treated. The rich natives have
many wives at once, and they are generally
very happy living in the harem together
where they live in great luxury and idle-
ness; but the pour suffer terribly from the
custom.
	As a rule the natives are a quiet, docile
race, and when treated kindly become
greatly attached to their employers. It is
the custom to keep a great number of ser-
vants, eight being the usual staff. Each one
has his or her duties to perform, and if the
mistress is a good manager she can conduct
her house with very little trouble. They
respect a master and mistress who insist
upon each doing his duty, and if they
know they will not be allowed to shirk it,
they will do it faithfully, and have no de-
sire to leave their service. One thing is
imperative on a master and mistress, that is
to keep their word at all times and pay them
honestly and regularly. It is well to treat
them as children; let them see you are
satisfied when they do well, and punish
them if they do wrong. They are keenly
alive to any injustice but ready to acknowl-
edge themselves in the wrong if it is proved
against them. When you have proved
it without doubt they will fall on their
knees and putting their hands together say,
“Put your foot on me,” and after obtaining
forgiveness will serve you well until found
out again. They can be trusted with un-
told gold if they think you know exactly
what you have placed in their care, but
should not like to trust them otherwise.
	There is a kind of freemasonry amongst
them, and they will never tell of one anoth-
er, consequently it is not an easy matter to
find out an offence. Their cunning and
duplicity is most extraordinary; it would

[Column 3]
never enter into the mind of any other race,
I think, to make the excuses and make up
the plausible tale that the native Indian can.
