					LAIE OAHU, July 4.
	This is a day of pleasure at home I pre-
sume, but here I am in school, and as my
pupils are writing their examples I thought
I would commence a letter to my dear
parent, for my mind is with you to-day.
I went across the yard previous to coming
to school to tell the sisters it was the Fourth
of July, they were quite astonished, but all
days are alike here. I try to be happy at
all times even when Santipedes give me a
visit. I was awakened last night at two
o'clock with a large Santipede being in my
bosom. I knew what it was although I was
hardly awake; I jerked it out and in so do-
ing it bit me just above my right breast
and on my right hand, I sprang from the
bed, being fully awakened by this time,
and applied ammonia to the bitten parts,
but got very little rest the remainder of the
night as they were very painful.
					July 24, 4 o'clock
	We have feasted on the rich things of
Laie to-day, beef, poi and potatoes which
were really nice. The natives have done
their best to-day, all came out in white.
The procession was formed just outside the
yard, they marched up in front of our
house and gave three cheers, placing us
white people in front next the band. They
really looked nice, twenty-four young la-
dies dressed in white, with wreaths around
their heads and necks; twenty-four young
men; twenty-four elderly ladies in white
dresses with lays, and twenty-four elderly
men. The house was decorated beautifully.
It takes this people to ornament a room;
besides the things grow here to do it with.
The words written over the stand were,
Aloha na ah chee, July 24th A. D. 1847, the
letters composed of flowers, also "WE OF
