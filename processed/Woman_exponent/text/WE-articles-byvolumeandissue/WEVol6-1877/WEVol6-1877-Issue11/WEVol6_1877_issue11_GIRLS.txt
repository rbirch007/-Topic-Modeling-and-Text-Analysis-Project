				GIRLS.

	I sometimes wonder if the young ladies who
have enrolled themselves as Retrenchment
girls, ever stop and reflect how many dollars
PER ANNUM are spent in various things: we
will say dress goods, for instance, which are
imported, when our home-made goods compare
favorably with eastern fabrics, and usually
wear better.
	At present we often wear things, that if they
were home-made, we would not be seen in.
	Now let us make a rough estimate of how
many dollars we sisters who belong to these
Associations could keep at home, making busi-
ness for our fathers and brothers, and some-
times ourselves, if we followed out practically
that which we profess to sustain. Usually
when we buy, we spend about three or more
dollars for any kind of a dress, except calico,
and say from five to ten for a cloak or shawl
for winter. We will say there are twenty So-
cieties in this city, with at least twenty-five
members in each; if these two articles alone
were home-made, and each member made a
point of wearing them, just see what a nice,
snug little sum would be circulating in our
own community; and then if all the Relief
Societies did the same, don't you think it
would tell? And would not our merchants al-
ways have home-made goods on hand? Of
course they would. And don't you think that
Brother Culter at the Home Commission Store,
and all those who are laboring to build up
Zion, would look smiling, and feel better?
Certainly they would. And if we want to
make our fathers, husbands, brothers or friends
a present, and it was some home-made arti-
cle, don't you think they would prize it all the
more? Of course they would; if they did not
they have not got the good sense I give them
credit for, and if they hav'n't, don't give them
any present; that's my advice to you.
	Girls, let us try and stem the current just a
little, and not destroy and pull down, but seek
to strengthen and build up.
