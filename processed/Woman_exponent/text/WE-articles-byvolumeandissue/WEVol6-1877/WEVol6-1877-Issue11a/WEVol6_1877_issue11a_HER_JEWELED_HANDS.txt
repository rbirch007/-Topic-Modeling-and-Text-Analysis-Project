	As a faithful delineator I must state that
Madame Kut-a-dashe's beauty lies entirely
in her wardrobe. Her face is pale and her
eyes sunken from the effects of late hours,
dissipation, rich viands and fashionable
dressing. Her hair is red, and was, of
course, arranged in the latest and, to such
a face as hers, most unbecoming style. On
the topmost point of the pyramid of hair
was perched an affair of velvet and flowers,
which Madame De Fuss, the milliner, calls
a bonnet, but which much more resembles
the mortars used for compounding drugs in
