"﻿I WAS KEPT IN."

	"Why didn't you come home to lunch?"
asked a mother of a pale, nervous little girl
as she came listlessly into the room one sul-
try afternoon, after school was out for the
day.
	"I was kept in," answered the child, and
the tired look and pitiful quiver of the voice
roused tenderness in the mother's heart
and indignation in ours.
	We are not one of those who complain or
find fault with school discipline, but it is
barbarity in the extreme to keep children
in their seats for any reason whatever dur-
ing the regular and needed seasons for rec-
reation. Then to keep a delicate little girl
whose brain is being worked to death at the
expense of her bodily forces, as any one
can see, in during the entire noon, conning
over in her dizzy little brain problems and
propositions that will come to her readily
and freely after a little rest, is barbarity
that is simply outrageous. We never hear
the expression, "I was kept in," at recess or
noon, coming from a girl or boy under six-
teen years of age, without feeling an in-
stant throb of indignation, and a desire to
give the teacher a lecture on hygiene and
humanity.—Ex.


Half of the people in the world are idle
for want of some overseeing eye to set
them to work. The advice which Haydon
gave to the erratic poet Keats, to settle
down to some definite purpose, needs be
given to almost one half of mankind.
There are but few persons but would find
themselves comfortably well off if they
would take hold of any one of a hundred
pursuits and stick to it. Industry and econ-
omy will make a most wonderful change in
many households.
