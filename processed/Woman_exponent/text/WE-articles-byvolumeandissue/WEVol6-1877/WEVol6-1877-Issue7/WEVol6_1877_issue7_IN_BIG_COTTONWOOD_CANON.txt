	IN BIG COTTONWOOD CANON.—
The night father arrived we had a very
nice time around the camp-fire singing jok-
ing and talking until nine o'clock, then we
sang "Nearer my God to Thee," then fath-
er had prayers, after that we sung "Praise
God from whom all blessings flow." Every-
thing around us seemed grand and sublime;
the great fire of pine logs, the clear blue
sky above, in which the moon shone
brightly as if to rival our fire, the tall pine
trees towering above us, the mountains sur-
rounding us, father in all his goodness
standing among us, his noble features and
gray beard commanding reverence and re-
spect—it was a picture that so impressed
me, that I shall never forget it. If I were
an artist I should try to paint it, but it
would be impossible to paint the grateful
hearts, and the noble thoughts that filled
our minds at that time.
	We all went up to Lake Mary and such a
lovely sight I never saw; in order to get to
the lake we pass through a large grove, or
forest of tall pines so straight and high
they seem almost to reach to the sky, and
we fancy they say to us poor little frail be-
ings below, "We're nearer to heaven than
you." The lovliest grass and flowers cover
the earth underneath the trees; can you im-
agine a more charming picture?
	M.
Emiline Wells
