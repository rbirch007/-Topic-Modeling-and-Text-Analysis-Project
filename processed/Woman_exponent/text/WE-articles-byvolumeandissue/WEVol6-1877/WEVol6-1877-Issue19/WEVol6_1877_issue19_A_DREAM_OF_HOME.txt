A DREAM OF HOME.

Oh mother! last night I was with you,
	And many glad stories I told;
It was Summer, and sunset, the valley
	All curtained in crimson and gold.

We were down on the farm and the breezes
	Came sweet from the meadow and creek;
I heard their soft sighs and responsive,
	My heart throbs were happy and quick.

I swept and cleaned house with my sisters—
	They were there with us, mother, all three—
And we sang while we worked all together,
	As happy as happy could be.

I sported and laughed with my brothers,
	And frightened the fish in the spring,
And waltzed round the room with babies;
	The gladdest and merriest thing.

Through the barn-yard I scouted and scrambled
	And rollicked the hill-tops about,
Like a young colt, which, stabled all Winter,
	Has been for the Summer "turned out."

Half wearied at last, I was sitting,
	In soft, dusky twilight alone:
Save one—my beloved one—was near me,
	And spoke in his kind, wooing tone.

I awoke, and grim Winter's stiff daylight,
	The window had coldly o'erspread;
I suppose 'twas the evenings's light prattle
	That brought it all into my head.

But say, when the Springtime is over,
	And Summer has certainly come,
May I live for a brief little season,
	Again with my mother at home?

When the city grows dusty and snltry,
	May I lay care and labors all by,
And come to the farm for a respite,
	And spend happy June and July?

		LULA.
S. L. City, Feb.1874.	Lula G. Richards
