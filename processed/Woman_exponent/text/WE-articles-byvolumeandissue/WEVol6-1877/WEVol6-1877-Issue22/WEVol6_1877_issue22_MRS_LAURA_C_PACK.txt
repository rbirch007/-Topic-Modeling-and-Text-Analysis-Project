					MRS. LAURA C. PACK.

	SISTER POLLY Angell, wife of Truman O
Angell, a widely known and highly esteemed
lady, died very suddenly at her residence in
the 13th Ward of this city, on the 1st of April.
The cause of death was supposed to have been
disease of the heart. Sister Angell was
among the pioneer women of the Latter-day
Work, and faithfully bore her part in the per-
secutions and hardships to which the Saints in
the early days of the church were subjected.
She was a friend to the poor and always ready
to succor and comfort the distressed. Among
the many friends and relatives left to mourn
for her, including children and grand-child-
ren, none, perhaps, will feel her loss so keenly
as Miss Polly Tollman, an orphaned grand-
daughter and namesake of the deceased, who
had for years resided with, and been all almost
constant companion to her. Our most sincere
sympathies are with this young lady. The fu-
neral services were conducted at the 13th Ward
Assembly Rooms on Thursday, the 4th inst.,
and were largely attended. Addresses were
made by Bishop Woolley, Elders John W.
Young, F. Little and Truman O. Angell. All
testified to the excellent character and fine per-
sonal qualities of the deceased. Sister Angell
was 64 years, 9 months and 26 days, old when
she died.


A LITERARY society at Delaware, O., has
a letter written by Horace Greeley, in 1849,
in which he thus refers to his educational
attainments: "I know a little newspaper
Latin and French, and might have mastered
a little Greek the same way, if the bar-
barians had known enough to use an intel-
ligible alphabet. Of mathematics I learned
what is contained in Adam's arithmetic;
of grammar, just enough to see clearly that
Lindley Murray knew very little and blun-
dered shockingly. As to chirography,
mine speaks for itself, not clearly as to mat-
ter, but quite distinctly enough as to man-
ner."
