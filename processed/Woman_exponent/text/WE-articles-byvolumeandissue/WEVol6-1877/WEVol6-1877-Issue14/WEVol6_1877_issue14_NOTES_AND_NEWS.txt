NOTES AND NEWS.

	"I have an idea to ventilate," writes a
correspondent. "In looking over the draw-
ings in an iron company's office, I was
struck with their beauty and delicacy, and
I could not help saying, Why couldn't wo-
men be draughtsman? With a practical
knowledge of drawing, algebra, geometry,
and a little taste and perseverance, I am
sure an intelligent girl could learn to con-
struct bridges. There is little muscular
effort, and the work is indoors." This idea,
or one nearly similar, has been already in
successful operation in London. In Febru-
ary, 1876, an office was established, and the
management undertaken by a Miss Crosby,
who had acquired a thorough knowledge of
the work in the house of an eminent firm
of engineers. Her superintendence was
given gratuitously. During the first year
the workers earned over $1,300.—Woman's
Words.
	Woman's Printing Association.—This
Association is organized for the purpose of
giving special facilities for the thorough
training and employment of girls and wo-
men in type-setting, engraving, and other
light branches of printing. Also for the
purpose of publishing books, tracts, and
papers of special interest to women, for
their education, encouragement and ad-
vancment in all the trades, arts and sciences.
	Those who desire to assist in promoting
this object are invited to take shares in the
Association, and to send orders for printing.
	Branch agencies will be established in
the leading cities, and correspondents are
wanted in all the principal towns.
	Further infromation may be obtained by
addressing the secretary,
	MRS. JUAN LEWIS,
