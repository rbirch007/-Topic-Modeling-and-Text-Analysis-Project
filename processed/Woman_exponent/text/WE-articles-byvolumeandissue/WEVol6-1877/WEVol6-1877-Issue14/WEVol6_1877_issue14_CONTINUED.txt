	Mrs. M. I. Horne said: "I look on the
grain movement as a very important one;
we have done pretty well, considering the
amount of discouragement we had to con-
tend with. I often think that some of the
brethren need converting in this cause, for
they ridicule the idea of women being
called on to store and take care of wheat. I
feel that in the future we will accomplish
the object we have set out to perform—I
feel the Lord will bless our efforts.
	"We should ascertain the best way to
keep our grain good and sound; every set-
tlement should have a store-house, built in
such a manner as to keep the grain in good
condition, and it should be turned over and
examined once or twice a year, to see that
no insect, or anything that would destroy
it, is in it. We must not weary in well-
doing. I am pleased with the remarks of
our brethren; we know that the man and
the woman is one before the Lord. If we
educate and qualify ourselves to be wise
mothers, we will elevate the coming gener-
ations. I trust that our report next year
will be much larger than it is this, that we
may have a larger than it is this, that we
may have a large amount of wheat on hand,
well and carefully stored."
	Miss E. R. Snow said: "If our sisters will
use their persuasive powers, the young
brethren will help them to put up the gran-
aries, and I am sure they will feel honored
in being solicited to assist in such impor-
tant business. It has been reported that the
wheat has been sold: I hope my sisters will
pay no attention to any such story; no one
has any right to touch a grain of it, only to
see that it is in good condition."
	Mrs. E. B. Wells said: "I feel that this is
an important occasion. I am thankful for
the good instruction we have received from
our Presiding Bishop and from President
Taylor. It looks as though we had not
done very much, but considering every-
thing I think we have done very well. I
hope we will be able to report 50,000 bushels
stored, at our next Annual meeting. There
are some wards in this city, and several set-
tlements, that have not sent in any report,

[Column 2]
and we know they have wheat stored. We
keep separate books for the wheat, and at
our first meeting we advised all the Socie-
ttes to do so.
	Our reports that we have received specify
that the wheat reported is in good condi-
tion, and how stored; whether in bins
owned by the Societies, or whether in bins
lent them by the Bishops, or other brethren.
We have had the wheat stored by the Cen-
tral Committee examined, and I feel safe in
reporting to you that it is in good condition
and all there. It should be fully under-
stood, that the wheat we are now using our
energies to collect and store away, is stored
for Zion.
	I do not think it wise to take the wheat
that has been donated for building store-
houses; but if the sisters will, as Sister
Snow says, use their persuasive powers,'
the young men will help in erecting store-
houses. I believe that the sisters all feel
united to do our best to carry out the instruc-
tions we have received. There is a little
more time, and I would like to hear from
some of the sisters."
	Bishop Hunter said: "It is very import-
ant and highly commendable to gather and
store wheat, I wish to say that the dona-
tions for the Temple, from the Relief Socie
ty, have been very liberal indeed; they
have amounted to thousands of dollars.
The donations sent in for the St. George
Temple by the sisters were truly astonish-
ing; even this week there has come into
the Tithing Office dollars and dollars, as
donations to our Temple here. For your
information, I will try and get my clerk,
Brother Goddard, to write out a state-
ment of what has been given in a short
time. I know that the people will take
pleasure in giving in their tithing and their
offerings, and the Lord will bless them for
it. I know that many would have suffered
only for the efficient aid rendered them by
the Relief Societies. Many things are in
your power to do, and in doing them you
will be blessed, and you are blessed."
	Mrs. Jane S. Richards, from Ogden, said:
"I came here to-day to receive instruction,
but I can say that Weber County is trying
to help in this good work; we have as our
report to the President tells, over 500 bush-
els stored, and we hope to do better next
year. I would have been glad to have heard
some instructions or suggestions given in
regard to taking care of our wheat, as we
are young in the business we need some
counsel."
	Mrs. E. A. Whitney said:  "I am glad to
be with you, my sisters; I have been away
resting for some time past, and am thank-
ful to be able to come among you again.
Joseph Smith said that the Relief Society, if
they followed the connsel of the Priesthood,
would be the greatest help in doing good
that ever was known on the earth, and
would be the means of doing a great work.
The teachings we have heard to-day are
true. They are blessings given by the Holy
Spirit, and they must not to be passed over
carelessly. I have held on to the promises
given us in early days in this church. Jo-
seph also said that the cities that would reject
the Gospel would feel the judgments, and I
thought then I should not live to see it ful-
filled, but I have. These things are being
fulfilled every day. I know there is great
faith among the sisters; I have felt the in-
fluence of their prayers, and been healed
when the sisters have prayed for me in
their meetings." Sister Whitney then sang
a song of Zion. Meeting adjourned until
Nov. 18, 1878. The congregation rose and
sand "Praise God," &c. Benediction by
President John Taylor.
