				GOSHEN NOV. 2nd, 1877.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	The Relief Society of this place held an
annual meeting Oct. 8, giving a report of

[Column 3]
finance, and exhibiting a variety of home
industries. There were present on the
stand by invitation, Mrs. M. A. Hyde, and the
Presidents of Santiquin and Payson, with
their Counselors and Secretary; Bishop Wil-
liam Price, his Counselors, and Brother
Barry Wride of Payson. The meeting was
presided over by President Mary Johnsen.
After the usual opening exercises our Bish-
op addressed the meeting, giving us good
counsel and expressed himself pleased with
our progress and financial report; compared
us to the honey-bee and congratulated us
in being engaged in such a laudable work,
and felt to bless all those enaged therein;
Mrs. M. A. Hyde, Mrs. J. B. Simons, Mrs.
Elizabeth Stickney, Mrs. Agnes Douglas
and Bro. Barry Wride spoke and said many
things calculated to comfort and help us.
Our President read a poem composed for
the occasion, by Mrs. Christina Higginson.
The young ladies of the Mutual Improve-
ment Association took an active part in the
meeting and in contributing specimens of
handiwork, and also in reading their paper
which was very interesting, and the min-
utes of their last meeting which plainly
showed they are alive to their duties and
are striving in the right direction.
	Our financial report from June 1st, 1876,
to June 4th, 1877:
Donations received........................... $ 87 75
Disbursed to the poor.......................    47 27
Donated to Arizona mission.............    16 00
Building material for Relief So-
	ciety House.................................   160 00
Shares in Co-op Store.......................      30 00
Shares in Provo Factory...................    140 00
Cash in hand......................................      46 00
Goods and produce on hand..........       90 00
	Our President arose and thanked the
brethren and sisters for their attendance,
she had been very much edified by their re-
marks and as the storing of grain had been
mentioned she would report the quantity on
hand; the articles exhibited were the work
of the sisters of this place; she hoped our
next annual meeting would be held in our
new Society House. Among the home in-
dustries exhibited was "Our Motto, Refuge
for the Needy," and representing the Bee-
hive all worked in Berlin wool. The dox-
ology also worked in Berlin wool by one of
our young sisters, straw and coral baskets,
home-made flowers, hair work, pin cushions,
rag carpets, rag mats, quilts of various
kinds; wool-daisy mats, woolen shawls, mus-
lin needle-work, neckties of different mate-
rials, crotchet table covers, straw picture
frames, and many other articles.
	The meeting was a gratifying one to all
present, and they seemed to enjoy them-
selves very much, and a good spirit prevail-
ed in our midst. There seems to be a spirit
of unity among the sisters of this place;
we feel well in our meetings, and are de-
termined to help roll on this Latter-day
work. May we follow the counsels of our
Lord, and walk in His footsteps, remember-
i[-]g our beloved leader who has passed be-
hind the veil, and imitate his noble exam-
ple so that we may be prepared when we
are called to pass to the spirit world.
	Wishing you and your valuable paper
every success, I remain as ever, your sister
in the New and Everlasting Covenant.
			LOUISA TAYLOR, Secretary.


THROUGH THE recent strike of cigar-
makers in New York City, 4,000 girls have
been employed. The girls have succeeded
wonderfully with the work, it is said; and
if the strike continues, the men’s places
will be filled with girls. 12,000 men are
engaged in the strike.
