HOME AFFAIRS.

	REPORTS have been received from Tres Ala-
mis, Clarkston, Cedar City, Malad, Ephraim,
Rockville and Herriman, which we will give to
the public as early as possible. We have also
on hand other productions in poetry and prose,
but we solicit more matter from our contribu-
tors that we may be able to cull the best.

	MRS ELLIS R. SHIPP, M.D., having recently
returned from Philadelphia, has opened an of-
fice for practice in Bishop Hunter's former resi-
dence, on Main Street, well known to all old
settlers. Her advertisement appears now, for
the first time, in our columns. Mrs. Shipp hav-
ing thoroughly qualified herself for the profes-
sion, we trust will receive the patronage of the
public.

	WE CALL attention to the card of Dr. Ellen
B. Ferguson, in another column. This lady
who has been engaged in the practice of medi-
cine for the past fifteen years, has met with re-
markable success in the treatment of diseases
of children, especially diphtheria and other
diseases affecting the throat and lungs. We
would also announce that the classes in Music,
French and Drawing conducted by Dr. Fergu-
son, at her residence, will re-assemble next
week. She will also organize a class of young
ladies and gentlemen for the study of elocution
and dramatic reading.

	THE transit of Mercury which took place on
the 7th inst., gave to the scientists an opportu-
nity to test the photohelioscope, the invention
of M. Angot, which proved to be effective for ob-
servations of this nature and wi[-]l be useful
probably in witnessing the next transit of Ve-
nus in 1882. Photographic views were taken as
the planet traveled across the sun. It is ex-
pected the labors of these scientists during the
transit will materially aid in a more perfect
observation of the next transit of Venus, and
in determining the exact distance of the sun
from the Earth.
