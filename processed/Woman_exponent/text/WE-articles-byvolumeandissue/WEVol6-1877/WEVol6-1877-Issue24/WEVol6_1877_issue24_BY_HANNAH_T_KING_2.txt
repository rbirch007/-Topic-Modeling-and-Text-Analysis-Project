BY HANNAH T. KING.

	Farewell loved harbingers of Spring!
		Thy glory's past, thy reign is o'er,
	No more shall I thy beauties sing
		Thy luscious sweets inhale no more!

	How many a moment of delight
		Thy sweets bestowed thy presence gave,
	But now thou'rt fading from my sight
		And passing to thy humble grave!

	Tis ever thus, with things of earth
		The loveliest e'er the lost one is—
	But all must die that e'er had birth
		Though 'bodying all our earthly bliss.

	Though thou art gone, on memory's page
		Thy modest worth shall cling to me,
	And thou through life, in good or ill,
		My humble monitor still shall be.

	Like thee, may I in humble guise
		Far from the world live out my days
	Its pomps and vanities despise
		Upheld amid its luring maze—

	And when the hand of death appears
		Which no vain mortal can control,
	And friends have shed the parting tears
		And Christ, my God received my soul,

	Then raise for me no "storied Urn,"
		No gorgeous Temple be my bed,
	My native earth upon me turn
		And plant the Violet at my head;

	There, like that sweet and humble flower
		May I (the grave's cold winter past)
	Bloom forth again in Heaven's own bower
		When summoned by the trumpet's blast.

Salt Lake City, May, 1878.
