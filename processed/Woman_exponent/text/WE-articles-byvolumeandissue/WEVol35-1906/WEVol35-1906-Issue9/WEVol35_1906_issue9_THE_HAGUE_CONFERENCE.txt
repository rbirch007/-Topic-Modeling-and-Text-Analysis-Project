	THE HAGUE CONFERENCE.
	The Hague conference will be held this
year in a thirteenth century castle, “The
House in the Woods,” it is said will be
entirely too small, for the delegates rep-
resenting 46 countries. The Dutch gov-
ernment decided to fit up the ancient cas-
tle latterly known as “The Hall of
Knights.” It stands in the center of a
paved court, and is reached through half
a dozen picturesque portals over which
are chiseled the arms of the counts of
Holland. The hall in the interior is an
enormous one which occupies the entire
space within the structure in front and a
series of smaller rooms in the rear. The
large hall was the great banqueting room
of the counts and their knights, when they
made merry during the hunting season.
Here is where the sessions of the confer-
ence will be held.
	Since 1702 the main hall has been used
by the queen for the ceremony of making
the address of the throne to the houses
of parliament in joint session. The hall
has a vaulted roof with huge beams and
rafters after the Dutch fashion. The
desks are covered with green baize, and
the rostrum for the presiding officers in
front and at the back desks for the secre-
taries arranged in a semicircle. The whole
seems to be to bring the delegates as
close together as possible, for exchange

simplicity.
	The quarters for the delegates will be
something of a serious problem, as there
is but one hotel within the city proper
fitted for entertaining distinguished diplo-
mats. This is the Hotel des Indes, and is
splendidly appointed.
