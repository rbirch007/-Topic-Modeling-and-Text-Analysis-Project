	Of Mr. Crawford it may be said, some
of his observations, are so shrewd and
well said that they may almost rank as
aphorisms; for example: “Women like to
be understood before they speak, and the
grosser intellect of man, seldom more
than half understands them when they
have spoken.”
	“Can it be hard, he says, to get a wo-
man’s love,” then changes presently and
says, “Truly it is less easy than is supposed,
for the heart of a woman who does not
love, is like a desert, when the wind blows
over it, and there are neither tracks nor
land marks. And I am wandering in the
desert like a man seeking lost camels.”
From the story of “Khalea.”
	“What is a beautiful woman without
love?” he asks. 
	“She is like a garden wherein are all
kinds of rare flowers and much grass, and
deep shade, but in which a man cannot
live, because nothing grows there which
he can eat when he is hungry.”
	Sometimes he is good in dialogue, but
sometimes weak. By some authors he is
thought to be the most versatile and
vivacious of modern writers. 
	He is an American though born on
Italian soil. Any critical estimate of Mr.
Crawford’s rank, depends much on the 
cast of mind of the reader. 
	A polished man of the world his writ-
ings are free from jarring notes. His
style has personal quality and he possesses
the gift of throwing a romantic halo
around his personages, their speech is
crisp and bright, developing character
without strain or undue effort at wit. 
	Mr. Crawford must take a high rank
among modern novelists. He excels in
his description of the human heart. His
great breadth of view, keen intuitions
and artistic feelings, making his works of
interest and value, perfectly pure, with-
out taint of vulgarity or coarseness.
