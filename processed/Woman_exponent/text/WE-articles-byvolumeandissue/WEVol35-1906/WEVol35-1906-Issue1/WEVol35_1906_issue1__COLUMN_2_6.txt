		TOOELE STAKE.
			Lecture 1.
	    Our Boys and Our Girls.
	(a)	What shall they be taught and who
shall teach it.
	(b) Ignorance is not innocence.
	(c) The results of foolish modesty.
	(d) At what age should a boy or girl be
ready to share father’s and mother’s
work?
	(e) What would be the effect upon the
boy or girl, morally, physically and men-
tally by being allowed to go into society
too young?
	(f) How to retain the confidence of the
boy or girl as they grow up to manhood
and womanhood.
	(g) Parents should look well to the
company their boys and girls keep, for
therein lies the key to their success or
failure.
			Lecture 2.
		Purity of Thought.
	(a) From the abundance of the heart
the mouth speaketh.
	(b) Are not our minds and characters
made in a great measure by our thoughts.
	(c) Encourage pure conversation in the
home.
	(d) What is the result of impure con-
versation among our boys and girls.
	(e) To have pure thoughts, keep mind
employed in pure studies.
	(f) Can the Spirit of God dwell with
with those who encourage impure
thoughts and conversations?
			Lecture 3.
		Rights of Children.
	(a) We must not only be ready to talk
to and advise children, but also be ready
to listen while they talk.
	(b) The talk we have now with our
children truly “heart to heart” talk will
help them as the years go by to decide
for right and truth.
	(c) Children’s rights should not inter-
fere with the rights of others, and the
sooner they learn that most important
lesson, the better parents and citizens
will they in turn become.
	(d) One fault of parents is, that they
too often exact blind obedience from
their children.
	(e) A boy or girl in knowing the law
and obeying it, becomes a free moral
agent whom even God would not restrict.
	(f) Should we not respect the rights of
our children in public gatherings, that
when they arrive early and get a good
seat, should they be crowded out to make
room for some one who arrives there
late?
	(This might be modified.)
			Lecture 4.
		Partiality in the Home.
	(a) A simple hour of justice is worth
several years of prayer. 

[Column 3]
	(b) Justice knows no law but equity. 
	(c) No mother should ever speak in
her children’s presence of the ‘‘awkward
age,” thereby increasing the self-con-
sciousness of that period.
	(d) Necessity of parents being impar-
tial in the family circle.
	(e) Many children become discouraged
and fall by the wayside because their pa-
rents are manifestly partial in the be-
stowal of their love and encouragement.
	(f) God help our parents to all live aright,
	And may our homes all love and
		truth unfold,
	(g) Since life for us no loftier aim can
	hold
       Than leading little children to the
	right.
			Lecture 5.
		    Moral Training.
	(a) The object of moral and religious
training isto enable the child to recognize
the divine laws and learn to obey them.
	(b) Intellectual and moral training re-
quire personal and individual effort on
the part of each mother, which she often
fails to meet.
	(c) Be your children’s companion
yourself.
	(d) A truly religious person will be a
truly moral person.
	(e) The mother is the child’s first
teacher, and must teach her children
daily lessons in morality, patience, indus-
try and truthfulness.
	(f) Fortunate are the children whose
training and environments are such as to
establish habits of all the virtues. We
are trained in habits of morality, rever-
ence. honesty and courtesy,
	(h) The children of today are the so-
ciety, the church, and the nation of to-
morrow.
			Lecture 6.
			 Loyalty.
	(a) What is more beautiful than loyal-
ty in the home, where parents and chil-
dren enjoy each other’s society.
	(b) Parents must work,together and in
perfect harmony if true loyalty is to be
established in the home.
	(c) How can we create an atmosphere
of love and kindness in our home that
will be felt by all.
	(d) The necessity of children being
loyal to their parents and each other.
	(e) Honoring the priesthood of God
and those in authority over them.
	(f) The lives of our prophets and other
great men are examples of loyalty in
every sense of the word.
	(g) This above all to thine self be true,
thou canst not then be false to any one.
