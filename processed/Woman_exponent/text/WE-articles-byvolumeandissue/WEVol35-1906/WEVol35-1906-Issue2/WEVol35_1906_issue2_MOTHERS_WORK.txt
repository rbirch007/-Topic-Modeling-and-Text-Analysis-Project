MOTHERS' WORK.
BEAR LAKE STAKE.
Lecture I.—Honor to the aged.
	(a) Duties of children to parents. (b)
Social obligations to the old. (c) Grey
hairs a sermon.
Lecture 2.—Veneration for sacred
things.
	(a) Proper conduct in houses of wor-
ship. (b) Respect for the Sabbath, (c).
Veneration for sacred ordinances.
Lecture 3.—Purity of thought.


(a) Impure thoughts precede impure ac-
tions. (b) To the pure in heart all things
are pure. (c) How to gain pure thoughts
Lecture 4.—Sacredness of covenants.
	(a) Value of a promise. (b) When a
promise may be legitimately broken. (c)
What should be as sacred as a vow.
Lecture 5.—Pride.
	(a) Pride rightly controlled is one of
the grandest attributes of the human
mind. (b) True pride to be cultivated.
(c) False pride to be shunned. (d) True
pride the keynote of all noble aspira-
tions.
Lecture 6.—Cheerfulness.
	(a) Cheerfulness smooths the way of
life’s weary travels. (b) Brings sunshine
however dark the day. (c) How to make
home cheerful.
Lecture 7.—Kindness.
	(a) Power of kindness. (b) All crea-
tures susceptible to kindness. (c) Kind-
ness misapplied.
Lecture 8.—True nobility.
	(a) How to be truly noble. (b) The
Savior the grandest type of true nobility.
(c) Illustrate by sketch of noble life.
Lecture 9.—Slander.
	(a) Persons who willfully vilify their
neighbors are guilty of serious crime,
often causing the downfall of the innocent
and unsuspecting. (b) Define slander.
(c) How to avoid the habit of slander.
Lecture 10.—Idleness.
	(a) The root of all evil. (b) Cause and
effect. (c) How to prevent idleness.
Lecture 11.—Proper respect for others'
rights.
	(a) Application of the Golden Rule.
(b) Courtesy due from one to the other.
(b) In helping others to stand you insure
your own standing.
Lecture 12.—Evils to be avoided.
	(a) To sustain by uplifted hand the au-
thorities of the Church, stake, ward or as-
sociation, then use your influence against
them. (b) Unkind criticism. (c) Imputing
evil to actions not understood.
ELIZABETH C. PIGGOTT,
LOUISA A. COLE,
MARGARET MORGAN,
Committee.


Mrs. Elizabeth Hunt of Brooklyn
Borough, New York, who will be 106 years
old next month, is making her annual tour
of Connecticut. The trip is being made
in an automobile, which travels by easy
stages from one town to another. Hither-
to the annual tour which Mrs. Hunt in-
sists on making each year has been ac-
complished mostly by train.—Ex.
