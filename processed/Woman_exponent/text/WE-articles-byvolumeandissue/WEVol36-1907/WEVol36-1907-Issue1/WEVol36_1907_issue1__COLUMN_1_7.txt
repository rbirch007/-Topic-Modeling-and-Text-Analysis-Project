	Lesson I. Industry.
	(a) For children to realize that home
is the dearest place on earth, they must
be taught to make it so by their own in-
dustry. (b) Teach them to take pride in
their surroundings and in the way they
care for them, (by precept or example).
(c) Let them share responsibility, (why?).
(d) Teach them the value of time in all
their undertakings. (e) Value of method.
(f) Their work should be arranged so
they will have time for study and recrea-
tion, (by whom?). (g) Encourage men-
tal development, (how?). (h) Aid them
in harmless pastimes, (how?). (i) As the
sapling is bent so it will grow, so the in-
dustrious boy makes a good husband and
the industrious girl a thrifty wife, (give
example). (j) A mother’s duty is not
wholly fulfilled in working for her chil-
dren, but in teaching them to work for
themselves, (why not?). (k) Fit your
children for the work they must do in la-
ter life. (l) Teach them that while they
are assisting you, they are preparing
themselves for similar positions of respon-
sibility. (m) To train our children to
work for others will foster in them a
spirit of love and unselfishness particular-
ly for those dependent on us—true love is
self-sacrificing. (n) Evil effects of idle-
ness. (o) Bad habits formed in the idle
moments. (p) Degenerating acquaint-
ances made. (q) Vulgar tastes cultivated?
	“For idle hands some mischief still will
ever find to do.”
	Lesson II. Hospitality.
	(a) True spirit of hospitality. (b) From
a social standpoint. (c) From a religious
standpoint. (Titus 1:8). (d) True hospi-
tality does not look for a return. (e) To
whom should be extended our hospitality?
(f) Social indebtedness. (g) Christian
indebtedness. (Rom. 12:13; I Peter 4:9).
(h) Better err on the wrong side than
to turn away hungry a fellow creature.
(Heb. 13:2). (i) Why should we en-
courage our children to be hospitable?
(j) Reward of hospitality. (Matt. 25:
31-46).

	Lesson III. Influence of Prayer in the
Home.
	“Prayer is the key with which the door
of each day should be opened and closed.”
	(a) Prayer secures divine aid for parents
in their home management. (b) It de-
velops in the hearts of the parents and
children a trust and confidence in God’s
power and promises, that bring peace and
contentment in the home. (c) Prayer
gives courage and strength to perform
faithfully the duties of life. (d) It is by
prayer that we express gratitude to God
for blessings, and supplicate from a con-
tinuation of mercies. (e) The prayer of
righteous parents has and can turn way-
ward children from the error of their
ways. (Book of Mormon, Mosiah 27:14).
(f) Prayer is a safeguard that protects
from sin and gives strength to resist evil
influences. (Book of Mormon, Alma 36).
(g) Joseph Smith unlocked the heavens
by prayer—we can have the same key—
God is no respector of persons—the great
and the small can approach Him through
the prayer of humility and faith. (h) Give
instances where prayer has been answered

	Lesson IV. Proper Home Ideals.
	“The love of the beautiful is a handmaid
of civilization.”
	“For as a man thinketh in his heart, so
is he.” (Prov. 23:7).
	“Be ye perfect, even as your Father in
heaven is perfect.” (Matt. 5:48; II Sam.
22;31).
	(a) An aspiration to excel in every thing
that is good or praiseworthy should be
instilled and encouraged and developed
in our children. (Why? Articles of Faith
13). (b) The love of order and clean-
liness should be so highly developed that
every member of the family would be un-
comfortable when conditions were other-
wise, (Why?). (b) The spiritual and moral
atmosphere is kept pure and healthy and
invigorating, (Why?). (d) Willing cheerful
obedience to wise, just impartial home
rules and regulations. (e) Polite courteous
consideration for each other and their
rights. (Show how it refines and ennobles).
(f) A deep love for honesty, purity, truth,
justice and mercy. (What kind of char-
acter does it develop?). (g) Unquestion-
ed loyalty to God, country and home.
(What are its fruits?). (h) An affection-
ate reverence for the aged; and sacred
places and things. (How does it effect the
public?).

	LESSON V. Father’s Mother’s, Chil-
dren’s Rights in the Home.
	(a) Father’s privilege to provide a home
and preside over it. (b) To the love and
respect and co-operation of the mother.
(c) To the love and obedience of the chil-
dren. Why? (d) To counsel and instruct
and direct the household. (e) The moth-
er’s right to preside over the home in the
absence of the father. Why? (f) En-
titled to the support, to the confidence, to
the love and courtesies of the father.
Why is she? (g) To the affection, obedi-
ence and help of the children. (h) Chil-
dren have a right to be well born. What
is well born? (i) To be cared for tenderly
and intelligently in infancy. (j) To a
cleanly, comfortable, peaceful home. (k)
To spiritual, moral and physical training.
(l) To the best educational opportunities
that the parents are able to give. (m)
To be disciplined, by precept and ex-
ample, in honesty, purity, truthfulness,
industry, economy, perseverance, kind-
ness, gentleness, and patience. (n) To
be treated kindly, courteously and affec-
tionately by their father and mother. (o)
To sympathy, compassion, and forgive-
ness when overtaken in sin and transgres-
sion. (p) To be reproved, corrected and
controlled. (q) To be encouraged, trust-
ed and made self-reliant. (Give the good
results that follow granting children’s
rights and the evil effects where their
rights have been disregarded).
