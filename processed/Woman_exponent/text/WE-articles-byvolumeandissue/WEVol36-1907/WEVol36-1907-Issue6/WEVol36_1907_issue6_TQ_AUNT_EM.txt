TQ AUNT EM.
There’s a poet who tells in delectable verse
Of the things we might part with and not be the
	worse
For the missing—his list, running through the
	whole range
Of pleasures and treasures of travels and change,
To end with a neat little rhyme of repining, 
That whatever we part with—it can’t be our din-
	ing.
And his thought gave me thought inspiration to-
	day,
For the tribute we’ve gathered together to pay,
To our dear guest of honor, with heart and with
	lip,
In the viands we eat and the liquids we sip.

But not dining nor wining is that I must choose,
As the object we’d miss most, if once we should
	lose,
Nor of treasures of art, nor of gold, nor of gem,—
Its name in our hearts is just known as “Aunt
	Em.”
For who in the years that have passed o’er us all
Cannot in their vista her image recall?
Through scenes that have gladdened or saddened
	the days
Since the valley first dawned on the pioneers’
	gaze
Through all their privation and hardship her
	hand
Was foremost to toil for the good of the land;
In the struggle for woman’s high honor alway
Her name as a beacon has lighted the way.

And over the murk of the battle has hung
Framed in laurels of pen, and of deed and of
	tongue.
And not only the pathway of note and renown
Her footsteps have trodden—but better adown
The still paths of sympathy sewn with sore hearts,
They have wandered, full oft giving balm to their
	smarts.
While her word has met young aspiration’s meek
	voicing
With hope, inspiration and meed of rejoicing;
There are few of us here but have owed her some
	measure,
Of hope, help or praise in our duty or pleasure.

So I say in the lilt of the poet I borrow
(And I hope his dark shade may not brood here
	to sorrow,
At the poor imitation I make of his ditty)
That I cannot conceive how our state or our city,
Our banquet halls, offices, homes or the street—
Could ever seem quite to our senses complete.
Could we not on the cheering reflection rely.
That somewhere or other Aunt Em we should
	spy!
And since, as we know, she is barely nineteen,
For years yet to come she will move in the
	scene—
Three keen-witted years having chalked out the
	day
Of her birth; that among us she longer might
	stay.

So here’s to Aunt Em—with the hearts of the
	Club
In the liquid we drink when our glasses we rub.
May the leap-years increase,—and the almanac
	keep
A trick up its sleeve, that old Time may not reap
Her life with its scythe, till he reaches the end
Of his flight—and in all of the years she may
	spend
May the Lord hold her fast in a loving caress
As we do—that’s one meaning, you know, of the
	“Press.”
					JOSEPNINE SPENCER,
				(republished from Feb. 1907).
