HISTORY OF THE U. W. PRESS CLUB.

From October 31, 1901 to October 31, 1906,
	with a brief history of the first decade.
	Lydia D. Alder historian.

	Mrs. E. B. Wells came in as Harriet
Martineau, the English writer of works
upon political economy, and others re-
markable for a Woman in that day; her
costume was of the finest black silk
velvet, such as she had heard her mother
say was worn at that period, for her moth-
er lived in the same period as Miss
Harriet Martineau.
	Next came Mrs. Lizzie S. Wilcox, who
gave the most humorous representation
of all, and strange to state though it was
so well done, the classical scholars of
modern date were not able to guess. She
had the tailor’s goose, patches in profu-
sion and wore a man’s coat, sat at the
table not exactly tailor-fashion, however,
and the fun she provoked was quite ex-
hilarating, as Sartor Resartus.
	After delicate refreshments were served
and music and chatting had charmed the
happy throng the Club evening of 1897
ended, amid this tastefully decorated
scene of autumn flowers. Under date of
May 11th, 1899, the Club discussed, and
entered into the brightness and better-
ment of the public schools, as an incen-
tive to the development of our children.
“The Kindergarten” was discussed and
approved for the schools, and the Club
was pledged to aid in this new feature in
our schools.
	Among the distinguished people enter-
tained by the Club, during its first de-
cade, are Miss Phebe Cousins, suffragist
also Miss Helen Ring, F. Marion Craw-
ford, Countess of Wachtmeister, who
spoke on Theosophy; Miss Elizabeth
Yates of Maine, just returned from China,
and others.
	The Club, during its first decade, kept

[Column 2]
abreast of the times. World questions
have been discussed, and world events
considered. Especially the trend of
events has been closely watched. Origi-
nal work of its members has improved,
both in poetry and literature. A notable
event of the first decade was the publi-
cation of the work “Musings and Mem-
ories” by the Club’s founder, Mrs. E. B.
Wells. For delicacy of thought and
touch of imagination it is the peer of any-
thing of its kind published in Utah, and
it is a book that will live, ever giving
pleasure to the reader. A little booklet,
“The Mormon Tabernacle Choir in Cali- 
fornia,” in verse, was issued by Lydia D.
Alder. In it we have a breath of Cali-
fornia, the sunny land of flowers and gold,
and an account of the choir’s triumphal
visit to that land in 1896.
