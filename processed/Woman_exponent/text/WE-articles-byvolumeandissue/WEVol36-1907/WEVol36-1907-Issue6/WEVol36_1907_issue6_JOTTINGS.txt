JOTTINGS.

	With the withdrawal of winterand opening
of spring, one may look for many changes,
not only atmospheric, but in the political
and commercial elements of society and the
world at large. Already one hears of ru-
mors of the campaigns about to be launched.
There are several great men being talked of
as candidates for the Presidency of the
United States, and there are strong argu-
ments made in print why certain men
should be elected, and yet it would appear
looking all around that the majority of the 
people favor the re-election of Theo-
dore Roosevelt. Those, who admire
his methods and his administration rank
him in ability and fearlessness to Abraham
Lincoln, or his equal in statesmanship. No
doubt there will be great excitement during
the present year, as there has invariably
been heretofore, the year of the presidential
election, both parties are pretty well de-
termined to win, and the people who are
not taking an active part will watch with
great interest. There is every reason to
presume that the government of the United
States will be strong enough to maintain the
position it holds among the nations of the
world even if conditions were to change
somewhat. For some years there has been
a time of general prosperity, and the country
is even now in comparatively good circum-
stances, although there have been a few
exposures brought to light that were un-
pleasant for the participants.
	Let us hope no great calamity may befall
our beloved country, and that the coming
year may be one of plenty and prosperity,
and success for the grand government under
which the people have every right possible
for their permanent happiness. The statis-
tics given of fewer saloons are encourag-
ing in the sense of temperance, and even of
prohibition in many states, and women are
doing brave and heroic work along these
lines of moral reform, even though they
have not yet (only in four states) been
given the ballot.
