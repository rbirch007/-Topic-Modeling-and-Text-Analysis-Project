	But the nineteenth century was the
woman’s century, and mothers of that
day kept pace with the times, and they
became grandmas’ with their advanced
ideas and education. A wonderful trans-
formation took place, so that the twenti-
eth century baby finds its grandma a mar-
velously busy, up-to-date woman, dressed
in the best and latest of apparel, with
hands flashing with jewels instead of knit-
ting needles. She has her clubs and 
her philanthropic work. She sits on 
school boards and civic leagues, and ar-
gues and works for the renovation of
cities and the hygienic and artistic con-
dition of our public schools; and some-
times she, too, at evening, tells us stories
of the long ago in most perfect and de-
lightful phraseology, and she always has
something sweet and good in her pretty
reticule, so she is just as sweet and dear,
and much more interesting, than the
grandma of long ago. What though her
hair be white, it is dressed in the latest
style, with combs and pins becoming,
and the lines in the sweet face may but
mark the care she may have felt for us,
and both give a halo and refinement be-
tokening well spent years.
	All hail the new grandmother! She is
just one symbol of an advanced age.
						A. W. C.
