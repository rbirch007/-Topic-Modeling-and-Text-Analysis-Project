"MAYN'T I BE A BOY?"
"Mayn't I be a boy?" asked our Mary,
	With tears in her great eyes of blue;
"I'm only a wee little lassie—
	And there's NOTHING a WOMAN can do!
"It's so; I heard cousin John say so—
	He's home from the great college too;
And he said it just now in the parlor—
	There's nothing a woman can do!"
"See here, my wee lassie, my darling,"
	Said I, putting back her soft hair,
"I want you, my own little daughter,
	To smooth away all mother's care.
"Who is it when pa comes home weary,
	That runs for his slippers and gown?
What eyes does he watch for at morning,
	Looking out from their lashes of brown?
"Is there nought you can do, little maiden?
	What was it that pa said last night?
My own little sunbeam is coming;
	I know, for the room is so bright.'
"And there is a secret, my Mary,
	Perhaps you will learn it some day;
The hand that's most willing and loving
	Will do the most work on the way.
"The work that is sweetest and dearest,
	Which many are failing to do,
Of making folks happy around them,
	May be done by a lassie like you."
