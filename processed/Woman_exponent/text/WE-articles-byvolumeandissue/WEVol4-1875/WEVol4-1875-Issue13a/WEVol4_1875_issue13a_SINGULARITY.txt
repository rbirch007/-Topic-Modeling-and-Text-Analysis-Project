SINGULARITY.

	The essence of singularity is the dispro-
portionate development of some essential
characteristic or quality. Almost any of
the common attributes of human nature
may so predominate in an individual as to
place him beyond the sympathy of men in
general, and mark him out as a peculiar or
singular person.
	It by no means follows that because a per-
son is singular, he is thereby an offender
against morality, and unworthy of respect.
Singularity is more apt to proceed from un-
due pride and egotism than from any baser
instinct, and leads to a manner and to habits
which are rather above than below the ordi-
nary level. Sterne's saying that a singular
person may be compared to a monster—more
admired than esteemed—indicates the dis-
tinction which usually attaches to singular-
ity.
	A refined conscientiousness will render a
person peculiar in the easy-going society of
the day. Polished manners and good taste
often seem to justify the charge of singu-
larity against educated men. Emerson re-
marks, in one of his essays, that if good
manners were not established, an original
gentlemen would be sure to invent them.


The inventor in such a case would certalniy
be charged with singularity.
	But persons who get the name of being
singular or eccentric usually do so through
some traits less admirable than these. A
reserved demeanor and way of life; indif-
ference to the influence of neighborhood
and public opinion; carelessness and non-
conformity to customs and fashions; self-ab-
sorption and an obstinate persistence in un-
social habits. Such characteristics will gen-
erally be found to have been derived in
early life from a blow to ambition or affec-
tion, or from an inherited tendency. In
nearly all cases singularity is to be deplored
rather then condemned, and the victim is
generally worthy of both respect and sym-
pathy.
