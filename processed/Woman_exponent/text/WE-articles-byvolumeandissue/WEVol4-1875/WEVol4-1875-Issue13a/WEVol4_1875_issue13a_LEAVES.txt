			LEAVES.

	There are various methods of preserving
autumn leaves, to make them really beauti-
ful for ornamentation, but, like all other
things, it requires a combination of taste
and skill in gathering, preparing and ar-
ranging. A great deal depends upon the
selection of certain varieties. Maple, Hick-
ory, Quivering Aspen, Cottonwood, Pear,
Shrub and Oak are among the best. The
Sumac and Sassafras are very best. Gather
the leaves and iron them the same day, and
iron them dry; your iron must not be too
hot, and it will be necessary to have a good
supply of leaves, as many of them will be
spoiled in the ironing. For making up bou-
quets, bunches of leaves may be kept upon
the twigs. Leaves can be pressed and
dipped in melted wax, or ironed with a
waxed iron; but the colors will be more
brilliant if ironed and then varnished. If
you wish to ornament frames with them,
the frame need not be made very neatly as
the leaves will hide the imperfections.
Strips of pasteboard tacked upon the frame
are better as some leaves will not adhere to
wood. Put in your glass and picture first,
and fasten firmly with strips of muslin
pasted around the edge; common glue put
into a can and soaked in water over night,
and then boiled slowly, will answer; use it
thick and hot. Pour out a little in a saucer,
and dip in the lower part of the leaf; then
press it on the frame, leaving the upper half
loose; they will then curl slightly in time,
and have a much more natural appearance.
If the frame is oval begin at the top and
finish the bottom with a rosette of leaves.
On square frames groups of leaves can be
put on either side, or corners, as the taste
may suggest; for the centre of the group
use everlasting flowers, berries, or acorns.
	To preserve choice leaves, arrange on
cards, or form into bouquets on heavy pa-
per. Leaves for cards varnish on one side,
for bouquets on both sides. Ornamental
grasses and everlasting flowers should be
more extensively cultivated.
