NOTES AND NEWS.

	A NEW poem by Whittier, with illustra-
tions, has just been published in Boston. It
is entitled, "Mabel Martin," and is in his
most happy, touching and pathetic style. It
is warmly eulogized by many of the leading
papers. The illustrations are unusually ex-
pressive of the sentiments and incidents of
the poem.

	A NEW Bible translation is about to be
brought out in Hartford, by Miss Julia E.
Smith. She has alone translated the whole
work from the original. She has written it
out five times, twice from the Greek, twice
from the Hebrew and once from the Latin.
She says she has been careful to put the
same English word for the same Greek or
Hebrew word, instead of giving her own
sense of it. It will be remembered by our
readers, that this lady is one of the Smith
sisters of Connecticut, who suffered their
homestead to be taken for taxes, because
they were not allowed the privileges of
citizens, on account of sex.

	THE STEAMER "Pacific," which sailed
from Victoria and foundered on the evening
of the same day, has been reported by
Henry F. Jelly, supposed to have been the
only survivor of the wreck. He was picked
up by the American ship "Messenger,"
twenty miles south of Cape Flattery, where
the terrible scene of the wreck occurred.
His testimony is that there were two hundred
people on board. Mr. Jelly was in a very
feeble condition when found, having been
floating on the pilot house belonging to the
wrecked vessel from Thursday evening un-
til Saturday morning. He says the Pacific
collided with another vessel, but persons
experienced in navigation think she struck
upon a sunken rock. Steamers have been
sent to look for survivors of the wreck, and
some have been found.
