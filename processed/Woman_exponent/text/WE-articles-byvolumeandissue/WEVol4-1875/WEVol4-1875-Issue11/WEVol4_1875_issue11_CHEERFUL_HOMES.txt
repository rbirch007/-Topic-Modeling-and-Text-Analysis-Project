CHEERFUL HOMES.

	Frugal wives, good housewives, always
look as neat at breakfast as circumstances
will permit. And the circumstances usually
"beyond her control," if any, are her hus-
band and her sons. The amount of litter,
of things lost, broken or misplaced, which
can be maintained by two or three, or six
or ten boys and men is something wonder-
ful; and the marvel is, not that here and
there a mother comes to the breakfast table
with a stitch dropped, or a string or ribbon
unadjusted, but that the wearied woman can
get there at all.
	If the wife is required to keep order, the
husband and sons should avoid the making
of disorder. If they want hot coffee—and
cold is disappointing—they must come
down in season to breakfast. And if a man
desires that his wife should look pleasantly
and speak pleasantly, he should find time to
permit her to speak, and speak himself to
her, between dinner one day and breakfast
the next. There are some men, and boys
too, for whom home has no attraction in the
evening. There are sons who seem to regard
the home as only a place to eat and sleep in.
The mother of such sons cannot be expected
to look cheerful at breakfast.—Phila. Led-
ger.
