AIRY PEOPLE.

	Have you got in your neighborhood any
of those airy people that have the faculty
of making everybody uncomfortable where
they are? People that, when you enter
their presence, act as if an apology was due
them for your existence. They are to be
found in all classes, sometimes among the
poorest people we have, and I have often
thought it was wisely ordered that some
should be poor, for if they had been rich no
one else could have lived in their vicinity.
You may, perchance, some time in your life,
have had some of these "stuck up people,"
visit you, and if you did not feel like sing-
ing "Hallelujah" when they left, you are
not like me. Nothing is good enough for
them. You may wait upon them until your
weary limbs refuse to move, and they do
not thank yon; the honor of having them
for company is sufficient. If you chance to


have such a man or woman to help you, how
soon you find yourself thinking you are not
your own master. They very soon give
you to understand that they do as they
please and you must do as you can. I have
often met young women (not ladies), in the
street, covered with flash jewelry and cot-
ton lace, with their heads high, whose ap-
pearance seemed to say, "Give the road,"
and I generally turn out. I have seen min-
isters, physicians, lawyers, students of all
kinds, farmers, and everyday laborers, all
afflicted with this stuckupishness, and I
never can account for it in only one way.
The trouble is in the top of the head—a 
lack of brains. You never see any one with
a good stock of common sense, but can con-
form to anything and make themselves
agreeable anywhere, and I would recom-
mend every one that has an idea they are so
much better than anybody else, to try and
see if they cannot get a little more into
their heads.
						AMELIA.
	Cayuga, N. Y., Oct., 1875.
