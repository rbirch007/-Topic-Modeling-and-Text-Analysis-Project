NOTES AND NEWS.

	THERE is to be a Masonic congress in
London, in 1877 in which all parts of the
world are to be represented.
	A VERY destructive fire in Virginia City,
Nevada, Tuesday, Oct. 26th, which de-
stroyed nearly all the business portions of
the city; it is said ten thousand people are
rendered homeless.
	BRET HART, the humorous poet and nov-
elist, has gone to New York with his family,
and taken up his abode for the Winter at
Fifth Avenue Hotel.
	ONE OF the products of California, that
will be on exhibition at the Centennial is a
section of one of the big trees of that state.
It was taken from a tree 373 feet high with
a diameter at the base of 26 feet, and is, ac-
cording to the yearly rings, 2,120 years
old.
	THE LADY who was the subject of Byron's
poem "Maid of Athens," according to a let-
ter from the United States Counsul agent,
published in the "Woman's Journal" of Oct.
16th, is still living at Athens, with her
daughter, who is said to be a lovely lady
of exquisite manners. It is said Lord By-
ron wrote that poem after the refusal of the
Lady's hand in marriage.
	A WOMAN'S Missionary meeting was con-
vened in St. Paul, Minnesota, Oct. 7th; re-
ports were given from different places of
their labors in the Missionary societies of
the West. During the meeting an inter-
esting paper entitled, "Do Foreign Missions
Pay?" was read by one of the ladies. The
women of the world are awakened to a reali-
zation of what they think duty, and are
desirous even to make sacrifices and prac-
tice self-denial for the sake of promulgating
those principles they believe to be true;
ought not the women of the Latter-day
Saints, who profess to know the truth by
testimony for themselves, to come forward
and show themselves valiant in laboring to
do good to their fellow beings?
