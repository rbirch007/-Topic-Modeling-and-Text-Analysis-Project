PART OF A LETTER.

	A letter written by Mrs. H. C. Young who
has been visiting in the Eastern States, and
addressed to Miss E. R. Snow of this city
contains some interesting paragraphs and
gives one some idea of our relative position
to the world at the present time. After ex-
pressing her pleasure in hearing of the pros-
perity of the home societies, she goes on to
explain some things as they exist there:
said it was becoming a serious question,
what to do with the surplus of women; they
are not usually educated to any employ-
ment that will command a living.
	She alludes to the rapid spread of Catho-
licism and infidelity and says many who
believe in religion are ashamed to own it.
	Many inquire of her concerning our faith,
and some believe it, but are trammeled in
some way and cannot free themselves.
	In regard to dress she says her male rela-
tives say, it is refreshing to see a plainly
dressed woman again, and think there is
hope their wives and daughters may return
to their senses. Her neices wished to make
her some new dresses, but when she spoke
of having them almost entirely plain, they
were not so anxious. She realized in ming-
ling with the world, the true value of our
most holy religion, and the freedom it gives
to our sex. She met several people who
were well acquainted with Joseph Smith,
H. C. Kimball, and others, and many who
have friends in Utah.
	She made many inquiries concerning
silk-worms &c. Cleanliness seems one es-
sential requisite in regard to them; even 
one's hands must be very clean, they are
dainty little creatures; onions are poisonous
to them either to handle or to eat them
so they can smell your breath, and they re-
quire keeping in a light place.
