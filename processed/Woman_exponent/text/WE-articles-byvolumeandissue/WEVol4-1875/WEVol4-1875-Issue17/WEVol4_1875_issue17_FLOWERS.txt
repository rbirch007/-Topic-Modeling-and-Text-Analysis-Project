FLOWERS.
	HE that preaches gratitude, pleads the
cause both of God and men; for without it
we can neither be sociable nor religious.
	NO story is the same to us after a lapse of
time; or, rather we who read it are no long-
er the same interpreter.
	LOVE is the fruit of the soul, that when
friends drop away (as leaves thinning) is
more perfectly ripened by the sun that
reaches it from on high.
	THERE is a sort of fascination in all sin-
cere unpremeditated eloquence, which
opens to one the inward drama of the
speaker's emotions.
	THERE is no greater thing than for two
human souls to feel that they are joined for
life—to strengthen each other in all labor,
to rest on each other in all sorrow, to minis-
ter to each other in all pain, to be one with 
each other in unspeakable memories at the
moment of parting in this life, and to feel
a firm and full conviction and assurance
that they shall meet again to spend an eter-
nity in each other's society, where pain and
sorrow and death are no more.
