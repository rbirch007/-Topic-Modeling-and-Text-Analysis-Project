THE SEX IN PERSIA.
	There are women in Persia—real ladies—
who are as fair and beautiful as ever was
Lalla Rookh, whom the Cape-Cod girls call
for in Boston bookstores under the title of
"Laura Hooker." This mention is neces-
sary because the Shah in his European tour
sought the society of the Schneiders and
her sort. Very many of the Persian wo-
men of the higher classes have the fair com-
plexion and regular features of the Circas-
sian and Georgian races, with which the
native blood has been so beneficially and
freely mixed. They are clever, lively, and
frequently wear in the highest household
the metaphorical br——, as well as their
own elaborately embroidered trowsers; in
other words they reign supreme. But too
many of them lead a lazy, luxurious, and
terribly monotonous life. Most of them
learn to read and write, and they devote
hours to the perusal of the Persian poets.
Contrary to the opinion which has gener-
ally prevailed in Christendom the Persian
men are not commonly jealous, and their
men and women and daughters enjoy much
liberty, going unattended to the public
baths and bazaars and visiting their friends.
There are two kinds of marriage in Persia,
one permanent, which restricts the poor
husband to four wives only, and the other
when the contract is made for a stated per-
iod, permitting an unlimited number of
wives, who are generally women of an in-
ferior class, and who act as servants for the
permanent wives. But the children of
both classes are equal in rights and status,
and are ranked as legitimate. The great
mass of men in Persia marry but one wife
—silks and jewels are as expensive there as
elsewhere—and such wives generally lead a
contented and happy life.
