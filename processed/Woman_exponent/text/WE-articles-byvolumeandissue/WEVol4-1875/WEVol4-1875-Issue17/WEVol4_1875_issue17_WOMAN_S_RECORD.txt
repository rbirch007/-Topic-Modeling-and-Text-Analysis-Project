WOMAN'S RECORD.

	THE Princess Louise is a most accom-
plished needle-woman, and is expert in de-
signing as well as imitating. She occupies
her time during the day in art studies, and
seems wholly absorbed in them.
	QUEEN Victoria of Great Britian, person-
ally conducts a Sunday School for the child-
ren connected with Windsor Castle. She is
certainly an estimable lady individually,
and does not depend upon her position as
Queen, for dignity or nobility. Young la-
dies of fashion would do well to imitate her
private life, and would be less afflicted with
"ennuie."
	THE Hon. Mrs. Norton, whose approach-
ing marriage to Sir W. Stirling Maxwell is
announced in London, is rather advaneed
in years for a bride, being now sixty-seven
years old. Her grandfather was the cele-
brated dramatist, orator and statesman,
Richard Brinsley Sheridan. Her first mar-
riage to Hon. George Chapple Norton, took
place nearly half a century ago, in 1837.
She was a precious rhymester, and a volume
of her juvenile poems was published in 1820,
when George IV had just begun to reign,
and James Monroe was President of the
United States. Her most widely known
poem, "Bingen on the Rhine," was the
schoolboy declamation of men now past mid-
dle age. Miss Sedgwick, in her "Letters
from Abroad" (published in 1841), describ-
ed Mrs. Norton as "the perfection of intel-
lectual and physical beauty, uniting mascu-
line force with feminine delicacy."
