THE CULTIVATION OF THE HOME.

	Almost all men have an instinctive re-
spect if not love for the mere words home
and mother, but it rests with women to lift
them up out of the region of simple ab-
straction, vitalize them and render them
significant of all that fulness of peace and
rest and joy and satisfaction which they do
mean to those who have known the blessed
realities of which they are the weak repre-
sentatives. Patience and self-denial are
needed, for few people know what is good
for them morally, any more than they do
physically; but the working out of a plain
duty always pays in the end—pays! that is,
indeed, a poor and unworthy name for it—
it yields infinite satisfaction and comfort,
while merely consulting one's own wishes
and inclinations fail signally and inevitably
to yield even the small return of individual
satisfaction and enjoyment. Women have
no conception of the power for good or for
evil which they hold in their hands, by the
proper growth and cultivation of the home.
	Here the power of the woman, by its ju-
dicious exercise, can indeed in time, if not
at first, become supreme. Lawless instincts
can be checked, order, which is heaven's
first law, encouraged, and an example of
grace, gentleness and refinement set, which
lays the foundation for the finest manners
of the most cultivated people in the world.
Women are natural conservators; men look
to them to formulate and preserve these
domestic and social amenities which beautify
existence and render the neglect of the
world or the kicks and scorns of fortune
more endurable. But they can do more
than this; they can make home-life an edu-
cation for the heart, a purifier of the mind,
at once a solace and a stimulus to the hap-
py participants, as it is the basis of good
government and highest incentive to patri-
otic endeavor. The homes of a people are
an index to the character of a community.
—Chronicle.
