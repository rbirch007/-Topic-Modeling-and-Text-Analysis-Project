SOCIETY, FEB. 1st, 1875.

	As this is our chosen subject I will make
an effort to write a few of my thoughts on
the same, though I fear they will be very
inefficiently expressed. Retrenchment, as
it is now introduced, teaches some of the
most exalting and self improving principles
that can be presented for our consideration.
These associations embrace all that is good,
pure and noble, and eschew all that is evil,
vile and degrading. If we heed their teach-
ings we will cultivate all talents implanted
in our natures, exert every faculty to be-
come useful to all by whom we are surround-
ed. Though it is beyond our finite under-
standings to imagine the great blessings to
be gained by availing ourselves of every
opportunity to do good, and to live the lives
of Saints, but are we not old enough, and
is it not time we possessed wisdom sufficient
to enable us to partially comprehend our
mission here on the earth? that in this highly enlightened age even
God's own pure religion is greatly ignored
by many who are too thoughtless to realize
what the end of such willing ignorance will
be, and also that they who try to do right
and be self-denying are often ridiculed and
made sport of, accused of making false pre-
tences, and many other things which are
apt to wound the heart, yet it is far better
to bear patiently the taunts of frail human-
ity than to have the heavenly hosts gazing
with frowning disapproval upon us; and if
the conscience is not entirely hardened it
will smite us for neglect of duty; and what
is there in this turbulent world more pain-
ful and harder to flee from than a conscience
weighed down with sin and remorse? All
the world may believe in our truth, yet if
the inward monitor condemns us, it is like
the worm gnawing at our very hearts, and 
go where we will it is still there, and ever
will be, save we humble ourselves before
Heaven and begin anew.
	Hitherto very little has been said of dress
as there were other things more needed,
and claimed our first attention. Retrench
from folly and ignorance. Were we to do this
where would the limits be? Knowledge is
boundless, and if we were to study a life-
time with the greatest of zeal, we could not
sit down and say, "I have learned all and
am perfect." In these societies we are
taught to engage in the useful as well as
the elegant pursuits of life. To do this we
must discontinue every habit which does
not tend to elevate the mind; not avoiding
simplicity of manner, which is often so
charming. We all know that if every mo-
ment is taken to ornament the person,
there is no time to spend in studying useful
books or learning from observation; besides
it injures the health, enfeebles the mind, to
live in such constant excitement of dress
and fashion. Happiness and contentment
are banished, and in their stead dwell envy
and covetousness. This is why there is sin
in excessive love of dress. It destroys all
unity, all peaceful relations that should ex-
ist between man and man. The aristocracy
scorn the middle classes, and they in turn,
while striving to become equal, are willing
to crush those of inferior fortunes. Does
the votary of vashion think of going out for 
pure enjoyment? Far from it; every nerve
is exerted to try and surpass all who may
be seen, and the excitement becomes so in-
tense, that desparate means are often re-
sorted to. Many a good man, I am satis-
fied, has gone to a premature grave, or been
driven to end his own life, by the unlimited
extravagance of his wife, or daughters.
Yet men are far from being blameless, and
if they had shown more wisdom in choosing
their lady associates, and not been partial
to the butterflies of the day, women would
have been less fond of dress. They have
proved to a great extent that they have no
taste for the real and true-hearted woman
though they might if choosing a life part-
ner, but for the shorter and less important
periods of time, the enjoyment of which is
as pleasant to woman as to man, they will
select the gaily dressed belle, the studied
coquette, no matter whether she possesses
a heart or not if she is stylish, elegantly
attired, and will  only smile, her cold, soul-
less smile, then man thinks himself tri-
umphant. He will not deign to notice the
neatly dressed and unpresuming maiden,
but after  has grown weary of, or been
rejected by the fair siren, he will humbly
sue for the hand of one he deemed unfit to
be his companion in the gay scenes of sin-
gle life. Is it any wonder then, that wo-
man, with all her frailties, seeks to make
herself suited to the exquiste though ex-
travagant ideas of the sterner sex. Though
there are some women, I am proud to say,
who will show them that they must prize
true worth; not outward display. I know
there are also many noble and good men,
but I am tired of hearing some rave of our
extravagance.
