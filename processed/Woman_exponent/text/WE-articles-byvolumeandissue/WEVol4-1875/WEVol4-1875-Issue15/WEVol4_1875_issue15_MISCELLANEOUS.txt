MISCELLANEOUS.
	JULIUS Caesar, when he founded the first
public library in Rome, with the books of
which he had plundered the conquered
provinces—for those renowned robbers, the
Romans, made books as well as pictures and
statues the object of pillage—appointed
Varro as his librarian, the most learned
man of his time, whom Dante calls the third
great light of Rome, reckoning him in the
illustrious list after Virgil and Cicero—
Varro, a republican, a follower of Pompey,
and an honest man, who flattered nobody,
not even the Dictator. Varro also assisted
Augustus with his advice when he founded
two public libraries in the metropolis of his
empire.

	LOVE-LETTERS.—Love-letters! There are
women whom the world call single who are
as truly wedded to a tear-stained package
as if it really were the being that it repre-
sents to them—who lived in the old sweet
time these missives once belonged to, and
who will keep their hearts apart from the
dull reality that makes up their present
world. Years may have passed, and noth-
ing may have remained the same, save the
dear dream that never knew reality; yet,
held in their love life by their fragile pa-
per bond, they dwell in that fair unsubtan-
tial spring time, while antumn fades, and
winter cold reigns abroad in all the world.
