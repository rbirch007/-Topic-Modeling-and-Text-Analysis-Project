NOTES AND NEWS.

	A TELEGRAPHIC cable was laid last
month across Vineyard Sound connecting
Martha's Vineyard with the main-land.
The laying was performed in  two hours by
a United States revenue steamer.

	AT HARRISBURG Penn. Dr. J. R. Hayes
has lately invented a new apparatus for press-
ing coal dust into fuel.  The machine is
said to be simple, cheap and practicable.
It has been put in operation at the Harris-
burg machine shops.

	DR. AMES in his "Sex In Industry" tells
about the physical unfitness of women for
type-setting. There are instances enough
on record to prove that women in this pro-
fession do not absent themselves more fre-
quently than men on account of their health.

	A NEW paper is announced to appear in
Boston Oct. 25th, edited by J. M. L. Bab-
cock, it will be called "The New Age;" and
the prospectus says, is to be devoted to no
sect or party religious, political or social,
but aim at the highest comprehensive view
of all true interests in thought or action.

	THE LARAMIE Sentinel (Wyoming) gives
an encouraging account of the courts where
women had been admitted to the Grand
Jury.  With ladies in their midst, every-
thing of the nature of levity or blackguard-
ism, everything offensive to decency and
morality was banished, whereas, heretofore
the prevailing elements were drunkenness,
rowdyism, quarreling, fighting etc.

	SOMETHING NEW in the way of enter-
tainments was recently given by Mrs. Ju-
lia Ward Howe at Newport, and styled "A
Blue Tea." Each guest was required to
contribute a verse or prose article for the
occasion; one lady honored the party with a
pair of blue stockings.  Every one present
was dressed in blue and the decorations
were all of that color; knots of blue ribbon
ornamented the cream pitchers and sugar-
bowls. It was pronounced "a unique so-
cial success."
