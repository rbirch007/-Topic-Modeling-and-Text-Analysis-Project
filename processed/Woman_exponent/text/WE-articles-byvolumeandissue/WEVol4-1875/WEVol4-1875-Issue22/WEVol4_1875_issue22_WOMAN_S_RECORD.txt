WOMAN'S RECORD.

	THE Misses Muller, Max Muller's two
daughters, have passed successful examina-
tions at Oxford, in English, French, and
German.
	ELIZABETH Stuart Phelps contributes
regularly to the Atlantic and Independent.
Her Florida Sketches are specially enter-
taining, and give to those who wish to vis-
it there for their health, many useful hints
and suggestions.
	AN old record book of a church in Conn-
ecticut, dated 1702 contains the following:
"For making a noise in church, Ann Bol-
ton, spinster, is to sit three days in the poor
pew, and pay a fine of five shillings."
	MADAME Sand, although in the evening
of life, is still industrious in her literary la-
bors, in fact it is said she accomplishes as
much in that line now, as when she was in
her prime, and has not lost any of her pow-
ers to fascinate the general public.
	LUCY Larcom it is said, began to write
verses when only seven years old: the edi-
tor who published them, wrote they were
written under the inspiration of the "mu-
ses," but the compositor made the amusing
blunder of "nurses."
	THE English Minister at Washington,
Sir Edward Thornton, has some daughters
who are spoken of as models of young la-
dies, being the personifications of bloom
and health; they dress very plainly, wear
thick-soled shoes, plain dresses, no pin-
backs and when they go out, wear heavy,
old-fashioned cloaks.
	MRS. Lucy E. Burbeck, now ninety-four
years of age, is the widow of Gen. Henry
Burbeck of the Revolution. She receives a
quarterly pension from Hartford Conn., and
is still able to sign her own vouchers.
