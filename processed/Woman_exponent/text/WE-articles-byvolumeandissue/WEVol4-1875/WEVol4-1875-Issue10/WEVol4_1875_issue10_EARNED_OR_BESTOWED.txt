EARNED OR BESTOWED?
In our census reports no account is taken
of women,—wives and mothers who do no
work outside their homes.  They swell the 
numbers of non-producers, and searchers of 
statistics in connection with questions of 
political economy, take no account of them.
And does any one think that the toil of a 
woman who between twenty-five and forty,
bears and rears four children, giving them
the usual care of mothers, is less valuable to 
the state than that of her husband, who
works as a carpenter at two dollars and a 
half a day, and by economy of himself and 
wife saves enough after buying the bread,
meat and clothes for the six, to buy a cot-
tage and lot, of which he holds the title
deeds.  Again, is her labor less valuable to 
the state than that of a single woman. who 
works in a kitchen or makes paper boxes in
a factory, and also saves enough to buy a 
house and lot of which she holds the title
deeds?
Let us not be deceived; the wife and 
mother is supported by her husband; works
for her board and clothes with such love as
she can command thrown in, and is called a
non-producer.  It is desirable that her posi-
tion should be a more independent and bet-
ter rewarded one?  In other words, is it de-
sirable that matrimony should be the ref-
uge of women who cannot do better for
themselves? or should it be the opportu-
nity for the strongest, noblest aud best of 
women, to give their best to the society in
which they live? These are some of the 
problems which human intelligence and 
wisdom must sooner or later solve, under
penalties.—Ex.
