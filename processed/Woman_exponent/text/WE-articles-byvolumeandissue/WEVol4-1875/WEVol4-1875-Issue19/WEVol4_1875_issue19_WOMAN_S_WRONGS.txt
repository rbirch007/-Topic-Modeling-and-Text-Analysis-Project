WOMAN'S WRONGS.

	Although the condition of woman has
materially improved, it is still far from
what it should be. She is still hampered
with social fetters, still enslaved, still ig-
nored the right of self maintenance co-
equal with man. She is still debarred the
privilege of living, unless at a sacrifice of
self-respect, in the prostitution of the no-
blest traits of her nature. If she is will-
ing to work, she discovers few avenues of
employment open. Man has monopolized
nearly all, and she must compete with him
in the battle for existence. Strong though
she may be in heart, her tender nature is
illy fitted to cope with the stronger attri-
butes of her masculine rival, and she is gen-
erally compelled to quit the field, and eith-
er starve, sell herself in public, or under
the guise of marriage, immolate her wo-
manly purity. Truly, woman has many
wrongs, but the greatest of all is the diffi-
culties she encounters in earning an honest
livelihood."		MARY ANN.
—The Gem.


"HE that can apprehend and consider
vice, with all her baits and seeming pleas-
ure, and yet abstain, and yet distinguish,
and prefer that which is truly better, he is a
true wayfaring Christian. I cannot praise
a fugitive, form cloistered virtue, unexer-
cised and unbreathed, that never sallies out
and seeks her adversary, but steps out of
the way when that immortal garland is to
be run for not without dust and heat."—
John Milton.
