PIPSEY PAPERS.

	Another sad accident has occurred through
the use of concentrated lye for washing. Mr.
Doolan, of Provo, has just lost a beautiful
and interesting child, fifteen months old,
from drinking lye.
	Mothers, for the sake of your little ones,
be careful of your lye; or still better, if pos-
sible do not use it. If it is necessary to use
it, handle it with fear and trembling. Be
as careful as you would with a bottle of
strychnine; you would not put that down
carelessly for fear of an accident. The lye is
just as deadly, and perhaps more danger-
ous, for it is so often carelessly handled.
Every one who uses it knows, that after the
box has been wet, it continues to dissolve,
often running over the box and dropping
from the shelf, making its way into unex-
pected places.
	My sister set a cup of tea on the shelf
thinking she would drink it after a while.
In the mean time the lye box dripped into
it. She burned her mouth very badly, but
fortunately did not get any in her throat.
It caused her family-great alarm for awhile.
	Ice melted is as good as snow or rain wa-
ter, no matter how hard or impure the
water before freezing, it is pure and soft
afterward. Sal-soda is good to cleanse water
but is more expensive than lye.
	The lady's name mentioned in Pipsey
Paper of Feb. 15, is Heward, not Hencord,
as printed.
	I have not time to write much now, I
have been so crowded with Society business.
I will tell you something about it next time.
			MARY.
