HER DREAM.

	She lieth quietly; And o'er her silvered hair
The moonlight pours its vail,
Her clasped hands lie upon her breast—
For sleep hath closed her eyes.
And in her sleep the curtain of a dream
Hath lifted up:
And showed to her a room;
A room, both light and warm,
And filled with whisperers;
Who softly glide about with pity in their eyes,
	for her, the sleeper, as she moans with pain;
The sufferings keen; but as the dreamer feels the
	pang, she crieth—
No matter Lord, how much I bear,
So I may be a mother.
For she was childless.

	The watchers take the child when born,
And lay it to the mother's breast,
She feels the velvet hands and tiny face
Close to her heart, and as she does she murmureth,
My little baby, all mine own!
He belongeth not to some one else,
But is mine own! my very own!
Then as they take the child so that the mother
	rest, she chargeth them,
That they hold not the baby to the window
Lest he might suffer—
And as she gives the careful charge,
She feels her heart-strings thrill and quiver with the
	love-chords of sweet motherhood.

	The vision faded—God pity her!
And who but they who have felt,
The passionate wish for a little babe,
Can know the longing,
For such a dream to be fulfilled.

	But in eternity the bliss of motherhood
May be the greater and diviner to those who, in this life
Have known the emptiness
The weary lonliness of that word childless.
