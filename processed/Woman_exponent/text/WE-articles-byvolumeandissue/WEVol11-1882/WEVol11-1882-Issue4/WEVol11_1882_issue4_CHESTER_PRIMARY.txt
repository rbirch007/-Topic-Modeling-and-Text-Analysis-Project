CHESTER PRIMARY.
	There being no Primary "organ," I write
you a brief statement of the first Primary Fair
in this little place, held yesterday under the
patient, persevering presdient, Sister Emma
Heyward's careful supervision.
	The exhibition of work made by the boys,
of field-rollers, rakes andp laited bridle, etc.,
was particularly gratifying and somewhat in-
genious, but the little girls bore off the palm
in matters of taste, house adornments, etc.; to
enumerate occupies space, and to specially no-
tice where all was excellent would wound some
of the dear little ones who in honest pride
showed their best and prettiest.
 We were treated to a nice exhibition of well
selected pieces, among them "The little peace-
maker," "The beautiful snow," "Purse proud,"
"Fixing for Grandma," "Baby Lella," "Both
sides," "We are coming, Mary," and "Country
Girls."
	The committing of the dear impressional
souls to the care of such patient grandames as
Sister Heyward is wise and judicious; they
seem to live over again their girl life in the
enjoyment of the children; they are like min-
istering angels walking the earth for good.
These Primary nurseries are a good insti-
tution, and I sometimes think they need a
cheap organ to gather up and preserve their
history.
