NOTES AND NEWS.

	Miss Anthony has become the sole proprie-
tor of "The History of Woman Suffrage."

	Henry B.Stanton, the husband of Elizabeth
Cady Stanton, died this week at an advanced 
age.

	Miss Rose Elizabeth Cleveland has written a
narrative poem of 400 lines, which is called 
"The Dilemma of the Nineteenth Century,"
and treats on the woman question.  

	Dress reform is taken up with much earnest 
ness by the Scandinavian progressive women.
In Christiana, it is already taken up in prac- 
tice by some independent individuals. Eye
witnesses have declared that the Norwegian
reformed dress has the great advantage of es-
caping the spirit of mockery, because its char-
acter is of the greatest simplicity. In Stock-
holm is a Dress-Reform Association, and it has
offered a premium for hygienic boots and shoes
for women. Lectures about women's dress, 
the need of reform, and how to reform, are
frequently given by intelligent women and by 
humanitarian men physicians in the Scandi- 
navian countries.—Ex.	
	A new Portia, who defended, however, not
her lover’s friend, but her own father, has ap- 
peared at Montpelier, France. A farmer
living near that town had been tried and con- 
demned for breach of trust. He took an ap-
peal, which was granted, and on the day ap-
pointed for his hearing he walked into court
accompanied by his daughter, a tall, good-
looking, and intelligent young woman, about
twenty-six years old. To the surprise of the
judges, the young woman opened her father’s
case in a masterly manner, argued the defence 
with the sill of a practical lawyer, and finally
gained for her client a verdict of acquittal. 
She then walked triumphantly out of the
court, hanging on her father’s arm.—Ex.
