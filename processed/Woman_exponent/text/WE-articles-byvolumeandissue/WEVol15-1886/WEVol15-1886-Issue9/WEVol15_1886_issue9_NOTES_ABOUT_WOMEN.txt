NOTES ABOUT WOMEN.

	Miss Cleveland has contributed to the Oc-
tober Chautauquan a paper on "Woman in the
Home."
	At the opening of Parliament on August 5th,
in the House of Lords, some thirty or forty
ladies occupied seats in the body of the House,
while several were also seated in the stranger's
gallery usually reserved for gentlemen only.
	"Ouida" expresses the belief, in the current
number of the North American Review, that
women have not sufficient intellectual capacity
for the suffrage. One evidence may be that
they sometimes read "Ouida's" novels.—Provi-
dence Journal.
	The Committee of the Cobden Club have
elected Miss Florence Nightingale, Mrs. Cob-
den Sickert (daughter of Richard Cobden,
Mrs. G. M. Crawford, of Paris, and Mdle. M.
Raffalsvich, members of the club. They are
the first ladies whose names have been en-
rolled.
	The "literary gentleman" who wrote to the
Boston Herald that Mrs. Julia Ward Howe
had been invited to reply to Ouida in the
North American Review and had declined be-
cause Ouida was so corrupt, was misinformed.
The Editor of the North American says,
"Mrs. Julia Ward Howe never was invited by
the North American Review to write a reply to
Ouida."
	Women should be more trusted and confided
in as wives, mothers, and sisters. They have
a quick perception of right and wrong, and
without always knowing why, read the present
and future, read characters and acts, designs
and probabilities, where man sees no letter or
sign. What else do we mean by the adage
"mother wit," save that woman has a quicker
perception and readier invention than man?
