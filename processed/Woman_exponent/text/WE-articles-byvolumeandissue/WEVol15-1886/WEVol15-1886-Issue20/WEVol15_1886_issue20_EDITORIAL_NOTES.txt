EDITORIAL NOTES.

	WE are pleased to be able to announce that the
picture of Lucy Smith, the mother of the Prophet
Joseph, has just been received (yesterday, the 14th
inst.), and copies will be sent as soon as practica-
ble. Persons entitled to them, living near, had
better call for them, as it will save rolling and
perhaps creasing them.

	WE have received, by courtesy of the respective
members of Congress who made them, several in-
teresting and excellent speeches. Senator H. W.
Blair of New Hampshire, on Woman's Suffrage,
December 8, 1886, presenting logical and, one 
would think, conclusive arguments in favor of the 
enfranchisement of women. Senator Joseph E.
Brown of Georgia, on the same subject, taking
the opposite view of the matter, and presenting
all those beautiful and flowery reasons that a re-
fined and thoroughly cultivated Southern gentle-
man is accustomed to use in favor of woman's 
delicacy and incapacity for public duties. If
every woman had a husband like Senator Brown,
perhaps this question would never have been
raised, but women as well as men have to fight
the battle of life, and therefore they need the pro-
tection of the ballot and courage to stand, and
such practical education as will prepare them to
defend their rights, politically, and stand equal
with man as well as educationally. The stirring
and timely speech of the Delegate from Utah, of
which mention has been made before in our
columns, and the minority report in favor of the 
resolution asking for a constitutional amendment
providing for woman's suffrage. This minority
report is signed by members of the Judiciary
Committee of the House, E. B. Taylor, W. P.
Hepburn, L. B. Caswell, and also A. A. Ranney,
who, although not one of the Committee who pre-
pared the report, adds his name below and says,
"I concur in the opinion of the minority, that the
resolution ought to be adopted."
