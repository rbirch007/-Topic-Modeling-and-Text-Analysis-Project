NOTES ABOUT WOMEN.

	Jenny Lind's daughter, who is spoken of as
possessing a marvelous voice, is coming to this 
country some time next winter.
	A new prose work by Miss Jean Ingelow
will be brought out by Messrs. Roberts Bros.
during the early autumn. It will be entitled
"John Jerome: His Thoughts and Ways."
	Miss M. E. Braddon, the novelist, is very 
methodical in her habits. She works only 
three days a week, but on those days she puts 
in full time, working from 10 a.m. to 7 p.m. 
without intermission. 
	The biography of Queen Victoria, which
Barnett Smith has had in hand for some time,
will be published this month in one volume of
400 pages. It will contain several portraits.
	The Fowler & Wells Co., of New York, an-
nounce the publication of "The American Kin-
dergarten and Primary Teacher." As the
name implies, it is especially devoted to kin-
dergarten methods of teaching.
	The death is announced of Miss Anne Bow-
man, of Yorkshire, Eng., author of "The Boy
Foresters," "The Kangaroo Hunters." "The
Castaways," and other popular juvenile stories.
Miss Bowman was ninety-one years of age.
	Mrs. E. M. Ames ("Eleanir Kirk") is pre-
paring a volume called "Beecher as a Humor-
ist." Mrs. Ames was formerly a reporter of
Mr. Beecher's sermous, and has long been in-
timately acquainted with him.
	Miss Annie L. Dawes has written, and will 
soon publish in bertou a book on the Modern


Jew," the outgrowth of a paper on the subject
read by her before a literary society in Wash-
ington, two years ago.
	A romantic young lady in London obtained
recently the desire of her heart—she met
Tennyson, the poet Laureate, at dinner, and
the sympathetic hostess even arranged that
she should be placed next to him. One re-
mark, and one alone, did the poet address to
the gushing maiden at his side, and it was this:
"I like my mutton in wedges."
