WOMEN'S WAGES IN AMERICA.

	The State of New York, following in the 
wake of Massachusetts, has commenced to
collect and publish annually a mass of valu-
able information respecting labor.  The prac-
tice of the Bureau is to make an investigation
of one or more special subjects within the scope
of its legal inquiry, and last year it took up
as a subject for investigation Working women:


their wages, and home condition.” Setting
out with the determination to take evidence
from none but representative manufacturers
and mechanics, the Commissioner has been
able to collect much trustworthy evidence, and
he gives elaborate tables bearing directly upon
the question of women’s wages. It appears
that for the making of clothing the following
prices are paid by a large representative firm:
Overcoat, 6s 2d; pea jacket, 3s 9d; cotton
trousers,1s; wollen trousers, 1s 6d; vests, ls 5d
to 1s 8d. At dressmaking wages are 4s 2d per
day; work from 8 a.m. till 6 p.m. Many
women, however, are employed in large stores,
who receive as much as from £3 10s to £8 per
week; but these, of course, are superintendents
or specially skillful workwomen. Many sew-
ing women told the Commissioner that by
piece-work they can make from 20s to 28s per
week. Ordinary wages for housework in pri-
vate families, are £2 10s per month; cham-
bermaids, £3; cooks, £3 10s to £7. It need
hardly be added that there are a great number 
of working women in these different occupa-
tions who earn less than the figures given
above; they represent the average.
