DEAR AUNT EM:

	I hardly know why I have not written you
oftener, for I have so longed for a visit with
you, particularly since your eastern trip. I
have read the pieces about your visit to the
old home with much interest, for I have listened 
with childish delight to the tales of that same
New England village, for was it not also the
home of my mother? I can almost fancy I 
should feel at home there, although I never
saw the place.
	We are all in the usual health; indeed this is
a very healthy climate, and we may, with ordin-
ary care, enjoy the greatest blessing vouchsafed
to man—good health.
	We have very good opportunities for edu-
cating our children; much better, in fact, than
we thought we would have in a new country;
still not exactly as we would like, for we are
obliged to conform to the territorial law, which
is very good of its kind. Our Primary and
Young Ladies’ Associations, also our Sunday 
Schools, are in a prosperous condition.
	I wonder if in your travels sometime you
will not drift this way long enough to make us
a visit? We would find much to interest you.
We are in the midst of pre-historic ruins. A
gentleman by the name of Cushman has been

[Column 2]
excavating and examining some of them quite
recently, in fact he is not through yet. The
Smithsonian Institute of Washington are di-
recting his labors.
	Yours affectionately,
