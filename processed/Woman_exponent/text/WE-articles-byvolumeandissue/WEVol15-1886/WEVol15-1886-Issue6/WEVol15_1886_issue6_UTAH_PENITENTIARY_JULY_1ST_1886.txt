	UTAH PENITENTIARY July 1st, 1886.
TO MY FAMILY:
	Sometime has passed since I wrote you, and I
am something at a loss where to begin or what
would prove most interesting.
	There are some scenes in prison experience, oc-
curing now and then, which may be anything but
agreeable, yet even here, we find no cloud so per-
fectly enveloped in blackness, but it may bear a
bright and silver lining; no picture so dark and
sombre, but has some redeeming feature, some
bright and beautiful point which may be seen here
and there when very closely observed. This ob-
servation is truly applicable to us in our experi-
rence of prison life. I will relate some incidents
embracing the opposites—light and shade—seen
in the picture of life in the Utah Penitentiary. In
the first place I will mention an interesting occur-
rence which happened on the 20th of last month,
at an ovation given in honor of Brother Henry
Gale, on his liberation from the Penitentiary, hav-
ing served his term of imprisonment. The follow-
ing minutes describe the circumstance:
