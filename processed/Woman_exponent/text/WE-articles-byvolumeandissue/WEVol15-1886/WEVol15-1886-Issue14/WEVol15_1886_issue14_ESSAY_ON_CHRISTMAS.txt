ESSAY ON CHRISTMAS.
     Christmas time, of all the year, is a time of
rejoicing for all Christian sects.  It is a time
of festivity and merry making, and I sometimes
think that in our mirth and pleasure we are
apt to forget the real and most worthy cause of
joy and rejoicing—the birth of our Savior, the
babe in Bethlehem, the meek and lowly Jesus,
who came to this earth in the meridian of time,
as it had been ordained in the courts above, be-
fore the creation of this world, to be despised
and ridiculed among His own, and finally offer
up His life for the sins of the world.  This is
something worthy of contemplation, because
upon His mission depends our eternal salvation,
and, also, to a great extent, our temporal salva-


tion; for His is the only name in which we can
approach the Father and receive the blessing;
and our temporal and eternal welfare are so
closely connected that they are not easily
separated.
	Now, there are many things that are worth
considering in connection with Christmas.
There is one thing which those who are blest
with a goodly portion of this world’s goods
should not forget; that is, the poor.  The Savior
said, in His day, in speaking of two classes of
people in the day of judgment.  Of one class
He said: “When I was naked ye clothed me;
when I was hungry ye fed me; when I was sick
and in prison ye visited me.” They shall an-
swer: “Lord, when saw we Thee in this condi-
tion?” He shall answer: “Inasmuch as ye
have done it to the least of mine, ye have done
it unto me.” To the other class he shall say:
“Depart from me, I know ye not.  When I was
naked ye clothed me not; when I was hungry
ye fed me not; when I was sick and in prison
ye did not visit me.” This would be a severe
rebuke. 
	Let us try to remember the obligations we
are under, that when we have finished our
work on earth it may be said of us: “Well
done, good and faithful servant, enter into the
joy of thy Lord.”
								M. M.
