WE SPEAK IN OUR OWN DEFENSE.

	Inasmuch as we, the veritable women of Utah,
have enjoyed the right of franchise for ten years
with good results, we as Americans would not be
honoring the heritage given us by the patriotism
bequeathed us by our forefathers, who were wil-
[-]ing to give their lives to maintain their honor
and obtain freedom for their posterity—unless we
protested against this attempt to take away our
privileges. It is our duty to express our indigna-
tion at this unheard of political injustice.
	We ask not mercy, but justice. We vote for
men of integrity that have ever honored the
constitution of our country. We are again as-
sailed in our rights; we know for what purpose.
Would it not be wiser to foster and protect vir-
tue, encourage industry and the sacred rights of
affection, than to make us feel the degradation
of the dram shop and its terrible train of conse-
quences, and take the power from us to restrain
these evils in our midst? We may well dread
what next will be required of us.
	In Cache County, there are 9,000 people with-
out a liquor store, or its attendant miseries. And
this is a fair sample of other counties in Utah
Territory, with the exception of those places
where Gentile civilization abounds. Can the
world set us a better example? And now after
our toils and endurance in settling this desert,
that even our accusers may have bread and but-
ter, and the blessing of God upon our labors,
shall we allow the tombstone of policy, which
is being carved by politicians, to be erected over
the grave of principle and honor?
	The ladies of Utah answer, NO!
