THE  VALUE  OF SOCIABILITY.
	Instead of living and enjoying life, the world
seems  to be striving—striving ever  in  getting
ready to live.  As soon  as a person gets every-
thing in  order for  real living— ah! most gen-
erally before—he dies! Improvement  is the or-
der of the day with all respectable classes,  and
this is right. ‘'Work” and “business” occupy
the hands and  brains of the  masses, and  these
are excellent mottoes.  But, I have lately come
to think that perhaps  this  world  need not  be
such a prosy,practical,work-a-day sort of a world
after all, if  the majority of the  people  would
not so take it and so make it.
	There might be more sociability,a little more
of the old-fashioned calls and  visiting  indulged
in, among neighbors  and  friends, and  still  be
just as many, perhaps more  good works accom-
plished.  How  do  we  know  what  good  we
might have done for some  poor creature in our
neighborhood, to  whom  misfortune has  come,
had we known  of  the  circumstance  in  time?
Often, were neighbors  a little  more sociable, it
would be easy to avert  evils which are now un-
avoidable  because  of  the  isolation  to  which
people subject themselves.
	I am not  writing to women  who have their
time entirely  at  their  own disposal;  who  can
attend to their toilets,  make  fashionable  calls
and be in  trim for receiving fashionable callers
every day.  Neither do I write for the  benefit
of those whose time and  attention  are devoted
to the public  weal,  who  go to  more  meetings
than the most of men could endure, and see and
talk to more people  in a  week  than  they  can
possibly remember  the names  and  faces  of  in
twelve months.  But I would  speak  by means
of pen and paper to the  thousands  of mothers,
who are very properly living for their own little
worlds, their families,  Those whose babies and
household cares  necessarily  tie  them to  their
homes very closely.  I would like  to  persuade
them that staying in the house from morning
till night, day after day from  one year’s end to
another. working  and brooding over work that
it is impossible for them to accomplish, and suf-
fering with  the headache nearly  all  the  time,
does not pay, and is not  exactly fulfilling their
mission. They need a little of the fresh,  pure,
open air of heaven  as well  as  the  babies  and
the  older  children  and  the  fathers.  They
should accompany the babies when  they go for
their  morning  walk  sometimes,  two  or  three
times a week at least. They should occasionally
call on their neighbors who are like themselves,
and insist in the friendliest  manner upon their 
calls  being  returned. Lace  ruffles  and  em-
broidered cuffs need not  be thought of in  con-
nection  with  these  homely sociabilities. The
neat old-fashioned, plain linen answers quite as
well in such cases;  and, indeed, you may really
enjoy your walk and a pleasant chit-chat, with-
out having either collar or cuffs, if you can only
think it. If you  hold  to  the  belief  that you
must wait  to  "dress up"  before  taking  these
little outs, you will  never take them, you  will
never have the time. If you have been fretted
and vexed at home, do  not  manifest anything
of the kind  where  you  make  your  call.  Let
your  conversation be something  to cheer  and
strengthen both your  neighbor and yourself.
 A few weeks ago, I was suffering with a sore
throat which  lasted for  several  days,  By  re-
sorting to simple remedies, such as are generally
known, I suppose  we kept it from becoming a
serious case. But  it would  not get well. One
day, early in  the  afternoon, a friend  called  to
see me, accompanied  by her  daughter, a pleas-
ant spirited girl  at  the happy  age of fourteen. 
My two  little boys  were praised  and  admired
after the usual custom; our house--as my friend
had not visited me  here  before--was  explored
and commented upon; we talked of the different
society meetings,the "Assembly Hall," remark.
able instances  of faith in  children, the  admin-
istration  of  the  Elders  the safest,  surest  and
only  reliable  remedy  in  all  cases  of  sickness,
and kindred  topics;  interspersing  bits  of  rail-
lery and indulging in little laughs where occasion
warranted. We enjoyed ourselves together and
my friend and her young daughter remained with
us till  evening. At  the supper table I observed
that my throat was  much better. Next morn-
ing the soreness was quite  gone and I have felt
nothing of it since.
 That such charming remedies as the one cited
above would prove  effectual  in all  cases, I do
not  pretend  to  say. But  they  are harmless,
and in many instances very  good. My sisters
try them.

of delegates from the several counties is to be
held in the City Hall in this city on Thursday,
the 7th inst., at 6 p. m., to nominate a candidate
for Delegate to the Forty-seventh Congress, and
to transact such other business as may properly
come before the convention.
