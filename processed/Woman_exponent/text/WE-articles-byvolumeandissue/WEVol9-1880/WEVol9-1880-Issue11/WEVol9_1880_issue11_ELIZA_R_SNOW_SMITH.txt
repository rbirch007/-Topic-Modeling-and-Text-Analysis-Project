ELIZA R. SNOW SMITH.

(Continued.)

	During the seven years Sister Eliza resided
in Nauvoo, she wrote many of the articles
contained in her two volumes of poems. Her
mind was fertile, and fresh subjects and inci-
dents were constantly developing her talent
for writing, and inspiring her muse to pour
forth rich melodies in song and verse. Mean-
time, persecution raged, until at last the exo-
dus of the Saints from Nauvoo was decided
upon, and the cry went forth "to your tents,
O Israel." She was among the first to leave
the beautiful city, the graves of her lamented
dead, and the scenes and places round which
so many tender recollections clustered. Early
in the month of February, 1846, she crossed
the Mississippi River, in company of Col.
Stephen Markham and family, and camped
with thousands of others who had left their
homes, and were living in tents and wagons,
preparatory to starting upon their journey to
the Rocky Mountains. Sister Eliza's pen was
ever busy; the following is an extract from
a poem written after leaving the city of Nau-
voo, "West side Mississippi, February 19, 1846:"

	"Although in woods and tents we dwell,
	Shout! Shout! O Camp of Israel!
	No Christian mobs on earth can bind
	Our thoughts, or steal our peace of mind.
