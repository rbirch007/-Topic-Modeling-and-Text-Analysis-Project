WOMAN'S VICTORY.

	An interesting convention of the Woman's
Suffrage Association was held in the Empire
State, at Poughkeepsie, Oct. 24, commencing
at 10 o'clock A.M. Able and eloquent speeches
were delivered by Matilda Joslyn Gage, Lillie
Devereaux Blake, Helen M. Slocum, Albert O.
Wilcox and Clara Neyman, One of the princi-
pal features of this convention was the protest
against the election of Lucius Robinson for
Governor of New York, and although the wo-
men citizens of that State have not a vote they
have a voice, and they protested with a will
against Robinson as Governor, not only because
he vetoed the woman's school-bill in 1877, but
by his frequent pardon of criminals guilty of
the grossest outrages against their sex, he made
himself specially obnoxious; and also because
"they were impressed with the fact that the
questions before the country are far larger, of
wider scope, and more difficult to deal with
than at any previous time in the history of this
nation; and that the New York campaign had
far reaching effects for national weal or woe."
	Immediately after the convention Pough-
keepsie, Mrs. Gage went down to New York
and aided in the Anti-Robinson campaign.
	Mrs. Susan A. King, Lillie D. Blake, Helen
M. Slocum and Dr. Clemence S. Lozier worked
faithfully to obtain Robinson's defeat. Wo-
men's Anti-Robinson meetings were held in
every part of the city of New York and suburb-
an towns.
	Thousands and tens of thousands of the wo-
men's printed protests were circulated, not
only in every part of the city of New York, but
all over the State.
	Besides having defeated Robinson, they have
got Cornell's promise to sign the woman's
school-bill vetoed by Robinson, and to sustain
the Woman's Presidential Suffrage Bill, which
will secure to women the right to vote for elec-
tion of President and Vice-President.


Altogether, we think the women of New
York have stormed the castle and forced an
entrance that will give them firmer foothold
hereafter.
