HERALD.—NO. 2.

	It was once "stamped upon the mind of the
leading men" in the British Parliament that
the English could most wisely and judiciously
decide all governmental questions relating to
Americans, whom they considered their sub-
jects and inferiors. A few Americans wore
different colored glasses, and, looking to the fu-
ture good, allowed their motives to be misun-
derstood by some and misrepresented by oth-
ers, while they (vainly, shall I say?) peti-
tioned.
	Parliament, in its assumed dignity and
strength, spurned respectful petitions from
American men; but the "backbone" of the
justice and right of said petition was thereby
strengthened. American men as tax payers,
with no decided opinions upon public policy,
were "admirable;" but when they expressed a
wish to help guide the Ship of State to such
harbor as seemed to them desirable and good
for them and their generations after them, not
only was a Parliamentary majority's cold
shoulder turned, but radicals talked of pros-
pective office-hunters, and the rabble foamed,
while the more conservative contented them-
selves with an undefinable—UGH.,
	Lomond, with all his clear sightedness, has
not discovered that it is only the favored few
women who are permitted to realize that the
man is responsible to a greater extent than the
woman for providing for his family; and that
the thoughtful woman who need not provide
the bread feels a moral responsibility relating
to the laws and associations that must have an
important bearing on her household, in com-
mon with the community.

	A member opposing woman's disabilities
bill says, "woman's voting works well enough,
and I am disposed to let well enough alone.'
This, put in other words, reads, the women
have by their votes placed me in this well-
cushioned seat, where my voice has weight,
and that is enough. I prefer to have them oc-
cupy the back, hard benches.
