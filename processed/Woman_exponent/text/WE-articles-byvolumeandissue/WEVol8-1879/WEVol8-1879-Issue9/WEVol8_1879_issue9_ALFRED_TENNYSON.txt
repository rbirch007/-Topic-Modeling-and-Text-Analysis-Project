ALFRED TENNYSON.

	Alfred Tennyson, D. C., is a popular
English poet, the third son of George Clay-
ton Tennyson, rector of Clayton Parish.
He was born at Somerby, in Lincolnshire,
in 1809. He was educated at Trinity Col-
lege, Cambridge, where he obtained a prize
for an English poem in blank verse on
"Timbuctoo," in 1820. He was still an
undergraduate of Trinity Collage when he
published a volume entitled, "Poems,"
chiefly lyrical, in 1830. In 1833 he
published another volume of poems, includ-
ing among others, that exquisite thing,
"The May Queen." (As I write this, the
dear old song and music comes back to me,
when so free and careless I sang it so often
in days of Lang Syne). He also wrote
about this time "The Palace of Art," "A
Dream of Fair Women," and others, which
compared with his earlier efforts, show
great improvement in richness of thought 
and beauty of imagery. His "Princess: A
Medley," is an admirable poem. "In
Memoriam,, is a beautiful tribute to the 
memory of his friend, Arthur H. Hallam.
"Maud" and, "Enoch Arden" are especially
liked by all poetry lovers, also "Idyls of the 
King." Those were published, respective-
ly, in 1859, and 1864.
	Tennyson succeeded Wordsworth as poet
laureate, in 1851.
	The Quareterly Review says of Mr. Ten-
nyson's poetry:—"After the descriptive
beauty of the laureate's verse, perhaps no
quality of it is more highly praised than its
measure," and truly the purity and delicacy
of its movements is almost beyond praise.
Though certain common excellences may
make a plausible resemblance, yet in truth,
no contrast is greater than that between the
jeweled beauty of Mr. Tennyson's style and
the crystalline effulgence—the "non imit-
able [-]ulgur"—of the great ancients. Ten-
nyson has resided on the Isle of Wight for
some years.
	In 1870 he published the "Holy Grail."
The readers of the HOME are doubtless
familiar with his later poems, so I will not
take the space to enumerate them.
					OLIVE HARRINGTON.
