THE DEATH of William Loyd Garrison
made heavy the hearts of thousands who
had been accustomed to look up to him as
one of the great leaders and advocates of
woman's rights and woman's suffrage, It
is said that in London he refused to take
part in a World's Temperance Convention
which rejected women as delegates. The
"Woman's Journal says, "Such funeral ser-
vices were never before rendered to any
man, because no such man has ever before
lived as William Loyd Garrison." The fu-
neral took place the 28th of May, in Dr.
Putnam's Church in Eliot Square. The pul-
pit was beautifully decorated with flowers,
and over all settled the calm that comes
with the sense of fitness and peace. Rev.
Samuel May, Lucy Stone, Samuel Johnson,
Theodore D. Wild and Wendall Phillips
were the speakers, and between the ad-
dreses the hymns were sung which Mr.
Garrison had loved to sing.


AN UNLUCKY MAN.—"You see," said
the despondent man on the pickle barrel
addressing the grocer, who was spearing
the top of a cracker box with a cheese
knife, "you see some people has good luck
and some people has bad luck. Now I re-
member once I was walking along the
street with Tom Jillicks, and he went down
on one side of it and I went down on the
other. We hadn't gone more'n half way
down when he found a pocket-book with
$261 in it, and I stepped on a woman's
dress and got acquainted with my present
wife. It was always so," he said with a 
sigh, that Tom Jillicks was the luckiest
man in the world and that I never had no
luck.—EX.
