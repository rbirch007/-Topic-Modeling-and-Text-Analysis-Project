	BY HANNAH T. KING

	Let me recall one long gathered to his
rest, but one of the planets that radiated
my orbit in the days of child and girlhood;
and in those days I coveted his approba-
tion as one of nature’s gentlemen, and one
who, by the relationship in which we stood,
must throw a powerful influence over my
future life, as the head of the family into
which I was then, by marriage, passing.
Let me describe him as best I can. Figure,
above the middle height, powerful, broad
and muscular, a form cast in a mould sel-
dom seen—must have been of peculiar and
ancient lineage, the limbs, foot and hand
denoting this—chest broad, deep and full;
hair dark, whiskers and beard tanned, up-
per part of the head bald, features large,
forehead broad rather than high; nose ex-
panding towards the end, denoting almost
reckless generosity, mouth large, lips full,
teeth good but large; the beauty of this
face lay in the expression, which was sin-
gularly good; the general expression of the
eyes calm, gentle and meditative, but they
were capable of the most complex expres-
sion, from the most happy and playful to
the most sad and melancholy, from the
most peaceful and benevolent to the most
concentrated rage, from the most repulsive
to the most attractive, from the most easy
to the most despotic—but not to elaborate
that which we might carry out through all
extremes, suffice that they were capable of
all extremes which a complicated nature
is capable of expressing; yet how bland,
how bright, how winning was that counte-
nance by nature! and on such a countenance
the extremes are ever more apparent; yet
'twas a face all liked, and to which all were
attracted. Manners affable, hospitable and
fascinating; when in a happy mood irresisti-
ble. I shall remember to my dying day
that loving, gentle encouraging look when
he approved; alas! the reverse—as though
he would annihilate one with a glance!
But time taught us a better lesson, as we
knew each other better; yes, I found that
strong man could be bound by the fetters
forged by love and kindness, to whose laws
he was ever sensitive; and when I heard

from his lips the one or two words of ap-
proval, 'twas enough, for I knew his judg-
ment was never hoodwinked, and I rejoiced
that I had "stooped to conquer" and had
won the victory! Yet he was one of those
beings so surrounded by adamantine chains
of guardedness and collectedness that no
one could use an undue freedom with him.
Generous in the most extended sense of the
word, causing him sometimes to forget jus-
tice; a heart that could not resist a tale of
woe, without palpable demonstration of his
sympathy; his hand, his purse, his house
were ever open in such a case, and women
and children were irresistible objects at 
such times. I have seen that l[-]on spirit,
that passionate soul, that "strong man,"
softened so that the eyes would swim in
liquid feeling, and that voice lose a tone,
and the heart would prompt the ready hand,
and something would he done, if possible.
	Oh! what a soul was there, had training
and cultivation been exercised in early life.
Alas! he was a posthumous son, and moth-
erless as a babe, a colossal nature cast upon
grand parents of the olden time, and they
loved him, and left him to grow and vege-
tate, like any other animal, or vegetable,
and he grew up nature’s wild, untamed,
untutored son; but in such a case and such
a nature do not the virtues, as well as the
vices, strengthen? He retained a softness,
a tenderness, that was most touching and
that woke every heart to sympathize with
him in his trials and bereavements and his
disappointments. Yes, all these, with
years of sickness, were freely mingled in
his cup of life. Religion, as defined by the
world’s Lexicon, was not shown forth in
him, but there was ever on that solemn
theme a respect, a veneration, a silence
that spoke! like the cripple by the pool of
Bethesday, he seemed waiting for "the
moving of the waters," and when death
threatened, yet delayed to strike, there
was in him that mute, passive, enduring
submission that said, 'tis right, 'tis good
that I should suffer, and calmly did he en-
dure all that the Great Father saw fit to in-
flict upon him. In one of those scenes he
blessed me snd committed the orphans to
my care. How kind, how considerate, how
manly were his words, and his parental
kiss was the seal of the compact. Has he
witnessed the fulfillment as far as in me
lay? I believe so. Nearly forty years have
passed since that scene, and he has lain in
the grave a great part of that long period,
but the dead, the dead, are living still! Yes;
may he not at this very moment be bending
over me, and watching the traces of my
pen? And well do I know the expression
that face would wear; strong emotion would
be depicted upon it, as I have seen it when
tender feelings ran over in that heart.
	Let me now continue to trace the char-
acter. Temper passionate, in anger vio-
lent; yet have I seen a mighty burst of
anger quelled by as mighty an effort, and
in the briefest space he would turn round
with that bland expression, which was the
only one natural to his countenance, the
hand would be extended and all would be
harmony. There was an immense degree
of self-control and concentratedness, when
he chose to exercise it; and to those who
had tact enough to handle that nature, and
he knew them sincere, melted wax was not
more easy of impression, and that for good.
To them he gave himself, and his posses-
sions into their keeping, to be moulded to
their will. He was nature’s gentleman,
and society had assisted in polishing him,
and few could equal him in doing the hon-
ors of his house and table. His guests

 laid himself ont to make them so. He was
a man of few words, too few, but an admir-
able listener, and richly capable of appre-
ciation. His nature was warm, vigorous
and sincere, but unapproachably reserved;
'twas a soil for friendship to take deep
root in, and if such he coveted, he must 
have been amply gratified. He was ad-
mired and respected by his superiors, be-
loved by his equals, and ever looked upon
by the poor man as his friend, A kind
parent, a faithful friend, an exemplary
master, none could render him a kindness,
however trivial, that his generous nature
ever forgot to acknowledge. He was a
widower before I became acquainted with
him—being left with five children at a very
early age. And what shall I say more? That
he died too soon for us; had he lived longer,
I know those whose gentle hands he would 
have loved to grasp, I know those to whom
he would have been all that I have por-
trayed him; yes, those deep blue eyes
would often have run over whilst gazing
on them, and he would have been so proud
of them, and their society would have
softened and improved him, for he would
have felt there could not be such blessings
only from the great Giver of all good! But
this was not to be; no, he was cut off in 
the prime of manhood, after years of pre-
paratory premonitions—cut off in a moment,
almost "in the twinkling of an eye;" the 
flat went forth thou shalt die! and that
earthly tabernacle bowed to earth, the spir-
it had fled to God who gave it—illustrat-
ing that "the race is not to the swift, nor
the battle to the strong," and teaching us,
in language trumpet-tongued that "man is
like a thing of nought, his time passeth
away as a shadow and as a flower of the 
field, so he withereth." "Be still, and 
know that I am God."
