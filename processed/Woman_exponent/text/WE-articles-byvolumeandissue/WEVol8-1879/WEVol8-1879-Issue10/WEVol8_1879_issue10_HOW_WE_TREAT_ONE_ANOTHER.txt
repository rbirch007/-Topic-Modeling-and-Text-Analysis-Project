	HOW WE TREAT ONE ANOTHER.

	When I look around me, and see the lack
of consideration we have for each other's
feelings and circumstances, the lack of
courtesy in our habits and address towards
one another, I am led to think that we are
very far from realizing and performing our
duties as Latter-day Saints. As fathers,
we are careless and indifferent, to a certain
degree, to the welfare and comfort of our
families, and to the example we set before
them. As mothers, we are impatient, un-
wise, and apt to do many things in haste,
to repent at leisure. As sons and daughters
we are many times disrespectful, disobe-
dient and heedless in our demeanor towards
our parents. As husbands and wives, we
are, to a great extent, indifferent to the
courtesy, affection and forbearance due one
another. As friends and acquaintances we
are insincere and impolite. Some may
think this a harsh judgment, observation
will prove it a true one. I would say to
parents, be affectionate, just and impartial
towards your children, commanding, by
your example and precept, their love and
obedience; study to realize your duties,
and responsibilities, and fit and prepare
yourselves for the performance of the same.
To sons and daughters, love and respect
your parents as your best earthly friends,
repaying in love and obedience the anxiety
and care you have caused them. To hus-
bands and wives I can say nothing better
than the words of Cowper;

"The kindest and the happiest pair,
Will find occasion to forbear;
And something every day they live,
To pity and perhaps forgive."

To friends and acquaintances, be sincere,
polite and kind. To all, think, speak and
live truly, give honor to whom honor is
due, respect true manhood and womanhood
in whatever garb you may find it, and by
your example, courtesy, intelligence, sobri-
ety and integrity, show to the world at
large that a Latter-day Saint is an honor to
his country and his God.
								L. E.
