	SALT LAKE CITY, Dec. 11th. 1878.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	I feel that I should be omitting a sacred
duty if I were not to bear my humble testi-
mony, through your valuable paper, to the
good times we are enjoying here in Utah,
compared with the poverty and distress now
being endured by the masses in the world.
We should be truly grateful for the bless-
ings we enjoy, that we are here, where our-
selves and children are free to enjoy the
blessings of life, and where all can at least
have plenty of food to eat, and raiment to
wear, and where when we have become aged
and not able to earn our own livelihood;
there is something better than a paupers
home, or a paupers-grave awaits us.
	I have lately returned from a visit to my
native land, England; where I can truly
say, I have witnessed more poverty, dis-
tress and drunkenness, in one short year
than I had ever imagined in the quarter of
a century I had previously spent in Utah.
What a contrast! here the servants of God

[Column 2]

are striving to save the human family, both
temporally and spiritually. There, the great
aim is to destroy, true there are some who
are trying to benefit mankind but how puny
is their effort without the true guide. the
strong arm, the spirit of God, the priesthood
on which we lean for support. The Latter-
day Saints in Europe seem to be more alive
than ever to the importance of gathering
"home," and all who have the spirit of our
holy religion, are striving with all their
might to emancipate themselves and fami-
lies, though many have very little prospects
apparently of being able to do so; yet they
will often say, "I have faith that the way
will open for us soon," and it is often mar-
velous to them, how the way will open.
	I will here relate an incident that come
under my notice, a young girl left her par-
ents in the spring of the present year, to
join her brother who had previously come
to Utah. I saw her mother some weeks
afterwards, she had just received a letter
from her daughter, written after her arrival
here; after telling her of the bright pros-
pects before her, she urged her parents to
come if possible, at any sacrifice; she told
them there was plenty here for all, that they
were not afraid to eat all they wanted, for
fear the bread would not last, for when it
was all gone they could soon make more,
neither were they afraid the butter would
not last the week out, for when that was
all gone they could go to work and churn
more, for there was always plenty. The
parents then had very little prospect of com-
ing, but they had faith, and at last a kind
friend was raised np for them, and they
came with the last company this year, and
are made happy with their children and

them to have faith that they might be
privileged soon to gather with the saints in
Zion. Many are looking forward to the
time when they can do so, but it is only the
poor, those who have means are afraid they
will not be as well off here as there, but the
"poor and meek" are to inherit the King-
dom. Hoping to be one of the number, I
remain, &c.
