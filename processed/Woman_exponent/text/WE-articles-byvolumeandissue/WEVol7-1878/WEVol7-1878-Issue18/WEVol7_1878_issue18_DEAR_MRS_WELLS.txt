DEAR MRS. WELLS:
*	*	*	I wish I could do something
to encourage and cheer you. I sent you the
first notice I saw of the movement of the
Anti-polygamy Crusade. I was greatly sur-
prised as well as grieved to know that there
were women so utterly ignorant of God's laws
as to inaugurate such a movement. I do not,
of course, know the laws of your church, but
take it for granted that they are binding, just
as they should be. That women are degraded,
enslaved, and cruelly treated in the polygamic
creed I do not believe. My knowledge of wo-
man teaches me that the conditions of women
in plural marriage must be more consistent
with the laws of life and health than in mo-
nogamy.
	Prostitution and [-]eath are broadcast in our
land to-day from this very cause, and there
seems to be no stay to the foul fiend. "Death
mocks at it with a h[-]-ha—society hisses and
scorns," itself reeking in the filthy slough.
Polygamy a curs[-]?  Nay, not that, but the
greatest blessing ever bestowed upon woman;
and thousands to-day would flock to the stand-
ard did they but understand and know the
true laws governing them. I do not believe
that there is a true woman in our land to-day
but grieves and silently sorrows at the down-
fall of any wom[-]n, but s[-]e is helpless and can
do nothing, not even give a helping hand to


the fallen one; and it seems to me this is the
only way out of it. You have my heartiest
prayers and deepest sympathies, and would to 
God I could do something to help elevate and
ennoble woman. I am doing something, but
it is so little compared to the field before me
that I feel humble, and it seems to me some-
times that I am a stumbling block instead of
an aid.
