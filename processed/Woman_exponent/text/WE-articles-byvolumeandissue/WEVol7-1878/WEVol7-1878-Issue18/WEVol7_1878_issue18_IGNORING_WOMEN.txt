IGNORING WOMEN.

	It is true that Secretary Sherman in the
course of his speech, as an illustration, said
that "the nation's faith should be like the
chastity of a woman above suspicion," it
must hardly be said, that in presence of
the gentlemen there assembled, he did not
speak of the "chastity of man," since such
a remark would probably have been looked
upon as a personal allusion by every man
present. With this single exception, how-
ever, a diligent perusal of the speeches has
failed to discover the slightest hint of the
existence, past or present, of the female
half of our people. A great deal was said
of the hardy men who colonized the land,
of the struggles of the men who built up
the country, and in reading these after din-
ner efforts one would actually think that a
race of grim masculine creatures had really
settled, populated and adorned New Eng-
land without the aid of a solitary woman.
Mr. James G. Blaine capping the climax of
forgetfulness by speaking of the hardships
endured by the men and declaring that
these "21,000 men were the progenitors" of
a great race! As if even in the matter of
increasing the census and raising families
men were quite independent of all feminine
assistance.
