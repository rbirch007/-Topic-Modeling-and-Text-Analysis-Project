	It has been said we are becoming an emo-
tional race. A writer in a prominent maga-
zine, in an article published some time
since, advocated this idea and carried his
theory to such an extent as to declare that
the language would ultimately become ob-
solete, and the interchange of ideas be car-
ried on by means of gesticulations and emo-
tional signs. That such an event is among
the possibilites sound arguments may be
adduced to maintain, and reflection would
lead to the conclusion that, by a system of
education, an emotional language might be
substituted for speech without seriously af-
fecting the necessary interchange of opin-
ions. The assertion of the ancient orator,
that he could express an idea by actions in
as many different ways as a fluent writer
could express it in words, and the actual
verification of the assertion, stands strongly
in support of this theory, but that that
wonderful and delicate organ, the tongue,
given to man by the Creator, and designed
for such remarkable uses, capable of pro-
ducing such unmeasured joy, and causing
such profound sorrow, should fall into gen-
eral disuse by the substitution of emotional
language is not only improbable but verges
on the ridiculous. However, emotional
language, or, as it might be better denomi-
nated, expression, should be cultivated.
	Expression is as old as mankind, and, as
many other things, serves to show the de-
gree of civilization attained not only by
individuals but by nations. It is essentially
the child of the soul and is nourished by
the heart; and admitting this it is at once
established as the sister of sincerity. Rea-
son for a moment. What pleasure is de-
rived from listening to a song in which
there is no expression? or what feelings
are awakened at hearing a beautiful and
expressive ballad sung by one in whose
soul the musical ideas and cadences find no
response? What is it that creates poetry
but the outburst of the fire of the soul into
expressions that rekindle similar fires in
the breasts of others? and how long does
the soul of poetry exist when it is read by
one to whom it gives no sense of joy or sor-
row? Imagine an actor reciting a passage

from Shakespeare in a strain that is com-
mon to schoolboys-while reciting their les-
sons; or conjecture, if you can, what would
be your feelings to be met by those you
love, after years of absence, with a coolness
of manner that almost freezes you and an
expression of countenance that is as placid
and inanimate as a mirror.
	A few illustrations like these serve to
show the beauty of expression, but it must
always be borne in mind that true expres-
sion is found only in true souls. There is
wisdom in the saying, "Assume a virtue if
thou hast it not," but there is something
angelic in possessing the virtue itself. You
cannot cultivate expression by assuming an
exterior smile when the heart is as cold and
chilling as winter—for the frost is transpa-
rent; you cannot be wholesouled by clasp-
ing the hand firmly, or embracing fondly,
when the heart lacks fondness and firmness
as the diamond lacks warmth. The smile
comes willingly, the sunshine must come in
the heart. Then the smile reflects the sun-
shine, and the warmth brings gladness to
others as the warmth of the sun brings
gladness to earth. No one need then ask
if you have compassion, for the lip quivers;
if you have pity, for the tear trembles in
the eye; if you love, for the whole being
shows it; or if you feel anger, for it is too
plain. In this latter respect, however, it is
safe to say that the virtue is unassumed.
