	Learn to adapt yourself to circumstances;
circumstances cannot adapt themselves to
you. There was a preacher once, so the
papers say, who was better able to accom-
modate himself to circumstances than any
one we have lately heard of.  His sermon
were as well assorted as the colors of the
rainbow; if he was invited to a Presbyte-
rian pulpit, he selected his manuscript from
the true blue; if to a Calvinistic pulpit, he
chose one from the yellow, the color of fire;
and if to a Universalist pulpit - for he is
many sided, and bright on all sides, and is
not so prejudiced but he can preach any
where when the fee is alluring - he takes a
sermon from some of the paler and more
indistinct colors.  He works on the Euro-
pean plan, giving to each church whatever
it wants and will pay for.  Yes, he adapted
himself to circumstances, but we know a
better minister than that, and something
better than adapting oneself to circum-
stances, it is knowing how to occupy one's
time. This minister we were speaking of
used to say to his flock, "Brethren, you
may desire to know how I have occupied
my time during the past year.  It took just
six months of the year for me to attend to
my own business, and the other six months
to let other people's business alone."
	Don't tell secrets to any one, not even
your best friend; for "A secret once told is
a secret no longer," and we once heard a
young lady say that she dearly loved to
have some one confide a great secret in her,
for then she always had something inter-
esting to talk about at the next sewing bee.
Our advice is don't have secrets.
Don't sulk over split milk, it does not
pay. Some men, when they have split
their milk on the ground, sit down by the
wet spot and dilute the lost lacteal fluid
with their briny tears; there is a lesson they
haven't learned yet that the boy who lost
his kite might teach them - at first he
thought he would cry about it, but imme-
diately afterward determined not to and
went home whistling; when asked if he
was not sorry to lose his treasure he re-
plied, "Yes, but I can't fool away much
time in being sorry; I'm just going to make
a new one, and I guess a better one." Chil-
dren tell us as much truth as the catechism.
A grave school inspector, with a face which
resembled the lengthened line of misery,
was trying to impress some infant minds
with awful facts; sternly looking into their
happy faces he asked, with "a voice from
the tombs," "Children, do you know who
loves all men?"  The silence was oppress-
ive for a moment, but the conundrum must
be answered, and one little bit of humanity
in the far-off corner timidly lifted her head.
"Ah!" glumly observed the tragic inspect-
or; "well, child, you will tell the whole
school who loves all men."  She rose,
stroked the wrinkles out of her pretty
white apron, and answered with a shrill
voice, "All women, sir!"
	Don't gossip; it isn't right.  Don't com-
mence it, for if ever you begin  it is the
hardest work in the world to stop.  The
hardest thing to get a good hold on is your
own tongue.  If you could only keep still
at the right time you would save yourself
many a heartache.  A young friend of
mine once remarked, on leaving the house
where several of her acquaintances were
visiting, "I did so hate to leave, I wonder
if any one there will speak good of me; do
you think they will criticise my appearance
and manner, or talk of what I have been
doing within the last week?"  "Both," re-
plied her companion; "they'll pick you all
to pieces."  At a reception not long ago,
the hostess seeing that the conversation was
dragging considerably, asked one of her
guests to go home so that the others might
have something to talk about.
	Never speak much of your own perform-
ances.  I knew a gentleman who used to
talk so much about his good singing that
when it was asked if he could sing people
used to say, "He says he can."
	Never fail to be punctual at the time ap-
pointed.  It is hardly necessary to elabor-
ate that theme, since it is so widely
preached.  Never make yourself the hero
of your own story.  Many a person has
failed to gain popularity from that one thing.
	Never exaggerate.  If exaggeration does
add color to your story, sometimes the
color spoils the cloth.  I'd much rather
have the true story than the hyperbole,
and who would not rather drink water than
paint. Color adds beauty, but it adds poi-
son too.
