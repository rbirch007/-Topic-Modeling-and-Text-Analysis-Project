BY THE BROOK.

	I've been sitting by the brook to-day,
And listening to its flow;
And lightly on its laughing way,
	It gurgled sweet and low.
I sat down on its mossy bank,
	And leaned against a tree,
And asked, and answered, as I sat.
	Swift questions, it and me.

	"Why art thou still and sad," it said,
	"With brow knit tight with pain?
Why canst thou not the world enjoy,
	When joy chants high her strain?"

	"O brook," I answered, "thou canst tell
	Of joys and pleasures moving:
But how canst thou know aught, in truth,
	Of pain, born oft of loving.
Thou hast not had my baby's arms.
	Around thee close enfolded;
Thou hast not known her gentle power,
	Or nature, angel-moulded.

"Her dimpled fingers never clasped
Your own in baby pressure;

And how can you, a simple brook,
	Know why I love my treasure?"

	"But how can all that you have told,
	Bring sorrow to your heart?
Has death's dark hand, with cruel grasp,
	Compelled you two to part?"

	"Ah, no, sweet brook; and were it so,
	My brow were all unknit;
And here to-day with quiet eyes
	And calmest heart I'd sit.
For, knowing that my floweret rare
	Were in His careful keeping,
With patience I would pray and wait,
	And rest my heart with weeping.

	"By grasp more fierce than death's, sweet brook,
	She's been removed from me;
And all her loving ways and looks,
	No more, no more I see.
Man's cruel word betrayed my heart,
	And left it cold and bleeding.--
What cares that man, in all his pride,
	For the wife's tears and pleading! 

	"I prayed them with a mother's tears,
	And offered all I owned,
To only keep my little ones,
	That Heaven had to me loaned.
Soften, O God, the hardened hearts
	That flung aside my trust,
And heeded neither tears nor prayers!
	But said it was but just. 

	"But just! O God, didst Thou
	E'er ordain such thing?
That we must suffer all the pain,
	And only know the sting?
Must woman ever toil and weep,
	And travail for the world,
And man sit over her, and judge	
	With scornful lip upcurled?

	"And so, dear brook, my heart is sad,
	And crushed with rarest pain;
And I have come in solitude
	To pour forth my refrain."

	"Full well I see why thou art sad.
	With darkly-clouded eyes;
And surely more than human thou;
	If it were otherwise.
But let me tell you what I know,
	A brook though I may be;
The triumph of the right will come,
	For God doth all things see.

	"Too long has woman been the one
	To suffer and to weep,
The time has come when men will find
	That justice will not sleep.
And woman will uprise and show
	How cruel is her wrong;
And conscious of her pow'r for good,
	She'll move amid the throng.
