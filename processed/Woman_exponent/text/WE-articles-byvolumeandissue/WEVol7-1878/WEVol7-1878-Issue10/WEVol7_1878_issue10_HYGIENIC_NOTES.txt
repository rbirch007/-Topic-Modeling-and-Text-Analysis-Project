	Cure for Diptheria.—Take two ounces each of
olive oil and coal oil; mix well by shaking. Ad-
minister, internally, every hour, a quantity accord-
ing to the age of the patient. Dose for a child
five years old half a teaspoonful of the mixture;
half that age, half the quantity, and so on.
	Twenty minutes afterwards, give the same quan-
tity of dry sulphur. Twenty minutes after that
the same quantity of dry cream of tartar.
	This treatment is to be repeated for three hours,
when great improvement is generally perceived, in
which case the time between the doses is to bə
gradually increased, until a cure is effected. A
little common sense and appreciation of the differ-
ence of constitution, &c., in various cases will
suggest modifications of the treatment.—Deseret
News.
	Bathing Children.—Some mothers, writes a
physician, think when their children get beyond
two or three years of age the frequent entire bath
can be dispensed with. If some of the main
facts of physiology were well known and under-
stood, every one would perceive that cleanliness
of the skin is one of the conditions of good health.
It happens when bathing is disregarded that the
lungs, kidneys or bowels have more than their ap-
portionment of work. If these are strong and
healthy, they may bear the tax with little appa-
rent injury, but, in most cases, a lowering of the
vitality and tone of the system ensues. Large
bath tubs are pleasant and convenient, but not in-
dispensable to the proper cleansing of the skin.
A speedy sponging of the body in pure water,
followed by friction in pure air, is all that is nec-
essary.
	When disinclined to use water, I find thorough
application of the flesh brush to the whole person
an admirable substitute, especially on retiring; it
relieves nervousness, equalizes the circulation and
induces quiet sleep[-] Mothers, above all, should
see that their children are well bathed; If their
skins are kept active and healthy there will not be
half the danger from fever, cold and eruptive dis-
eases. If your little one is cross or troublesome,
and finds no occupation that pleases him, try the
effect of a bath; sometimes it is magical, and, if
tried, he will go to sleep and awaken bright; cheer[-]
ful and happy. Do not, though, as I have seen
some parents do, plunge a child into water, when
he screams and shrinks from it, thinking they are
doing a good deed. Nature must be the guide; if
your child has a nervous constitution, a shock of

WE would say to our subscribers through-
out the country, as there has been some
misunderstanding in regard to postage,
that the postage for one year is ten cents to
all parts of the Territory and United States,
to Europe, twenty-four cents,this may seem
very little to speak about to one person, but.
makes a vast difference in the whole.
 THE Lord has blessed this people with
one of the most abundant harvests that ever
crowned the labors of the farmer, and we
trust our sisters will not be unmindful of
the mission given them  store up grain
against a day of need.  It is now just two
years since this mission was given to the
sisters, and particulars published for their
benefit in the columns of the Exponent.
President Young had great faith in the
women of Zion and, with the organizations
as they now now exist it is a comparatively
easy matter to gather in means.  The sis-
ters have been liberal in their donations,
and quite a large amount has been stored in
different parts of the Territory, but this is
a work that is not done in a year or two, but
is a continous labor, and certainly the sisters
should not feel it a burden, but continue in
well doing, knowing that “the race is not
to the swift,” but to those who endure to
the end.  There are some granaries already
built, and others begun, and we trust that
the sisters will be earnest in this good cause,
for such it is; what could be greater or
grander than to disburse food to the desti-
tute in a time of need?  If the sisters are
striving for usefulness, and wanting a high
sphere to labor in, here it is.  Let there be
some Joseph’s in Utah even it they are
women: it will matter but little when men
are hungry if even a woman dispenses
charity to them.  Sisters let us be wise in
this respect, and lay up of the abundance
God has given us here in these valleys, and
manifest to Him and to all the world our
liberality in the things which we have been
commanded to do.  Never mind what those
say who are not co-workers with us, but
prove our faith in the prophecies of the an-
cient and modern prophets, by storing up
grain for ourselves and our friends, and
those who will come from afar to ask of us,
when the land is laid desolate with the ter-
rible scourges that await it.  Let all the
Relief Societies throughout the length and
breadth of the Territory see what can be
done in this matter, and send us word pre-
vious to the Annual Grain Meeting which
will be held in this city Nov. 16, in the in-
terest of the Grain Question.
