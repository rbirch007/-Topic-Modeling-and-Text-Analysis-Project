VOL. I. 			SALT LAKE CITY, UTAH, JUNE 15, 1872.			No. 2.


﻿NEWS AND VIEWS.
		  —
  NEW YORK blacksmiths are striking—for
wages. 
  THE Democratic cauldron is bubbling ; it
will boil at Baltimore on the 9th proximo.
  PRESIDENT Grant was nominated for re-
election by the Philadelphia Convention.
Will he be elected ?
   ADMIRERS of John Brown are anxious to
erect a monument to his memory at Har-
per’s Ferry or Charleston. Is he likely to
be soon forgotten in those places ?
  MISS Alice Ingham, of Boston, is making
a fortune at Buenos Ayres, having estab-
lished a wholesale millinery store. When
all the milliners have made their fortunes,
it is to be hoped they will begin making
head-dresses of a more common-sense style
than many that are now made and worn.
OVER a thousand American newspapers
collapsed last year, a single fact that shows
how prone our “independent sovereigns”
are to rush into the using of printer’s ink
without a secure footing on which to stand.
More than two millions of money, it is cal-
culated, was lost by the experimental enter-
prises.
 SIX Presidential tickets are already in the
field for the coming election, with several
conventions in prospective ; and Utah has
not a single effective voice in the exciting
contest. Six Presidential elections have
been held since Utah was settled, and yet
she has never cast a vote for President.
 TO BANDY the lie in the halls of Congress,
like street brawlers in a quarrel, as was done
in the House of Representatives on last
Monday night, is a poor way to manifest the
dignity which should characterize the leg-
islators of a great country. If the men who
lead in politics and aspire to govern the
people, would first learn to govern them-
selves, they would be better fitted for the
high duties which they are so anxious to as-
sume.
  AN ILLINOIS clerk refuses to marry be-
cause the women have so many faults. His
objections to one are :	“She walks per-
fectly natural, just as women used to do
years ago, and wears her own hair, like
some old grandmother, which would make
her appear so awkward among his fashiona-
ble friends!” Poor young man ! He’s too
wise for this mad world ! But, could he not
make a choice from some modern bundle of
fashionable millinery ?
  CONGRESS adjourned on Monday last, the
10th inst. The women of Utah are under
obligations to it, that it declined to pass
Representative Wheeler’s bill to deprive
them of the ballot, which the act of the Ter-
ritorial Legislature and the signature of Hon.
S. A. Mann conferred upon them. Had the
women of Utah done violence to their feel-
ings and intelligence, and at the polls voted
against the men they have proved and know
to be worthy of confidence, there would
have been no bill presented to Congress to
deprive them of the elective franchise.
AT THE International Typographical
Union, which met in convention, in Rich-
mond, Virginia, last week, an amendment
to the constitution of the organization was
[end of column; start of new column]
offered, discountenencing the chartering of
female unions, and striking out the word
“male.” Under the rules this will lay over
for a year. It is discreditable to the intelli-
gence of the typographical profession that
such distinctions should exist. Where wo-
men make as good compositors as men they
should enjoy all the rights and privileges
possessed by male compositors, and the ef-
fort to continue a class distinction because
of sex is as unjust as it is ungallant.
  IT IS again announced that Dr. Living-
stone, the indomitable Scotchman who has
made his memory famous through coming
time by his African explorations, has been
again heard from and is safe. He has fur-
nished a large fund of material for small
wits that can neither appreciate his daring,
enterprise, energy, nor the love for science
and the advancement of knowledge which
has sustained him in his many perilous un-
dertakings. Yet he has done more to pene-
trate the mysteries of that hitherto unknown
world, which lies buried in the vast African
continent, than any other man, living or
dead, of whom past or contemporaneous his-
tory furnishes any account.
 THE platform adopted by the National
Rebublican Convention which met at Phila-
delphia on the 5th inst., acknowledges that
the claims of those who advocate woman
suffrage are entitled to respect. This has
the appearance of a bid for the services of
Mrs. Hooker and other women lecturers, to
take the stump in support of the party nom-
inations. But, inasmuch as the Republicans
have had a large majority in both houses of
Congress for some years, why is it that the
sought-for sixteenth amendment to the Con-
stitution, to confer upon women the suffrage,
has never been able to command a respecta-
ble vote in Congress that weuld send it to
the country for ratification.  Plausable plat-
forms sound well, but consistent acts are
better.
  Two more Polar expeditions have started.
  Theodore Tilton is for Greeloy “first, last,
and all the time.”	
  Florida will soon have her first street railway,'
at Cedar Keys. Utah will beat her.
The Boston “Peace Jubilee” will be a great
crash. It opens on Monday, the 17th inst.
  “Scissors editors” is the latest and most appro-
priate name for a numerous class of the editorial
fraternity.
  ILLUSTRATED papers arc growing. They teach
through the eye, which is a pleasant mode of acquir-
ing knowledge when the object is agreeable.
CHINA’s religious institutions, it is said, cost her
four hundred.millions of dollars annually; less than
a dollar a head. Why, Christendom pays nearly
as much per caput for religion, and’ yet see how
prisons, madhouses, dens of infamy and general in-
iquity thrive !
TO BECOME acquainted with the “ strong points”
of a dishonorable or disreputable character, which
the principal men of the country have made during
the past few years, a stranger should take in for
the next three months about half a dozen political
papers sustaining different parties.
CHARLES JAMES LEVER, the author, is dead.
With a rare fund of humor, a wonderful talent for
delineating the rollicking character of the Irish in
all its exuberance, and a wealth of plot and incident
[end of column; start of new column]
rarely excelled, he furnished much pleasant read-
ing for all English-speaking, countries,'which will
long keep his memory green.
  TWENTY thousand singers and eight thousand in-
strumental performers will participate in the “Bos-
ton International Musical Peace Jubilee;” and
provision has been made for seating a hundred
thousand people. One clever Irishman has suc-
ceeded in adding a new feature of notoriety to the
“ Hub of the Universe,” and Boston rejoices.
Fearful storms have swept over different places
in the Eastern and Middle States within the past
two weeks, washing away railroad bridges, houses,
fences, farm-produce, and cattle, leaving buildings
shattered by lightning, and killing a number of
people. It seemed as if the Storm King had gone
forth in his fury, spreading devastation in his track.
  STRIKES have become plenty of late, especially
in New York, and the demand is that eight hours’
work and ten hours’ pay shall constitute the labor
and compensation for a day’s toil. We propose
two other strikes, to include two classes of “ white
slaves.” Let seamstresses and editors demand a
reduction of labor and an increase of pay. Both
classes need them, and both, as classes, are harder
worked than are the men of any trade engaged in
the New York strikes.
  JUST now there is only one subject of surpassing
interest in America:	Who	shall	distribute	the offi-
cial patronage during the four years commencing
March 4, 1873. When men were honest and lost
money by holding office, the offices had often to seek
the men; but now they have found a way to realize
a fortune in a year or two out of a starvation salary,
and the number of people seeking offices is so great,
that they could scarcely be suitably supplied if ev-
ery cell in every penitentiary in the country was
prepared for their especial use.
 ANOTHER appalling domestic tragedy occurred in
New York. The circumstances are one Emil
Andrea, a Frenchman, whose wife had left him on
account of disfiguration by small-pox, was living
with another man. The husband went to her resi-
dence to obtain her signature to a paper for separa-
tion. She refused and fled; he caught her on the
comer of First avenue. and Fifteenth street, took
her by the hair, put a pistol to her head, shot her
dead and waited for the police to arrest him. Sad,
indeed !
REV. HUSTON, of Baltimore, was tried by an eccle-
siastical court, with closed doors, for alleged immor-
ality with young women of his acquaintance. lie
was acquitted, and proposes to bring suit for libel
against the “Baltimore American” newspaper.
Meantime he has been indicted by a grand jury on
the same charge; so that between the libel prose-
cution in which he will be plaintiff, and the crim-
inal prosecution in which he will be defendant,
there is a chance that the public will get at facts
which it is generally believed the ecclesiastical court
concealed.
 FOR jealous and infatuated “ lovers” to shoot
and stab the objects of their insane desires, if the
latter refuse to accept their matrimonial offers, is
become somewhat fashionable. One of the latest
cases is that of a painter in San Francisco named.
Mansfield, who twico shot a Miss Hein, daughter
of an importing merchant, and then put a bullet
through his own body, but without killing either.
William the Conqueror, of England, secured his
wife by seizing her on the street, tearing her hair
—women wore their own hair then—pounding her,
and rolling her in the mud. The modern style is
not so rough, but it is far more fatal. How we im-
prove !

WOMAN'S EXPONENT.


THE FEMALE RELIEF SOCIETY.
A BRIEF SKETCH OF ITS ORGANIZATION AND WORK-
INGS IN THE CITY OF NAUVOO, HANCOCK CO.. ILL.
		BY ELIZA R. SNOW.
			(Concluded.)
	When organized, the Society consisted of
nineteen members, exclusive of its officers.
This number augmented from time to time,
until at the close of the fiscal year it
amounted to 1158. The meetings were so
large that the largest hall in the city was
densely crowded and it became necessary to
adjourn to the Grove, the usual place in
the Summer for holding Sabbath meeting,
which was provided with a suitable stand
and a supply of seats for large assemblies.
	The Society soon became so popular that
even those of doubtful character in several
instances applied for admission, and to pre-
vent imposition by extending membership

who is possessed of the greater power of
persuasion, or the more gifted as a reasoner,

just to give him, for a companion and help
mate, so inferior an object as woman must 
have been. Had He made woman the supe-
rior in most respects, the arrangement would 
have been equally unjust. But He created
them for each other, as nearly alike as it was
wisdom in Him to form them, and so that
one is of little or no consequence without
the other.

Quick Atlantic Passage. — The Na-
tional Line of steamers, which have become
deservedly popular with European tourists,
as sumptuously appointed and substantially
constructed vessels, are also acquiring celeb-
rity for their speed, as well as their safety
and splendor. The "Spain,” which is one
of the finest specimens of naval architecture
in this favorite line, has just completed one
of the quickest passages across the Atlantic
on record. She sailed from Queenstown on
the 18th ultimo, at 2 P. M., and arrived at
Sandy Hook on the 27th at 3 A. M., making
the run in the remarkably short period of
eight days and thirteen hours. Invention
and science are so shortening distance, in
point of time, that a trip from Salt Lake to
London, made in seven days, is within the
bounds of probability within ten years.

HOUSEHOLD HINTS.
	One special care of every housekeeper
should be to know that the current expenses
of her household do not exceed the general
income. By keeping a regular account of
every article purchased, this object is not
only attained, but the value of things may
be learned, and the judgment and memory
strengthened. It is one mark of good un-
derstanding not to get anything that is not
really needed, and if it can possibly be
avoided, never to take what cannot be paid
for at the time. But if, as at times such
things will occur, it is necessary to ask for
trust, keeping a small account book and
having the tradesman charge both himself
and the purchaser at the same time, often
saves money and future trouble and uncer-
tainty. A lady of experience remarks that
in large families it is best to purchase large
quantities of household requisites at a time;
but less where a family is small, as, where
plenty is known to exist, there is apt to be
too lavish use. Buy articles of clothing that
will wear and wash well. A beautiful con-
sistency should pervade the whole house,
and if there must be stint, let it not be in
the kitchen comforts and conveniences. Let
there not be a well furnished parlor and
shabby sleeping rooms; or all these things
in abundance, and an ill supplied kitchen
and pantry. A correct system of economy
and how to make the proper and best use of
things in these respects, can no more be
learned without experience and pains-tak-
ing, than any other trade or science. Yet,
these are principles that every woman should
understand, and practice understandingly.
It need not and should not occupy the whole
time of a woman’s life to keep her house
and all pertaining to it in perfect order and
neatness. A portion of her time should
wisely be appropriated to the culture and
development of the intellectual faculties
[end of column; start of new column]
with which she has been blessed ; arrange-
ments should be made, if possible, so that
religious services, the reception of friends,
or the acceptance of a cordial invitation to
wait on friends, need not be hindered by, or
interfere with, the general domestic duties.
And because usefulness is to be considered
paramount to everything else in selecting
household furniture, utensils, etc., the proper
adornments need not be neglected. Pictures
and flowers, and everything that is truly
beautiful and pleasing to the eye, enhance
general happiness, and the lowliest home
should not be without a proportionate share
of them.
	Using the Mop.—Mrs. L. H. C. Wayne,
of New York, writes : “Where one has a
hardwood, oak or maple floor, strong, hot
soapsuds may be freely used. If, on the
contrary, the floor is pine and nicely painted,
it will need painting two or three times a
year, if hot or even strong soapsuds are used.
Hired girls and washer-women often make
sad havoc with the paint, soaping, and even
scouring with hot ashes. With a clean mop
and clean, soft, warm, or hot water, first run
the mop all along the mop boards, in the
corners, on the thresholds, over the oil-
cloths and zincs, in the pantry and all the
particular places. Change the water before
it assumes a muddy appearance. Draw
out the table a little and run a clean mop
behind that. There is no need of breaking
one’s back stooping down, if both mop and
water are quite clean.”

flannel. The application of kerosene oil is a
preventive of vermin.

must not be too powerful, or injury may be

﻿WEATHER AND HEALTH.
			  
  The weather has a great influence—great-	
er than is commonly supposed—upon our 
physical systems. Every person has a time 
for being especially sick, if ever sick at all,
apart from all effects of diet, occupation, or
general habits. Some cannot abide a pro-
longed “dry spell." Many individuals
are surely depressed by the approach and	
reign of Spring; others have their weak	
spots probed sharply by the heats and	
draughts of Summer ; the majority of peo-
ple suffer pains and disabilities from the pe-
culiar atmosphere and sudden changes of
Autumn; and the chill rigors of Winter in-
capacitate multitudes from physical and
mental ease and comfort. In certain cli-
mates, there are “fatal” months, during
which everybody should be most careful of
their health, using every endeavor to be
clothed properly, and to be in every other
respect protected from baleful and climatic
injuries. In the majority of the United
States, the months of March and November
exercise the most pernicious influences, and 
witness the greatest number of deaths. In the 
Southern States, and in what may be termed
the Middle States, the Summer months reap 
for grim Death the greatest harvest. But 
even the effects of the extreme heat could,
in many cases, be prevented from operating 
fatally, or even to moderation of great suf-
fering, by prudential habits and systemati-
cal observance of common-sense sanitary
rules. Taking the country at large, we
have more deaths in the six colder months 
of January, February, March, April, Novem-
ber, and December, than in the other six
months of the year. It is in the colder
months that the most terrible maladies are
contracted—those which hang on, and tor-
ture, and punish, and linger only (if not 
treated according to the best and only sensi-
ble method) to kill. The warmer months
are made especially the season of the quick
fatalities, which make short work when
they get hold of systems that have been at
any other season improperly medicated, or
in any other way enfeebled, and thrown
open to the assaults of malignant disease.
Children come in for a larger share of suffer-
ing in the Summer than in any other season.
If their parents and guardians would see
that they are kept properly clean, and have
free range out of doors (never at mid-day,
or in the night air), subsist on a plain and
mildly stimulating diet, of which cooked
fruits, or the ripe fruits of the season un-
cooked, should form a lagre proportion,
sleep in well-aired and well-sunned apart-
ments, and avoid the usual course of drug-
ging, relying for medicines upon what Na-
ture so amply provides during the Summer
season, in the fields, woods, and by the
roadside, mortality among the juveniles
would be decreased by a large percentage.
Grown people would do well to adapt this 
advice to their own cases.—“Shakesperian  ' 
Almanac.”

﻿IMPORTANT INDUSTRIES.
			   
  Virtue reigns in the rural districts. This
truism though old will bear repeating. And
every industry which offers employment for
the young in their own homes, with equal pe-
cuniary compensation to that which would be
obtained in large factories, where contamina-
tion with immoral tendencies is too frequent,
should be encouraged.
  Just now two industries of this character pre-
sent themselves for consideration and a season-
able word in their favor. One is straw-plait-
ing; the other is sericulture. Both are easily
learned, light and cleanly. Many women in
Utah understand the straw-plaiting business
thoroughly, and we have seen as handsome
work made by some of them as we have ever
seen on imported straw goods. It is an occupa-
tion that can be followed in the Winter; and
when Spring comes, the plaited straw can be
worked up into the desired fashions that vari-
ous tastes demand. The outlay is compara-
tively nothing. A bunch of rye or wheat straw
furnishes the material and lithe young fingers
can soon learn to deftly weave the plaits with
neatness and dispatch. One great objection
made against patronizing home-manufactured
straw goods Is that they cost more than impor-
ted goods. This should be met by workers not
demanding too high pay, and by dealers doing
with the lowest possible profits. By and by,
when a thriving business has been successfully
created,it will be found that Utah straw goods
can be made and furnished as cheap as those
imported, if not cheaper.
   Sericulture only occupies a portion of the time
during the Summer. The mulberry trees grow,
and when Spring time comes they put forth
their leaves as food for the busy worms, which
eat and work, spinning the silken home for
their eggs. These eggs are valuable as an arti-


cle of commerce. Apart from the fact that the
manufacture of silk is a pleasant as well as
profitable occupation, from the breeding and
sale of the eggs alone a handsome income can
be secured with little trouble and less outlay.
  We do not design to elaborate on either topic
now, but merely present them to keep them
before the public mind, for they have often been
dwelt upon through the press and from the
stand, and should be familiar to most of the
readers of the EXPONENT.


﻿DANGEROUS EXCESSES.
			   
   Revolutions are ever attended by excesses.
No matter how wisely designed, or how well
conducted, there are always found associated
with them people of ill-balanced minds and in-
temperate judgments who run riot in extremes.
There is a social revolution now in progress in
the United States. While it affects all classes,
colors, and creeds, and both sexes, its progress
promises to be directly fraught with grave re-
sults to women. The mere recognition of a de-
fined and extended political status would be
comparatively unimportant, when weighed
with the sweeping changes in the very struc-
ture of society which some advocates of wo-
man’s rights contemplate and defend. Time
was when the retiring grace of modesty, with
which maidens and matrons were adorned, was
considered their greatest charm, when home-
life and domestic affections were considered
holy and sacred things, and when a union of
hearts and hands was held as enduring as exist-
ence. True, there were exceptions as there are
to all general rules, and many of them ; but
this was the prevailing sentiment. Now, to
the demand for a wider recognition of the claims
which are justly preferred for women, are added
others which aim at securing full license to
gratify every passion or wild desire by which
ungoverned minds may be swayed. With the
right to vote, and the right to earn honest mo-
ney by honest industry in pursuits from which
women have been debarred, is claimed the
privilege to set natural laws and sound social
regulations at defiance. It is demanded that
the sacred bonds of marriage shall be disre-
garded whenever fleeting fancy desires change;
that the holy feelings which unite parents and
children shall be discountenanced ; and that
the offspring of a relationship unhallowed by
any stronger matrimonial tie than mutual de-
sire shall become the property of the State, thus
seeking to destroy the family circle, filial love,
parental care, and fraternal affection.
  These are some of the dangerous excesses into
which certain advocates of woman's rights have
already run. That they will encourage immo-
rality, increase vice and work serious evil to a
limited extent, is beyond question; but that
they will ever find an abiding place, or an en-
dorsement, in the sober thoughts and calm re-
flections of the women of America, we  do not
for a moment imagine. Still, as children should
be taught carefulness in handling dangerous
weapons, or instructed on the fatal effects of
deadly poisons, so parents should earnestly
warn the young against specious doctrines
which, under the sacred name of liberty, claim
license of the most revolting and unrestrained
character ; and, if universally adopted, would
make society a chaos and a scene of unbridled
passions, from which every pure intelligence
would shrink with loathing unutterable.

rect land entries and receiving patents up to a later
date than any other place in the country.
Brief as has been the EXPONENT's existence so

Large iron-fronted stores are being multiplied, busi-
ness crowds the streets, massive chignons and
 “Dolly Vardens” dot the sidewalks, and small

ful to the municipal authorities of Salt Lake City

for a needed periodical day of rest, which ' might
otherwise be denied some of them.

Utah, is expected home to-morrow night, the l6th.
He deserves well of the people of Utah, for he has
been an energetic, faithful and unflinching advocate
of their rights.   Hons. Geo. Q. Cannon and Frank
Fuller, who have also been in Washington acting in
behalf of the citizens of this Territory accompany
him.

  THE JUDGE who from his place on the bench
sought to brand thousands of the purest women in
Utah—matrons whose blameless lives would sanctify
any community on the continent—with the foulest
stigma, returns to again occupy the same position.
He may exercise the power, but he will fail to
command the respect which his office should receive.
The Supreme Court of the United States has so pro-
nounced, and the country at large has endorsed the
justness of the decision.

   WE HAVE received numerous words of encour-
agement, by letter and orally, since the first number
of the Exponent was issued, These are gratify-
ing, and in several instances they have been accom-
panied by more substantial tokens in the shape of
lists of subscribers. It is pleasing to know that
the paper meets the general approval; and to re-
ciprocate the kind wishes offered for its success,
we shall endeavor to make it as interesting, instruc-
tive and valuable as we possibly can.

  SEVERAL CASES of drowning and other serious
accidents have occurred within the last few days in
this and neighboring localities. One of the saddest
was the case of Mrs. Whitehead, of Sugar House
Ward, who on the eve of approaching maternity
accidentally fell into the swollen creek and was
swept down stream and drowned. Her name
stands upon the EXPONENT'S subscription list, but
the day the first number was carried out to Sugar

earth.

﻿DIGNITY OF APPEARANCE.
			   
  It was a worthy thought expressed by a lady of
sense, only a few days ago, in something like the
following words:
  "If a man looks more dignified in a plain black
suit, with white collar and cuffs, than in any other
style of dress (and he certainly does to me), why
should not a woman appear most dignified in a sim-
iIar sort of dress?”
  Sure enough ! Is there so much difference be-
tween man and woman that, while one would look
worse than dandified, perfectly ridiculous, with rib-
bons and laces, and artificials placed upon the per-
son as adorning ornaments, such things can add to
the beauty, grace or dignified appearance of the
other ?
  This verisimilar idea may justly be extended to
the inner being, the soul of man and woman; for
there, if at all, true dignity and nobility must exist,
If it is frippery in man to spend his precious mo-
ments in idle gossip concerning fashion, flirtations,
or other nonsense, is it not vanity in woman to do
likewise? And if it is wise and ennobling in man
to cultivate higher principles of real refinement and
lasting wisdom, must not the same course be judi-
cious and elevating when pursued by woman ?
  This short article is not intended to convey the
the notion that men and women should be clothed
without distinction in many ways, or that it is ne-
cessary for woman to be educated in the arts of war,
and other things that are hardly fit for man to med-
dle with, lest evil come of it. But, as every true
woman is better pleased with a real dignified man
than a fop, it is supposable that every true man
must be more favorably impressed with a genuine
dignified woman than with a butterfly of fashion.
And as a varied display of feathers, flowers, and
fripperies would serve to completely change the
appearance of the one, it seems impossible for the
same things to enhance the appearance of the other.

﻿A PRESIDENTIAL ASPIRANT.

	The “ Fat Contributor” thus humorously

speed:
	The hour has arrived. I can hesitate no
longer. The highest interests of the nation
demand that I present myself as a candidate
for President of the United States. I have
waited for some one else to bring me out,
but in the multitude of candidates no one
seems to have thought of me. And I don’t
think much of myself; but a man don’t
want to be much of himself to be a candidate
for President now a days. If he had any
self-regard at the outset he would think very
little of himself by the time he got through
with the campaign.
	I am one of the people—I might say, one
of the boys. I came up from obscurity, and
I have brought up a good deal of obscurity
with me. I never had any politics—or
much else. I am “ Liberal” to a fault, and
ready to receive votes from any quarter, al-
though I am not ready to give quarters for
votes.
	As for a platform, suit yourself, gentle-
men. The lecture platform would probably
suit me as well as any other. Having stood
upon nearly every platform in the West, it
would be hard for you to get up one I could
not stand on.
	I am the special friend of the laboring
man. No one likes to see a man work bet-
ter than I do. In fact, I had rather see a
man work than work myself. I am not
only averse to working more than eight
hours, but I am opposed to working a single
hour! I shan’t even work for my election,
leaving that for the men who want the of-
fices.
	I am in favor of paying the national debt.
It is in fact the only debt I am in favor of
paying. And rather than not see it paid
during my administration I will pay it out
of my own pocket.
	In the matter of civil service reform I in-
tend to do the civil thing by the nation if
the nation does the civil thing by me. Be-
ing civil is so rare a condition now a days in
the varied walks of life (to say nothing
about the runs) that reform is urgently cal-
led for.
	Retrenchment is my motto. If you can’t
put a retrenchment plank in the platform
put in a board. I am ready to work with-
out any salary, but I shall insist upon my
board.
	I am rather inclined to Free Trade, pre-
ferring to feel free to trade wherever I
please, but if a Tariff plank is necessary to
my election, put it in. I shall not get on a
Tar-iff I ain’t elected.
	Pledge me as strong as you please to the
Temperance men. The temperance pledge
won’t hurt me one bit.
	No relative shall hold office, no matter
whose relation he may be. I shall appoint
none but old bachelors, childless widows,
and orphans. Any man who has a relation
in the world need not apply for an office un-
der my administration. I have a few rela-
tives of my own, holding office now, but they
shall be promptly kicked out as soon as I
am elected. One brother-in-law has a little
coal office on the river. He must give it
up. A third cousin on my neighbor’s side
drinks too much occasionally, and gets office
foot I shall give him notice to quit. An-
other relation has a hankering after George
Eliss’ “Office.” It won’t do him any good.
You see I am determined to reduce the
“relative” expenses of the Government.
[right column]
	I engage not to accept any gift, unless it
be the highest office in the gift of the peo-
ple. If over I am called “ Our present Chief
Magistrate,”-it won’t be a chief magistrate
of presents. Not being a man of command-
ing presence anyhow, there would probably
be few presents that I could command.
What few natural gifts I may have, how-
ever, I shall endeavor to retain. They are
not worth making any fuss about.
	I am not only in favor of woman’s rights,
but of woman’s rights and lefts. I am in
favor of women voting providing they vote
for me. I see no reason why a woman
should not hold an office, except, perhaps,
the difficulty of getting hold of it. Nor
should there be any bar to a woman’s accu-
mulating property and supporting the family
if she wants to.
	I may be asked how I would treat the In-
dians. I wouldn’t “ treat” them at all.. .They
have been treated too much and too often.
My private opinion, however, is that it will
be a treat when there isn’t an Injun left.
	I stand by the old constitution that has
been tried. Few men have tried their con-
stitution more than I have tried mine. I
accept the amendments, every one of them.
When it comes to amends, I am ready to
shout “ amen” as loud as anybody.
	I understand there is an ambitious man
named George Francis Train, who aspires to
be President on his promise to free Ireland.
I engage not only to free Ireland but make
Irish whisky free in the bargain. I shall at
least be able to tie George Francis in the.
popular voto unless' one of us is kept away
from the polls. Hang it! I could tie the
Davenport brothers!
	I shall inaugurate a wholesale emancipa-
tion businesses soon as I am inaugurated. No
goods retailed at the White House when I
am President. I engage to emancipate the
women from the thraldom of fashion, to
give the “ boys” their rights, and abolish
the custom which excludes children in arms
from the elevating and purifying influences
of the theatre. I have pledged myself to
free Ireland and to free postage; to free
housekeepers from the tyranny of servant
girls ; free pews, free passes, and free boot-
ers. To free soil, to free tickets to shows,
free drinks, free press, and “J. N.” Free.
I trust I am not making myself too free.

none but old bachelors, childless widows,
and orphans. Any man who has a relation
in the world need not apply for an office un-
der my administration. I have a few rela-
tives of my own, holding office now, but they
shall be promptly kicked out as soon as I
am elected. One brother-in-law has a little
coal office on the river. He must give it
up.   A third cousin on my neighbor’s side
drinks too much occasionally, and gets office
foot.  I shall give him notice to quit. An-
other relation has a hankering after George
Eliss’ “ Office.” It won’t do him any good.
You see I am determined to reduce the
“relative” expenses of the Government.
[end of column; start of new column]
I engage not to accept any gift, unless it
be the highest office in the gift of the peo-
ple. If over I am called “ Our present Chief
Magistrate,”-it won’t be a chief magistrate
of presents. Not being a man of command-
ing presence anyhow, there would probably
be few presents that I could command.
What few natural gifts I may have, how-
ever, I shall endeavor to retain. They are
not worth making any fuss about.
  I am not only in favor of woman’s rights,
but of woman’s rights and lefts. I am in
favor of women voting providing they vote
for me. I see no reason why a woman
should not hold an office, except, perhaps,
the difficulty of getting hold of it. Nor
should there be any bar to a woman’s accu-
mulating property and supporting the family
if she wants to.
   I may be asked how I would treat the In-
dians. I wouldn’t “ treat” them at all.. .They
have been treated too much and too often.
My private opinion, however, is that it will
be a treat when there isn’t an Injun left.
  I stand by the old constitution that has
been tried. Few men have tried their con-
stitution more than I have tried mine. I
accept the amendments, every one of them.
When it comes to amends, I am ready to
shout “ amen” as loud as anybody.
I understand there is an ambitious man
named George Francis Train, who aspires to
be President on his promise to free Ireland.
I engage not only to free Ireland but make
Irish whisky free in the bargain. I shall at
least be able to tie George Francis in the.
popular vote unless one of us is kept away
from the polls. Hang it! I could tie the
Davenport brothers !
   I shall inaugurate a wholesale emancipa-
tion businesses soon as I am inaugurated. No
goods retailed at the White House when I
am President.    I engage to emancipate the
women from the thraldom of fashion, to
give the “ boys” their rights, and abolish
the custom which excludes children in arms
from the elevating and purifying influences
of the theatre. I have pledged myself to
free Ireland and to free postage; to free
housekeepers from the tyranny of servant
girls ; free pews, free passes, and free boot-
ers. To free soil, to free tickets to shows,
free drinks, free press, and “J. N;” Free.
I trust I am not making myself too free.

escape.  I wish that you may be able to
say :  ‘ General, I have killed so many men
without making a single prisoner.’  My,
opinion is that to hold in check one-half of
the population, it is necessary to kill the
other half. I speak to you in the name of
the national Sovereign.”

	ON AND IN THE WATER.
			
Editor “ Woman’s Exponent :"
	Perhaps a brief notice of our boat excur-
sion and the little adventure we met with,
on Sunday, June 2d, might be somewhat
interesting to a portion, at least, of your
readers. Indeed the adventure itself proved
very interesting, intensely so, to us, before
we got through with it.
Mr. H. was going to start to work on the
railroad on Monday morning, so he wished
us all to go down to his farm to spend Sun-
day and take dinner. he came for us with
his horses and wagon, and all hands went, 
	After dinner it was proposed that we
should have a ride in the boat. Accordingly,
four of us, ladies, and two small children,
seated ourselves in the boat for a ride, and
Mr. H. took the position of oarsman. He
had gallantly rowed twice across the little
lake, or big pond, and we were just starting
out again, when “ the treacherous bark”
capsized and threw us all overboard into the
water. The boat went over the head of one
lady, but Mr. H. succeeded in shoving it off
from her, and out further into the water,
where the two ladies who were in the great-
est danger, being farthest from our hero, (Mr.
H.) could get hold of it; which they did, and
thus kept themselves and the infant they
had with them from sinking. Mr. H. first
gave the little boy he had taken to a lady
on shore; then helped the lady who had
been slightly hurt by the boat, to get on
shore; then myself, and then he went to
the rescue of those who were still clinging
to the boat. Through the mercies of a kind
Providence, we were all saved, and none of
us were seriously injured, not even our pre-
cious children.
	We conclude, though, that Sunday is not
the day for dinners and boat excursions;
and that if we are able to go anywhere on
that day, the proper place to go is to the
house of the Lord, to learn of His goodness
and feast upon His pleasing word.
	True, we had that day a great manifesta-
tion of His long-suffering and marvelous
kindness toward the children of men; yet
it might be looked upon as a warning, I
think. The scene was certainly an exciting
one. Imagine the position for a moment!
Smithfield.	M. G. H.

﻿INTELLIGENCE AND INTEGRITY.
			    
   It seems a pity, sometimes, to see persons who
are highly gifted with a noble portion of intelli-
gence, give way to evil passions, yield to the pur-
suasive voice of temptation, and fall into vice. The
query very often arises, How can they, knowing so
well the difference between the proper and improper
course, act as they do ? But the question is easily
answered.  They lack integrity; the love of truth,
the appreciation of justice and right. Yet, by their
eloquence, their ingenuity, the pleasing display of
their superior intellectual abilities, they often win
friends and obtain favors, where others, possessed
of genuine integrity and trustworthy principles, but
less brilliant in ideas and consequent appearance,
are passed by unnoticed.
   Where both these elements richly abound and
are finely cultivated in one being, we find superior-
ity indeed.  And that superiority all should, and
all may, obtain, by carefully cultivating the funda-
mental parts of these two pre-eminent principles.
For, certain it is, the Lord in His infinite wisdom
and mercy has ordered a seed of each to be planted
in every little perfect human form, that all who de-
sire it, may be both wise and good.			L.

﻿THE RIGHT WAY FOR WOMEN.
			
  A thoughtful female writer has published
the following reflections:—“After toiling
all day, how refreshing to a weary man is
the peaceful quiet of home! A woman
ought to study, in everything, the comfort
of those who are passing their days shut out
from the pleasant green fields and shady
lanes, toiling where the sunbeams cannot
reach them, to cheer their sinking hearts,
in order that she may live in comfort; such
a thought should guide the actions of every
mother, wife, and sister.
    Cleanliness and a judicious expenditure of
funds are most admirable qualifications in a
wife, yet do not constitute entire happiness;
they are only parts of the ingredients em-
ployed in forming domestic happiness.
Many women never think of conversing on
any subject beyond dress and flirtations, so
the father, husband, or brother, goes out in
search of a friend with whom he can discuss
more interesting matters.
   It is a great loss to females not to be able,
on all occasions, to converse on general sub-
jects.  We are happy to state that children
of the working classes are now, in many
towns, amply provided with the means of
securing an excellent education, despite pe-
cuniary deficiency ; an education that will fit
them to hold, with credit, superior situa-
tions. They are taught to think and to rea-
son upon all learned subjects as children, so
that in the days of maturity they may enjoy
and add to the enjoyment of an intelligent
circle of friends.”


﻿THE CORONADO ISLANDS.

	One of the first sights that attracts the
stranger on his arrival here, and one that
the old residents of San Diego never tire
looking at, is the group of islands known as
the Coronados.  In plain view from every
portion of the city, with nothing beyond
them but the waters of the immense Pacific,
they form an attractive and graceful con-
trast to the high hills south and east and the
mesa land north of the town. They are al-
ways beautiful, but are especially so when
the atmosphere is intensely clear, as it was
yesterday.  The first day of May was, even
for a San Diego day, unusually lovely.  Its
pleasant breezes made every one feel cheer-
ful, and drew many into the open air away
from their occupations to sit and gaze with
delight on the islands, which seemed, al-
though miles away, to be within a hundred
yards of the beach, and looked as if nothing
intervened between them and the shore but
a narrow strip of blue water.  But a still
more attractive phase under which the
group of islands can be seen, is when the
atmosphere causes them to change their ap-
pearance, producing the most beautiful mir-
ages imaginable. On Tuesday an opportu-
nity was afforded to observe these atmos-
pheric effects. The rapid changes in the
appearance of the Coronados as seen from
the city on this day were wonderful; and
the shapes were as puzzling and the opin-
ions of observers as conflicting as were those
of Polonius, who saw a camel, weasel and
whale in the same cloud. These curious
mirages are not rare, but may be seen fre-
quently.  Another peculiarity connected
with these islands is that at times they are
not visible from the city, although with the
exception of a slight and almost undiscerni-
ble haze, the day may be perfectly clear,
with not a single cloud in the sky.—“San
Diego Union.”

	The women of Utah to-day occupy a position which
attracts the attention of intelligent thinking men and
women everywhere. They are engaged in the prac-
tical solution of some of the greatest social and moral
problems of the age, and a powerful interest is mani-
fested throughout the United States, and, it may be
said, the entire civilized world, to learn from reliable
sources the views honestly entertained by them on
these questions.

  They have been grossly misrepresented through
the press, by active enemies who permit no opportu-
nity to pass of maligning and slandering them ; and
with but limited opportunity of appealing to the in-
telligence and candor of their fellow countrymen, and
countrywomen in reply.

  Who are so well able to speak for the women of
Utah as the women of Utah themselves ?    "It is bet-
ter to represent ourselves than to be misrepresented
by others!"

other by the diffusion of knowledge and information
possessed by many and suitable to all, the publica-
tion of WOMAN'S EXPONENT, a journal owned by,
controlled by and edited by Utah ladies, has boen
commenced.

subject interesting and valuable to women. It will
contain a brief.and graphic summary of current news
local and general, household hints, educational mat-
ters, articles on health and dress, correspondence,
editorials on leading topics of interest suitable to its
columns and miscellaneous reading.

   It will aim to defend the right, inculcate sound
principles, and disseminate useful knowledge.

matters of interest connected with their workings; 
and to this end the Presidents and Secretaries of the 
various Societies throughout the Territory are re-
quested to furnish communications which.will receive 
due attention.

Relief Societies, cordially approves of the journal,
