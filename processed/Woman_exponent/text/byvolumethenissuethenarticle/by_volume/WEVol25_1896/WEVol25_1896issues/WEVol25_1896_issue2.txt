JUNE. 
The breath of June is in the air, 
And that exquisite fragrance rare, 
That thrills the soul with glad delight, 
And over all the verdant land, 
Beauty hath waved her magic wand. 
And earth is bathed in radiant light. 

The forest songsters tune their lays, 
And thro' the woodland, wilding ways, 
Or fields and meadows, gay with flowr’s, 
Their melody in chorus sweet,
Makes earth seem fairly-land complete, 
A foretaste of fair Eden’s bow’rs.
The June of life again we feel,
And softly o’er our senses steal, 
The calmness, and the sweet repose. 
Rejuvenating all our powers, 
And the glad muse of summer hours,
Within the heart of sunshine glows. 

The sunsets’ blaze of colors bright
Pales to an amethystine light, 
As gently falls the evening dew, 
And mellow moonlight softly sheds,
Its healing balm on aching heads
And stars bespangle heavn’s pale blue. 

Such respite from life’s toil and woe,
Is given us, while here below
As visions, of the world unseen;
To stimulate our lives to bear, 
The ills and griefs, we needs must share, 
Until we reach the heights serene. 

O, how we long to climb those hills,
And quaff from out the sparkling rills, 
The honeyed nectar of our youth;
For there it flows in limpid streams, 
Of which our fancy only dreams, 
Springing from wells of living truth. 

O nights of June! Fairer than all!
From out the silence voices call, 
In echoes from the fountains deep, 
Or from the far-off woods and vales, 
Or distant seas, whose mournful wails, 
Tell where the dead, unnumber’d sleep.
June, 1896 E. B. W.

A CONGRATULATION.

[To my venerated friends Brother and Sister
Horne on the sixtieth anniversary of their
Wedding Day.
EMILY H. WOODMANSEE.
Happy their fate, who truly mate, 
	Who wear the yoke, that love makes light,
Who joyfully can celebrate
	Their marriage day, and count it bright;
But few are favor’d thus to see—
Its sixtieth anniversary.

Exactly, one decade ago,
	Upon your golden wedding day;
Your friends, a happy overflow,
	The old and young, the grave and gay
Met ’neath your sheltering roof—to share
The joy of that occasion rare.

Since then, a host of friends have died,
	And many strange events we’ve seen,
Unlooked for woes have multiplied,
	And life an uphill road has been,
And every one must needs confess,
All things are shaken, more or less.

Whether or no, ’tis so designed
	As Saints believe, and scoffers doubt,
Amid the general strife we find
	That something good, has been wrought out,
And Zion! Once “the trodden down”
	Is gaining favor and renown.

Now, unto Zion, are secured
	Great Rights, withheld, in days gone by—
No longer friendless and obscur’d,
	But like a "lily set on high” 
Or like a bright attractive star,
	Lo! Zion’s light, shines forth afar.

So visible to eyes unshut
	Is God’s own marvelous enterprise;
“The Stone” from out the mountain cut,
	Is rolling on and gaining size,
A Nucleus, gath’ring power apace,
	To fill the earth, and bless the race.

And you my friends, who from the first,
	Dared to espouse the cause of Truth?
Who many times have braved the worst,
	In Truth’s defense, are glad forsooth
To know that come, whatever, may—
	Established Truth, on earth will stay.

Especially you can rejoice,
	That sons and daughters ’round you throng.
That each and all with heart and voice,
	Are helping Zion’s cause along,
Truth’s representatives are they,
Who bear your honored name today.

Albeit, that you’re greatly blest
	Perchance, like other folk you’re tried,
Earth is not yet a place of rest,
	Nor are we all yet sanctified;
And trouble, bolder than before,
Walks in, at nearly every door.

Yet side by side, in woe or weal, 
	Linked hand and heart, along you go,
As happily united still,
	As in youth’s early morning glow
And may you hence enjoy—I pray—
An everlasting wedding day.
May 9, 1896.

REAPERS’ CLUB.
	REPORT of the Reapers’ Club read at the
Stake Federation of Clubs, Salt Lake City, 
May 20th, 1896. By Romania B. Pratt.
The Reapers Club is now in its fourth year
of useful instruction, with a membership
roll of forty-three, and an average atten-
dance of twenty-five. It convenes every
two weeks at the WOMAN’S EXPONENT
parlors, on Monday afternoon. The course
of study is Miscellany, Literature, Political 
Science and Current Events. The club
color is red and the badge is a wheat head. 
	The President and Secretary retire at
each meeting inviting two members to fill
these positions, to promote general education
in presiding and taking notes. Under the
head of Current Literature there have been
written and commented upon about twenty
sketches of the lives and works of noted
novelists, poets, philosophers, divines
theorists, musicians and artists. 
	The later sessions have been devoted to
the Study of Political Science followed by
questions and discussion. 
	The object of this club is social and in-
tellectual development. Its design is to
cultivate the heart as well as the brain
which will form a good mental equilibrium
and fit woman not only to shine as a greater
light around her own hearthstone, but to be
a more efficient custodian of home interests
in the wider domain of the world. 
	Progress is improvement upon the past;
and the study of the lives and works of
eminent men and women who have lived
before us or are contemporary, stimulates
to emulation. 
	The club women of Utah are the high
priestessess at the altar of the home, often
consecrated to plain living but also to high
thinking, where the noblest ideals are cher-
ished and where money is not King. 
	The Reapers Club of busy and earnest
women is as a small streamlet singing a
glad song of pride and thankfulness as it
glides into the larger stream of the State
Federation, and with it sweeps into the
General Federation of Woman’s Clubs,
helping to create a mighty force of womanly
power which will raise the standard of mor-


[Column 1]
als in the world and spiritualize and refine
the material and physical in man and thus
hasten the era of peace on earth and good
will to all men.
	The women of the Reapers’ Club do not
intend to be gleaners only from the good
works of others, but hope through industry,
to leave to posterity a legacy of their own
noble thoughts and improved methods of
life, that it may truly be said of them that
"the world is better for their having lived
in it.” 
	The women of the Reapers Club aim to
grasp the sickle of industry and enter the
fields of science and knowledge to reap and
bind into sheaves, golden truths, with
which to store the granaries of the intellect
as food for thought and action in a daily
progressive life, in all that is helpful and
uplifting to the human race.

paramount in importance to any preceding

	Ladies and Gentlemen: I have the
honor of representing the Utah Woman’s
Press Club. Since making our last report
to this noted organization, we have made
most gratifying progress, having admitted
during the past year many new members
all bright, active workers, ladies of high
literary taste and talents.
	We do not limit our membership, one of
the primary objects of our organization be-
ing to encourage young writers and culti-
vate all their literary possibilities, our hon-
ored president being an adept in bringing
out latent talent. Although many of the
results would not perhaps shine with
Shakespearean brilliancy yet we have
listened to many choice thoughts that
would otherwise never had birth. Our
motto is “The most good to the greatest
number." Our membership not being con-
fined to the environs of Salt Lake City
only, but extending through the domain of
the State of Utah.
	In reviewing our past year’s work one is
surprised with the amount of original work
done by this club, the greater part of every 

[Column 3]
session being devoted to this line of work.
	Our subjects for consideration and dis-
cussion have been historical, biographical,
scientific, poetical and polit cal. We have
debated the “Double money standard for
America,” the “Single tax” question, and
have recently formulated a plan for the
study of Parliamentary Law.
	Our meetings are held but once a month
unless called by the President for the
transaction of business.
	According to our by-laws we elect officers
once a year, no one is permitted to hold an
office longer than two successive years, thus
giving the greater number the opportunity
for the experience.
	During the past year it has been our
privilege to entertain a number of notable
people. In August last the gentlemen of
the local press courteously invited the Wo-
man’s Press Club to assist in entertaining
the Convention of Western editors then
enroute for the Pacific Coast. The Misses
Chase of Massachusetts gave us a glowing
description of their foreign travels in various
parts of the world; also Dr. Sarah J. Elliot
who awakened in our minds many
new ideas by her life-drawn pictures of the
noble self-sacrificing labors of Miss Clara
Barton. Mrs. Laura M. Johns the suffrage
speaker and organizer was also entertained
by us quite recently, and last though far
from least—the officers of the Utah Federa-
tion of Woman’s Clubs, by special invita-
tion honored us with their presence.
	Frequently is our club entertained most
bounteously at the homes of its members,
thus fostering a spirit of sociability which
in our case is the main-spring of love and
unity, this in turn being the true secret of
strength and power.
	In closing we would express as the senti-
ment of the Utah Woman’s Press Club:
The press—the friend of justice and reason,
the best exponent of virtue and a potent
subduer of vice—the promoter of intelli-
gence, knowledge and education. As
Phillips has said: “From the hour printing
was invented the brain and not the arm,
the thinker and not the soldier, books and
not kings, were to rule the world. Weap-
ons forged in the mind, keen-edged and
brighter than the sunbeam were to supplant
the sword and the battleax.”

THE SPRING OF 1896.
WHEN it is evening, ye say: It will be fair weather
	For the sky is red,
And in the morning, it will be foul weather
	Today, for the sky is red and lowering.

March did not come in like a gentle lamb;
	And it left like a roaring lion;
But we expected storms for ’tis well known
	March is not a month to rely on,

But we thought perchance that April,
	The month for shine and showers,
Would gladden earth with warmth enough
	To bring forth waiting flowers.

But we never thought that May time
	Would bring us frost and snow;
For surely if we harvest
	We must have time to sow.

The sun has gone down in splendor,
	The morning brought forth rain,
The clouds have hung low in the morning,
	Ere night we had sunshine again.

The bow of the moon would hold the horn, 
	Yet sunshine has come with the rain;
So what e’er our faith in by-gone years
	We cannot trust the moon again.

The Snipe’s sad cry more rain, more rain
	Has been answered with Meadowlarks thrill;
And in spite of frost, hail and snow,
	The grass and wee blossoms still

Have been growing; and green is showing
	All through the furrowed field:
And yester the bow of promise
	Gave hope for a harvest yield.

We had better be up and doing;
	“Improve the shining hours”
If God wills that we must have rain
	We should work between the showers.
						M. E. T. Griffeth. 
Fairview, Idaho.

LEAVES FROM MY JOURNAL. 
	PARIS, Oct. 13, 1895. A merry party
left Hotel Central for the depot, taking the
first class “Raapide” at 9 a. m. for
Marsailles, going via Dijon, Macon and
Lyons. The train was most comfortable
with conveniences such as we have at home,
but divided into compartments, our party
had two, we had our meals aboard, lunch
and dinner being excellent. We drank
mineral water which I did not like. We
passed some beautiful scenery and saw
many vineyards. Dijon is now the centre
of trade for the wines of Upper Burgundy.
Arrived just at nightfall at Lyons, the
capital of the department, of the Rhone,
situated on the tongue of land, inclosed
between the Rhone and the Saone. We
saw from the windows the lighted city,
the Church of Notre Dame de Tourvieres.
A square which we supposed was that of
Louis le Grand, and the quays along the
Rhone, the lamps were numerous so we
could see very well, and regretted not stop-
ping a day to visit the manufactories and
the Palais de Beaux Arts which contains
interesting Roman Antiquities. 
	At eleven p. m. we arrived at Marsailles
the city was “en fete” the scene very gay
the square was beautiful with lights, flowers
tropical plants and fruits, the people were
singing and laughing and serving refresh-
ments. The soldiers were preparing to sail
for Algiers, on the morrow, and seemed as
happy as if going on a pleasure trip.
	We drove to Grand Hotel de Noailles,
the entrance to court is beautiful with

palms and potted plants, and as we always
find in France, the hotel is most comfortable
the beds and rooms delightfully clean; it is
a pleasure to rest in them. Much fatigued
we all retired. 
	Friday, 14th Oct. Directly after we had
finished our breakfast, Mr. Mc V. ordered
carriages and we drove about the city.
Marsailles, is an ancient city being founded
by the Phoceians 600 B. C., in S. France,
the capital of the department of the months
of the Rhone—on the Meditteranean, and 
on the east coast of the Gulf of Lyons, en-
closed by a succession of rocky hills extend-
ing in the form of a crescent, until each
extremity reaches the sea. The form of
the city is nearly square; it is surrounded by
walls and separated into the Old and New
town by “one of the finest streets in
Europe.” The Old town has mean and
dirty streets—the New, is beautiful and
much like Paris, the Hotel de Ville, is an
architectural beauty—the Ancient Cathedral
is Gothic and said to have been built on the
ruins of a temple of Diana. The public
library is large and there are cabinets of
natural history, botanical gardens, and an
observatory, the Arch erected by Nap. I.
is very beautiful and much like those of
Paris, the Water Castle and fountain is of
extreme beauty and must be seen to be ap-
preciated. Next we drove to the water, the
Port of Marsailles, is called a Salt Water
Lake, and is completely sheltered from all
winds. The entrance does not exceed a
hundred yards, being confined by two pro-
jecting rocks, one on each side; on which
are situated two forts—for its defence, it is
protected by other works and completely
surrounded by quays; in driving on the
street fronting the entrance our guide told
us, when the mistrel wind blows, 'twas
said a battalion of men standing two
abreast could not stand against it; from
here we had our first peep at the Meditter-
ranean, and a magnificent view of the Bay
and Islands—the water was beautifully
blue and not a puff of wind to tell us of the
“Mistral.” On the hill to the south of the
Harbor, is a place of pilgrimage, The
old church has been replaced by a modern
building, Byzantine style, on the facade is
a belfry one hundred and fifty feet high,
surmounted by a statue of the Virgin, one
hundred and forty steps leads to the lower
church, one hundred and seventy four to
the upper—the view repays one for the
climb—the view at the foot is magnificent.

ADDRESS TO RELIEF SOCIETY.

	To the Relief Society of Centerville. I
ask you to pardon me if I may appear to be
obtrusive in the liberty I have taken, by ad-
dressing you in this manner. While at the
Relief Society union today I was impressed
with a very pleasant feeling of pleasure,
which is characteristic of this organization.
It was not only a repetition of what I al-
ways enjoy at these meetings, but while
there I was led to reflect upon the object of
the Relief Society and what it has done, I
think language will fail me, but still
enough can be said, to encourage the sisters
in that noble work. In the language of
scripture, to feed the hungry, clothe the
naked, to nurse and administer to the sick
embraces the highest point of the moral
law that can be expected of humanity; the
fact of which, is so well acknowledged by
all sects, all creeds, and all people whether
Christian or barbarian; and woman, with
her feminine nature and her sympathetic
feelings has so well adapted her for the
position that she has done her work so well
that it really appears to be one of the great
est essentials to human happiness. She has
labored for 6000 years in the good work
and no wonder that the Prophet Joseph
Smith was inspired to organize the Relief
Society, as he saw how much more good
could be done in a united capacity; may
the organization always have that support
that they deserve, and now the franchise
has been given her, it is to be hoped her
new obligations may not lessen her useful
ness in her acts of mercy nor burden her
with life’s cares, that she may not share in
all that pertains to human happiness. I be-
lieve with suffrage, woman will be able to
extend her usefulness in helping to improve
the civil affairs of society in other ways,
beside relieving the needy, and so far as
patronage in civil government she will find
every place for which she i[-] by nature and
qualification adapted.
JOHN WAYMAN.

THE WORLD IN COMMOTION.

	THE world at large was never in greater
commotion than at the present time; even
although desolating wars are not raging so
extensively as at some periods, yet there
are wars of various kinds, of the elements
of earth and air, and of men’s unbridled
passions fearful and terrible calamities,
cyclones, floods, disasters by sea, and on
land, until the very atmosphere seems
freighted heavily with destructive forces of
many kinds. In view of all these things 
one is forced to contemplate causes, and to
look forward to results; to contrast that
which exists today with the history of the
past, and compare it with the outlook for
the future of the world. Verily knowledge
increaseth and wisdom hideth itself, for
“Knowledge is proud that he has learned so much
Wisdom is humble that he knows no more.”
	Looking at the events transpiring around
the world today, one must necessarily feel
that with all the boasted knowledge of this
century of civilization, the hidden paths of
wisdom are obscure, and only found by a
few, who look upward for light and search
after eternal truth, as the prophets of old
and who were despised and scoffed at, but
whose words were revered by later genera-
tions, and if one would profit by such les-
sons he must beseech the Lord in all
humility for guidance.
	The signs of the times warn those who
have studied the Scriptures and believe in
the fulfillment of prophecy not only that
“in the last days perilous times shall come”
but that such perilous times have already
come, and such wide spread destruction
that men’s hearts quake for fear, and it is a
vexation to read of these things, and how
much more so when they are where one
must needs see, and feel them. It certainly
does not seem to be a time of rejoicing,
when notwithstanding the efforts made by
philanthropists and reformers to create an
era of peace, the facts prove that “there is
no peace,” because of the wickedness that
stalks broadcast and must of necessity pro-
voke the vengeance of the Almighty, who
delights not in unrighteousness, and who
will not be mocked.
	The terrible cyclone in St. Louis, the
terrific tornadoes in Texas, in Ohio and in
Kansas, the disastrous fire in Philadelphia
which incurred loss of life as well as
property, the burning of Cripple Creek,
Colorado all these fatalities seem near
enough to strike home to the hearts of all
who are interested in current events. The


political disturbances which have been fer-
menting, and likely to culminate in the dis-
ruption of the old parties, one cannot help
deploring, even though Utah the new star
in the horizon of the Western empire may
remain secure and undisturbed; surely one
cannot help realizing she has come into her
proud estate on the eve of dangerous times
to the nation, of which her people have been
so proud. But the hopeful should look for
a happy outcome from this agitation and
commotion. The women of Utah who
have just come into their political rights,
should be calm and dignified in the midst
of the tumult of ideas and opinions, and ex-
ercise such prudence and caution in times of
disorder and confusion, as will entitle them
to respect at home and admiration from all
who desire to see order come out of chaos.

splendid courage that conquers the world.

grown dusty in our years of conflict. To-
night we shall gather great hope and en-
couragement from their voices.”

	ARMENIA.

	BY PHŒBE C. YOUNG.

	ARMENIA is,according to ancient tradition,
the original seat of one of the oldest civilized
people in the world; they claim
to be descended from Haik; whom they
say was the son of Tagarmah the son of
Gomer, the son of Japheth, the son of
Noah. He according to their tradition
established the Armenian kingdom in the
vicinity of Ararat the mountain on which
the Ark is said to have rested after the
flood; this was done at an uncertain date,
but is supposed to be about two thousand
years before the Christian Era.
	As a race they belong to the Indo
Germanic family of nations. The country
they possess is a high table land on the
southern slope of the Caucasus stretching
down towards Mesopotamia though its
boundaries have been changed many times
during the centuries of its history. Its
area is estimated at ninety thousand square
miles. Their oldest records contain noth-
ing beyond the facts that in ancient time
they were governed by independent kings
and afterwards became tributary to the
Assyrians and Medes,a fact that established
their antiquity as a nation in which it is
difficult to tell where myth ends and
history begins. We learn that about the
middle of the sixth century before Christ a
certain king Dikran of the Haiig dynasty
restored the independence of the kingdom,
the last king of this line was slain in battle
by Alexander the great, who conquered the
country and appointed Governors over it;
it is impossible in the space of one short
article to give even a faint description of the
changes and vicissitudes through which
Armenia passed through the next six hun-
dred years. Its subsequent history
exhibits an unbroken series of tumults and
wars; of violent successions to the throne;
despotic reigns and rapid decay. Subject
to the vigorous assaults of the Parthians
from the East and the more powerful
Romans from the West, “they sometimes
become partly dependent on one nation
and sometimes on the other. While in-
ternally the Nobles often broke through the
restraints of a feeble monarchy and claimed
the privileges of petty kings.”
	In six hundred thirty two the unhappy
country was subject to another form of
despotism under the Arabian Califs and
suffered terribly during their contest with
the Byzantine emperors. The rule of the
Sassanides was marked chiefly by their
sanguinary but unsuccessful attempts to
extirpate Christanity.
	The Byzantine Greeks on one side and
the Persians on the other seemed to regard
Armenia as their lawful or unlawful prey.
	In eight hundred and eighty five Aschod
of an old and powerful Armenian family
ascended the throne with the permission of
the Califs and founded the third Armenian
Dynasty; under this monarch and his suc- 
cessors Armenia was prosperous till the
eleventh century when divisions and inter-
nal strife began to weaken the country;
till at length the Greeks having murdered
the last monarch of the line seized part of the
kingdom, while the Turks and Kurds made
themselves masters of the rest.
	In thirteen hundred seventy four Asia
Minor was conquered by the Egyptian Sul-
tan Schalean, since that time Armenia has
remained subject to the despotism of the
Turks and Persians with the exception of

[Column 2]
the parts won from Persia in the present
century by Russia, and which are better
governed.
	Notwithstanding the political storms and
the persecutions passed through by this
much enduring people they have steadily
persevered their nationality both in its
physical and moral lineaments. They
have preserved their faith, although and
through persecution,oppression and sorrow;
they only possess to day a relic of their
ancient culture; they can still boast a higher
civilization than their conquerors. In con-
sequence of the great disturbances and
religious persecutions of the middle ages,
many of the Armenians sought refuge in
other countries and we find them scattered
over all Asia and Europe; in Hungary, in
Transylvania and Galicia they number over
ten thousand and they are very numerous
in Russia, but most in Asia Minor, in that
neighborhood alone they number over two
hundred thousand. The north east portion
of Armenia, about one third of the whole
was wrested from Persia by Russia in 1828
and is under the rule of the Czar which is to
be preferred to that of the Turk. About
a sixth part to the south east still belongs to
Persia, two thirds of the Armenian area is
Turkish.
	After the war of 1877—78 between
Russia and Turkey the Berlin conference
sanctioned the session of a strip of Armenia
to Russia, and the Sultan of Turkey under-
took to guarantee the Armenians security
against the Circassians and Kurds, also to
report to the European powers the measures
taken for much needed reforms. It is need-
less to say that this promise has been broken
in every particular.
	Christianity was introduced into Armenia
as early as the second century, but not
firmly established till about the third
century. It afterwards became the centre
of a great controversy regarding the two-
fold nature of Christ. Numbers of learned
men flocked to their colleges because of the
spirit of scientific inquiry that early mani-
fested itself among them, to a far greater
extent than in any other of the Eastern
Churches. The Bible was translated into
the Armenian language early in the fifth
century which was no doubt the reason of
the religious fervor that prevailed.
	Great efforts have been made, and great
influence has been brought at different
times to bear, to induce this people to ac-
knowledge the supremacy and infallibility
of the Popes of Rome, such efforts have
always failed, for notwithstanding all
changes the masses of the people have re-
mained true to their own peculiar opinions.
The Armenian Church entertains peculiar
ideas with regard to what is called the
seven sacraments. “They believe that at
baptism one must be sprinkled three times
and as often immersed. That confirmation
must be conjoined with baptism; that the
Lord’s supper must be celebrated with pure
wine and leavened bread, that the bread
must be dipped in the wine before being
handed round; that extreme unction is to
be administered to the Priests alone, and
that not before but after death. It also be-
lieves in the worship of Saints but not in
the doctrine of purgatory; secular Priests
must marry once, but none are at liberty to
marry a second time.”
	As a race the Armenians are remarkable
for their physical beauty and intelligence,
this last is noticable both in their literature
and their great capacity for business; in fact,

[Column 3]
this is so well known that they are employed
by all the leading business houses of Con-
stantinople, whenever men are needed for
positions of trust and dexterity in business.
It has been remarked that however much
the Armenians may be scattered in what-
ever countries they may be found; the
printing office is always a leading feature in
their colonies.
	The Armenian women as a rule are fine-
looking with intelligent faces and womanly
bearing. It is a well known fact that
among the Oriental races the women as they
advance in age become decrepit and lose
every vestige of beauty before we should
consider they had arrived at middle age.
	This is not the case with Armenian wo-
men as they increase in years their intel-
ligence and good looks become more mani-
fest. The history of the Armenians during
the last twenty years has been a series of
suffering,oppression and misrule; subject to
a nation “whose government is said to be a
praise to evil doers and a terror to those
who do well.”
	For notwithstanding the promise of the
Sultan at the treaty of Berlin to prevent
and punish the depredation of the Kurds
and to rectify the abuses of the Government
their condition far from growing better has
been growing steadily worse. We are
naturally led to inquire the causes that have
brought about the existing state of events
that are occuring in Armenia. One of
these reasons seems to be the greater educa-
tion and growing sentiment of independence
and nationality among the Armenians
themselves and “though they desire to lead
a peaceful life, peace is something they are
forbidden to enjoy.”

IN MEMORIAM.

	DIED at her home in Manti, of old age, on
March 4th, 1896.
	Emeline Whiting Cox. Deceased was the
daughter of Elisha Whiting and Sally Hewlitt
who were natives of Vermont, but removed to
Nelson, Portage Co. Ohio, in the early settlement
of that state where the subject of this sketch was
born July 23, 1817
	In 1835 she was married to Frederick Walter
Cox. Sometime after she and her husband em-
braced the Gospel and removed to Far West, Mo.
They endured all the persecutions, hardships and
privations to which the Saints were subjected dur-
ing their residence in that state.
	Brother Cox, being a man of great faith and

[Column 2]
marked ability, was frequently called to perform
missions in different parts of the United States,
consequently his wife was often left to care for
her little ones alone, but as she was a woman of
rare executive ability, and by nature specially
adapted to the care of children, she performed
these duties wisely and well.
	After being driven from Missouri, they settled
in Lima where they remained for nearly six years,
but were finally driven from their home which to-
gether with their stacks of grain out buildings
&c, were burned to the ground before their eyes,
leaving the family almost penniless but glad to
escape with their lives.
	They next settled in Nauvoo, but before a year
had passed were again compelled to move, this
time they took up their line of march towards the
setting sun.
	At the next halting place Mt. Pisgah, Sister
Cox had more than her full share of the sickness
and sorrow which seemed to be the portion of the
Saints during their soiourn at that place.
	Her beloved father and mother and two of her
own little daughters all within the space of a few
weeks followed each other to the grave and while
she was sick—indeed so low that her life was
despaired of, Br. Cox was making the coffins for
these little ones and bearing them in his own
arms to the grave.
	They afterwards removed to Council Bluffs—
where they were called to part with another
child, this time their infant boy.
	In the spring of 1852 Brother Cox and his
family crossed the Missouri River and began their
long wearisome journey across the plains.
	On the banks of the Platte River another little
daughter was born, who lived and prospered in
spite of the unfavorable circumstances attending
her advent.
	On the 4th of October 1852, Sister Cox once
more found a resting place in Manti, where she
has since resided ever helping in a heroic manner
to bear the hardships and burdens incident to a
pioneer life in a hostile Indian country.
	In the spring of 1863 Brother Cox was called to
take a mission to England, in August her last
child a little girl was born, and eighteen months
later Sister Cox was afflicted with a cancer, the
removal of which almost cost her, her life and
the following summer when the baby was two
years old she was called from earth thus adding
another grievous trial which was greatly aggra-
vated by the absence of the husband and father.
Sister Cox was an exemplary wife and mother a
faithful friend, and a conscientious Latter-day
Saint.
	She was a woman of intelligence, and refine-
ment, an agreeable companion with a keen sense
of humor and an inexhaustible fund of anecdote
and reminiscenses of her early life.
	She was the mother of twelve children—eight
of whom survive her—their respect and love for
her was unbounded and of her it can be truly
said “Her children rise up and call her blessed”—
and also that she has fought the good fight, has
kept the faith and has gone to receive the reward
which her long years of trial, toil and sacrifice
entitle her to; her remains were tenderly borne to
their last resting place by her grandsons who hold
her memory in grateful remembrance.
	After life’s fitful fever she sleeps well.

		THE STARLESS CROWN

	"They that turn many to righteouness shall
shine as the stars forever and ever.”—Daniel
XII. 3. 
Wearied and worn with earthly cares, I yielded
	to repose.
And soon, before my raptured sight, a glorious
	vision rose. 
I thought whilst slumbering on my couch in
	midnight’s solemn gloom,
I heard an angel’s silvery voice, and radiance
	fill’d my room. 
A gentle touch awakened me,—a gentle whisper
	said,
“Arise, O sleeper, follow me,” and through the
	air we fled. 
We left the earth so far away that like a speck
	it seemed,
And heavenly glory, calm and pure, across our
	pathway stream’d
Still on we went— my soul was wrapt in silent
	ecstacy;
I wondered what the end would be, what next
	should meet mine eye. 
I knew not how we journey’d thro’ the pathless
	fields of light,
When suddenly a change was wrought and I was
	clothed in white.
We stood before a city’s walls and glorious to 
	behold;
We passed thro’ gates of glistening pearl, o’er
	streets of purest gold;
It needed not the sun by day, the, silver moon
	by night,
The glory of the Lord was there, the Lamb him-
	self its light;
Bright angels paced the shining streets, sweet
	music filled the air,
And white-robed saints with glittering crowns,
	from every land were there,
And some that I had loved on earth, stood with
	them round the throne,
“All worthy is the Lamb,” they sang “the glory
	His alone.”
But fairer far than all beside, I saw my Savior’s
	face;
And as I gazed He smiled on me with wondrous
	love and grace. 
Lowly I bowed before His throne, o’er-joyed that
	I at last‘
Had gained the object of my hopes, that earth at
	length was past. 
And then in solemn tones He said “Where is the
	diadem

[Column 2]
That ought to sparkle on thy brow—adorned
	with many a gem?
I know thou hast believed in Me, and life through
	Me is thine,
But where are all those radiant stars that in thy 
	crown should shine?
Yonder thou seest a glorious throng, and stars on 
	every brow!
For every soul they led to Me they wear a jewel
	now!
And such thy bright reward had been if such had
	been thy deed,
If thou hadst sought some wand’ring feet in paths
	of peace to lead;
I did not mean that thou shouldst tread the way
	of life alone,
But that the clear and shining light which round
	thy foot-steps shone,
Should guide some other weary feet to my bright
	home of rest,
And thus in blessing those around, thou hadst thy-
	self been blest. 

The vision faded from my sight, the voice no 
	longer spake, 
A spell seemed brooding o’er my soul which
	long I feared to break,
And when at last I gazed around in memory’s
	glimmering light,
My spirit fell o’erwhelmed beneath that vision’s
	awful might
I rose and wept with chastened joy that yet I
	dwelt below, 
That yet another hour was mine my faith by
	works to show, 
And yet some sinner I might tell of Jesus’ dying
	love,
And help to lead some weary soul to seek a home
	above.
And now while on the earth I stay, my motto
	this shall be,
“To live no longer to myself but Him who died
	for me!”
And graven on my inmost soul this word of truth
	divine,
They that turn many to the Lord bright as the
	stars shall shine."

ARMENIA.

BY PHOEBE C. YOUNG.
	A PEOPLE of great natural gifts. They
have caught enough of the fire and in-
spiration of the liberty of the nineteenth
century to assert more independence; and
sometimes raise their voices against the
unremiting tyranny and oppression of the
Turkish government to which they are
subject.
	Perhaps they have found the more cour-
age to do this, because they had a right
under the treaty of Berlin to consider
themselves under the protection of the six
great European princes.
	But alas for them that put confidence in
princes; they have been doomed to disap-
pointment and left to suffer and die; for al-
though Great Britian through its embassy
at Constantinople and its consuls in the
interior have constantly remonstrated with

[Column 2]
the Sultan and sought to compel him to
bringing about a better state of affairs, they
have been met with the dogged accusations
and stubborn resistance of the Turks, and
received until lately very little support from
the other powers.
	Another reason for the great trouble ex-
isting is to be found in that great factor of
dissension in the world’s history; religious
fanaticism. The Sultan himself claiming to
be the spiritual as well as the temporal
head of the Mohammedan world; that is to
say a Calif. This has revived the old
spirit of hatred and intolerance between
Mussulmen and Christian and the Turk has
taken up his arms with his ancient motto
that all means are lawful for the extermina-
tion of the Christian. The life of a
Christian being considered of no more
worth than the life of a dog; which is in
their estimation a meritorious act to take.
 	This is an unequal fight. The Mussul-
men holding all the power. The Christians
so much fewer in numbers and almost en-
tirely without arms; arms being forbidden
by the Sultan to the Armenian population.
“The situation throughout the summer of
eighteen eighty four was one bordering
on anarchy,” from every section of the
country came word of the most outragous
treatment by the Turks and Kurds of the
Christian subjects.
	Laws were imposed that in the already
impoverished condition of the country were
simply ravenous and very bitter feeling
existed on every hand. In a detailed state-
ment sent to the English papers by the
Vienna Correspondent of the Daily News,
regarding the treatment of prisoners in
Central Asia Minor, the following state-
ment is made, “Hundreds of them were
cast into prison, stripped of their clothes and
tortured in the most diabolical manner:
women were outraged in the presence of
their husbands and fathers.” An Armenian
at Marsovar was flogged until his back was
raw flesh, to force him to sign a declaration
that certain Americans were plotting with
Armenians an insurrection.
	In the early years of the century the
residents of Constantinople had been ac-
customed to hear the most insulting refer-
ence made to their religion; but under the
influence of Abdul Medjid this diminished
greatly and as a consequence social relations
improved, but during the five years
previous to eighteen seventy four a marked
change was noticed everywhere and an in-
creasing lack of regard for the customs of
the Christians.
	Leading merchants were ordered to open
their shops on Sunday; if they refused they
were punished and told they were disobey-
ing the orders of the Sultan. The officers
would enter a Church during service and
demand anything they might want; and if
requested to wait its close, they would
draw their swords and order the preacher to
stop and most vilely abuse everything most
sacred pertaining to the religion of Christ.
“In viewing the situation we cannot but
receive the impression of a plan officially
laid to crush out the Christian population
to make room for a Moslem population.”


[Column 1]
	We are the more inclined to believe this
from the fact that the Greek villages suf-
fered only in a secondary measure while the
Christian population of Mesopotamia suf-
fered fully as much; and because the Priests
and women were the two noncombative
parties they have suffered the most, because
the Priest represented the detested religion,
he has not only been slain but mutilated
and the sign of the cross cut in his fore-
head by murderous swords; outrages worse
than death have been endured by helpless
women always preceded by the promise
that they would be spared if they would
abjure their faith. And in no instance have
they hesitated to face their double agony
rather than disclaim allegiance to their faith.
It is unnecessary to rehearse the ghastly
terrible and revoltiug details of the totally
unprovoked Massacre of Sassoun. Of the
barbarous cruelties inflicted on the men,
 and the more frightful tortures endured by
the women, in which they suffered a hun-
dred times worse than death through the
brutality and revolting lust of their merci-
less conquerers. We have read all these
horrors in the public prints till our brains
have turned giddy and our hearts faint,
until we are [-]eady to exclaim:
	“In what age do we live? Have we gone
back to the blackness of the dark ages?
Are all the horrors of the inquisition again
revived? Or is the Massacre of Saint
Bartholmew to be re-enacted with added 
horrors before the eyes of all Christendom?”
But no! We are mistaken; we are living

	SOCIAL PURITY.
	FOLLOWING the address a paper on
“Social Purity” was read by Mrs. May P.
Moyle of the Cleofan. Mrs. Moyle treated
the subject exhaustively.
	“The subject upon which I am to address
you is one of such importance that it can-
not be over-estimated. It is a matter for
the deepest and most earnest consideration
of every member of this noble Republic of
ours, for it is the basis upon which must
rest the high civilization toward which we,
as a nation, think we are advancing. If we
consider the subject honestly and dispas-
sionately, we must agree with the writer
who says, that nothing but virtue and intel-
ligence can save a republic from ending in
despotism, corruption and anarchy. Our
most eminent sociologists have arrived at
the conclusion that whatever promotes the
health, happiness and personal welfare of
the greatest number, should be the care of
every individual member of a highly
civilized society. If this precept were car-
ried into practice, what a glory would be
our advancement. The golden ages of
Greece and Rome would be as nothing in
comparison, and Utopia would be a reality
instead of the beautiful dream that it is.
	Instead of seeing the realization of this
ideal, however, we are confronted by what?
A condition of society that in some respects
is simply appalling. We have here not
such object pictures as those living in the
great metropolitan centers of our country,
but what we do see, gives us a faint idea of
the conditions that exist elsewhere. One
of the greatest causes of social impurity is
poverty—bitter, grinding poverty. It
would seem that the first thing to be done
in order to purify society is to improve the
physical conditions of those who form the
lower classes in society. We can all help
in our small way toward the uplifting of
humanity, and I am sure we agree that
social purity should be the objective point
in all our strivings, and is the only basis
upon which can rest moral and physical ad-
vancement. Methinks that purity for the
individual, the family and the nation could
be the battle cry of victory, spreading a
light in the earth that would reach as far
as heaven.
	Parents are under the greatest obligation
to their children. They are born into the
world not of their own volition, and have a
right to expect moral surroundings, and
surroundings cannot be moral unless an
equal standard be maintained.
	Women and children have a right to demand
purity on the part of men. Man must rise
to woman’s standard before he can become
the great and noble being his Master in-
tended he should become. At the very
root of social impurity lies the inequality
forever destroying the source from whence
must come purity and advancement.”

THE OTHER WORLD.

BY HARRIET BEECHER STOWE.
IT lies around us like a cloud, 
	The world we do not see; 
Yet the sweet closing of an eye
 	May bring us there to be.
Its gentle breezes fan our cheeks
  	Amid our worldly cares;
Its gentle voices whisper love,
   	And mingle with our prayers.
Sweet hearts around us throb and beat,
	Sweet helping hands are stirred,
And palpitates the veil between
   	With beatings almost heard.
The silence, awful, sweet and calm,
   	They have no power to break;
For mortal words are not for them
	To utter or partake.
So thin, so soft, so sweet they glide,
	So near to press they seem,
They lull us gently to o[-]r rest, 
	They melt into our dream.
And in the hush of rest they bring, 
	’Tis easy now to see
How lovely and how sweet a pass
	The hour of death may be;
To close the eye and close the ear,
  	Wrapt in a trance of bliss, 
And gently drawn in loving arms,
      	To swoon from that to fhis;
Scarce knowing if we wake or sleep,
	Scarce asking where we are,
To feel all evil sink away,
      	All sorrow and all care;
Sweet souls around us watch us still,
      	Press nearer to our side,
Into our thoughts, into our prayers,
      	With gentle helping glide.
Let death between us be as naught,
      	A dried and vanished stream;
Your joy be the reality,
     	Our suffering life the dream.

HARRIET BEECHER STOWE.

	MRS. HARRIET BEECHER STOWE died
at her home in Forest Street, Hartford,
Wednesday, July 1.
	Mrs. Stowe, the daughter of Lyman
Beecher, was born at Litchfield, Conn.,
June 15, 1811. At an early age she mani-
fested a great eagerness for books, and
read the prosy theological works of her
father’s library with eagerness. The early
years of her life were spent in the intellect-
ual atmosphere of Litchfield, which was a
famous resort for ministers, judges lawyers
and professional men of culture and educa-
tion. When about twelve years of age she
went to Hartford, where her sister, Cath-
erine, had opened a school. While here,
she was known as an absent-minded and
moody young lady, odd in manners and
habits, but a fine scholar exceling especi-
ally in composition writing. In 1832 she
went to Cincinnati, to which place her
father had removed, where they opened a
school. On Jan. 5, 1836, she married Rev.
Calvin Stowe. D. D , professor of Biblical
history in Lane Seminary, over which her
father presided.
 	During Mrs. Stowe’s intervals of leisure
amidst family cares, she contributed to the
periodicals, sketches and short stories but
apart from one or two Sunday school books
she attempted nothing of more importance
until 1850, when her husband having

[Column 2]
accepted the professorship of Natural and
Revealed Religion in Bowdoin College,
Brunswick, Me., she contributed to the
National Era, an anti-slavery paper, pub-
lished at Washington, “Uncle Tom’s
Cabin,” as a serial. When the story was
completed, she long and vainly sought a
publisher for it, until a young Boston
publisher finally consented to bring it out
in 1852. It met with unequaled success,
313,000 copies were sold in the United
States within three years and a half, and
in all, over half a million copies, including
a German edition prepared there. In Great
Britain its sale was enormous. It was
translated into twenty different languages,
including Welsh, Russian, Armenian,
Arabic, Chinese and Japanese there were
fourteen different German and four different
French versions and it was dramatized in
various forms.
  	She subsequently published “A Peep
into Uncle Tom’s Cabin for Children”
(1853); and “The Christian Slave; a
Drama,” founded on “Uncle Tom’sCabin”
(1855). As some of the statements of fact
in the work were denied, Mrs. Stowe
published in 1853, “A Key to Uncle Tom’s 
Cabin,” giving the original facts and state-
ments on which it was based. She visited
Europe in 1853, and the results of her
observations were published in 1854, in
“Sunny Memories of Foreign Lands.”
A little work entitled “Geography For My
Children,” was published in 1855, and the
next year appeared her second anti-slavery
novel “Dred: a Tale of the Dismal
Swamp.” In her subsequent work Mrs.
Stowe often delineated the quaint old
domestic life of New England of fifty or a
hundred years ago.
 	In September, 1869, Mrs. Stowe con-
tributed to the Atlantic Monthly and to
Macmillans Magazine, as in some sort a
reply to Countess Guiccioli’s “Recollec-
tions of Lord Byron,” an article entitled
“The True Story of Lady Byron’s Life.”
This article evoked a storm of literary
criticism almost unprecedented, and the
tempest was by no means allayed by the
publication in 1870 of Mrs’ Stowe’s work,
entitled “Lady Byron Vindicated.” She
was also for some time one of the editors of
Hearth and Home and a frequent con-
tributor to the Atlantic, Independent and
other periodicals.
	A pleasant episode was the observance of
her seventieth birthday by a garden party,
given by the publishers of the Atlantic
Monthly. Governor Claflin tendered the
use of his extensive grounds at Newton, and
a large company of literary men and wo-
men were present.
 	In 1852 her husband was appointed to fill
the chair of Sacred Literature at Andover
(Mass.) Seminary, and she resided there
until 1864, when, owing to failing health,
he resigned and moved to Hartford, Conn.,
where he died Aug. 22, 1886.
 	Mrs. Stowe has since her husband’s
death, lived in Hartford, occasionally pass-
ing her winters in Florida, where she own-
ed an extensive orange grove.

SIXTY YEARS MARRIED.

	THE wedding anniversary of Brother and
Sister Nathan Tanner of Cotton Wood,
was celebrated at the elegant residence of
Mr. and Mrs. F. S. Richards in this City
June 30, 1896. Mrs. Richards, who is the
daughter of these aged veterans, had made
every preparation possible for the comfort
and happiness of her father and mother and
their special old-time friends and relatives
who were near enough to participate in the
happy occasion.
	The spacious parlors were, prettily
decorated and the dining room decorations
were very effective. It was a beautiful
day and the very season of the year for
flowers and fragrance, and the sweet per-
fume of roses and honey suckle with other
varieties of sweet-scented blossoms, lent a
delightful charm to the entertainment.
	Brother and Sister Tanner made a strik-
ing picture of themselves, both are fine
specimens of physical endurance; their
years have apparently set lightly upon
them, though they have passed through
many hardships, having been among the
early pioneers in the West in their younger
married life, and come to the Valley of
Salt Lake in 1847, and helped to colonize
here among the very first who settled these
barren vales.
	President George Q. Cannon and wife, Pres.
Lorenzo Snow and wife, and of the Quorum
of the Apostles Franklin D. Richards,
F. M. Lyman and his aged mother, who is
Brother Tanner’s sister, Patriarch Joseph
Horne and wife who so recently celebrated
their sixtieth wedding day, President
Angus M. Cannon and wife and Counselor
Joseph E. Taylor, Counselor C. W Pen-
rose and wife, Hon. John T. Caine and
wife, George Crismon, wife and daughter,
Mrs. Zina D. H. Young, Mrs. Wyne-
gar, Mrs. Sarah M. Kimball, Mrs. E. B.
Wells, Mr. and Mrs. James Brown and
others. The afternoon was spent in social
converse mostly reminiscences of the early
days in the Church. Brother Tanner is one
of the very few surviving members of the
famous Zion’s camp. There was some
excellent instrumental music by the young
people, and Miss Crismon sang very sweet-
ly some favorite selections which awakened
old memories.
	It is needless to speak of the banquet
given to the honored couple and the
assembled guests, including all the season’s
delicacies and a wedding cake proportion-
ately large and rich for the sixty years.


The evening entertainment was diver-
sified by a panorama of scenes and places
here and elsewhere mainly historical,
thrown upon the wall by means of a magic
lamp of some sort, and which was much
enjoyed by the company. The friends
showered congratulations and good wishes
upon the happy couple, who had already
passed three score years of life together.
May their prosperity and love grow strong-
er and purer as their days lengthen, and
their evening of life be full of the peace
that passeth understanding.


“VOTE WISELY.”

	The political conditions of this country
at the present time are anything but
satisfactory. It is impossible to tell what a
day may bring forth, or what new scheme
may be developed by those who manipulate
the machinery of politics. New parties are
evidently in embryo, and old parties divid-
ing on new lines, or questions that have
arisen within the last few years or even
months. Disturbing elements are at work,
and whether the outcome bodes for good or
ill, one cannot well determine.  Every
man is asking his neighbor what the issues
are likely to produce, and in Utah at least,
men are wondering how the women of the
new state are going to vote, and women are
asking similar questions of men, who have
been voting always, and seemingly are as
much at sea on the live questions of the
time, as the women who have never yet cast
a vote.
	Certainly it behooves every man and wo-
man who can exert an influence or cast a
vote, to stand upon principle and maintain
the right according to conviction and one’s
conscience, and not depend upon one or
two ideas that seem especially important,but
take into account the entire subject and
situation as much as possible it does seem
without parallel that women have come in-
to possession of the privilege of equal suf-
frage at a time when the nation is in such a
state of doubt and uncertainty on such grave
problems as are now imminent, and the
solution of which no man knoweth.
	It seems a harbinger of good too that the
first votes of the women of this new State
should be cast for the highest official in the
Republic. Many men who have lived in
the West and helped to colonize, have
never yet voted for the President of the
United States. This seems an uncommon
privilege given to the women just entering
into this new ‘‘land of promise” so long
waited for and so tediously delayed. It is
a time when every vote should be cast, and
wisely, if one were only sure, but women
should not be too timid and lose their
opportunity; seek wisdom from the Lord,
and He has promised to all those who seek
Him that they shall know. It is im-
portant too that women should register
properly, and see to it that all those who
are not citizens, but who can become so by
being naturalized, take the correct steps neces-
sary that they may have the privilege accord-
ed them under the law of the grand new State.
	It is a matter of congratulation to both
men and women, and one we should be
grateful for and proud of, that on the day
celebrated by all patriotic Americans
the 4th of July 1896, the flag of our country
bore to the breeze the forty-fifth star for
the first time at the Nation’s capital, and on
all public buildings in that great City, as
well as other places, significant of the en-


larged freedom of the people who have
knocked so often and so long for admit-
tance into the great galaxy of States.
	It is a solemn time as well as a time of
extraordinary privilege, for one cannot
contemplate the condition of this nation
under the present outlook without feelings
of sadness, and only to those who trust in
God can there come the hope that is up-
lifting.


KATE FIELD

	THE tidings of the demise of Miss Field
in far off Honolulu, seemed very sad when 
one remembers her brilliant career before
the public for so many years, as well as
her extensive acquaintance here and
abroad. To die alone in a foreign land,
without a loving friend near to say one
tender word, or drop one tear upon the
couch of suffering is saddening. To be
alone in the world, apart from family ties,
seems to us an affliction. Miss Field went
to Honolulu to regain her health, she suf-
fered from overwork and depression,
brought about by financial embarrassments. 
She was an accomplished journalist, and
her paper “Kate Field's Washington” was
a reflex or a mirror of her pronounced
opinions upon all subjects.

RULES AND REGULATIONS.

OF THE UTAH KINDERGARTEN ASSOCIATION.

	ARTICLE I.—Name.
	THE name of this association shall be
"The Utah Kindergarten Association.”
	ARTICLE II.—Object.
	The object of this association shall be to
found and maintain Kindergarten schools
for the benefit of the children of Utah.
	Membership. 
	Section 1. Any person or persons recom-
mended by two members may become a
member of this Association by paying an
annual fee of fifty cents, and conforming to
the rules and regulations of the Associa-
tion.
	ARTICLE III.—Officers.
	The officers of this association shall be
composed of a board of thirty five directors
out of the number shall be elected
a president, two vice-presidents, a
recording secretary, an assistant recording
secretary, a corresponding secretary and a
treasurer. These officers shall be chosen
annually from their own number.
	ARTICLE IV. Duties of Officers.
	The president and officers herein named,
together with the board of directors shall
be authorized to transact all business per-
taining to the association and the schools.
	ARTICLE V. Special Duties.
	Section 1.—The president shall preside at
all meetings and shall have a voice in all
matters connected with the welfare of this
institution. In the absence of the president,
the vice president shall preside and perform
such duties as pertain to the office.
	Recording Secretary.
	Section 2.—The recording secretary or
her assistant shall keep a roll of the mem-
bers and call the same at each meeting re-
cording the presence or absence of each
member and shall also take the minutes of
each meeting, and an account of all money
received, which shall be submitted for cor-
rections or approval and duly recorded.
	Corresponding Secretary And Treasurer.
	Section 3.—It shall be the duty of the
corresponding secretary to conduct the cor-
respondence, of the association, under, the
direction of the president and perform such
other duties as pertain to the office.
	Section 4.—The treasurer shall have
charge of all funds of the association and
shall deposit in such bank as the directors
shall designate, and shall pay all indebted-
ness of the association by checks signed by

LEAVES FROM MY JOURNAL.

	Saturday, 16th, Nov. 1895.—Arrived in
time for Table d’ hotel, at Nice, Hotel
Westminister on the promenade des
Anglais, facing the Sea, with an
“Ascenseur” (elevator.) If anything ever
tried my nerves, it was the “Ascenseur.”
You step in, sit down, in front there is
nothing to keep you from falling headlong,
they touch a button and up you go.
	Where will we land exclaimed Mrs. C—
when for the first time we went sailing up
to the sixth floor. Doubtful—and I felt
weak. Nice the largest and gayest town
on the “Riviera,’’ formerly of the Sardinian
States; but ceded to France with Savoy,
by the King of Sardinia, in 1859, for Mili-
tary services against Austria, is situated on
the mouth of the river Paglion, on the
Mediterranean, is surrounded with a ram-
part and divided into the Old and New
town, the former with crowded and narrow
streets, the latter straight and regular.

[Column 2]
We walked about the town looked into
the jewelry stores, the gems were real,
we did the winter garden and finding there
would be no concert, made them give us
back our money. Mr. Mc V. said perhaps
you think that is gall, but we paid for a
concert advertised outside. The garden is
a beautiful place with reading rooms, where
you are requested not to talk, then you
walk into the grounds, under cover, where
you chat and listen to music.
	The bathing pavilion is something like
that at Saltair, not nearly so large,
nor does it extend out into the
the water so far. We saw gaily dressed
people all along the promenades, it re-
minded me of Cony Island.
	The gardens are just as beautiful as at
Cannes, even the green hedges are covered
with flowers—we saw the blue plumbago,
yellow acacia, white jasmine and climbing
roses, with heliotrope and scarlet geranium
all over the hedges, and the walls at the
Villas and Hotels, were covered with a
framework of foliage and flowers. The
evening was warm, the perfume delightful,
I opened my window and watched the
people pass, until the swish of the waves
made me sleepy, early in the morning, was
awakened by the Fishermen drawing in
their seine.
	At noon we left, with pleasant recollec-
tions of Nice, after leaving the station, a
curve in the road gave us a splendid view of
the ramparts and that part of the city seen
in pictures.
	The journey to Monaco was short, it is
difficult to recall all the various and charm-
ing villas, landscapes, castles and Islands
and most interesting old manesteries, all
along the journey we have the Sea to our
right—to the left the Thames—and in the
background the Alps. While the gardens
contain olive, lemon, orange and magnolia
trees and do not become monotonous as they
differ according to the ideas of the landscape
gardener.
	All too soon we arrive at the capital
town of Monaco—it stands on a rocky
promontory in the Mediterranean, eight
miles from Nice, it was fortified at the time 
of Louis XIV.
	Monaco, is a small principality of Italy— 
between the province of Nice and Genoa,
governed by a prince. Everywhere are
pleasant rambles, our object was Monte
Carlo—a short walk from Monaco, it is
picturesquely situated on a prominent rock
overlooking the Sea, with the Maritime
Alps, in the back
	Everything is beautifully clean, no where, 
not even at the station did we see a speck
of dirt. We did not take the “Ascenseur”
up to the Casino, prefered walking up the
flight of steps, which were covered with
flowers and plants from all climes; the
grounds are fine and the promenades extend
around the rocky point, with fine views
which ever way you turn.
	Then I went into the threatre the music 
is of the best—one piece was Paderewski
Minuet, rendered exactly as composed.
	Just at evening we walked upon the ter-
race, looking to our right was a dazzling
view of Monaco, all the lamps were lighted
around the promontory and along the shore, 
looking, as Mrs. B. remarked, like a neck-
lace of diamonds, they were sparkling and
bright, while back of us over the moun-
tains hung clouds. We sauntered slowly,

OBITUARIES.

	DIED April 14, 1896, in Castle Dale, Emery Co.
Utah, at her residence, Sister Annette Johnson
Larson at the age of fifty four years, nine months
and one day.
	Deceased was born in Sweden on the thirteenth
day of July, 1841. Received the gospel at the
age of fifteen and emigrated to Utah in 1861;
lived in Brigham Young’s family for some time,
after which she went to Spring City, Sanpete Co.,
on a visit to her sisters who resided there, while
there she became acquainted with Elder Chris-
tian G. Larsen and in 1864 was married to him in
Salt Lake City. She ever proved to be a faith-
ful wife and loving mother. She was blessed
with six children, two of whom have passed away
before her; the other four were at her beside in
her last moments and still live to mourn her loss.
	Her life has been an eventful one. She having
borne testimony of the truth of the Gospel to
many in her native country as well as in Zion.
For many years she was President of the Primary
Association in this place and as such won the
love and respect of all the children under her
charge; but owing to her failing health she was
forced to resign her office and for the last two
years was confined a great deal of the time to her
room, and much of the time to her bed. During
all her sickness and affliction she never mur-
mured, but bore her troubles bravely. Through
the mercy of the Lord she was able to go to the
Temple at Manti nearly two years ago and there
received all the blessings that can be obtained in
the house of the Lord in connection with her
husband and family. Thus she is fully prepared


to go into the presence of our Heavenly
Father with a record to be proud of.
	On account of her being a faithful member of
the Relief Society of Castle Dale, we the mem-
bers of said society
	Resolve, to mourn her loss and sympathize
with her grief stricken family.

	DIED at her residence in Castle Dale Emery Co.
Utah, Nov. 18, 1895, Sister Annie E. Seely, wife
Justus Wellington Seely.
	Sister Seeley met her death by being thrown
from a vehicle while on her way to Orangeville.
The horses became fractious and the driver
Brother W. L. Cash unable to control them; they
ran away throwing both from the vehicle break-
ing Brother Cash’s foot and caused fatal injuries
to Sister Seely from which she died the same
evening. She leaves a husband and ten children
to mourn her loss. Sister Seely was one of the
pioneer women of this valley as also one who has
lived and labored among the Ute Indians in
Thistle Valley making many friends among those
natives of America. For many years past she
has been a faithful member of the Relief Society
of Castle Dale as also first counselor in the Y. L.
M. I. Association. Has also offiiciated as the
President of the Primary Association of this
place and at the time of her death was an officer
in the Woman Suffrage Association. In all these
callings she perfermed a good work and has ever
been gentle and kind to all.
	Therefore be it resolved that we the members
of the Relief Society of Castle Dale do
sympathise with her bereaved husband and
family.
	Resolved that this obituary be placed upon the
records of the society and a copy be sent to the
WOMAN'S EXPONENT.
MARIE OLSEN, President.
NATHALIE ANDERSEN, Sec.
Castle Dale Emery Co. Utah, May 1896

A BRILLIANT WEDDING.

	AN extremely enjoyable event was the
wedding reception of Mr. and Mrs. Henry
Slaughter which took place at Parowan
Iron Co., July 6. The sweet bride has
been Co. Sec. of the W. S. A. ever since its
organization, faithful and untiring, so
immediately after the family reception a
grand ball was tendered the young couple
by the W. S. A. of this place, all members
through the county were invited. The
ball was given in our beautiful new social
hall, which was neatly decorated with the
national and suffrage colors as well as a
profusion of fragrant flowers, the wedding
bell was conspicuous with the motto “Joy


and Peace” on a large yellow banner,
which brightly reigned throughout the
entertainment. Ice cream and cake was
served to 135 appreciative guests.
	Of all the officers of the County not one
could be missed more than our “Ella”
beside being our efficient Secretary, she has
been an earnest worker in every way that
she could to forward our cause.
	We fully realize our loss, but are happy
in knowing that Beaver (where the bride
makes her future home) will have added to
her corps from our ranks, a noble promoter
of all good causes.
	Mrs. Pheobe Evans, a suffrage member
and worker, was married to Mr. Frank
Westbrook the day of the ball, although
they intended it to be a quiet affair we dis-
covered the fact in time to divide the hon-
ors with them.
	May unbounded joy and prosperity ever
attend these worthy pairs is the fervent
prayer of,
E. CRANE WATSON,
President Iron Co. W. S. A.


LOVINGLY INSCRIBED.

[To My Friend Alice, on her Fortieth Birthday
April 3rd, 1896.]
DEAR Friend, you say you are growing old,
	That today completes your two-score years;
Dost grieve! Nay, rejoice, each milestone passed
	Counts thee one less in this vale of tears.

Has Time, with his hoary fingers, wrought
	Silvery threads, ’mong thy darker hair,
Traced lightly faint lines, which, year by year
	Will deepen to furrows, and dost thou care?

Time laughs and mocks us at every change,
	From youth’s sweet spring e’en to wintry age;
Faded cheeks, dimmed vision, the stiffened limb,
	And kindred ills that fill up life’s page.

Let him laugh and enjoy his frolics,
	Shall not we, at the end of life’s race,
Throw off this marred, outer cov’ring, when,
	Lo, a soul, which he cannot deface.

This immortal soul will ne’er grow old,
	Age adds lustre instead of decay,
Our bodies of dust, to dust returns.
	Our spirits live and progress for aye.

Soon this school we call life will be o’er,
	All its sorrows and burdens laid down,
Our education on earth complete,
	And life’s cross be exchanged for a crown.

And though our lessons today seem hard,
	Only by work can we reach the goal;
Only those who are tried come forth as gold;
	’Tis suffring that sanctifies the soul.

The uncut stone is of little worth;
	Tempered with care is the choicest steel;
So our lives are made the more sublime,
	By the tears we shed and the woes we feel.

Let Father “Do what seemeth Him good;
	As clay in the hands of the potter be,
When moulded and fashioned fit for use,
	He’ll appoint thy place in eternity.

The discipline that each soul requires,
	As meted out by a loving hand;
When fully developed, thy call will be;
	Daughter return to thy Fatherland.
								NELLIE.

SURPRISE PARTY.

	At 2 o,clock June 22nd, the members of
the Relief Society met at the ward meeting
house, to do honor to their beloved sister
and Secretary, Sarah Eggersen Cluff, who
has recently removed to the 2nd Ward.
After opening exercises Pres. M. M. Wat-
son expressed her appreciation for Sister
Cluff and the good work she has been
enabled to accomplish while residing in the
4th Ward. Sister Watson was followed by
Stake President, Mary John, Counselor
Marilla Daniels, Secretary, Caroline Daniels
Mary M. Boyden, Caroline Pratt, Cor.
Secretary, Barbara Silck, Hannah Arrow-
Smith, Sarah Baker, Mattie Keeler and
Brother J. E. Booth, who all testified to
the estimable qualities of our sister. By
special request Bro. S. E. Eggersen and
Sister Fannie S. Taylor recited. The
exercises were varied by congregational
singing. President M. M. Watson in an
appropriate speech presented Sister Cluff
with an autograph album. Sister Cluff
expressed her gratitude in a few well chosen
words, after which all retired to an adjoin-
ing room, and feasted on strawberries, ice
cream and cake.
	All join in wishing our dear sister much
joy and happiness in her future career.
A FRIEND.
Provo 4th Ward Relief Society.
