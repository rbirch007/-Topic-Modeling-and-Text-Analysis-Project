EDITORIAL NOTES.

	ONE STRANGE fact in relation to literature
is, that a number of English writers have
gained reputations in America before re-
ceiving any attention at home.

	NATURALISTS HAVE lately decided that
crocodiles are voiceless. The young, it has
been ascertained, are endowed with a bleat-
ing voice, but the adults are utterly power-
less, so far as utterance is concerned. They
may be tortured to death without giving
forth a sound. This phenomenon is sup-
posed to be due to the gradual hardening of
the ligaments of the larynx and of the
whole internal structure of the mouth as
they advance in age. A good remedy for
the afflictions of some men who find such
great annoyances in the tongues of women,
and think they should not speak, would be
to reside a while with a family of croco-
diles.

	A LITTLE four year old girl, of Indiana,
named Rosa Mansfield Eversole, is reported
to be a musical prodigy which eclipses all
others ever known. Several meritorious
pieces of music from her compositions have
already been published in musical papers;
and after once hearing an operatic selection
on the piano she is able to repeat it. Will
she be wisely induced by her friends to let
music alone as much as possible, until she is
older and stronger, when she may become a
brilliant and powerful attraction to the mu-
sical world; or will the little heart and brain
be encouraged to hoard and bear up load
after load of melodious treasures, until they
sink and break down under too great a pres-
sure? Most likely the latter. It is usually
so.


HYGIENIC NOTES.
		
     TO REMOVE SUBSTANCES FROM THE EYE.
—To remove foreign bodies from the eye-
lid, take hold of the upper eyelid, near its
angles, with the index finger and thumb of
each hand. Draw it gently forward, and as
low down as possible over the lower eyelid,
and retain it in this position for about a
minute, taking care to prevent the tears
flowing out. When, at the end of this time,
you allow the eyelid to resume its place, a
flood of tears washes out the foreign body,
which will be found adhering to, or near to
the lower lid.

     THE WEAR OF THE BRAIN.—The notion
that those who work only with their brains
need less food than those who labor with
their hands, has been the cause of untold
mischief. Students and literary men have
often been the victims of a slow starvation
from their ignorance of the fact that mental
labor causes as much waste of tissue as an
equal amount of muscular labor. The im-
portance of the brain as a working organ is
shown by the amount of blood it receives,
which is proportionately greater than that of
any other part of the body. One-fifth of the
blood goes to the brain, though its average
weight is only one-fortieth of the weight of
the body. This fact alone would be suffi-
cient to prove that brain-workers need more
food and better food than mechanics and


farm laborers.—“Boston Journal of Chem-
istry."

     DIETARY IDIOSYNCRSIES.—We see our
friends freely indulging, with benefit instead
of harm, in kinds of food which experience
too painfully assures us we can eat only with
certain injury. To this fact the attention of
parents and guardians should be seriously
given, that by it they may learn to avoid
the petty tyranny and folly of insisting on
children eating food for which they mani-
fest repugnance. It is too common to treat
the child’s repugnance as mere caprice, to
condemn it as “stuff and nonsense” when
he refuses to eat fat, or eggs, or certain
vegetables and “wholesome” puddings.
Now, even a caprice in such matters should
not be altogether slighted, especially when
it takes the form of refusal; because this
caprice is nothing less than the expression
of a particular and temporary state of his
organism, which we should do wrong to dis-
regard. And whenever a refusal is con-
stant, it indicates a positive unfitness in the
food. Only gross ignorance of physiology,
an ignorance unhappily too widely spread,
can argue that because a certain article is
wholesome to many, it must necessarily be
wholesome to all. Each individual organ-
ism is specifically different from every
other. However much it may resemble
others, it necessarily in some points differs
from them, and the amount of these differ-
ences is often considerable.


A TESTIMONY.

     To my Sisters, women of the Latter-day
Saints:—I feel that my whole heart is in this
Kingdom, and my soul rejoices in the Uni-
ted Order that is being established among
this people. I think the time approaches
which was spoken of by Malachi the Pro-
phet.—“Then shall ye return and discern
between the righteous and the wicked, be-
tween him that serveth God and him that
serveth Him not.” My prayer is, that we,
as handmaids of the Lord, shall not prove,
in any instance a draw-back in this great
work of establishing God’s Kingdom upon
the earth. Let us pray continually that
judgment may be given to the Saints, that
they may be enabled to take the kingdom
and possess it forever. I can bear testimony
that this uniting together of the people is
of God; for in His mercy and by His Holy
Spirit, He showed me the Order, more than
twenty-seven years ago. The winter after
we left Nauvoo, when we were stopped at
Council Bluffs, and were destitute of even
daily bread, when our men had to go to
Missouri and work for a little corn and ba-
con to keep their families alive, while the
women would stay at home and pray and
fast—the latter they were sometimes obliged
to do, I can speak for one at least—then we
trusted in the Lord—we had nothing else
to trust in. One day I got so faint and weak
I had to lie down on the bed, and my heart
was drawn out in prayer and supplication to
my Heavenly Father. I began to think how
many good things there were in the world,
how many people lived in pride and luxury,
while others were famishing for the com-
monest necessaries of life. And the vision


of my mind was opened, and the Lord
showed me the time when the Saints should
inherit the earth, when the Spirit of the
Lord should be poured out upon them, and
the Kingdom and the greatness of the King-
dom would be given to them and they
should possess it forever. This has often
been a great consolation to me since that
time, in days of poverty and distress. And
in the darkest hours I have never doubted
that the Lord would yet do for His Saints
what He then showed me He would do.
But I will tell you further of His goodness
to me at that time. How true it is that He
never forsakes those who trust in him!
There was a good Samaritan at hand; a sis-
ter living about three quarters of a mile
from me was impressed to come and see how
I was getting along, knowing that I was
alone with my little ones. In obedience to
the impulse she came bringing with her a
prairie chicken which they had caught in a
trap. It was all dressed ready for cooking,
and she was not long in making me some
broth, just what my weak and delicate state
needed. And did I not thank my Heavenly
Father and that dear sister for their good-
ness to me? Can I doubt that God’s care
has been over His Saints, or that He will
cease to care for them?		S. K. G.


For the EXPONENT.
		MRS. WOODHULL.

     She is most decidedly a splendid speaker.
Listening to her lecture of May 12th, given
in the Salt Lake Theatre, was to me a grand
treat, so far as eloquence was concerned.
Then, she evidently speaks what she has to
say in all sincerity, believing that her state-
ments ALL, as many of them most certainly
are, ponderous truths. Her correct showing
of the general ignorance of woman, coming
as it does from a woman, and spoken to
women, should assist in rousing, many to a
sense of the importance of their making
earnest endeavors to become woman worthy
of the names of wives and mothers.
     The face of Mrs. W. as seen through an
opera glass, tells that she is a women of sor-
row, and acquainted with grief. And listen-
ing to her appealing tones and touching ex-
planations, after she has thrown aside her
notes, and begins to speak upon the impulse
of the moment, one can feel that at some
time in her life she has known deep wrongs
which have embittered what must have
been a beautiful soul. Then arises before
the mind's eye a picture of French women
when they gather in mobs for vengance;
and the thought occurs, “if they had such a
woman to lead them, what works of ruin
would they leave behind them!” And if
the American nation is in the state she rep-
resents it, a vast body of corruption, who
can tell when the seething mass may arise
and devastate this whole nation? Reigns of
terror may not be confined to France.

For the EXPONENT
		WOMAN'S RIGHTS.

	A woman's truest empire is her home;
Nor should she idly from its precints roam;
Nor in her neighbors seek some fault to find;
But strive her own affairs alone to mind.
	
	High as a good man's place will always be.
As high is a true woman's destiny.
Quietly working at the side of man,
Both striving to do all the good they can.

	'Tis woman's right to lead in virtue's way
The opening mind, from childhood's earliest day.
Teaching to ardent and aspiring youth,
Self sacrifice, pure love, unshaken truth.

	With loving zeal to charm the cares of age,
The pangs of want and the sickness to a[-]suage:
To be a comforter when trials pain,
In sorrow and adversity, sustain.

	To seek the homes where striken sisters lie,
To teach the trembling sinner how to live or die;
Eager and glad each holy work to aid,
To share the burdens upon others laid.

	By gentle words win erring hearts from wrong,
And show what power to Woman does belong.
Nor turning from small deeds which must be done,
And by which must of lasting good is won.

	May we each strive our duties to fulfill,
With earnest purpose and determined will;
Helpmeet for man, not rivals, should we prove;
Our empire home, our "Rights" maintained by love.
CENTREVILLE.						      A. H.

	From HYRUM, CACHE CO. May 13th, 1874.
	On Saturday, the 9th inst., the Relief So-
ciety of this Ward held its sixth Annual
meeting. Although the day was stormy,
there was a good attendance. The Presi-
dent, Mrs. Christina Liljenquist, after con-
gratulating the Society on the arrival of its
Sixth Anniversary, spoke upon their past
labors. They had been one in the past,
there had been nothing but peace and har-
mony among them, and the result was, that
God had abundantly blessed their humble 
endeavors to do good. All the officers had
been diligent and faithful in the discharge 
of their duties, as the report of the year
would show. The Secretary, Mrs. M. M.
Nielson then read the financial statement.
The books showed that there had been re-
ceived $226.65, and that a goodly portion of
the amount had been disbursed to aid the
sick, the poor, and to assist in other good
works. The report was unanimously ac-
cepted. Addresses were then delivered by
Mrs. Lucy Wilson and Martha Ward, (Coun-
selors,) also by several of the Teachers.
Each speaker congratulated the Society upon
its improvements, and the good feeling that
had existed among its members during the
past six years, which was something to be
proud of, and was a strong proof that nearly
two hundred women, associated together in
the Gospel of Jesus Christ for the mutual
benefit of themselves and all within their
influence, were not only capable of intelli-
gently exercising the right of franchise
that had been conferred on them, but also
of filling woman's mission on earth, as 
Mothers, Wives and Daughters, dispensing
aid and comfort to all who were in need.
The standing officers were unanimously sus-
tained for the ensuing year. The EXPO-
NENT was well spoken of, and arrangements
were made for an increased list for the com-
ing Volume. Elders C. C. Shaw and H. H.
Petersen addressed the meeting, encourag-
ing the ladies in their good works. The
Sunday School Choir, under the direction of
Professor H. H. Peterson, added greatly to
the interest of the meeting by their sweet
singing. The Society has three shares of
ten dollars each in the Hyrum Branch of Z.
C. M. I. Bishop Liljenquist has just re-
turned from Salt Lake City, having pur-
chased a Library which will be managed by
the Society. Hoping you will pardon me
for trespassing on your valuable space, and
praying God to bless you in your enterprise
which has already done much good among
your sisters, I remain yours very respect-
fully,			HARRIET A. SHAW.

FLORAL HINTS.	

DEAR EXPONENT:—
	Your appreciated remembrance I noted
last evening, as I sit down after the day’s
toil to read all the good things you carefully
store up like honey in the comb, for the en-
lightenment and elevation of us “poor
downtrodden.”	
    To-day has been a charming one and I
have enjoyed it, lingering among the roses,
breathing fragrance of the lillies and honey-
suckles, admiring the curious markings,
shades and colors of pansies, pinks, verbe-
nas and sweet peas; and even stooping to
cull the scarlet strawberry from out its close,
nestling, verdant foliage. The roses are all
aglow in rarest beauty, of every hue from
pure white to crimson and yellow; and in
such masses—such wonderful abundance,
that the name of “Rose Cottage” might not
be inappropriate for our humble, vine clad
home.	
      The subject of flowers is so apropos to my
feelings, now that we are in the midst of
their beauty and fragrance, that I thought I
would respond to your suggestion, in May
first. So as soon as I had got the children
off to Sunday School and Willie “sleeked
up,” I hunted out my writing things and
began.
    I should have told you at the first that our
Willie is most a year old, has seven teeth,
can say “ma, pa, up,” eat strawberries like
anything, tear a rose to pieces in less than
two minutes and—we DO think—is just the
smartest, prettiest, “blue-eyedest” boy ever
seen.— But—Oh yes! the flowers. Well, a
lady called yesterday to see our garden.
When she came in sight of the roses, lilies,
peonies and such like brilliant flowers, she
stopped, raised her hands and shouted, “O
dear!!,” and then stood in almost mute ad-
miration. Finally she asked, “Why can-
not I, who love a garden and flowers so
much, have them to make my heart glad?”
   Said I,  “My dear woman, you can as well
as not.” 
    “But,” said she, “I don’t know how to
take care of them.” 
    Said I, “A child can learn, and so can
you.”	
    Then she said, “I don’t get time.”
Said I, “It don’t take much time. A wo- 
man may have a nice flower garden for
twenty minutes daily, in the Summer; and
this you can save when “Mrs. Gad-a-bout”
calls. Take her out to see the garden, then
you go to work while she repeats her gos-
sip or errand.
     “Well,” said, she, “I never thought of
that. I might really, but I’ve got no seeds,
and don’t know how to plant them.” 
    Said I, “I have nicest of seeds, some I
raised and some I sent to the States for. I
can let you have all you want.”
     “But,” said she, “How can I pay you?”
     “O never mind” said I, “I'll take it in
butter when the cow comes in; or you may
make Willie his first pants.” (I think of
putting him in pants next month. Don’t
you think he will look delightful? Little
man!)
     “Well,” said she, “thank you, I’ll do it.
Or I’ll send Betsy up some day when you	
have a big washing.”
      “All right,” said I. 
      “But I don’t know' what sort of seeds I
want,” said she. 
    I’ll tell you what kind I like best,” said I,
“and which are easiest raised.”
    "And you'll tell me how to plant them," 
said she. 
	"Certainly," said I, "and come down and


see them, and show you how to take care of
them."
	And so we walked on to see the flowers.
	"What is this big bunch of tall buds and
leaves, flowers opening red, white and
pink?" enquired my visitor.
	"Peonies," said I. "You must get a root
in fall or spring, and if you take care of it, in
two or three years it will be as nice as this.
And that tall spike of curious shaped flowers
is the snap-dragon or antirrhinum—blossoms
the second year and keeps on for years—is
easily raised and very showy. So is the
canterbury belle over there, just now about
to bloom. Those gay flowers are sweet peas,
which I planted early in the fall; and you
see they bloom so early and look deliciously
fresh and lovely."
	"Well," asked my apreciative visitor,
after admiring all the flowers in bloom,
"what are all these little plants along the
borders?"
	"These," I answered, "are the sorts of 
posies I shall select you seeds of. These
are balsams, that mass is phlox, those double
zinnias and chrysanthemums, this is a bed
of China pinks and these my corner poppies.
All these are easily grown and will make a 
charming display for annuals."
	So we went and selected the seeds, add-
ing to those above named, dwarf convolvu-
lus, nasturtian, verbenas, scarlet cypress,
portulacca, &c.
	"Now," said she, "tell me how to plant
them." 
	Said I, "You must scald the cypress seed,
the portulacca you must not cover more
than a quarter of an inch deep, and the
poppy about the same. The others may be
covered half an inch deep. Keep the
ground damp until the plants are up. It
should first be well spaded and pulverized;
then spread over it about three inches thick-
ness of old, well rotted, fine manure and spade
again. Then if the ground is clayey ground,
put on about three inches of sand and spade 
all up again."
	"O dear that'll make one's back ache,"
observed my visitor.
	"But you must be a little cute," said I,
"and when you present your husband or
son with a new shirt of pair of socks or
pants, then put in for your flower garden. 
Or if they like custard pie or anything else,
particularly, fix it up, whatever the men
folks like best, and then intercede for the
spading and preparation of your flower gar-
den. And it will be done if you manage
right, as a woman. Then, I continued,
make little drills, sow your seeds and cover
the ground with an old piece of carpet until
the plants begin to come up. Then remove
it and keep them properly moist until
they shade the ground. If too thick, trans-
plant some of them to other places where
you wish flowers to grow."
	"Well," said she, "there's work in this,
and in fact I never had the knack of getting
things I didn't work for, as some people can.
However, I like your directions and will go
and follow them." And she went off smil-
ing.
	Now any body may do as well as my lady
caller, and have flowers plenty. Don't you
think so? I'm sure I took much pleasure in 
telling her all I could to help her; and I'd
tell and help a hundred others with the same
pleasure. So, if any of your readers should
want farther advice, now or at any time,
just let them ask and Flora will respond,
and—Hark! Willie is waking, dear little
one!						FLORA.
	St. George, May 10th 1874.

FROM THE SANDWICH ISLANDS.

	Through the politeness of Mrs. E. Goddard,
we are permitted to publish the following 
extracts from a very interesting letter:

 	LAIE, OAHU, HAWAIIAN ISLANDS,
						April 26th, 1874.
   *   *   *   *   The weather of the past few
days has been extremely warm. Our Con-
ference passed off in a very spirited man-
ner, the white breathren having a good
flow of the spirit of the Gospel, and the
natives seeming to partake of the same in-
fluence. *  *  *  This is a strange people,
many of them will acknowledge their faults
to-day and seem to repent, and to-morrow
commit the same sins over again. But
when they would do right, evil is ever be-
fore them to tempt them from the paths of
virtue. We cannot be surprised at this,
when we take into consideration the life of
idleness and profligacy they led for so many
years before the Gospel found them. And
it did not find them any too soon, for the
seeds of death are wide spread among them,
and it will only be by the redeeming power
of the Gospel that a remnant will be saved.
	We had the honor of entertaining the
King of the Islands last Wednesday, in
company with about two hundred ladies
and gentlemen of high social and political
rank; on their way round the Island. They
started from Honolula on Monday and de-
sign making the round trip by Saturday
night. You can imagine my feelings when
on Tuesday afternoon a message came in-
forming us of his intention to stop here
Wednesday, and his desire to see all the
people on the Island. I acknowledge
that I did not know how to commence to
prepare for such a reception. Living, as we
do, so far from Honolula, our only source of
supplies, as to render it impossible to send
there for want of time, we were obliged to
make the best of what we had. And after
it was all over, I had proved the saying to
be true, “Where there’s a will there’s a
way.” I went to work with all the faith I
could get, and provided food upon which
sixty or more persons dined, and then there
was some left. We entertained all of his
Majesty's particular friends at the house;
the rest were provided for at the meeting
house, where they had all the beef, pork,
fish, poi and sweet potatoes they could eat.
Brother Mitchell painted two mottoes; one,
"Welcome KALAKANA to Laie," was placed
where he first came upon the land; the
other, “Long live the King,” at the en-
trance of the big gate a short distance from
the house. Each formed an arch and was
ornamented with fern leaves and vines.
At half past eleven, his Majesty rode up
with the whole company dressed in uniform;
the gentlemen in red over shirts and black
pantaloons, and the ladies in short red jack-
ets and black dresses. They were all upon
horseback except the Governor of Oahu and
his lady, his Majesty's sister Mrs. Cleghorn,
and two other ladies; these occupied three
buggies. It was a grand sight, the morn-
ing was beautiful and the good people of
Laie had put on their “best bib and tucker”
and assembled in our yard. On the approach
of the party they formed in two lines from
the gate to the house. At the former place
the King and Queen alighted and were met
by Brother Mitchell who welcomed them to
our humble accommodations and accompa-
nied them to the house. I could not have
believed that the presence of the King of
a semi-barbarous people could produce so
solemn and grand an effect. He walked
with uncovered head, saluting his people as
he passed them. His conduct was noble and
manly, his manners, entirely free from af-
fectation, were cordial and easy. He pre-
sented in person his friends to us; after
which he walked among the crowd and
shook hands, it seemed to me, with every-
body, and was extremely sociable with all.
The choir and school children sang for him,
and he delivered a short address, express-
ing his high appreciation of the prosperous
appearance of the people of Laie; said he
was deeply impressed with the sentiments
expressed in Brother Mitchell's prayer,
(which was offered at his solicitation.) he
had heard a great deal about the people of
Laie and had come in person to see them;
the sentiments of their shepherd were such
as he wished them to observe; he was aston-
ished to see the number of children, he had
never seen so many for the number of
grown people in any district he had visited.
His Majesty was followed by his Excellency
Governor Noa of Kanai, a real clever look-
ing old gentlemen, who was perfectly de-
lighted with all he saw. The speechifying
closed with a short one from Brother Mitch-
ell who seemed fully charged for the occa-
sion. He explained some of the objects of
our being here, which were to teach the
people the principles of life, how to improve
their social condition to be industrious and
self-sustaining; that they, being a branch
of the house of Israel, the Lord would
gather a remnant of them into His fold.
He blest the King and told him that if he
would aid in the good work of redeeeming
his people, his reign should be filled with
peace, prosperity and longevity. The spirit
of the Lord manifestly rested upon us all.
The King said he was greatly impressed
with what had been spoken, and we have 
since heard of his high appreciation of it.
Dinner being ready they partook of our
hospitality to their hearts content and de-
parted with a “God speed” to all.
	Yesterday, Bros. M. B. Young, B. String-
ham, and W. H. Branch, left for the fields
of labor appointed them at our late Confer-
ence. Bro. Y. goes to Maui, Bro. S. to
Kanai, and Bro. B. to Hawaii. They will
be separated from us and from each other
until next Conference. They are brave fel-
lows, full of faith and good aspirations.
God bless them!	*	*	*	*	*
	Brother Mitchell's labors are such that he
never rests a moment it seems to me. But
he is where duty calls, his duties being so
numerous that time for needful recreation
he has none. He tells me to do as the Na-

OUR THIRD BIRTHDAY.

	As we enter upon our third year, there is an
agreeable charm to us, in reviewing the labors
of the past two years, in which we have realized
much satisfaction and, comparatively, little dis-
appointment. So far, the WOMAN'S EXPONENT
of Salt Lake City has unquestionably reached
the object of its designers, even more fully than
their brightest hopes could at first have led
them to anticipate. It has greatly assisted, by
the influence which it has created and sus-
tained, meager though it may appear, in arous-
ing thousands of women, in the midst of the
Latter-day Saints, to a new sense of the obli-
gations resting upon them as daughters, sisters,
wives and mothers; as social beings, as citizens,
as WOMEN. Many who might never have be-
lieved themselves capable of writing for the
press, or scarcely of forming in their own minds,
ideas which could be of public interest, and
who could find no time even for reading, are
becoming happily familiarized with these pro-
fitable means of intellectual culture. We have
received numerous little "gems" for publica-
tion, accompanied with the apology, "It is my
first attempt." In reading such, a feeling of
sympathetic pride that the effort has been made,
and a realization of the diffidence with which it
has been forwarded is always present with us,


and we hope, for the author's sake, it may not
be the last.
	We would urge our Sisters to continue their
reading, thinking and writing; for these exer-
cises, properly pursued, are true methods of ac-
quiring and communicating intelligence. And
because a woman is a wife and mother, with
home and family cares to claim the larger por-
tion of her time and attention, is no reason
why the privilege of improving her mental
faculties should be withheld from her; or that
she should voluntarily lay it aside for things of
less importance to herself and to future genera-
tions.	
	In contemplating the vastness of the future
extending before the women of Utah, we would
impress upon them, so far as we have the power
of doing so, the necessity of THEIR being dili-
gent, enterprising and above all United in the
noble works which they will be called to per-
form, to assist in the regeneration of mankind.
	And for their EXPONENT, inasmuch as it has
made nearly as many friends as acquaintances
during its humble career so far—as little cause
for regrets have occured in the events of its
past life, and as its present prospects are favor-
able considering the universal cry of "hard
times," we cheerfully and hopefully prognosti-
cate for it a lively and useful future; thanking
God and our many friends for past blessings
and favors and trusting to the same powers for
encouragement and aid in times to come.

A PETITION.

FOR THE SUPPRESSION OF THE LIQUOR TRAFFIC.
	The following petition which was presented
to the Mayor and City Council, on the 19th ult.,
was read, and received by that body, and a mo-


tion was passed to grant, as far as the laws will
admit, the solicitations therein offered.

"To the Mayor and City Council of Salt Lake
			City.

	"Gentlemen.—You will all admit the fact
that the present traffic in spirituous liquor is
demoralizing to society. Through those influ-
ences which are connected with drinking sa-
loons the unsuspecting, and especially the
young, are exposed to and acquire pernicious
habits greatly to the detriment of the rising
generation. From causes which need not be
herein stated, this evil has been introduced
into our midst, and its increase has been, from
year to year, fearfully apparent.
	"To you, City Fathers, we look and fervently
appeal for the remedy against this growing evil,
this blighting traffic, destructive to both soul
and body.
	"However large the revenue your treasury
may receive from the issuing of licenses for
this polluting trade, we ask, what is that, in
comparison with the high consideration of
moral worth and manly, Godlike intellect now
being drawn into nets rife with poisonous influ-
ences? Viewing it as a pestilential disease,
which demands a speedy and powerful check,
we, the undersigned women of Salt Lake City,
do herein earnestly petition your honorable
board that you will not license the sale of in-
toxicating drink.
	"In appealing to you we feel confident that
we are pleading with men of upright conscien-
ces; men of intelligence and sound judgment;
thoughtful, reflecting and considerate men,
who regard the welfare of society; men to
whom words need not be multiplied, and to
whom, therefore, we cheerfully and respect-
fully submit this petition.
	"SALT LAKE CITY,
						"May 17th, 1874."

of which this step has been taken.

IMMORTALITY.

FROM THE "DESERET NEWS," DEC. 1865.

	Yes, Immortality: That bosom word,
To me, has inspiration in it. Love
Of life, is innate in the human soul:
'Tis interwoven in our natures. 'Twas
Decreed in the grand council of the Gods,
When canvassing the great eternal scheme
Concerning destinies of man and earth,
That mankind should inherit love of life;
Else, man, grown weary of a world of woes
And fickle tides of happiness, would haste
To make his exit, and e'en God Himself
Had failed to keep enough, as instruments,
On earth, to execute His purposes.

	Thus death, the happy counterpoise to life,
Has long been branded with fell hideousness—
False-styled "the king of terrors," 'monster' fiend,"
"Insatiate archer" and whole catalogues
Of horrid names; to form a barrier
Of fear, lest man, with suicidal hand.
Should clip the brittle thread of life and rush
   In multitudes, into eternity.

	"Christ came, them to redeem who thro' the fear
Of death, were all their life time subject to
Its bondage." To the faithful Saints of God,
Who live to do his will, death has no sting;
'Tis a kind porter to conduct us where
A realm of light and beauty shines around—
A world of glorious Immortality!
A World? Yes, worlds of vast immensity.
And what of us? We'll be our very selves,
Free from all imperfections consequent
Upon the curse, entailed thro' Adams fall:
T' enjoy life's sweet associations, such
As parents, children, husbands, wives and friends—
With Gods and Goddesses—with the noblesse
Of all eternities, and freely bask
In full, bright sunbeams of intelligence,
With legal access to its mighty fount—
A life divested of mortality,
Yet life as real as existence here.

	I've had a taste of mortal suffering:
I've seen my fellows drink its cup fill'd to
The brim and running o'er, until the pulse
Of life was clogg'd in every wheel—until
Nature's deep agonies outweigh'd the love
Of life, and yet the throbbing pulse beat on.
But, thanks to God, there is an end decreed
To human sorrow, pain and misery.
I aim—I live for Immortality,
Life, knowledge, bliss, without one stopping point.

	A thought that I should ever cease to be,
Would paralyze all other thought; would chill
The brightest beams of joy, and would crush out
Each holy aspiration of the heart—
Eradicate that precious organ, hope—
Embargo enterprize, and dry up all
The tributary streams of happiness.

	There's nothing short of Immortality,
Can satisfy the earnest cravings of
That spark of pure divinity, which God
Implanted, as the fine, constituent part
Of beings, organized with attributes.
Like His—the germ of an eternal life:
	
	Crown of all wisdom, sum of good to man,
Scheme of the God's, redemption's glorious plan;
This, through the resurrection's power combines
Immortal bodies and immortal minds.
								E. R. Snow.


HOUSEHOLD HINTS.

	Good Hints for Mothers.—Affection often fails
to attain the best ends, through unwise direction.
There was one known as a "devoted mother"—
untiring, patient, faithful, and wishing to do her
duty. But she failed to see that "many women
are admirable house keepers, but not home-makers."
When her children were quite small, she was, dili-
gently caring for their bodily needs, and forgot the
demands to be made by and through "the many
sidedness" of the immortal nature. She neglected
society, and so, for the want of the necessary
stimulus, grew careless in her dress, manners, and
speech; Having, as she said, no time to spare, she
gave up books and papers, until she had little
knowledge of anything outside her own home-
world, which she was unconsciously making piti-
fully narrow. What was the consequence? Her
family grew up with many natural gifts; but no
﻿grace of manner, taste in dress, or general literary
culture. As they went out into the world, they
began to blame their mother for being “old fash-
ioned,” and to lament that some of the money so
carefully hoarded “to give them a start in life,”
had not been spent earlier in making home pleasant,
and in cultivating a taste for books and music.
Correct language at home would, by the power of
example, have done more for them than all the
tedious rules studied at school. Simpler fare at
table, with the hospitality affording more social
intercourse, would have gained them life-long friends
and an ease of manner which after years could
never supply. A wiser economy of time would
have led the mother occasionally into the school-
room, to show a hearty co-operation with the teach-
ers and sympathy with the children in their work
there. Perhaps these children grew up to be
Christians, and accomplished much; but they
might have filled a larger sphere, and been nobler
in character, with a different training.

	Recipe for making good yeast.—Boil a handful
of hops and eight good-sized potatoes, pared in
one gallon of water. The hops should be tied
loosely in a rag. When the potatoes are done
mash them thoroughly or grate them, first pouring
the liquor into another vessel. Pour the liquor
over the potatoes again when mashed, adding one-
half cup of sugar and one-fourth cup of salt. Let
it boil a minute but stir to prevent sticking. Use
no flour, as it is more liable to sour when flour is
used. Pour off into an earthen vessel to cool, and
when nearly cold add one cup of good yeast. In
a few hours it is ready for use: This yeast will
keep sweet one month in the hottest weather, if
covered closely and placed in a cool cellar, and it
makes splendid bread if properly managed. Bread
should not be salted where this yeast is used. If
prefered, this yeast or part of it, may be made in-
to cakes and dried which will last six months—
though it is better to make fresh as often as once
in three months—by adding fresh corn meal until
a stiff dough is obtained. Work into a smooth
roll and slice with a knife into cakes one-half inch
thick. Dry in the open air but shaded from the
sun.

	Another.—Boil a pint bowl full of hops in two
gallons of water, strain and add a teacupful of
flour, one of brown sugar, a teaspoonful of salt.
No yeast is required to raise it. Let it stand
three days in a warm place, and it will then begin
to foam. Then boil three pounds of potatoes,
mash fine and add them to the yeast, and stir the
whole well together; then put into a jug and cork
tight and set in a cool place. It should be made
at least two weeks before using, and it will keep
any length of time, and grow better all the while.
A small teacupful is sufficient for six loaves of
bread. When this is gone, make a new jug full in
the same way, and keep it corked tight, and you
never need go to the baker’s or brewer’s for yeast.
Since writing the above, my wife opened a large
bottle full of this yeast, that had been sealed and
put in the cellar for more than a year, and the
usual quantity raised her bread splendidly.—Ru-
ral New Yorker.

	Salt and Milk Rising Bread.—Take half a tea-
spoonful of salt to a pint of warm water and stir
in flour enough to make a thick batter. The dish
used, which with the spoon should be thoroughly
cleansed and scalded, should be a little more than
two-thirds full, and set in a warm place until the
batter rises, so as to fill it. The most common
way is to put the rising in warm water and set it
where it will keep at quite a warm temperature;
but some use cloth or paper to exclude the cool air
instead of placing in water. The time in which
the rising will sufficiently ferment is usually from
five to seven hours, according to the degree of
warmth and the kind of flour used. When “light,”
take a quantity of flour corresponding to the
amount of bread desired and stir in scalding (not
boiling) water enough to scald one-third, then add
cold water (or milk, which is better,) enough to
[right column]
cool the scalded flour so that it will not scald the
rising; put in the rising and stir vigorously until
you have a thick batter and put in a warm place to 
rise. When light and puffy stir as thick as pos-
sible with a spoon, then mix with the hands until
kneadable, cut off in loaves of a size to half fill
your baking tins, knead thoroughly, shape to, and
put in your tins and again put in a warm place to 
rise. When the loaves have doubled in size put
in a moderately heated oven and bake from one-
half to three-quarters of an hour according to the
size of the loaves, and you will have bread as
moist, spongy and eatable as fermented bread can
be. Bread of unbolted flour can be made in the
same way, but is better stirred to a stiff batter
and baked in deep dishes instead of being kneaded
and baked in shallow tins. Milk rising and salt
rising bread are the same with the exception that
one half a teacup of new milk is added to the ris-
ing for the former and is preferable to all water.
As you value good bread and good health, never
put ginger, saleratus, or any of the other useless
things that some people deem necessary, in your
sponge or rising.

WOMAN'S VOICE.

DEAR EXPONENT:—
     I have long thought of writing a few of
my thoughts for your consideration, but
have never found courage to begin until
now, and I do not know how I will succeed,
even after waiting so long. I take much
pleasure in reading the EXPONENT; and
feel impressed to bear testimony through it
to the truth of this great work in which we
are engaged. For I know it is true. I was
much pleased with an article by Blanche
Beechwood,entitled “Our Daughters,” print-
ed in your columns some weeks ago. Many
of us do not realize the responsibilities that
are resting upon us, as mothers in Israel.
Do we not allow ourselves sometimes, to
make remarks in the presence of our
daughters, that are likely to prejudice their
minds against that one great and glorious
principle, Plurality? A principle which we
know is calculated to exalt us to a greater
glory than we would otherwise attain. My
dear sisters, let us strive to look on the
bright side, and not let our minds become
clouded; seek to cultivate a spirit of meek-
ness and cheerfulness in submitting to all
that is good. And if our husbands do not
always do just as WE think they should do,
let us remember they are mortal like our-
selves, and liable to err; therefore, instead 
of finding fault with them, let us look about
us, and see if we are not to blame; have we
not done or said something that deserves
the treatment we receive? If we find we
are not to blame, then in all sincerity we
can pray for them; and the Lord will hear
our supplications if we go to Him with the
right feeling. I know these principles to be
correct by experience; I do not guess at
what I write. I have lived in plurality
thirteen years, and my faith in that princi-
ple is stronger, much stronger than it was
in the beginning. Would I be released
from it if I could? Not for worlds! My
prayer is continually that I may hold out
faithful to the end, and gain a salvation with
my husband and the rest of his family.

A WOMAN'S DEFENSE.

   A lady writer thus communicates to the
“Santa Barbara Index”.
   My husband is a specimen of the Ameri-
can nobility, brimful of patriotism and
Websterian sentiment; and, of course, a
great believer in, and admirer of, the Ameri-
can Press.	
   The other evening he was reading aloud
the news of the week. Among the rest
was an eloquent article upon the extrava-
gance of American women. 
   The writer of the aforesaid article de-
clares that the main cause of our present
hard times is the excessive importation of
foreign goods, to the neglect of our own
manufactures. The earnest expressions of
the writer, and the evident discouragement
of my good husband at this shocking state
of affairs, really alarmed me. Now, in the
seven years of our wedded life, it has been
my first care to be truly a helpmeet for him.
So,in the loving spirit of self-sacrifice,I said:
“My dear Dick, if it please you, we won’t
use anything imported for a year, and see
how we get on.” With a grateful look, and
the smile which I like so well, he ex-
claimed:
   “Just like my own little wife! if every
woman in the land were so loyal to its in-
terests, the country would indeed be safe.”
   Now, I pride myself on being practical,
so I proposed beginning in the morning.
“Let me see. No coffee, no tea, no choco-
late! How is that Dick?”
   “We’ll except the coffee, wife; you know
it is the main part of my breakfast.”
   Morning came. When I set the table for
breakfast, I put on the brown table-cover, be-
cause my white table-linen is of Irish manu-
facture. Of course, there were no napkins;
but, then, it is well to be self denying in
small things.
   I had the very spirit of July Fourth, and
so drank none of the amber-colored fluid in
which Dick delighted, and my glass of wa-
ter madq him look rather uncomfortable,
but he said nothing. True to the instincts
of our “business man,” he took out his
watch. “Dick,” said I, softly, “English
chronometer.”	
   “Bless you! so it is. Please except that.”
Up to the store he went, and with his pleas-
ant “Good morning,” he added, as usual,
“Any commissions?”
   “No, dear, I am coming up by and by.”
   After making the house tidy, and seeing
sundry dinner duties done, I went up town
to my husband’s store, Now, in our small
town, every merchant has general merchan-
dise, from millinery to molasses. And our
family supplies are usually from the store
As I entered, Dick was showing stockings
and gloves to some ladies. “These are genu-
ine Alexandre’s, imported from Paris, the
latest style! And these, ladies, are the very
best English hosiery.” So he went on.
And he sold the articles too; for they were
really worth the price asked for them. Then
I produced my memoranda: “Pins, thread,
currants, raisins, citron, and cinnamon.”
   “Have you American pins?”
   “O yes,” said Dick; “but they are quite
inferior to the English article.”
   “Now, Dick, you know I’m to be a loyal
American woman; so give me those of our
home manufacture and the American Coates’
thread if you please.”
   “How about the American pudding mate-
rials?” queried Dick, with a serio-comic
face. “Please except them, wife; you know
the pudding is the crownng article at din-
ner.” Of course I excepted them; for was
not Dick’s mother coming over to dine with
us to-morrow, and accompany us to the
grand party of the winter? On my way
home, I met Dick’s sister—about my age.
“What shall you wear to the party?” was
her first question. Now, I had just made
up a handsome silk—a Christmas present
from Dick—and had intended to wear it.
Even the full ruffle of real Valenciennes
lace; and the new three-button gloves, and
the stylish French gaiters were all ready.
But it occurred to me that all these were
foreign frippery, such as was ruining our
country: and I cast about for some other ar-,
rangements, to the great disgust of Dick’s
sister who asserted that she “was out of all
patience with me.” I confess I did want
to wear them; but what true wife prefers
her own adornment to the desire of pleas-
ing her husband?
   Dinner-time came, and with it came Dick.
His face was all aglow with delight, over
the acquisition of a splendid looking horse,
which he called me out to see and admire.
“A real English thorough bred,brought over
by one of my friends.”
   “What you Dick, buying an IMPORTED
horse!”
   The question was out; before I realized
how bad it would sound. Just like fault-
finding, for all the world! Poor Dick! his
enthusiasm was gone. Dinner was a very
silent meal in comparison with our usually
cheerful time and I wasn’t sorry to see him
go. I put on my best new Merrimac print,
and fastened my plain linen color, after
many blunt points of my new pins had tried
my patience. There was a pile of work
ready for the sewing machine.  I adjusted
my new thread and began its cheerful click.
What was the matter? My thread broke,
knotted, unwound in several different
places at once. Resolutely keeping my
temper, in earnest devotion to home manu-
factures, I tried again and again; but had to
give up at last, rather than ruin my gar-
ments before they were made. The morrow
was fuller of trials and mortifications. My
best table-linen, and my best China were
from Dresden. My dinner was a success,
and was praised by Dick’s mother; but the
last remnant of my loyalty went utterly to
the winds when Dick whispered, “Mother
hopes your new dress is stylish, and that
your Elizabethan ruff is au fait.”
   His very words imported!
   And now, Mr. Editor, while my husband
is smoking his choice Havana cigar, I en-
treat you to save and protect us from the un-
sparing critics of the day. No doubt there
are a few hundred butterflies among us, who
are only brown-coated wrens, easily satis-
fied at a moderate cost, and doing the ser-
vice of our own households! Can you not
defend us from such dreadful charged?

	In reproving a friend, we should act
cautiously and gently, not to offend or
wound, and it always has a better effect
when the reproof is given privately. How
bitterly people may be wounded by having
their faults paraded before others, and their
peculiar weaknesses betrayed by, perhaps,
some loved one. I have seen children of
sensitive natures droop under this unneces-
sary punishment. I remember when our
Indian boy was quite a little one, I happened
to speak of a bad habit he had, to a neigh-
bor, not thinking he would notice me.
Looking around I saw his eyes full of tears
and his poor little lip quivering. My
heart condemned me in a moment, and that
was a good lesson to me.
	In watching my children, and trying to
cultivate the good traits of character and
check the evil, as manifested so diversely
in their natures, I have always found it
best, to encourage them to think themselves
capable of doing good; and to lean towards
the good side—the “bright side of the pic-
ture,” rather than to be always ready to
portray its defects. Here is an old maxim
which I think is nearer right, and will
work much better than the one quoted
above:
		"Be to his faults a little blind,
		And to his virtue very kind ”

	SUSTAINING THE EXPONENT.
We must do something more in relation
to printing. The Woman’s Relief Society
are publishing a paper called the WOMAN'S 
EXPONENT, which is a very ably edited
sheet, and one containing a great deal of in-
formation. I am surprised that all the gen-
tlemen in the Territory do not take it. I
invite all the elders, bishops, and presiding
officers in the stakes of Zion, on their re-
turn home, setting the example themselves,
to solicit all their brethren, and especially
the sisters, to become subscribers of this
little sheet, for I am sure that they will be
interested in the instruction and informa-
tion it contains. I will say that we expect
in a short time, through the patronage of
the brethren and sisters, that the ladies will
be able to enlarge this paper, and to extend
its influence far and wide.
		POWER OF THE LADIES.
   It has been my privilege to make visits
to, and to become acquainted with the Ladies’
Relief Societies in many of the settlements
in the Territory, and I am convinced that
great good results from the labors of these
organizations; and I feel certain that unless
the ladies take hold of any movement de-
signed to forward the work of the Lord in
the last days, its progress will be tardy. In
all parts of the world, when nations are at
war, unless the women take an interest in
the matter, the war goes on very heavily.
I am of the opinion that in the next war be-
tween France and Germany, the French will
get the best of it. Not but what I have a
great opinion of German skill, energy and
pluck, but I am satisfied, from traveling
and personal observation, that the women
of France are thoroughly aroused, and that
in the next war between those two nations,
the Prussians will have to fight the women
of France, and then France will be likely
to win.
	ADVICE TO RELIEF SOCIETIES.
     I say to our sisters of the Relief Societies,
be encouraged, meet together and discuss
all questions that are calculated to interest
or benefit the community, as you have the
ability; and as no man can be elected to
office in this Territory without the vote of
the ladies, make yourselves thoroughly ac-
quainted not only with the politics of the
country, but with every principle of local
government that may be advanced, and
then, whatever is calculated to benefit the
people in their private or domestic circles,
you will be enabled to vote intelligently
and to carry it through without difficulty.
	    WOMEN'S INFLUENCE. 
    We spend a great deal of our money in
following vain fashions, and in purchasing a
great many articles that are useless. These 
societies, if they choose, can make their
own fashions, and they can make them ac-
cording to wisdom, and so as to promote
health; a great many of the fashions of the
world are calculated to destroy health. A
hundred questions connected with domestic 
economy—housekeeping, cooking, making
bread and kindred subjects, that are of im-
portance to the stomach, health and longev-
ity of every man and woman in the Terri-
tory may be properly discussed in these Re-
lief Societies, and useful information dis-
seminated. A great many of the women
in these valleys have not had good oppor-
tunities to become acquainted with the art
of cooking, and that is an art which has
something to do with every person’s hap-
piness. The example of the ladies, and the
influence which they exercise, have a ten-
dency, above all things else, to maintain,
create, and preserve good morals. Men are
apt to behave themselves in the society of
women, and if women act wisely and pru-
dently in guiding and controlling the course
and conduct of each other, they will be able,
to a great extent, to guide, control, and regu-
late the morals and conduct of men. We
think, however, that the policy of the
Christian world, in throwing the responsi-
bility, so far as morality is concerned, en-
tirely upon the heads of women, is a blun-
der; the men should be held responsible for
their own acts, and when they are guilty
of that which is corrupt, low, or degrading,
they should be looked upon as transgressors
and cast aside until, by repentance and up-
rightness, they prove that they are worthy
of confidence.

For the Exponent.
			WHAT IS LIFE?
	All matter is either organic or inorganic.
We call those organic bodies which are made
up of parts, which, however diversified in
themselves, are mutually adapted and mu-
tually dependent, and each of them capable 
of performing a certain function, or action.
A wax flower has its various parts, mutually
adapted and mutually dependent, but neith-
er in their individual or combined state can
these parts perform any vital function. They
have no principle of life, and without vitali-
ty there can be no organization, since all or-
ganization implies a living arrangement of
elements. The sculptor may cut a block of
marble into the form and fashion of a man;
but when finished, it is after all, only inert
matter in an elegant form. In one word,
wherever we find the living principle in
connection with matter, there we have or-
ganization in vital arrangement. In the
absence of this principle we have nothing
but a dead mass. To the question, "What is
life?" we can give back no answer. That 
there is such a thing as life, or vital action,
cannot be denied, and the only thing which


we can say with certainty concerning it, is,
that life is that which admits of develop-
ment. We go out to the garden with a peb-
ble and a flower seed in our hand. We com-
mit them both to the soil, and thus subject
them to the same agencies and influences.
The seed will burst, and germinate, and de-
velop itself into a beautiful and fragrant
flower; while the pebble may lie there for
years on years without undergoing the least

	Of the California State Woman's Suffrage
			Association.
	The "New North West" of May 22nd, contains
an interesting account of the fifth anniversary
meeting of the Woman's Suffrage Association
of California. During four sessions which were
held, speeches were made, resolutions formed
and correspondence read, all of an animated
and earnest character. The President of the
State Board of Control, Mrs. M. A. Lewis, re-
ported that in the past year, thirty-six meetings
had been held by the Board, and that it had is-
sued and presented four circulars which con-
tained more than ten thousand pages, and had
written two hundred and fifty-one letters, of
which, one hundred and fifteen had been an-
swered. Mrs. Lewis also congratulated the So-
ciety upon the passage of the Educational Bill
by the Legislature, making women eligible to
every position of authority in the school depart-
ment of the State. The Bill referred to had
been passed in response to a lengthy petition,
containing over five thousand signatures which
was forwarded by the Board.
	The women of Utah have occasion to again
thank the California Woman Suffragists for in-
terposing in behalf of their rights which are
still threatened. At the first session of the
anniversary meeting, the committee on Reso-
lutions presented the following, which was read
and adopted.
	Whereas, The Legislative department of
the Territory of Utah several years since prac-
tically demonstrated its appreciation of and
confidence in the principles of political equal-
ity and self-government, as enunciated by the
founders of the American Republic, by confer-
ring the elective franchise upon the women
within its jurisdiction; and,
	Whereas, The general deportment of the
newly enfranchised class in Utah and Wyo-
ming has been so characterized by quiet, order
and respect for public authority as to attract the
attention and challenge the commendation of
high public officials; and,
	Whereas, There are several bills now before
the Federal Congress, embracing those of Sena-
tors Frelinghuysen, Logan and Sargent and
Representative McKee, each of which, while
aiming to suppress polygamy in Utah, provides,
with the exception of that of Mr. Sargent, to
remand the women back to political subjuga-
tion; therefore,
	Resolved, That the California W. S. Associa-
tion, at its Fifth Anniversary, in convention
assembled, on this 29th day of April, 1874, here-
by records its solemn protest against this crafty
effort on the part of the enemies of Human
Rights to induce the Congress of the United 
States to perform the despotic work of disfran-
chising the women of an entire commonwealth,
thereby, under the specious pretense of promo-
ting public morals and womanly purity, pro-
posing to inflict a punishment upon the polyga-
mic wives of Utah to which only felons should
be subjected, while inflicting no punishment
and imposing no restraint upon polygamous
husbands.
	Resolved, That the Board of Control is re-
quested to prepare and forward to Congress a
memorial asking that Body to protect the wo-
men of Utah in the enjoyment of all the rights
of self-government conferred upon them by the
Legislative department of that Territory, and
that a copy of these resolutions be forwarded
to each Senator and Representative in the Na-
tional Congress from the Pacific Coast, who are
earnestly requested to use their influence to de-
feat any bill which abridges or denies the right
of Suffrage to any person or class on account of
sex.

﻿HOUSEHOLD HINTS.
First Element of a Home.—I never saw a gar-
ment too fine for man or maid; there never was a
chair too good for a cobbler or cooper to sit in;
never a house too fine to shelter the human head.
These elements about us, the gorgeous sky, the im-
perial sun, are not too good for the human race.
Elegance fits man. But do we not value these tools
of house-keeping a little more than they are worth,
and sometimes mortgage home for the mahogany
we can bring into it? I would rather eat my din-
ner off the head of a barrel, or dress after the fash-
ion of John the Baptist in the wilderness, or sit on
a block all my life, than consume all myself before
I get home, and take so much pains with the out-
side that the inside was as hollow as an empty nut.
Beauty is a great thing, but beauty of garments,
house and furniture is a very tawdry ornament com-
pared with domestic love. All the elegance in the
world will not make a home, and I would give
more for a spoonful of hearty love than for whole
shiploads of furniture, and all the upholsterers of
the world could gather together.—“Theodore Par-
ker.”
	Why a Child loves Sugar.—The craving of chil-
dren for sweets is well known to be one of the
most imperious of their appetites. It has refer-
ence probably to that careless activity which espe-
cially characterizes the age of childhood. It is
known that sugar renders easier and more perfect
the digestion of the albuminous food upon which
their growth depends. In respect to these offices
it is therefore nearly essential to their well-being.
And yet how strong, for generations, has been the
prejudice against sugar! Under what difficulties,
and in the face of what discouragements and pro-
tests, have our children obtained the luxury!

HYGIENIC NOTES.

	TO REMOVE BOILS.—Dr. Simon, a phy-
sician of Loraine gives a new cure for boils,
by treating them with camphorated alcohol.
As soon as the culmination point of a boil
makes its appearance, he puts a little of the
liquor in a saucer, and dipping the ends of
his little fingers in it, rubs the inflamed sur-
face, especially the central part, repeating
the operation eight or ten times for about
half a minute. He then allows the surface
to dry, placing over it a slight coating of 
camphorated olive oil. He says that four
such applications will, in almost all cases,
cause boils to dry up and disappear. The
application should be made at morning, noon
and evening.

	EATING BETWEEN MEALS.—In regard to
eating between meals, if persons would eat
only at regular intervals there would be no
craving; but as the habit of eating irregu-
larly is continued, it becomes stronger. I
know it is a common practice for people to


eat as they feel a longing for it. When
children are allowed this privilege, as a gen-
eral rule, it makes them fretful and un-
healthy. When I was a child it was a rare
thing that I asked for anything to eat be-
tween meals; as I grew older I got into the
habit of eating whenever I felt the slight-
est degree of hunger, and was hungry near-
ly all of the time at that; feeling miserable
in the bargain: but, happily for me, I saw an
article in the "Rural" bitterly opposing this
injurious habit, and it so completely disgus-
ted me with it that I made a promise then 
and there never to eat between meals. Since
then I have felt better in both body and
mind and it is seldom that I care about 
breaking my promise; when I do I conquer
the feeling and feel far better than I would
if I had eaten. I speak from experience
having tried both ways.—M. Lang in R.
N.Y.

BENEFITS OF SUNSHINE.—The human
being is like a plant—neither will thrive in
the dark. Nor will either prosper in the
twilight or in the shade. Show me a fam-
ily that lives in a house heavily shaded by
overhanging boughs of trees, so that the
sunshine seldom or never falls upon any side
of it, and I will show you a people who
never enjoy good health. They are not ex-
actly wilted nor bleached; but they have
that bleached look presented by the unfortu-
nate inmates of prisons, and the drooping
appearance seen in plants that make a poor
show of growing in dark, out of the way
places. Vigor is altogether impassible in
the absence of sunshine. I know many
people who think themselves intelligent,
and who are filled with wise saws and sage
proverbs as to the preservation of health,
who live in darkened rooms. Sunshine
fades the carpets and warps the furniture,
they say; but faded carpets are better than
faded cheeks, and warped furniture is better
than crooked spines and bowed leggs and
arms. Flies will get into the house if it is
light, is another excuse for ample window 
window curtains and heavy shade trees—the
latter, in many cases, the poisonous and too 
odorous Chinese Ailanthus.	*	*
Sunshine is grand medicine for the invalid,
and coupled with plenty of fresh air, and
the restorative preparations drawn by the
plants from air and sunshine, it is one of the
finest and safest remedies. It is also one of
the best of the preventives of sickness.
Admit it, then, to every nook and corner of
your dwellings, that it may banish malaria,
and implant strength and vitality into your
minds and bodies. And, speaking of the
benefits of sunshine, we recommend a sun-
shine bath. It costs nothing, but, taken
properly, it yields health, strength and elas-
ticity of spirits.

	HARMONY OF COLORS.

	We, as a people, are having many splen-
did opportunities to beautify and embellish
our homes by the workmanship of our own
hands. And in order that we may do so
scientifically, that every addition we make
for improvement, may be in unison with the
furniture, wearing apparel, or other articles
already in use, it is necessary that we should
obtain, by diligent research and study of
the fine arts, a knowledge of how to arrange
and decide for ourselves the kind of colors
we wish to bring together, that the whole
may so blend and beautifully harmonize,
not only in things themselves but with all
their immediate surroundings, as to be pleas-
ing and satisfactory to the trained eye of
the most refined. I would refer in an espe-
cial manner to the selection of colors in the
manufacture, by our ladies, of beautiful bed
quilts, hearth rugs, &c., although these re-
marks apply equally as well to the laying
out of flower gardens, the arrangement of
bouquets, the trimming of hats and bonnets,
painting of rooms, papering of walls or the
adornment of the person; and as we ladies
are going into the printing business exten-
sively, my suggestions would also be appli-
cable in the printing of rainbow colored
cards, bills, and every instance where vari-
ous colors are brought in contact with each
other, so that nothing may be discordant or
displeasing to the observing or criticising
eye. One thing to be remembered is, that,
as to the musician a note inserted in the
wrong place is to his trained ear a discord,
so is the introduction of an inharmonious
color a discord to the eye of the artist. How
often is it the case in our associations to-
gether, that a sister will bring out, for us to
admire, that exquisite bed quilt that has
taken so much of her valuable time, atten-
tion and exertions to put together? But
she has embellished it with all the colors of
the prismatic spectrum, the hues of the
rainbow, with all the properties of the pho-
togenic and sciogenic elements of light and
shade without due regard to the harmony
that should exist in the distribution and ar-
rangement of the variety of colors inserted.
This brings to my mind quite forcibly, the
scene of the colors displayed by the thou-
sands of sisters when seated in our densely
crowded tabernacle. Perhaps in a future
time, I may have something to suggest on
this subject, through your columns, to my
sisters of the Relief Societies. Understand-
ing from experience that it is one thing to
find fault, and quite another thing to mend a
fault I purpose giving a few hints on this
subject, realizing that the diffusion of all
useful knowledge is a principle we, as a peo-
ple believe in; and from whatever source it
may emanate it is acceptable and instruct-
ive. Every one must have observed that
certain colors when brought together, set off
each other to advantage; while others have
altogether a different effect. This must be
carefully attended to by whoever would dis-
play beauty or elegance in her work. Any
one color will appear well on a white ground.
Red comes in between white, black or yel-
low; blue next to white or yellow, green
next to black or white, orange or gold color
must be contiguous to black or brown. Let
me here remark that a white article reflects
all the rays that constitute a white light, al-
though in some instances it may reflect some
colored light also, according as the surfaces
are smooth, rough, glossy or channelled. The
optical effect of a color is greatly modified
by the condition of the surface of the colored
article; thus, pieces of silk, cotton, woolen,
linen and velvet although dyed of exactly
the same hue and tone of color, appear to be
of quite different tints. The depth or in-
tensity of color, presented by velvets and
certain flowers, such as the heartsease, is
due to the surface being channelled, ridged,
or furrowed. Here are a few instances, how
colors can appear to be changed by contact
with other colors. Red, for example, by be-
ing placed in contact with blue, appears yel-
low, in contact with yellow it has a bluish
appearance, with green it appears purer and
brighter, in contact with black it appears
duller, with white it appears lighter and
brighter; gray also makes it appear more
bright. Place two pieces together of dif-
ferent colors but of the same tone or inten-
sity. If they are not too wide, they will
appear quite different from what they do
when viewed separately; this is called con-
trast of color. If we place, side by side,
two pieces of the same color but different
shades, the light one will appear lighter, and
the dark one darker; this is called contrast
of tone. Blue must not be placed by the
side of violet, because blue enters into the
composition of violet; the blue makes the
violet look faded, while the violet gives the
blue a greenish appearance. A deep blue
and a deep red both gain intensity of color
by contact with each other. Yellow and
blue are so dissimilar that their contrast is
sufficiently great to produce a favorable as-
sociation, while red and orange are quite
the reverse. Red and blue harmonize bet-
ter than red and violet; yellow and red har-
monize better than yellow and orange, al-
though these latter will harmonize better
than red and orange, because red predomi-
nates in the orange. For the same reason,
yellow and green appear better than blue
and green. When two light colors do not
look well together, separate them with
white, but the deep colors should be separa-
ted by black. Red and orange do not look
well together, yet if separated by black an
agreeable and harmonious effect is produced.
Much more could be said on this subject. I
know from experience that my sisters, the
ladies of Utah, have in many instances, to
use up whatever pieces they may happen to
have by them; but where we have to dye
any of them, we can have an eye to the har-
mony of colors. We must consider that
pink comes under the denomination of red,
because red enters largely into the composi-
tion of pink; as blue does in violet, yellow
in green and orange. In the prismatic spec-
trum and in the rainbow we look for our
standard colors; of these, there are three,
viz., red, blue and yellow. These are called
primary colors, and when transferred
through a prism upon a white surface, pro-
duce a white light; therefore, either one of
the three should always be omitted and
never placed beside the other two.
	As my communication has already assum-
ed such extensive dimensions, while the
subject in hand is not nearly exhausted, I
will defer further remarks till some future
period.				R. J. FILCE.

﻿THE HABIT OF FRETTING.
	Fretting is both useless and unnecessary;
it does no good and a great deal of harm,
yet it is almost a universal sin. More or less
we are all given to it. We fret over almost
everything. In  summer because it is too
hot, and in  winter because it is too cold; we
fret when it rains because it is wet; when
we are sick, or when anybody else is sick.
In short, if anything or everything dosn’t
go just to suit our particular whims and
fancies, we have one grand general refuge
—to fret over it. I am afraid fretting is
much more common among women than
among men. We may as well own the
truth, my fair sisters, if it isn’t altogether
pleasant. Perhaps it is because the little
worries, and cares and vexations of our daily
life harrass our sensitive nerves more than
the more extended enterprises which gen-
erally take the attention of men. Great
wants develop great resources, but the lit-
tle wants and worries are hardly provided
for, and like the nail which strikes against
the saw, they make not much of a mark,
but they turn the edges terribly. I think if
we looked upon all the little worries of one
day as a great united worry, self-control to
meet it would be developed. But as they
generally come only one or two little things
at a time, they seem so very little that we
give way, and the breach once in the wall
soon grows larger. Now, I don’t believe in
the cant that a woman must always, under
all and any circumstances, wear a smiling
face when her husband comes home, or that
she needs to take her hands out of the dough
or drop the baby on the floor to run and
meet him at the door. But I do believe—
nay. I know, for I have seen it with my own
eyes among my friends—that many a wo-
man has driven a kind husband away from
her, away from his home and its sacred in-
fluences, and caused him to spend his time
at a billiard table or in a drinking saloon,
amid their profane influences, simply by
her ceaseless fretting over trifles which
were not worth a word, much less the peace
and happiness of a home. I know that many
a mother has brought up and developed a
daughter just like herself, who, in her turn,
would wreck and ruin the comfort of an-
other family circle. I know that many a
mother has turned her son against her own
sex, and made him dread and dislike the
society of women, by her example, con-
stantly set before him. And knowing all
this, my sisters—and brothers, too, if they
need it—I know that we ought to set our
faces like flint against this useless, sinful,
peace-destroying and home-disturbing habit
of fretting.


WHO SHOULD NOT BE A WIFE.

	Has that woman a call to be a wife who
thinks more of her silk dress than her chil-
dren, and visits her nursery no oftener than
once a day? Has a woman a call to be a
wife who calls for a cashmere shawl when
her husband's notes are being protested?
Has that woman a call to be a wife who sits
reading the last new novel while her hus-
band stands before the glass vainly trying
to pin together a bottomless shirt bosom?
Has that woman a call to be a wife who ex-
pects her husband to swallow diluted coffee,
soggy bread, smoky tea, and watery pota-
toes six days out of the seven? Has she
the call to be a wife who flirts with every
man she meets, and reserves her frowns
for the home fireside? Has she a call to be
a wife who comes down to breakfast in
abominable curl papers, a soiled dressing-


gown, and shoes down at the heel? Has
she a call to be a wife whose husband's love
weighs naught in the balance with her
next-door neighbor’s damask curtains or
velvet carpet? Has she a call to be a wife
who would take advantage of a moment of
conjugal weakness to extort money or ex-
tract a promise? Has she a call to be a wife
who takes a journey for pleasure, leaving
her husband to toil in a close shop, and have
an eye, when at home, to the servants and
children? Has she a call to be a wife to
whom a good husband’s society is not the
greatest earthly blessings, and a house full
of rosy children its best furnishing and pret-
tiest ornaments?


﻿FROM KANAB.
				May 22nd, 1874.
DEAR EXPONENT:—
	It is very pleasant here now. The wind
has blown a good deal this spring, but for
the past two weeks the weather has been
fine and gardens are looking well.
	Kanab grows slowly, owing to the poverty
of the people. I should not say poverty
either; what I mean is, that we have had so
much to contend with, that it has taken all
the time and means to feed us, without
building much. But I like the place for all
that. We were sent here by those whose
right it is to direct us, and I have never for
one moment felt to murmur. My children
have been deprived of many advantages,
which seems a draw back, but if they only
have the Spirit of God with them and learn
of His ways, they will be all right. So long
as we are in the line of duty, what matters
it?
	The family organization works well here,
so far. The Saints feel well, and all looks
bright. We may be a little short of some
conveniences for a while, until we get into
working order, but I believe we will also be
greatly blest. We may yet have some trou-
ble with the Navajoes, but the Lord is able
to turn away the wrath of the Indians. And
now that the brethren are all on this side of
the river, I feel that all will be well.
	We have good meetings here, and a good
school now, which my children attend. And
I feel very thankful for these, with all the
other blessings. Respectfully, &c.
                                             H. M. B.
