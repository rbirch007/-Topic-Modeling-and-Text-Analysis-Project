TO LU DALTON.

ANSWER TO "QUESTIONINGS" IN EXPONENT
OF APRIL 1st.

Thy piteous "questionings" touch an answering chord
	Within my soul, which fain would breathe reply,
If I perchance by some low, gentle word
	Might help to soothe thy spirit's yearning cry;
For well I know that doubts bring dire unrest
	And naught in common should they have with us,
For how by faith in God, can we be blest,
	Unless we yield to Him in perfect trust.

That man has carped, and warped a woman's sphere,
	To suit his selfish needs, is too well known,
Yet galling though it be to woman's ear—
	We must admit the fault is part her own,
For has she not with free and gracious will,
	Such service rendered to the ones beloved,
Nor counted costs to future ages, till
	Man thinks her lack of agency is proved.

But sister, do not shrink from Heaven's decree,
	'Twas Christ the man-child came the world to save,
Thine Elder Brother died for you and me,
	That we might rise triumphant from the grave.
Methinks that he who brings the clay to life
	Mnst not be far removed from Christ's Estate
To claim from death his sister, friend or wife
	And pilot them,through the Celestial gate.

Should he to whom thy love and truth is given
	(And his to thee) should he I say aspire
To be as perfect as the Lord in heaven
	And call thee forth from death, wouldst then seek
		higher?

O breathe no thought of doubt in thy babe's ears
Nor tell her thy chafed heart's bewildering fears,
Break not the magic charm of infant trust,
Nor sow the seeds of canker, moth or rust,
But gently plant love's own immortal germ,
Too soon hard lessons she will have to learn,
And teach her trust in God's unfaltering will,
That her earth-mission she may nobly fill.

Though tardy justice may be long delayed,
	Our cause no longer lingers in the rear,
And Woman's onward course can not be stayed,
	Though man, reluctant, turns her plea to hear,
And in that life where we may hope to gain
	A respite from this world of pain,
Methinks where all is holy, pure and free,
	"Our Rights" will sink in Perfect Liberty.

	QUERY.

HEALTH AND DANCING.

	Next to moral and religious virtues,
health should be regarded as the greatest
blessing we can claim from God. And one
of our sacred duties is to prize and guard
this precious boon, as one which through
carelessness or mismanagement, may be
easily and irretrievably lost.
	The indifference shown by many, espec-
ially among the young, to the importance
of preserving health, must be grievous in
the sight of that kind and merciful Father
whose will it is to shower blessings upon
His children. Yet, the majority of sins in
these respects are, doubtless committed ig-
norantly; hence the marvelous goodness
still extended, which preserves life and
prevents injuries in thousands of cases
where unseen or unheeded dangers threat-
en. Still, nature's laws are most impera-
tive and save in instances of special provi-
dence, must be obeyed. In all ordinary
cases they cannot be broken, whether ig-
norantly or otherwise, but the transgressor
must pay the penalty.
	How carefully should our young people
be taught concerning these things! How

[Column 2]
deeply should their minds be impressed
with a sense of the extreme folly and ac-
tual sin of ignoring the laws of health!
They should be brought to learn and profit
by the experience of others, as far as possi-
ble, and not left to risk life and health in
gaining knowledge for themselves. True,
many things can only be realized and un-
derstood through practical experience; but
a great deal that is useful and necessary in
life, the young may obtain by observation,
reflection and giving heed to the admoni-
tions of their elders.
	In the olden times, when our grand-
mothers and great-grand-mothers—of
whose fortitude, ambition and abilities we
hear such wonderful stories—were rosy-
cheeked girls, not a hundredth part of the
gayety and fashionable amusements that
now exist in society were known or even
thought of. Dancing of any description
was but rarely engaged in; round dancing
or waltzing, in this country, was as foreign
to the minds and habits of the young as
the moon is from the earth. It is strange
that young women in these days, who
waste so much strength and vitality in dan-
cing and whirling, should be generally del-
icate in health, never vigorous and robust
as maidens were in days gone by? No, it
is not strange, it is simply a natural conse-
quence.
	Perhaps as great evils have been wrought
for the human family, through the intro-
duction of so much and so many different
styles of dancing as in any other way.
Physicians as well as moral reformers con-
demn the practice of round dancing, some
of them in the strongest terms.
	Young ladies of the present day are not
to blame for the great delight they take in
this most fascinating recreation. They
have not been shown the evil tendencies to
which it leads. Let mothers, and people of
studious thought and reflection come to un-
derstand the nature of these things first;
then teach it to the girls, and see how read-
ily they will listen and comply with those
instructions.
	With many young ladies, waltzing has
become almost like an intoxicating bever-
age to an habitual drinker. When it is pre-
sented to them, it would seem like a great
sacrifice not to indulge somewhat; and
when a little is accepted the temptation to
indulge too far is irresistible. But bring
our girls to realize that there is real danger,
positive wrong in round dancing, and a
majority of them will abandon the prac-
tice forthwith.
	Some of them may be hard to convince,
but surely they are worth laboring with.
Talk to them of future generations, of a
time when they are likely to become moth-
ers—a time for which they should be pre-
paring physically, mentally and spiritually.
Inspire them now with the thought, the
wish (which is sure to come in later years,
perhaps too late) to be among the most cap-
able, noble and honorable of mothers. Tell
them in connection with these things, how
very much depends upon the present care
and preservation of their health and
strength. And tell them in a way that
they must understand, that too much dan-
cing of any sort, now in their early years,
is one thing that is likely to make them

[Column 3]
frail, weakly women all their lives, and
doubtless shorten their days and retard
their usefulness.

	It may be considered by some, that it
should be sufficient to tell young folks
what is good for them and what is likely
to prove hurtful; but this is not sufficient
for most older people. The old as well as
the young, are liable to give too little at-
tention to the care of health, and to violate
the laws of nature. For this reason if for
no other, they should exercise great pa-
tience in teaching the youth.	INEZ.


NOTES AND NEWS.

	MRS. CAMPBELL a lady lecturer known
to fame, has been lecturing during the past
month in some of the most conservative
old towns of Mass., on Woman's Suffrage.
The Woman's Journal in alluding to the
fact, and speaking of Somerset, said it was
at first doubtful whether the lecture would
receive even respectful consideration. At
Taunton, Mrs. Campbell lectured under the
auspices of the Woman's Christian Temper-
ance Union. "The lady was self-possessed,
spoke eloquently, and commanded close at-
tention." She believes that the ballot would
be better used by women than by men, and 
thinks in the good time coming, when wo-
men help make the laws there will be some
improvement.
	A MEETING in support of the extension
of the franchise to women was held in the 
Temperance Hall, Leceister, England,
March 21st. A large number of ladies
were present. * * The Chairman
in opening the proceedings said they were
there to discuss a serious and interesting
question, which must arise in a highly civ-
ilized state of society, namely the proper
position of women.
	It was moved by one of the leading cit-
izens that a petition in favor of the Wo-
man's Suffrage Bill should be signed by the
Chairman in behalf of the meeting and for-
warded to Mr. Taylor, M. P. for presenta-
tion to the House of Commons. Another
gentleman seconded the motion, and Miss
Helen Taylor, member of the London
School Board, supported the motion as fol-
lows: The main interest of the question
to her was from the point of responsibility.
The Suffrage should be used by women not 
as a matter of right but of duty, and a wo-
man who had been successful in life, and who
said she did not want to vote, had a very
low idea of the responsibility attaching to 
her. There were questions affecting a large
class of women in the country, which wo-
men could understand better than men,
however noble they might be, and upon
which they should be allowed to exercise
their opinions. The interests of the whole
nation would profit by the franchise being
extended to women, and she hoped the
time was not far distant when their prayer
would be granted. Meetings of a similar
character to this have been held recently in
many of the large towns in England and
Scotland. These items were taken from
the April No. of the Woman's Suffrage
Journal of that country.

willingness to assist in starting a work

blessing in some future day.

A WOMAN MISSIONARY COMING.

	Our friends in the East are becoming ex-
tremely anxious about our salvation, they
feel our condition as women citizens to be
so deplorable, notwithstanding our having
the right of franchise, that they are con-
strained to resort to some method of re-
deeming us from the bondage in which we
are bound. They have decided to send us
a woman missionary to show us the error of
our ways, and help to redeem the Mormon
women from their load of sin and degrada-
tion. At the Presbyterian Church on Grav-
el, Hill, at Cream Ridge, Monmouth Co., the
minister informed his flock that he desired
to send out a lady-missionary to convert
the poor deluded Mormon Women, and that
money would be a necessary consequence
of his resolve; a meeting was called for the
sole purpose of obtaining sufficient means
to bring the lady hither on this special er-
rand! What peculiar ideas they must en-
tertain of us as a people.
	How full of Christian philanthropy these
good people must be to appropriate their
means to correcting our errors, and re-
forming our morals. What their ideas
may be concerning us we are not prepared
to say, but presuming from the fact that we
need women missionaries from abroad, one
would suppose they looked upon us as
heathen in our views, and uncivilized in
our social life. Well we shall be very glad
to enlighten their minds on these and oth-
er subjects, through this lady missionary,
and we will guarantee she will go home a
much wiser woman than when she came.
In a country where women have the Suf-
frage, and organizations of improvement
among themselves, where they publish a
paper specially devoted to the interests of
the women of the Territory, where every
facility available is theirs to make use of
for their advancement and progress, it
seems as if they would be wholly capable
of determining for themselves on matters
of religion, and of judging of the true and
false.
	If the good lady comes here honest in
her desires for our spiritual welfare she will


soon comprehend the true position of things,
and it will no doubt place Mormon women
upon another plane in the estimation of
the good people of Monmouth Co., New
Jersey. Every courtesy that could be ex-
tended to us if we went abroad to represent
our views, will no doubt be extended to 
this lady missionary if she comes here in
good faith as a missionary, but, as for con-
verting Mormon women, they have already
been converted to the truth and they not
only believe but know that the principles
they have espoused, including Celestial
Marriage are of God and not of man.
[handwritten] E. B. Wells 			EMILE.


CORRESPONDENCE.

	BLOOMSBURG, COLUMBIA CO, PENN,
		March 14, 1878.
EDITOR EXPONENT:
	Perhaps a few lines from this part of the
land may not be uninteresting to your read-
ers. I left my home and loved ones in
Zion on the 7th of Jan., to pay a visit to
my husband's relatives who reside here.
All was new and strange to me, having
been reared in Utah. I had the pleasure of
traveling with our honored Delegate and
his family. Having a desire to see the city
in which the laws of our nation are made,
I accompanied them to Washington. I was
delighted with the beauties of that great
city, but only remained three days and then
came here, where I found a kind, warm-
hearted people. I am treated as well as I
could possibly be if I did not belong to the
"Mormons." There is a liberal feeling
manifested toward our people, although
they do not understand us. They do not
deny but that our principles are in accord-
ance with the Scriptures, still their faith is
not very strong in that good old book.
	I have been asked many questions con-
cerning our faith and practice, all of which
I have endeavored to answer truthfully, to
the best of my ability. I take particular
pleasure in talking on the principle of CE-
LESTIAL MARRIAGE—bearing testimony to
the world that the women of Utah are not
kept, or forced, to live in that order against
their will; that we, as well as our husbands,
know it to be of divine origin, and we
would not desire to abolish it. They some-
times look at me as though they almost
doubted the sense of their own hearing,
and finally come to the conclusion that we
must be differently constituted to the rest
of the world. I tell them "not so;" it is
because we know that God has commanded,
and we are willing to bring self into sub
jection to His laws; and we can, with His
assistance, overcome selfishness, and jeal-
ousy, and be willing for others to enjoy
equally with ourselves; then we can be
happy, loving them and their children.
	I trust I may be the means of doing some
little good in allaying prejudice that has
hitherto existed towards us. I find that
their prejudice is born of ignorance; that
where there is a better understanding of
things the people are inclined to be Just—to
accord to us our rights as American citi-
zens.  I also find that the pure and good
have the least to say condemnatory of our
principles, which is a great proof that we
are right. I believe there are many honest
hearts here, who would obey the Gospel if
the opportunity was afforded them. Many
of them never saw a Latter-day Saint pre-
vious to seeing me. I have many times
wondered why there were never any Elders
sent to this region, for we read that "This
Gospel must be preached to every creature
before the end shall come."

A FEW WORDS TO YOUNG MEN.

	What are you doing for the improvement
of your minds? Are you aware that you
are on the great railway of time, rushing
and whirling past the station of youth?
The world is all before you—it is yours—God
gives you a lease of it! You see the phi-
losophers, the statesmen, and the teachers
of the present time—are their places to be
filled? What mean those telegraph posts
and wires? those cars and boats—those fast
printing-presses? They are monuments of
genius—the genius of men now passing
away. Up—bestir yourselves, with a firm
purpose and a stern resolve to penetrate
still farther into the arcana of nature, and
open to human gaze mysteries unrevaled
from the foundation of the world. Prove
the nobility of your natures by deeper div-
ing into scientific research, by a higher
flight of genius, and by a wider range of
thought. Shall the mind of the succeeding
age be charged with effeminacy or imbe-
cility? These questions we cannot answer.
We see what has been done in fifty years;
we are staggered by the sight, and exclaim
—how slight a prospect of such a vision in
the future! Who are to be men of emi-
nence in the age before us? Not you who
are lounging there on the sidewalks, or on
the streets—not you who are spending your
time with boisterous companions, repeating
unmeaning gibes and senseless jokes—not
you, who are wasting the midnight oil over
Eugene Sue and Paul de Kock—not you
who frequent the bowling saloons and bar-
rooms. No—you must pass through a dif-
ferent discipline; you must bend down the
undivided energies of your ardent souls, to
persevering, unremitting, and laborious
study. You must acquire an intimate and
thorough acquaintance with the world, dis-
cover the hidden intricacies of human
character, and acquaint yourselves with
the operation of natural laws. You must
bring truth from chaos to the golden sun-
light of order and system. This has been
the work of the mighty minds of the last
century.
	Say you that you have no time for this?
that your hours are those of toil? that the
workshop, the counter, and the mill de-
mand your time? So they do, but that toil
will indurate mind as well as body. It is
the union of manual and mental toil that
makes the iron men who grasp the mighty
things of the universe. You indulge an
idle whim. The mind can be revolving
weighty things while the hands are in mo-
tion. In your leisure, even if it be in the
silent hour of night, feed the immortal
mind with food worthy of it, and the hours
of toil will be cheered by the light of the
intellect. The mighty mind of Franklin
gave birth to sublime imaginings, even
while at the compositor's case and the press-
man's lever. Learn to think—think deep-
ly that you may act wisely.—Selected.

EDUCATION AND CULTURE OF
WOMEN.

THEIR MINDS AND MANNERS.

"The reasoning maid, above her sex's dread
Had dared to read, and dared to say she read."

	This quotation, simple as are its words, is
full of historical significance. It shows us that
at that period, about a hundred years ago, wo-
men not only did not read, but if they dared
to do so, must brave the opposition of their co-
temporaries, and even of their own sex. It
was considered superfluous for girls to be taught
even in the common branches of knowledge
thoroughly, but their deportment, their train-
ing for society, their grace of carriage, were of
the utmost importance. There were, to be
be sure, women who possessed, in an unusual
degree, native mental endowments, and conse-
quently attained literary excellence; but even
such ladies did not seem to comprehend that a
higher education would be advantageous for
the sex generally. Lady Montagu, whose
fame is still widely known, in writing of her
niece, gives us a good illustration in proof of
this assertion—"I am glad you intend sending
my eldest niece to a boarding school. What
girls learn at these schools is trifling, but they
unlearn what would be a great disservice—a
provincial dialect, which is extremely ungen-
teel, and other tricks that they learn in the
nursery. The carriage of the person, which is
of great importance, is well attended to, and
dancing is well taught. As for the French lan-
guage, I do not think it necessary, unless for
persons in high life. All the boarding schools
are on very much the same plan, so that you
may place her wherever there is a good air and
a good dance-master."
	This letter was written in 1773, and at that
date MEN were thoroughly trained as scholars
and gentlemen. Ladies were expected to be
graceful, to behave with a subdued dignity
suited to a subordinate position. If by some
natural pro[-]livities any lady became better
informed than the generality of the sex, such
an one was instructed to conceal it, for learn-
ing was looked upon as incompatible with the
requisites of a true lady.
	How materially have the manners and cus-
toms of people changed since that time. And
yet how far moderns still fall short of a true
balance of mind and manners in the education
and culture of women.
	Much of the culture of mind and deportment
depends upon the homelife, the early impres-
sions, the associations of childhood, the atmos-
phere of the surroundings, the tone of the ele-
ments which characterize the mother's train-
ing and influence. These constitute the funda-
mental grounds on which to rear a fabric of
exquisite elegance, beauty and sublimity, or

[Column 2]
that which will continually undermine all the
harmony of outward polish and years of ardu-
our labor.
	We believe that a positive revolution is now
being effected in the culture and education of
women. Very much has already been accom-
plished by women of exceptional ability and
capability, but it yet remains for future exer-
tions to effect a permanent revolution among
all classes, and enlist woman generally in the
noble work of higher culture in all that will
develop the strongest attributes of goodness
and excellence.
	We cannot but believe that religion has a
more deeply cultivating effect than any other
power. It subdues and tranquilizes those pas-
sions which are calculated to destroy and lay
waste the better germs of life and character.
Then however highly cultivated a woman may
be in intellect, or mind, religion will still add
a charm to her character, an enthusiasm to her
inspirations, a loveliness to enhance all other
attributes of her soul. If this be true religion,
founded upon correct principles of faith and
doctrine, how lofty will be the aspirations that
emanate therefrom, how deep the inner foun-
tains, from which flow peacefully those
thoughts and ideas that shape themselves into
harmony, purifying, elevating and refining all
other requisites in the completeness of wo-
manly character.
	The great fear in this age is not so much in
the lack of higher education of mind, for it
would seem woman has now so many opportu-
nities for the cultivating of the intellect, that
all studies which man pursues will ere long be
open to woman; on this point, then, we need
not quibble, neither is it that woman will be-
come neglectful of her husband or children
through the cultivation of mind; what then?
It is that the manners of the present time are
not so perfect as in the time when women were
disciplined more carefully in deportment and
carriage. Every observer knows that there is
a charm in a person whose carriage is easy and
whose address is courteous, and this charm is
intensified, in a very great degree, when it is
associated with a cultivated mind, a tender
heart, and a devotion to God.
	Such women should the daughters of Zion
become; but such women they never will, nor
never can be, without discipline of the most
careful kind. There is a work of exceeding
magnitude before the mothers, the teachers,
the women of Zion, who are desirous of im-
proving and elevating the condition of woman.
This work does not belong exclusively to any
one class, but to all; and it will do good to all;
—a refining tendency will diffuse itself through
the associations of the Saints, if there is here
and there a germ well cultivated. The chil-
dren of the Latter-day Saints, many of them,
not only have good physical organizations and
intellectual capacities, but possess those char-
acteristics which may develop the grandest
possibilities of which the human mind can
conceive.
	Fvery day school, every Sunday school,
every organization for the young people, every
home, should be so conducted as to have an
influence, not only on the MINDS of children
and youth, but on the MANNERS. It is never
too late to begin to do better, and if there has
been neglect of any duty in the past, there is
opportunity given to improve in the future.
We are aware the facilities for cultivation are
not so general as in older towns and settle-
ments, and the Saints have many hindrances
and difficulties which differ widely from the
world, yet, notwithstanding all these, there
might be a vast improvement in the manners
of "our girls and boys," if greater care was

[Column 3]
taken in their training and in the examples of
fathers and mothers.
	In our humble opinion, although it is not a
disgrace to be awkward, yet every school-teach-
er should study deportment, and their exam-
ple in this respect should be such as their pu-
pils can copy.
	We are aware that this subject, like many
others, is inexhaustible, but even a few words
may sometimes set people thinking, and the
result be something more real than words.
	As a people we are proud of our religion, we
feel it is something superior to any other; then
it ought to make the Saints superior in heart,
in mind and in manners. The Saints should
not only be more devoted to God and more
willing and obedient than the world, but they
should be kinder, more charitable, more hu-
mane, more hospitable, more merciful, more
tender, more equitable, more benevolent, more
truthful, more affable, and in their associations
with their fellow-creatures preserve that quiet
dignity of demeanor, that gentleness of man-
ners, that attract the heart and rivet the at-
tention, and demand the respect and admira-
tion of all who are capable of comprehending
true nobility.
	We would not be understood as speaking of
that sort of cultivation which leads women to
despise labor—far from it. All honest labor is
elevating, and any woman may manifest as
much ease and grace in the duty of housekeep-
ing as in the drawing-room. Cultivating grace
of attitude in idle languor is not half so charm-
ing as in those motions and movements that
are necesssry to the labor of a household. Have
not men often been led to praise, even in verse,
the simplicity and grace of manner, of girls
compeled to perform menial services, such as
bar-maid and hotel-waiter. And is there any-
thing sweeter or more lovely than a young girl
full of vigor, neat and graceful, waiting upon
father and mother, or entertaining guests, re-
lieving the mother, tired, perchance, with long
years of care and toil. We know ladies who
have had very few advantages, who are almost
perfect in manners, and draw all hearts to
them, who, by observation and practise, have
learned to converse so well that no one would
suspect how small a part of their knowledge
had been obtained from books.
	We thought to say only a few words to draw
attention to this snbject, but will now conclude
by remarking that such training and discipline
as is good for girls is also good for boys, with
only the differences of some peculiar charac-
teristics easily discriminated by the mother or
instructor. Good manners require thought,
attention, care and practise, and if a little
more consideration was given to this subject,
instead of letting children form their own
manners or go without, there would be an in-
creased degree of good feeling and happiness,
that would add to the general pleasure of hu-
man life.		[handwritten] E. B. Wells


HOME AFFAIRS.

	WE CORDIALLY welcome, as an exchange,
the "Woman's Suffrage Journal," published
in London and Manchester, and ably edited
by Miss Lydia E. Becker, a lady of superior at-
tainments and a successful champion of wo-
man's cause in the British Isles. Her letter
addressed to us was full of kindly feeling and
liberality. This Journal gives one a correct idea
of the advanced progress of the woman's work
in the British E[-]pire, and of the opinions and
sentiments entertained by men and women on
the popular question of woman's suffrage and
equality of sex, in a country where one wo-
man holds sovereign rule, and has had a peace-
ful and prosperous reign for forty years.


THROUGH COURTESY of Simeon C. Aratoon,
Esq., of Calcutta, Bengal, India, to whom we
return generous thanks, we have received some
books edited, in the English language, by one
of the learned women of that country, Raj
LakshiDebi, wife of BabooProsonnocomar, Sen.,
M. D. "The Gods of Greece, Italy and India"
and "The Chronology of the Hindu's" are the
titles. They seem to be well written, and con
tain many things which are of interest to those
who wish to inform themselves in regard to
customs and traditions of the ancients.
	WE WELCOME to our list of exchanges "The
American Home," published in Chicago, Mrs.
Theodore C. Campbell, Editor and Proprietor.
Several ladies of note contribute to its columns
—"Hyacinth," "Methusaleh," "Aunt Carrie,"
nom de plumes of favorite writers well-known
to the public. There are other contributors of
pleasing style of composition, with whose
writings the public are familiar. This maga-
zine has some pages appropriated to what is
termed a Home Letter Box, in which are some
very useful practical hints on all subjects, and
"lots of fun." There is a serial in course of
publication, and many interesting notes on
gardening plants, in fact, all the different de-
partments of home life, recipes for cooking,
advertisements, etc., etc. The paper is in its
second volume.
	WE HAD the pleasure of a call from Miss Isa-
bella L. Bird, of Edinburgh, Scotland. The
lady is traveling for the benefit of her health,
and while making the tour of this continent
has been taking notes by the way, anticipating
publishing a book of travels. After visiting
California and the adjacent country she ex-
pects to sail for Japan. She expressed herself
as pleased with the city, and surprised at the
wonderful improvement made in so short a
time, drew a favorable comparison to other
towns in the West that she had visited, and 
commended our people for their industry and
enterprise. She spoke of the neatness of the
cottages and their surroundings, as in striking
contrast with the squalor and untidiness of
those she had seen in many towns by the way;
said the surroundings of the dwellings remind-
ed her of the homes of the cottagers in Eng-
land. Miss Bird is an advocate of reform in
dress and economy in the various departments
of home and society. She was anxious to
learn the views of the ladies here on this sub-
ject. She was particularly interested in hear-
ing of the organization we have here for STOR-
ING GRAIN, and looked upon it as a proof that
we practically demonstrated our faith in the lit-
eral fulfilment of prophecy. It is pleasant to
meet ladies of culture, free from predjudice, and
anxious to obtain correct information concern-
ing our general interests as a people.

OBITUARIES.

	Died; of pneumonia, on March 24, 1878, at
5 30 a. m., in the 14th Ward, after four days
sickness, Eliza May, lovely daughter of Mary
Ann Ruth May and Jesse W. Fox, Jr., aged 2
years, 2 months, and 14 days. The funeral ser-
vices were conducted by Bishop Thomas Tay-
lor at the residence of Jesse W. Fox, Sen., at 1
o'clock, March 25, and were numerously at-
tended. Appropriate remarks were made by
President Angus M. Cannon and Bishop Rob-
ert T. Burton.

	Died, in the 14th Ward of this city, of mem-
branous croup, on April 10, 1878, at 9.45 p. m ,
John Roy, beloved son of Frankie E. Fox and
John H. Reese, aged 2 years, 4 months and 28


days. The funeral services were conducted by
President Angus M. Cannon at the residence
of J. W. Fox, Sen. Appropriate remarks were
made by Elder Joseph F. Smith.

	Died April 10th 1878, at her residence in
Fairview, Sanpete County, of lung fever, Sarah
Jane Anderson, wife of Archibald Anderson,
and daughter of Thomas G. and Margaret
Davis Reese. Deceased was born in Merthyr,
Tydvil, Glamorshire, South Wales, May 9th,
1844; was baptized into the Church of Jesus
Christ of Latter-day Saints in 1853, and
has remained a faithful member from that
time. She emigrated to this country in 1856,
crossing the plains in Captain Bunker's Hand-
cart Company. She was married August 23d,
1859, to Archibald Anderson, and leaves ten liv-
ing children, eight girls and two boys, the
youngest 5 weeks old. She proved herself to
be a true Latter-day Saint and a loving wife
and mother. She was a Teacher in the Relief
Society, which position she filled with honor.
She was universally beloved by all who knew
her; too much cannot be said in her praise.
We deeply sympathize with her husband for
his loss.—Com.

	At Mona, Juab County, January 26, 1878,
Mrs. Nancy Harrison, wife of John Harrison,
departed this life, aged 37 years, 2 months and
3 days. Deceased was born in St. Helens, Lan-
cashire, England. In the bloom of early wo-
manhood she has passed away, leaving a hus-
band and five children and numerous friends
to mourn her loss. She was an estimable lady,
endeared to all who knew her; she will be
greatly missed, not only by her family but in
the Church, in the Relief Society, and as a
leader in the choir. She endured her long,
painful sickness with singular fortitude; her
habitual cheerfulness and natural sweetness of
disposition seemed never to forsake her. She
was a faithful worker in the Church. In her
early life she received the additional charms
of Christian grace, which shone brighter as
she drew nearer to the light of eternal day.
Her youngest daughter preceded her in cross-
ing the River of Death, and now they rejoice
together in the brightness of the Heavenly
Home.
	I believe that God thus comforts us, by re-
flecting heavenly peace upon the countenance
of death, as if a ray of its light dropped earth-
ward as the pearly gates opened, or as if the
earthly tabernacle heard the "well done" and
smiled; "Thou hast been faithful, take this
crown of radiant whiteness." O will He say
this to me, have I been faithful?
	MRS. ANNIE DAVIS.


A FAREWELL TO EMMA.

	Am I not dreaming? Is it indeed a reality?
Emma, our beloved one, dead! How dearly
we loved her, how fondly we all prized her!
The silvery tones of her voice, her sunny, azure
smile, her sweet, winning ways, her number-
less acts of kindness, live only in remembrance
now. Beloved, darling Emma! our hearts
with grief are laden, and we weep, because
thou art gone; we feel it is hard to give thee
up, so good, so gifted, so loving and true, but
'Death hath all seasons for its own," and we
have no power to stay his hand. He has come,
an unbidden and unwelcome victor, and if we
look at the dark side only our poor human
hearts would break with grief, for we are but
human. But the angel of the Lord will roll
the stone away from the door of our hearts,
and enable us to look up above the tomb to the


beautiful gardens of paradise, where thou art
arrayed in the white robe of immortality; the
golden crown of salvation on thy sinless brow,
and the flaming harp of love—in thy hand, and
through faith we know that a merciful Parent,
a Father of loving kindness, hath called thee
thus early to dwell with the pure forever,
and the thought of thy goodness shall be as a 
healing balm to our stricken hearts. Farewell,
farewell, dear girl, but not forever.

	They have borne thee away, dearest Emma,
		To the silent and peaceful grave,
	Where the fairest flowers of summer
		Above thee will gently wave.
	They have carried away the casket.
		To rest 'neath the dark green sod;
	But thy soul is a brilliant jewel
		In the diadem of God.

	They have borne thee away, darling Emma,
		From the joys and scenes of earth,
	Away from the friends who loved thee;
		For thine own excelmg worth:
	Away from thy young companions,
		To whom thou wast ever dear;
	How oft they will miss thee, Emma,
		In their joyous gatherings here.

	They have borne thee away, precious Emma,
		Earthly love and parental care,
	From thy home and family circle,
		Which cherished thee fondly there.
	They will miss thee from their dwelling,
		Thy gentle and kindly tone,
	At morning, noon and evening,
		They'll watch for the dear one gone.

	How oft will the glittering teardrop
		From our innermost hearts arise,
	As we mourn thy early departure,
		Thou innocent "child of the skies".
	But the thought of thy numerous virtues
		Shall be as a healing balm,
	Which speaks to our murmuring spirits,
		And bids them be peaceful and calm.
	
	We have bade thee adieu, dearest Emma,
		A long and a sad farewell,
	But the memory of thy gooness
		Shall evermore with us dwell;
	And we'll bow to the will of "our Father,"
		He gave and taketh away,
	Holy Faith points a meeting hereafter,
		Through Christ, the truth and the way.
									L. M. HEWLINGS.
Salt Lake City, April, 1878.


EDITORIAL NOTES.

	WE have several articles and poems
from our contributors laid over, also re-
ports from Fourteenth Ward General Re-
trenchment Meeting, Clarkston, Miners-
ville, Pleasant Grove, Peoa, Mona, Heber and
Bloomington, and one from Rockville, stat-
ing their success in sericulture. They have
plenty of silk-worm eggs and have put out
10,000 mullberry cuttings, and planted a
quart of mulberry seed.
	THE Quarterly Conference of Weber Co.
Relief Society will be held in Ogden, Thurs-
day May 2, when it is expected a full re-
port will be made of the condition of each
branch of the Relief Society of that Stake
of Zion. Several sisters from this city have
been invited to attend. Miss E. R. Snow,
Mrs. Zina D. Young, and Mrs. E. Davis
will leave Ogden on the morning of the 3rd
of May to make a tour of Malad Valley and
settlements in that vicinity, holding meet-
ings with the Societies in the various set-
tlements and talking up home-industries.
especially sericulture, the storing of grain,
co-operative enterprises, encouraging the
sisters in their organizations, urging them
to obtain naturalization papers, and exert
ing their utmost influence in promoting the
interests of the Kingdom of God. These
sisters expect to be absent a week or more.

SCRIPTURE WOMEN.

BY HANNAH T. KING.
[continued.]

	The Woman of Samaria next appears;
she is also nameless but her conversation
with the Savior at the well, renders her re-
nowned. Jesus had walked a long way,
and coming to the well, which was built up
three or four feet above the level of the
ground, sat upon it to rest himself, while
his disciples were gone into the city to buy
food; while there, a woman comes to draw
water, and Jesus said to her "Give me
drink," the woman saith "how is it that
thou being a Jew, asketh drink of me, who
am a Samaritan?" the Jews having no deal-
ings with the Samaritans. This led to some
remarks from the Savior, and He shows her
that He know her previous life, which calls
forth from her the remark, "Sir I perceive
that thou art a prophet." They continue
talking and at last she said "I know that
Messias cometh, which is called Christ;
when He comes He will tell us all things;"
Jesus saith unto her "I that speak unto
thee, am He!" Then came the disciples,
and were surprised that he talked with the
woman, but no man said "Why talkest thou
with her?" The woman then left her water-
pot and ran into the city and saith unto the
men, "come see a man who told me all that
ever I did;" and many invited him to their
houses, and he abode with them two days.
Here, as is often the case, woman is the in-
strument to convey the first intimation of
the gospel of life and salvation, who like
this woman are nameless, but their names
are written in that Book which has yet to
be opened.
	And now comes another nameless wo-
man: nine verses only, tell this episode of
her life to unborn generations, yet that
brief history has inspired some of the
grandest Italian painters to depict on their
immortal canvass, and to any one who can
realize her position before the Savior, and
His God-like nature, can never behold that
marvelous delineation without their whole
soul being melted, and the deep fountains
of their heart being broken up. There are
the characters, which, stooping down, he
traced in the sand as they recounted to him
her sin. The Scribes and Pharisees were
her accusers, namely that she was taken in
adultery, the penalty of which in the Jew-
ish laws was stoning to death. Without
raising himself up He commanded "Let
him that is without sin, cast the first stone
at her." The woman is drawn in that re-
markable picture, kneeling with her face
nearly touching the ground; her long and
beautiful hair covers her as with a mantle.
The scene is laid at the moment he raises,
himself up, and looking round finds all have
gone out leaving him and the woman alone.
He then says "Woman where are thine ac-
cusers?—have none condemned thee?" she
saith "none Lord;" Jesus saith unto her,
neither do I condemn thee, go, and sin no
more." "Man sees the deed God the cir-
cumstance," probably the Savior might
know some extenuating features of the
case, at any rate he knew well her accusers
were gross hypocrites, and it was not so
much their horror of her crime, as it was
if possible to entrap him in his words, that
they might bring an accusation against
him, for they were ever seeking for his
life; but he beat them with their own wea-
pons in this case; and their own consciences
would not allow one of them to cast a stone
at her. Another phase of the case is, Je-
sus did not come in the capacity of a judge,

[Column 2]
and he was ever careful not to assume an
office to which He was not ordained, saying
on one particular occasion when they sought
his judgment on a certain case, tempting
him, that they might catch him in his
words; "who made me a judge?" These
words should be a mighty key, to all, both
men and women, not to take the judgment
seat unless they have been legally appoint-
ed to that most critical office. Oh! let us re-
member this august example, when we do
so thoughtlessly take the judgment seat
against our fellow mortals, henceforth let
us put our finger on our lips, and wait the
verdict from the rightful judge.
	The next two characters are, as it were,
blended in less than twenty verses of the
fifth chapter of St. Mark. A Ruler of the
Synagogue, Jarius by name, goeth to meet
the Savior, and falling at his feet, according
to the Eastern fashion, besought him say-
ing, "my little daughter lieth at the point
of death, I pray thee come and lay thy
hands upon her that she may be healed;
and she shall live." He seemed to have
just the right faith; and Jesus went with
him, and a great multitude followed him,
so that they pressed upon him; and a cer-
tain woman who had suffered severe sick-
ness for twelve years, and spent all that she
had with physicians and only grew worse,
came in the press behind him, and grasped
his robe, for she said, if I may but touch
his clothes, I shall be healed; and in a mo-
ment she was made whole! The Savior
looked around and said "who touched me?
for I perceive that virtue has gone out of 
me! The woman then fearing and trem-
bling, knowing she was healed, came and
fell down before him and told him all the
truth, when Jesus said to her, "daughter,
thy faith hath made thee whole, go in
peace and be whole of thy plague." No
name, no place of abode, nothing is told
but a woman sick and afflicted an earnest
woman full of faith and full of devotion,
and her faith saved her. I realize that were
the Savior now in our midst, there are
numberless women among us that would do
precisely the same, and meet the same
glorious result.
	The Savior passed on in his labor of love;
soon the friends meet them, and tell him
trouble not thyself the maid is dead; but
he turning to the father saith, "be not
afraid, only believe thy daughter is not
dead but sleepeth," and passed on till he
reached the room in which she lay appar-
ently dead, and taking her by the hand he
saith unto her, "damsel, I say unto thee
arise! and she arose and walked; and he de-
sired them to give her something to eat.
We seem to linger to hear what she said,
what her parents said, what the Savior said
on that momentous occasion; but no, silence
is the only commentary. Yet there is
much food for reflection, even from these
nameless women; and from this brief but
momentous epoch of their lives. One fea-
ture is ever visible throughout the Savior's
mission; his love and mercy, his gentle dig-
nity, his god-like demeanor, most especially
to women, yes, even the erring, that a 
species of worship is called forth by every
act of his deep and earnest life.
	Now I come to the sisters Martha and
Mary, they must not be separated; the very
difference of their characters, seem to
make the bond between them stronger;
many have supposed that the Savior did
not esteem Martha so highly as he did
Mary, but that is not how I read it, the
Scripture says expressly "Now Jesus loved
Martha and her sister, and Lazarus,"—if
the Savior loved once, it was forever; there

[Column 3]
could be nothing fickle or changeable about
him. Martha exhibits a most admirable
character, an exemplary housewife, a most
hospitable woman, blessed is the house
with such a woman at its head; she was the
intrepid soldier of Christ, she had all faith
in him, she worshipped him; she loved to
entertain him; she was exerting herself to
the utmost, even to over exertion, that she
might honor and bless him with the good
of her household; she was probably some-
what over earnest in the ears of the world,
such a temperament can hardly help it, and
who will censure her, when finding she
cannot accomplish all without help she hur-
ries to her sister and finds her lost to all
worldly cares sitting at the feet of her
Lord, and drinking in his gracious words,
his divine teaching, regardless of all else!
Imagine Martha breaking in upon that
rapt audience with the hasty words, "Lord,
dost thou not care that my sister hath left
me to serve alone? Bid her that she help
me." How "malapropos" these words read,
sounds even upon the most gracious ears.
By the mind's eye, I can see that god-like
face raised to hers, and I feel a sweet and
gentle smile accompanied the words, that
have often been deemed a severe rebuke, I
fancy I hear a low sweet voice saying in
low tones "Martha, Martha, thou art care-
ful and troubled about many things, but one
thing is needful and Mary has chosen that
good part which shall not be taken away
from her." Of course there is a gentle re-
proof runs through them, but how sweetly
they might be said, and whom the Lord
loveth He chastenth,, and I know by an
intuition that He did love Martha, she
was probably acting against His wishes, in
making so great an entertainment for him.
but do I fear his judgment of her? Not in
the least, the Scripture says, he loved her
—that is enough—the wounds of such a 
friend are better than the [-]isses of daily
life! The next episode we have in the life
of these sisters of Bethany, is the death
of their brother Lazarus. They sent unto
Jesus, saying "Lord, he whom thou loveth
is sick!" Jesus said to his disciples "This
sickness is not unto death, but for the glory
of God." And he remained where he was
two days; after that he saith to them, our
friend Lazarus sleepeth but I go that I may
awake him, at last he said Lazarus is dead!
and I am glad for your sakes that I was not
there to the intent ye might believe; let us
go to him. Then Martha, as soon as she
heard that Jesus was coming went and met
him, but Mary sat still in the house. Here
we see the characters of these sisters
strongly depicted, the one, full of life and
energy, the other swallowed up by love and
grief—and perhaps hurt that the Savior
had seemed to neglect them in their hour
of trial, by delaying his coming. Martha
in her warm and loving faith, exclaims,
"Lord hadst thou been here my brother
had not died! but I know that whatever
thou wilt ask of God, He will give it thee,"
Jesus saith, "thy brother shall rise again."
Martha responded, "I know he shall rise in
the resurrection at the last day; Jesus said
"I am the resurrection and the life, he that
believeth on me shall never die; believest
thou this? she saith "yea Lord, I believe
that thou art the Christ, the son of God!"
Then she went and called her sister secret-
ly saying, "the master hath come, and call-
eth for thee," and Mary arose, and went
quickly, and Jesus stood in the place where
Martha had met him; as soon as Mary was
come near to Jesus she fell at his feet say-
ing, "Lord if thou hadst been here my
brother had not died." And when Jesus


saw her sorrow, and those that were with
her, he groaned in spirit, and said, "where
have ye laid him? They say, "Lord come
and see" Jesus wept." Then said the Jews
"Behold how he loved him!" They reach
the grave. Jesus commands them to re-
move the stone, here Martha's faith fails
her, she exclaims, he hath been dead four
days and his body is decomposed. Jesus said
to her, "Saidst I not unto thee that if thou
wouldst believe thou shouldst see the glory
of God? Then he cried with a loud voice
"Lazarus come forth!" At the sound of that
Almighty voice, earth had no power to 
retain her prey, and he with grave clothes
bound, awakes to life.

A WILLING PEOPLE.

	By the most careful and judicious means
is the spirit of willingness and obedience
instilled into the hearts of the young.
Where compulsion is the governing power
of a household, the children are found to
be stubborn and willful, unjust and unrelia-
ble. It has become a well established fact
that to secure loving, cheerful and obedient
spirits for offspring, men and women must
educate and govern themselves according-
ingly, previous to taking upon themselves
the obligations of parentage. Possibly, the
responsibility of parents in this matter, is
even greater before than after the birth of
their children; however, it does not end
here, by any means. The same careful dis-
cipline is still requisite, for both parents
and children, in order to maintain in the
home circle, peace, gentleness and har-
mony which ever arises from a willingness
to do right, and be led not driven.
	There is no Latter-day Saint mother, who
does not desire most earnestly that HER
children may be counted worthy to be num-
bered among the people of God. Who
would not find joy unspeakable in the con-
templation of bearing and rearing sons who
should become noble, godlike men—pro-
phets, priests and kings in the midst of Is-
rael. And daughters pure and lovely
worthy in all respects to be the companions
of God's most righteous servants.
	According to the ancient prophet, the
people of God are to be WILLING in the day
of His power. WILLING, we may believe,
to walk in all holiness before the Lord.
WILLING to serve Him in unity and meek-
ness.  WILLING to abide His laws and keep


His commandments, not in part, but the
whole.
	Mothers in Zion see to it, that you dis-
cipline yourself and your children in such
a way that the truth may be easy for you
and them to comprehend and cling to, be-
cause of your willingness to love and serve
the Lord and forsake all evil. Remember
that the unwilling will not be numbered
among the people of God in the day of His
power.

good logic: after quoting a very touching
paragraph from Mrs. A. Elmore's temperance
article, "Specimen of Work Done Inside,"
the writer, Mrs. Harriet N. K. Goff, goes
on to say, we find no fault with this in-
struction as far as it goes: alluding to "wo-
man's love as a shield," etc., she says,
"But how is the affection of a wife which
simply clings to a besotted husband and
which oozes out the more, the more
he kicks and beats her, to be cultiva-
ted or approved?" She informs us that her

perance work among all classes, and all goes

to become less of the "vine" and more of
the "oak." Mrs. Goff gives some apt il-
lustrations of man's brutality and woman's
deficiences to meet the case, and says: "True
pure shielding love" is just what both hus-
band and wife need, that it must be recip-
rocal; they must work together and not be
continually crying out to each other "hands

AN ESSAY ON FAITH.

	We all know that Faith is one of the first
principles of the Gospel, and is at the founda-
tion of all truth. Certainly without faith we
could not comprehend the Gospel, as it is the
leading principle, and by it we draw near unto
God, our Heavenly Father, who is ready and
willing at all times to hear and answer our
prayers, if we go unto Him in faith, nothing
doubting.
	We are living in a momentous period, and it
behooves us to exercise faith, for if we have
sufficient faith we will not be afraid of what-
ever opposition and ridicule we may have to
encounter from the world, for faith enables us
to rely entirely upon our Father in heaven,
knowing that if we are faithful He will sustain
and uphold us in all trials and difficulties.
	When we look upon the so-called Christian
world what a contrast it presents to the faith
of the Latter-day Saints! they claim to believe
the Bible, and say that it is sufficient for their
salvation, and yet they do not believe in the
gifts and blessings which St. Paul said should
characterize the Church of Christ, but tell us
they are done away, and are not necessary in
our day; but we believe differently, for St. Paul,
in comparing the Church to the body of Christ,
says, 1st Cotinthians, 12th chapter, 23d verse:
"For God hath set some in the Church, first,
Apostles, secondly, Prophets, thirdly, Teachers,
after that miracles, then gifts of healing, helps,
governments, diversities of tongues." St.
Paul further says, in his Epistle to the Ephe-
sians, 14th chap., 12th verse, they were "given
for the perfecting of the Saints, for the work of
the ministry, for the edifying of the body of
Christ, till we all come to the unity of the
faith and of the knowledge of the Son of God,
unto a perfect man, unto the measure of the
stature of the fulness of Christ." I would ask,
"Have all come to a unity of the faith? It cer-
tainly does not look so. We all know we are
enabled by faith to lay hold of the promises
which are given us. Let us, then, try to walk
in accordance with the principles of faith,
truth and righteousness in which we are en-
gaged, and fight agatnst sin and temptation,
or anything of a destroying nature, that would
lead us astray from doing the will of our Fa-
ther.		J. F. Q.
