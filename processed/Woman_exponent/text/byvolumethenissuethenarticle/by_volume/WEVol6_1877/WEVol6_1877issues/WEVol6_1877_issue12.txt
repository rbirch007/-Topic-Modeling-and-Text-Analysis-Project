ODE TO ZION.

Afflicted Zion, rise and shine!
	For thy deliverance now draws near;
The time has come—the chosen time—
	That thou must gloriously appear.

The Lord has brought thee by His hand,
	And sheltered thee beneath the rock:
While all His judgments heavy fall
	On those who at His Gospel mock.

Thy bleating flocks in pastures green,
	In flowery meadows make their bed;
While bounteous sheaves of golden grain
	For very fulness droop their head.

And as the hills around thee stand,
	Thy towering Temples soon shall rise,
And incense burn, and chorus ring
	Continual praises to the skies.

The Lord in thy assemblies waits;
	Shonts of a king amongst them are;
In power and greatness He shall sit
	And judge the nations from afar.

Lift up your heads, ye gathering Saints,
	And sing your great Redeemer's praise:
That you upon Mount Zion stand,
	Amongst the Saints of Latter-days.

		AFTER LONG YEARS.
			LETTER II.

	I'm looking down the vista of long years,
		Watching the shadows as they flit and play
	O'er paths I've trod 'mid hopes and doubts and fears;
		Yet have I gathered trasures by the way. 

	What tender associations of friendship
we can recall when we revert to the past.
If we have been constant and true to the
ties we have formed, there is much of sat-
isfaction in it. And if we have bravely
withstood some severe storms and tempests,
leaving ourselves and the event in the
hands of Him Who rules on high, we can
but be glad and rejoice that He has carried
us safely through. It may be that we have
learned others to protect themselves under
similar circumstances. If so, the satisfac-
tion is doubled. We have not only had an
experience that has developed some of our
noblest qualities, but will be of value to
those who are willing to be guided through
difficult and thorny paths. In early years,
when the intelligence of youth is just open-
ing upon all the beauties of nature and of
life, no thought of sorrow or care has yet
impressed itself upon the mind: dreamy,
vague, indistinct imaginings of a world all
brightness, all beauty, all gladness; and
glowing in the distance yet to be developed,
are charming scenes, too deeply and wildly
grand, which fill the ideal soul with all
their graceful and beautiful imagery. If
this illusion could last, if our lives here
were only a pathway of flowers, then per-
haps it would be well for us to allow our
children, and those who are under our
guidance, to go on blindly.
	How many fond, foolish, doting mothers
there are in the world, who allow their
daughters to pass from childhood, and
emerge into womanhood even, without
having any practical knowledge of what
life contains. Innocent, confiding, un-
sophisticated, trusting implicitly, perhaps
blindly, in the purity and goodness of those
who offer them friendship and warmer at-

[Column 2]
tachments, little dreaming, sometimes even
all unconscious of the snares that are oft-
times set for unwary feet, or the many slip-
pery paths and thorny places through which
the journey of life winds. What a vast
deal of nice discrimination and wise judg-
ment a mother needs to give the proper
training and development to her children
to point out the path of virtue, and help
them to shun error, and discipline them in
duty that will result in happiness. To the
untaught and unguarded the terrific shock
may come unexpectedly, like a thunderbolt,
shaking the tender, trusting soul to the very
centre. How many of those we knew when
young, that petted, fondled and flattered,
have passed along their way as it were
gemmed with the beautiful only, a lnxu-
rious home, indulgent parents, walled about
on every hand with warm love, shielding
them from rough contact with the outside
world, where they might come in contact
with poverty, or sorrow, or care.
	Woman's innocent trust, man's wicked,
and ofttimes cruel betrayal, have been the
under-record of society in the past; is it
not time that after long years of bitter ex-
perience, some of us should learn that there
is a better part for women than to be man's
dupe, or slave, or drudge? Will mothers
ever learn that the training of daughters as
well as sons should be such as to develop
powers that will strengthen character, at-
tributes that will prepare them to put into
practical execution the finest talents they
may possess, so that they may learn how to
live without leaning wholly on, or trusting
blindly to another? It seems to me to be
wholly and radically wrong when girls, even
women, marry for a livelihood, marry be-
cause they are not practical enough to earn
their own bread, instead of choosing their
partner to add to the sum of human happi-
ness, and the higher development of wo-
manhood.
	There have been in the far past some wo-
men of intellect and culture, who sought to
inspire in the hearts and minds of their
contemporaries, a disposition to work out
the problem of woman's independence and
higher elevation—some noble souls who
could not see with the eyes of society—per-
chance through the bitter sorrow and long-
suffering of some one of their progenitors,
this enlightenment had come down to them,
working itself out as the problems of life
sometimes will. "Causes will produce ef-
fects." It is the inevitable law of nature.
And I believe mind will act upon mind in a
certain and positive manner, though we
poor, short-sighted mortals understand it
not. When the Lord said "the sins of the
fathers shall be visited upon the children
unto the third and fourth generation," &c.,
we have every reason to believe this worked
itself out through certain principles known
and understood by the Deity—laws which
pertain to the order of our being.

preached at the re-opening of Kirkhan
Church, rebuked the disposition among peo-
ple to joke on the subject of the Colorado

	WASHINGTON, Oct 23,—Mr. Robert Pur-

Association of Philadelphia, waited upon
Mrs. Rutherford B. Hayes to-day, and pre-
sented to her the address adopted by that

	Mr. Purvis writes: "I have just returned
from a very satisfactory and delightful in-
terview with Mrs. Hayes. She received
me most cordially. I read to her the elo-
quent address from the Woman's Suffrage
Association. She listened with marked at-

ferred upon her, and sent her best wishes
for the success of the cause. I made refer-

honored name of Lucretia Mott, which she
received with a ready acknowledgment of
her great worth and usefulness, pre-emi-
nently distinguished as a reformer and a
philanthropist."

R. S. REPORTS.

	The General Retrenchment Meeting in
the Fourteenth Ward Assembly Rooms on
Saturday, October 13th, was well attended.
Counselor E. Howard presided. The min-
utes of the previous meeting were a feast
in themselves, and especially to those who
had not been present on that occasion. The
minutes of the young secretaries from the
different wards were replete with food for
thought to both old and young. Counselor
E. Howard was the first speaker; in her re-
marks she alluded to the spirit and power
manifested at the late Conference. She
also made excellent remarks upon Celestial
Marriage and its practice among the Saints.
Miss E. R. Snow in her remarks commented
upon the improvement of the young peo-
ple, the hopes she had of their being com-
petent to do the great work which has yet
to be accomplished in building up Zion.
She explained the change in the business
of the Woman's Store: said that instead of
there being a decrease in the business, the
business was all the time on the increase;
she urged the sisters to sustain this institu-
tion, established expressly for the sale of 
home-manufactures, and to aid in develop-
ing the different branches of home indus-
tries, useful and ornamental. Mrs. E. B.
Wells made a few remarks on the advan-
tages enjoyed by the women of this Terri-
tory, and the extended circulation of the
EXPONENT, the good that was being effected
by it, and the representation it gave the
sisters abroad. Also spoke upon the storing
of grain by women, and read the notice of
the annual meeting and an article written
upon the grain subject. Mrs. Jane S. Rich-
ards, of Ogden, spoke upon obedience, and
the pleasure she felt in the work of the sis-
ters, and referred to the sisters' meetings
in Ogden, the good results which had al-
ready followed. Expressed her joy and
gratitude in the progress of the kingdom
of God, and said many good things. Mrs.
E. S. Barney made a few remarks, and read
part of a chapter from the "Women of Mor-
mondom."

	General Retrenchment Meeting, Four-
teenth Ward Assembly Rooms, Oct. 27, Mrs.
M. I. Horne presiding. The meeting was
a large one; after the usual exercises, read-
ing of minutes, etc., the President of the
Senior Association addressed the meeting.
She gave a synopsis of her visit to Bear
Lake Valley, spoke of the pleasure she had
in visiting the societies in the different set-
tlements. She also alluded briefly to the
sorrow of the brethren and sisters on learn-
ing of the death of President Young, which
occurred while she was in Paris. Re-
marked upon the peaceful influence and
heavenly spirit which seemed to rest upon
all who attended the funeral, and contrast-
ed the circumstances of his death with
that of the Prophet Joseph. Elder Joseph
E. Taylor being present was invited by the 
President to address the assembly. His
remarks were upon the present condition
of Israel, especially the youth; exhorted
mothers to wake up on the subject of re-
form among the young.
	Counselor S. M. Kimball made some pithy
remarks, alluding to the existing state of
governments and society. Mrs. Horne
then made some plain statements of the in-
struction which had been givon to mothers,
from time to time, in the meetings, ever
since the organization of the society. Mrs.
W. East made a few remarks upon the sub-
ject before the meeting.

EDITOR EXPONENT:
	The Young Ladies Meetings here in the
Sixteenth Ward have been suspended since
March 27th, as not receiving their portion
of the material for making decorative flow-
ers for the Tabernacle until then, they were
obliged to devote all the time they could
spare thereafter to that work. The meet-
ings are now recommenced. The first was
held on Oct. 25, when after singing and
prayer the roll was called and the minutes
of the last meeting read and accepted.
Counselor Miss Caroline Parry then read a
selection from the EXPONENT, after which
the meeting was addressed by Treasurer
Mrs. M. J. Thompson, President Miss Mary
Williams, and Secretary Miss E. S. Els-
more, all expressing their joy in their re-
union, and willingness to do all that might
be required of them, and expressed an in-
terest in the work of building and minister-
ing in Temples.
	Miss E. S. Elsmore then resigned her of-
fice of Secretary which she held the first
year of the organization as asssistant secre-
tary, and ever since that time as Secretary,
which will be four years January 24, 1878.
Her reason for resigning being that her
place of residence was changed and was
now so far from where they meet, that she
felt she would not be able to perform her
duty as she would wish in that important
office. President Miss Mary Williams then
presented Miss Elsmore in behalf of the
association, the work entitled "The Life of
Brigham Young or Utah and Her Found-
ers," as a token of their appreciation of her
faithfulness in the discharge of her duty as
secretary.
	Miss Elsmore accepted the gift, and said
she had not words wherewith to express
her feelings in this pleasant surprise, and
that words were inadequate to tell how she
would prize the gift, as much in memory
of the donors as for the valuable book itself.
She would regard it as one of her choicest
treasures. Said she had tried to do her
duty, but we do not merit any praise for
doing our duty, as we should do that al-
ways.
	Miss Martha Forsyth was then accepted
as Secretary by the unanimous vote of the
association. It was then moved that the
Association hereafter be known as Mutual
Improvement instead of Retrenchment As-
sociation. This motion was carried unani-
mously.
	 The Association will now meet each
Thursday afternoon at three o'clock p. m.
	MISS MARY WILLIAMS, Prest.
	MISS MARTHA FORSYTH, Sec'y.

				WAITING.

	How sad it is to see a pale weary face
pressed against the window-pane or stand-
ing on the threshold for hours, waiting for
some loved one who does not come, and
how sad to hear the weary sigh of disap-
pointment when, after hours of waiting, one
must turn away for we have waited until
twilight has shut out all objects and the
lamps within are lighted, the curtains
drawn, and we must not look any longer for
we cannot discern any object in the gather-
ing darkness. Now we can only sit and
wait to hear the sound of the footsteps that
have grown so familiar; but it does not
come and perhaps may never come again.

		"Ah! how many wait forever
			For the steps that do not come,
		Wait until the pitying angels
			Bear them to a peaceful home."


Sometimes we grow weary—of life wait-
ing. Waiting for promises that are never
fulfilled; for dreams that are never real-
ized, and hopes that are never gained. Our
lives are made up of waiting, from the 
cradle to the grave; the child waits for the
privilege of being a man, and the man
waits for his rest in heaven, for life has
enough trials and vexations to make us
sometimes wait impatiently for the end
when we can rest. When we are in poverty
we are waiting for prosperity, and when
we gain it, if we ever do, we then wait for
happiness, which wealth alas! does not al-
ways bring. I think though, that there
are a few sweet happy souls who can meet
disappointments bravely, who have learned
that cruel lesson of waiting patiently and
long; and when adversity overtakes them,
wait with a loving patience that is truly
noble, for the blessed sun of prosperity.
But alas! how many go down to the grave
waiting. We must all learn to wait, pa-
tiently if possible, always trusting in the
Alwise Father who overrules all things for
our good.					JUNE.


FIFTH WOMAN'S CONGRESS.

	This Woman's Congress was held in
Cleveland, Ohio Oct. 10, Miss Abby W.
May of Boston, President of the Executive
Committee presided; Dr. Alida C. Avery
of Colorado was elected Secretary pro tem.,
in the absence of the regular Secretary.
There were morning, afternoon and even-
ing sessions, continuing three days. The
papers read by the different contribu-
tors, are all of them deeply interesting to
all candid and earnest thinkers either men
or women. These several papers on the
various subjects discussed published in
pamphlet form and circulated throughout
the United States and Great Britian would 
certainly open the eyes of some of the in-
different on these vital subjects and cause
light to shine in upon the darkness which
exists in the minds of many. When wo-
men of such superior intellect and ability
unite together in a Grand Council the world
may readily anderstand that there is a
meaning attached to it, and they may look
for revolution or reform; for the consequen-
ces are sure to be of a nature characteristic
of the movement and of the instigators.
We are glad to see the women of America
so heroically espouse their own cause and
that of universal freedom and privileges
alike to men and women. One item in Mrs.
Sara J. Spencer's discussion of the paper of
the Rev. Antionette Brown Blackwell, on
"Work Adapted To The Workers," which
was read during the morning session of
the first day, struck us forcibly as being
peculiarly applicable to mothers wives etc.,
it was this. She spoke with considerable
sarcasm of "what some wives and mothers
receive for looking after the interests of
the household, and inquired if treating the
measles and whooping cough is not worthy
of pay what is? She thought the labors
performed by the mother above value, but
believed that a certain portion of the hus-
band's earnings were very justly the
wife's."

CONDEMNATION.

	So she is down; well, do your utmost part
To bring her lower, for you may have found
Some spot they have not stabbed, and the sharp wound
	May reach her shivering heart.
	But wait 'till you are sure that all the pack
Of loud defamers, with one wild accord
	Are fiercely baying at the culprit's back;
Then strike your blow, while thanking the dear Lord,
	That thou art not as she, whom all do hunt,
	And point the finger at with just affront.
We are so perfect, that in dread we cry
	At others' sins, and sometimes at a fault;
	We walk so straight, that if another halt
We cannot pass it by.
—North Hero, Vermont,			—Woman's Journal.


SATURDAY NIGHT.

				SELECTED.

Placing the little hats all in a row,
Ready for church on the morrow' you know;
Washing wee faces and little black fists,
Getting them ready and fit to be kissed;
Putting them into clean garments and white:
That is what mothers are doing to-night.

Spying out holes in the little white hose,
Laying by shoes that are worn through the toes,
Looking o'er garments so faded and thin—
Who but a mother knows where to begin?
Changing a button to make it look right—
This is what mothers are doing to-night.

Calling the little ones all round her chair,
Hearing them lisp forth their soft evening prayer,
Telling them stories of Jesus of old,
Who loves to gather the lambs to his fold;
Watching, they listen with childish delight—
That is what mothers are doing to-night.

Creeping so softly to take a last peep,
After the little ones all are asleep;
Anxious to know if the children are warm,
Tucking the blankets round each little form;
Kissing each little face, rosy and bright—
This is what mothers are doing to-night.

			MORMONISM.

			(CONTINUED.)

	"The Kingdom of God cometh not with
observation." How truly has the thinking,
observant mind realized this Scripture in
the Latter-day Work. When first this
Church of Jesus Christ arose it was not no-
ticed by the Erudite of the world. If the
religious gave it a thought it was only to
sneer and despise; some would glance at it
and say, "What crazy people those are"—
but it grew, and at last enough was thought
of it to raise a mob (even in the boasted
free country of America) to erase it from
the earth! but it "stooped to conquer," and
it did; it adjourned to the wilds of the con-
tinent; it traveled over the prairies and 
the hills, where the foot of the white man
had not been planted; it found a home in
the mountains; its people set to work and
soon made that home beautiful; it became
an Eldorado "in the tops of the mountains,"
where vegetation, spread far and wide, made
prolific by the virgin soil, and breathed up-
on by the Spirit of God! where the rose,
the queen of flowers, calmly took her floral
throne, and waved her lovely sceptre over
her floral kingdom. Travelers soon came,
and marveled at the beauty and the won-
drous utility that had sprung up in the bar-
ren desert, under the persevering hands of
an enterprising people; again realizing
Scripture, that "the desert should blossom
as the rose," and "the fountains of water
spring out of dry places." The pillar of
fire waved above it, though common eyes
saw it not; convoys of angels guarded it
day and night, but the eyes of the world-
ling could not behold them!

	A mighty man was the leader; whose
clear brain and unsinking heart rendered
him indomitable. He knew he was the
servant of God and guided by His hand,
and this gave him strength day by day,
and hour by hour, and made him firm and
invincible. It grew—yes, the Church of
Latter-day Saints grew and spread; it took
a form, it had a voice, a silent eloquence all
its own, that stuck even the dullest ear;
its form became palpable, and the world's
eye was caught at last! though it saw
through a glass very darkly; it began to be
talked about, written about, crowds rushed
to it "as doves to the windows" and enlist-
ed under its banner. These were the 
honest, the poor, yes, and the educated
and the intelligent minds, that were yearn-
ing to be advanced, desiring to break the
shackles that Time had caused to eat into
their very souls—bands, that only Time and
our Father had thrown, as it were, a sacred
mantle over. Simmer down the dogmas
by which they were held; and we find them
literally composed of the debris of the old
Mother Church in a new dress, with less
trappings about it. To-day the eyes of the
world are upon the church in the wilder-
ness, not needing now one of Herschell's
Monster Telescopes to see it! It has become
"as a beacon on a hill;" yes, and a "Heav-
en kissing hill," too! And the readers, and
the writers, and the thinkers, talk, read and
write about it, though they still wear a
sneer and a voice satirical. Ah! one mo-
ment they speak of Brigham Young as a 
marvel among men—"one of the most suc-
cessful men the present generation has
known"—and in the next sentence brand-
ing him as an arch-impostor—"a man of no
education or culture." They get a glimpse
of the grandeur of the man's character, and
they cannot but own it, or stamp themselves
as ignorant dolts, but they try to throw it
into the shade by speaking of his low birth,
his non-education, &c. What dupes the
writers of such articles must be to their own
narrow creed, to believe that a quarter of a
million people of the common-sense popu-
lation of every country, with minds unbi-
ased by the musty dogmas of scholastic jar-
gon, hearts honest, and brains clear, and
quick to receive the truth, with no worldly
emoluments held out before them, but
rather to sacrifice all, if need be, for the
Gospel's sake, could still be the dupes of ig-
norance and imposture! No! no! among
these people, branded as almost imbecile,
there are the "Mute, inglorious Miltons,"
the "Village Hampdens," the "Cromwells,
guiltless of their country's blood," or any
other. When the cloud is dispersed, light
shall issue from these luminaries, that shall

		WOMEN IN REFORM.

	Everywhere people are crying out for reform.
Reform in all circles, in all parties, is declared
positively necessary. The best methods for
bringing about this change of almost all class-
es, of society, and in nearly all countries is
being discussed in public and in private, in
writing and in speaking. It is the theme of
men and of women. They see the great need
but they do not yet find a specific remedy that
can be made practicable, or adapted to the exi-
gencies of the case. Great men and wise men
counsel together, and discuss plans and lay
down rules and regulations, adopt measures,
pass laws, and still the world grows rapidly
worse. Have they forgotten the Scripture that
says positively that "the cup of iniquity shall
be full and running over." Yet there shall be
a people raised up, who will work righteous-
ness in the earth, a chosen people, but these
are so few that they are only spoken of as a
remnant. The law which governs them is to
be the law of God. These are they who are to
build up Zion and redeem the nation. In that
day "men shall run to and fro, and knowledge
shall increase." Is not this the case? Can
anything be plainer than that these things are
being fulfilled? There are many honest heart-
ed and sincere who desire to see a better state
of things and they are seeking to adopt some
wise methods for the cleansing and purify-
ing of society. Many have entered into the
work believing they had a remedy that would
apply and have been disappointed. Some of
the truest hearted and of the soundest judg-
ment have seen the necessity of woman's help,
of woman's voice, of woman's nice discrimina-
tion on the gravest subjects, while others cry
out against her stepping out of her sphere.
Woman is quicker in intuition most men are
willing to admit. Yet they would have her
sit still by her own fireside, while her children
and the children of her neighbors are fast
wending their way to destruction; morally and
spiritually and physically becoming corrupted.
She must sit patiently by and wait for men to
correct these growing evils, and scarcely ven-
ture an opinion lest she should lose caste. In
addition to this she must bear the blame of not
having instilled into the minds of her child-
ren good principles while they were young, or
of some lack of discretion on her part at an
earlier date still; but the father is guiltless, in
common parlance, and yet she must not de-
fend herself in any way unless she can do it at
home—"In her womanly sphere" which men
declare is quite broad enough for her to work
in. How short-sighted are those who are will-
ing to be thus deprived of the blessing of free-
dom that God has given to man and woman
alike. "I do not want to vote, I do not want
anything to do outside of keeping home com-

[Column 2]
fortable." These are the words that we hear
echoed from one to another of the fair sex:
among those who are the good and true moth-
ers and wives. And another class are too much
occupied by frivolity and love of dress; callous
to everything but their own foolish pride and
vanity. Men flatter them, and they feast upon
it, until some day there comes a terrible awak-
ening. But these assertions do not apply to all;
there are many noble exceptions. Women
who are willing to brave the storms, and stand
fast for principle, though public opinion is
against them, sacrificing the finest feelings, and 
even allowing themselves to be deprecated by
persons whose esteem they would fain retain—
yet knowing there are great questions at issue,
at all hazards they lend their utmost aid, leav-
ing the consequences to time, or to "One who
sees not as man sees." The women of this Ter-
ritory have many privileges. They have the
right of Suffrage. Do they appreciate it as
they ought? They have many avenues through
which good may be accomplished, if they can
be made to realize it. The Lord will hold the
sisters responsible for opportunities lost, for has
He not said "where much is given much will
be required?" The women of Utah have some
blessings, social and political, as well as re-
ligious, which the world at large do not enjoy.
If the sisters will sustain, and maintain the in-
stitutions, and enterprises that are even now
open to them, it will not be long before some of
the wise women of the world, will be able to
discern "the better part," and will be willing
to receive knowledge of the laws of life and
measures for reform, from those whom they
now despise and deem ignorant. The Lord is
dealing with the nations, and there are among
them women of such fine intuition that they
comprehend that some steps must be taken;
yet great men, statesmen, diplomatists, and men
versed in law, and in the science of life, moral,
physical and social, stand upon the brink of a
precipice, and while woman reaches ont her
hand to avert the threatening danger—they in
their greatness, their boasted chivalry, refuse
the help, because, forsooth, 'tis offered by wo-
man. Gentlemen, consider well this momen-
tous question of suffrage—only let the trial be
made—have respect to the mothers, wives and
sisters who are now entreating and interceding
that they may help you in the great cause of
universal reform.

	ON TUESDAY, Oct. 30, in the Ogden Taber-
nacle, a Quarterly Meeting of the Weber Co.
Relief Society, was held, appointed at the sug-
gestion of President Brigham Young in July
last. A statistical report of the finances of the
Society was read dating back to the organiza-
tion of each separate branch of the Society in
that county. The condition of each Society
in all special respects was also reported in
writing. These reports in the aggregate,
amount to vast sums that have been gathered
in various ways and disbursed to many benevo-
lent purposes. And the Society in its various
branches still holds in trust means invested in
different enterprises, property, etc., such as
has been deemed proper and practicable. This
meeting appointed by President Young, seems
to be a precedent for other branches of the Re-
lief Society to follow. There is a system about
it and correctness such as should exist in trans-
acting all business whether for benevolent pur-
poses or any other. Every person knows that
if the poor are helped, or the sick taken care of
it all requires funds, and whether it is money
or means of another kind, it is nevertheless
tangible, it is not a myth. And a record has
been kept of all means donated in the order of
the Relief Society. Men have been a little
anxious in times past whether women would
make wise use of means at their disposal, but
we think these meetings, where such perfect
accounts are publicly read would dispel the
doubts of those who are troubled on these
points. The weather was inauspicious on that
day, but the meeting was one of great interest
and we trust there may be many similar meet-
ings held throughout the Territory, that the
most thorough understanding may exist on all
matters relating to the building up of the king-
dom of God. The remarks made on that oc-
casion by the sisters were full of wisdom and
rich with inspiration; and the words of bless-
ing and encouragement which fell from the
lips of Apostle Richards at that meeting were
"like apples of gold in pictures of silver."
Some other items of this report will be given
in our next.

PRESIDENT BRIGHAM YOUNG,
				DELIVERED

	Now I will talk to you mothers. If I were
invited to your house to take supper, or break-
fast to-morrow morning, if you have it within
your reach you will have a platter of meat
cooked, and will put this before your children.
They are hungry, and require something to
satisfy the demands of nature. You place this
before them, and, if they choose, in our coun-
try, they may gorge themselves to overflowing.
You do not stop to ask them if they have esten
sufficient, and ask them now to desist, and eat
moderately. You will let your children eat
green apples and berries of any kind; sit down
and eat fat meat, if they choose it and like it;
and fill their systems with swine flesh, which
is more susceptible of diseases than any other
flesh that we eat. It is not like fish or fowl.
It is susceptible of disease of every kind, and
will impregnate the system with disease far
quicker than any other food that we eat. Now,
mothers, it is well for you to think of these
things. I will tell you how you can enjoy
health. You let your children have a little
milk in the morning. I would prefer putting
it over the fire and boiling it, and put one-
third water in it, with a little flour and a par-
ticle of salt to make it palatable. Give them a
little bread with it—-not soft bread, teach your
children to eat crust—hard baked bread, that
the Americans would call stale, but the Eng-
lish would not. Teach them to eat this, and
to eat sparingly. Instead of drinking un-
healthy water, boil such waters, and let it
stand until it is cool. If the children are in
the least troubled with summer complaint,
and are weak in their bowels, make a weak
composition tea, sweeten it with loaf sugar,
and put a little nice cream in it; and let the
children make a practice of drinking composi-
tion instead of cold water. Mothers, keep the
children from eating meat; and let them eat
vegetables that are fully matured, not unripe,

[Column 2]
and bread that is well baked, not soft. Do not
put your loaf into the oven with a fire hot
enough to burn it before it is baked through, but
with a slow heat, and let it remain until it is
perfectly baked; and I would prefer, for my
own eating, each and every loaf to be no thick-
er than my two hands—can you tell how thick
they are—and I would want the crust as thick
as my hand.
	Now for experience. You see I am creeping
up into years; and I have been from my boy-
hood a person of observation. I have many
and many a time said to children when they
begged for the soft bread, that was not baked
thoroughly, "Look here; you will not live
very long; you will probably come to a prema-
ture grave.” I have noticed invariably the
child that selects the soft bread to be a short
lived person. The children that hunt around
after the crust and eat it, I have noticed en-
dure, live, and continue to live on. Have you
ever noticed this? I have quite aged sisters
here; and I am talking to many that have
children, grand-children, and great-grand-child-
ren, like myself. Have you ever observed this?
If you have not I wish you would commence
to reflect upon it.
	You say you are improving. These societies
are for improvement of our manners, our dress,
our habits, and our methods of living. Now,
sisters take notice, and instruct those who are
not here to-day, to adopt this rule—stop your
children from eating meat, and especially fat
meat; let them have composition to drink in-
stead of unhealthy water; let them eat a little
milk porridge; let them eat sparingly and not
oppress the stomach so as to create a fevar.
No matter whether it is a child or a middle-
aged person, whenever the stomach is over-
loaded and charged with more than is required
it creates a fever; this fever creates sickness,
until death relieves the sufferer. Now the peo-
ple do not think of this. You ought to have
thought of it. I have taught this for years
and years to the people. When we commence
to shape our lives according to the judgment
that is given us, and we exercise a proper por-
tion of thought, and study the laws of life, to
know what to give and how to guide and di-
rect our children and ourselves, we shall find
that the longevity of the people will increase.
Although it is a fact that the longevity of this
people is as great perhaps as that of any other
people at the present time; yet we shall find if
we will hearken to the wisdom our Heavenly
Father has given us, this will increase; and we
shall learn at once that we are enjoying better
health, we shall have greater amount of vital-
ity, and a stronger development of ability, and
by temperance and moderation lay the founda-
tion for the development of the mind. Now,
here let me throw in a side remark. I do not
mean to go without food and go to fasting.
This is the other extreme. A sufficient
amount of food that will agree with the stom-
ach is healthy, and should be partaken of.
Aged or middle-aged, youth or children should
never go without food until their stomachs are
faint, demanding something to sustain their
systems, and continue to undergo this; for this
lays the foundation of weakness, and this
weakness will tempt disease. But keep the
stomach in a perfectly healthy condition. Now
I do not mean fasting, but eating moder-
ately, and if my sisters will go home and com-
mence to adopt this rule, you will find that
you begin to get better, your children and
neighbors will get better. We do not expect
all to be free from sickness. I have had a
great deal of sickness in my life, I do not ex-
pect to be free from the ills, the weakness de-
bility and disease that prey upon the human

[Column 3]
family, but we can amend our ways, and
amend our life by being prudent; and I wish
the sisters to understand this, and to adopt
these instructions; and if you do not learn be-
fore the month of July is gone that your sick-
ness has departed, I shall be very much disap-
pointed. So much for the health of the people.
Will you listen?

﻿OBITUARY:—It becomes our painful duty to
record the death of our beloved sister Eunice
Haynes; who departed this life on Saturday,
Oct. 13. 1877. She died as she had lived, a true
Saint of God, with the hope of a glorious resur-
rection. She was born in the town of Rowe,
Mass., Sept. 28, 1812. She became a member
of the Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day
Saints in the year 1843. In the winter of 1846,
in company with her former husband, Walter
Fuller, and five children, she went to New
York for the purpose of emigrating, with a
company of saints who sailed from that port
on the 4th of February in the ship Brooklyn.
Being disappointed in this, she remained in
New York City four years, during which time
she was left without a companion. In 1850
she with her children, went to St. Louis, Mo.
and in Apr. 1852, she was married to John G.
B. Haynes. She had suffered many privations
and hardships which she bore with patience
and fortitude. She emigrated to Salt Lake
City in the Year 1854; has been a member of
the Relief Society of the Third Ward since its
first organization. She was president of the
teachers, always active in the discharge of her
duties which were faithfully performed. She
was ever ready to minister to the sick, the poor,
and the needy.
	We shall miss her in our little circle, but we
know that “our loss, is her eternal gain.”
		MRS. ELIZABETH WEILER, Pres’t.

	Synopsis of Annual Meeting of the First
Ward Relief Society, held Thursday June
7, 1877.  After the usual exercises, the
President, Mrs. Chase, made some appro-
priate remarks and introduced Mrs. M. I.
Horne to the audience.  Mrs. Horne ad-
dressed the sisters on the subject of home-
industries, referred specially to the silk-
culture and the tannery, also to the saving
of grain, the building of Temples and the
Woman's Store.  Mrs. Prescendia Kimball
arose and said she felt that Mrs. Horne had
just about summed up the whole field of
salvation for the human race.  One thing
we had which we should justly appreciate,
the fulness of the priesthood, the new and
everlasting covenant by which men and
women can attain unto an exaltation.
These principles, which embrace plural
marriage, should be taught to the daughters
of Zion in such a manner that they may
never allow themselves to speak against
them. Mrs. B. W. Smith remarked so
many good things had been said that she
felt refreshed, and the dinner the sisters
had prepared was refreshing too, especially
the strawberries, which were really delic-
ious.  The brethren taught the same things
that the sisters were teaching.  The com-
plete organization of the church is grand.
The Twelve to preach the Gospel to all the
world, and the First Presidency to watch
over all the organizations. The sisters
should teach their children to pray, and to
begin when young to pay tithing.  We
should watch ourselves and guard those un-
der our care faithfully.  We pray to the
Lord to watch over His kingdom here and
fulfill all righteousness.  We must do our
part. That we may be faithful to every
duty and be prepared to dwell in the pres-
ence of God, is my prayer.  Mrs. M. P.
Young expressed her pleasure in being as-
sociated with her sisters; spoke of home
industry and sustaining each other; also of
being in the United Order; that if we were
thus associated it would be like one strong
cord binding us together, and if one started
off to go alone the cord would be weakened,
but each one that is added makes the cord
stronger.
	Mrs. Lydia Ann Wells said she felt the
kingdom of God was rolling on very fast:
she never realized the extent of the work
of the redemption of the dead as she had
since she had been in the Temple at St.
George.  There were heavenly influences
there.  She loved her religion and desired
to live faithful to every principle contained
in it.  She felt she could bear testimony to
the principles of plural marriage, and taught
her children always to respect it.
	Mrs. Martin B. Young bore her testimony
to the work of the Latter days. She de-
sired to lend her influence toward the es-
tablishment of home manufactures. She
was interested in them all, and the silk-
culture was one of the subjects now before
the Relief Societies.  We should do all we
could in that direction as well as lay up
dried fruit, and dried corn which was an
excellent variety of food in the winter sea-
son.  Pickles was another variety that
could be made at home, they were much
better in quality than those imported; for
those from abroad contained adulterations
which were unwholesome.  In Zion things
prepared by our own people were blessed to
us. Mrs. W. East rejoiced in the privi-
leges and blessings of the Holy Gospel;
said she had received the testimony of Je-
sus, and could say as He said on one occas-
ion, these are my fathers, mothers, broth-
ers and sisters who do the will of God.  She
often contrasted her feelings when left to
herself and when filled with the spirit of
God. The work of God is rolling onward,
do not stand still but put your shoulder to
the wheel and help.  Do not oppose any
principle revealed, even though we may not
be able to see it clearly.  The Lord had
shown her and many other sisters the truth
in regard to plural marriage.  A veil is
drawn between us and the future which we
cannot pierce with our natural eye.  Those
who laid the foundation of this principle
have paved the way, yet no man or woman
could bring forth this mighty work alone,
it was and is beyond their power.  Men
and women without the blessing of God
and His assistance could never establish
home industries.  Great and mighty events
are transpiring and are about to transpire,
and in whatever position we may be called
to labor we are blessed.  When the elders
go among the nations the Lord watches
over them; here we are where the Lord has,
blessed us, and let us be wise virgins.
	Mrs. E. B. Wells bore testimony upon
plural marriage, related a little of her prac-
tical experience on the subject.  Said she
had formed many pleasant and tender as-
sociations which she trusted would be last-
ing, and she hoped to be associated with
those (who had in passing through this
school of discipline become so dear to her)
throughout the endless ages of eternity.
That many thought it was a terrible thing,
but she had found happiness in it.  She al-
so spoke on the storing of grain, of the la-
bor and care it would be for the sisters, but
believed they would be blest in it beyond
what had been anticipated.
	Mrs. E. S. Barney thought that the the-
ory of our religion was good, but we must
live it practically in order to receive the
benefit. We are all organized and classi-
fied in ourselves some of us have capacity
and ability for certain duties and others for
other duties, all of use to make up a whole
and perfect organization.  If we would
seek to understand ourselves and study
good books we could enrich our minds very
much, and we have the sentiments in our
own books and papers, our home literature
that is good for the youth and the aged.
We must add to our libraries.  I've made
it a point to read our Church Books, and
there are powerful testimonies in them of
the truth, some of us have abilities that
are not brought into use, we have not had
advantages in the past as we have now.
The Lord will help us if we look to Him
for assistance and guidance.  I once heard
Br. Brigham Young say "what am I expect
what God has made me?"  The Lord will
qualify His servants and handmaidens for
whatever position He calls upon them to
fill. She made some remarks upon home
industries, the land, the mulberry trees,
the Babylonish customs and fashions, re-
marked that the world was filling up the
cup of iniquity.
	Miss E. R. Snow then addressed the
meeting: said she had sat and feasted on
the words of the sisters; many subjects had
been brought forward that were of great
importance to this people.  Remarked we
were not sent here to consult our own con-
sciences.  She herself entered into the
United Order when she received the Gos-
pel; her whole soul was in the work she
had then embraced, and unless she could
live to accomplish something of worth, she
had no desire for life; her heart was in the
work, and yet how little it seemed one per-
son could do in comparison to the great
work to be accomplished.  The prophet Jo-
seph, in his lifetime, had said whenever
the Church was fully organized there would
always be a Relief Society, and every vir-
tous woman should be a member: chastity
was the test.  We are now so organized
that in doing our duty we are a strong
band, and we can accomplish a great deal
in one grand phalanx, and it is necessary
we should be united.  Are we exercising
all our abilities, all our influence for Zion?
We must put our trust in God, round up
our shoulders, and help to accomplish the
Storing of Grain for Zion.  The Lord has
not called upon us too soon; we can't do
too much in the right direction, and at the
right time.  We had our Grain Mission
late last fall, but we made a good beginning.
Miss Snow alluded to a conversation she
had with a brother in regard to storing up
grain, shortly after the mission had been
given to the sisters.  This brother inquired
of her whether she thought it necessary for
the people in the section of country where
he resided to store up grain, remarking
that it was a grain country, an agricultural
district.  Miss Snow answered him by ask-
ing the question whether they had good
crops secured to them for the next fifty
years.  The brother concluded that it would
be consistent for the sisters to store some
grain.  Miss Snow continued:  "We are co-
operating together—our interests are one,
and all tend to the same object, if we are
working with a true purpose in view." She
considered a sister who stepped forward
and assisted efficiently in home industries,
the silk culture, etc., was doing just as
much as an Elder who went forth to preach
the Gospel.  We must be clothed, and we
ought to manufacture our own cloth; and
to-day there is not as much interest mani-
fested in the subject as there was before
machinery was brought into the country.
We must stop this importation of Babylon-
ish goods.  We have not at present the ma-
chinery here we ought to have to aid us in
home enterprises.  Can the sisters do any-
thing in this direction?  How many of the
sisters in this house have homemade ap-
parel on?  Unless we are saved temporally,
it is useless to talk about being saved spir-
itually.  How many of the sisters here are
represented through home-manufacture in
the "Woman's Store?"  We have visitors
coming there from various nations to see
our home productions; we can represent
you there; it is a testimony that they can
take away with them.  They look around
and see the products of our hands.  Miss
Snow said, "You and I, my sisters, have a
responsibility resting upon us, but I do not
want to stand here making repetitions. I
am happy in my duties, because I know I
am working in the right direction.  We,
my sisters, can dispense with a great many
things, if we only think so.  I would nat-
urally like to have everything elegant and
beautiful around me, but I adapt myself to
my circumstances.  We must make our own
ornaments, our own clothing, and beautify
our own houses.  In the Woman's Store we
have the opportunity of bearing strong tes-
timonies; strangers in calling there are con-
vinced we are an industrious people. Many
good, candid people visit us."  Miss Snow,
in closing her remarks, said "God bless you
and strengthen your hands to labor, and
may you become so interested in Zion and
her future welfare, that you may be able to
rise above all the little perplexities and
annoyances of this life."  Miss Snow then
referred to a lady she had met in Athens,
Greece, whose father printed the first edi-
tion of the Book of Mormon.  This lady
had asked her plain, matter-of-fact questions
about plural marriage:  How men supported
many wives, how they lived together, etc.;
said she did not know what she should do


if her husband was commanded to take
other wives. Miss Snow replied to her,
“Supposing you know that the Lord had
appointed you to do a certain work, that
you had actually come here upon this earth
to perform that peculiar work, would you
not forget all about your own selfish feelings
and go to with all your might in order to
fill that mission?” This is the magnanim-
ity women show who enter into plural mar-
riage; and it refines and elevates them, and
is, one of the greatest cultivators in the
world. It helps womem to become sancti-
fied and holy, and have their names asso-
ciated with holy men, with Apostles and
men of God.
	Miss Snow then introduced to the assem-
bly Mrs. E. A. Whitney, who sang “A Song
of Zion."
	Mrs. Chase, President of the Society,
made a few remarks, expressing herself
pleased with the good instructions of the
sisters. Referred to the cultivation of a
spiritual disposition and gave her ideas of
the union that should  exist among the
Saints.  She felt it was good to have help
from her counselors, and to be united with
them in all duties.
	Counselor Margaret Steel said she was
glad to hear the glorious things that had
been spoken; she felt they had good sisters
in the First Ward, who were prompt in at-
tending their meetings. She made some
excellent remarks about the sisters taking
the EXPONENT; said she felt it was full of
the spirit of the Gospel; thought many
more of the sisters ought to take it and
would be blest in doing so.
	Mrs. E. Higgs thanked the sisters who
had spoken, for their good counsel and in-
struction; said she felt blest in doing her
duty, even in the smallest things; rejoiced
in the work of the Gospel.
	Bishop Warburton spoke encouragingly
to the sisters, applauded their good words
and works; acknowledged the help the Re-
lief Society had given him in assisting him
to care for the Ward, and thanked God for
their assistance.  He blest the sisters who
spent so much of their time in going from
ward to ward and trying to strengthen and
help the different branches of the Society,
felt the blessing of God would rest down
upon them; and desired the day to roll on
when Jesus should reign King of nations,
as he now does King of Saints.

A VOICE FROM DIXIE LAND	

Though we in this the southern extremity
of our Territory, this remote, obscure cor-
ner of the earth have been, as it were, shut
out from society and the world in general,
by rugged rocks and barren sand-plains, and
have fancied ourselves secure from the out-
side element, which has been floating
through other parts of the Territory, on ac-
count of the almost impassable roads which
have to be traversed to reach us, the mines
which have been opened up in this vicinity
are beginning to dispel such illusions. They
are, and will be, the means of introducing
into our midst (with the aid of some who
think more of a few dimes than of their
religion, or the well-being of the communi-
ty) the filth and corruption which usually
attend, and I may say are the predominat-
ing  elements of mining camps. Some of
the settlements not far distant from St.
George are already beginning to feel the
effects of their close proximity to these
camps, with their accompanying dens of
vice and iniquity, and their corrupt, de-
graded inhabitants; not but there may be
and are, many honorable men who follow
the occupation of mining, and upon this
class I desire not to cast an unjust, or an un-
fair reflection.
	Up to the present time this has usually
been a quiet little place, with little or
nothing to break the monotony of every-
day life. Many of our young people, who
have lived here since early childhood, know
nothing of the outside world, except what
they have read and heard.
	But will that always be the case? I fear
not; the indications at present are, that
this quiet little nook, where a few years
ago every face we met was familiar, will
soon be overrun with tramps, and a disrep-
utable class of various grades. Now the
question arises, to what class of men will
these terms apply? I will give my version,
though I may be wrong.
 The first is the man who is honorable
enough to show his colors; who is too idle
and shiftless to work and gain an honest
livelihood, and makes the fact manifest by
wandering about from place to place in filth
and rags, obtaining a subsistence as best he
can by sponging, and, when hard pushed,
will perhaps resort to theft; and though
many of them are dangerous characters,
they are no worse than their appearance
would indicate, so that people may be on
their guard.
	Another class, from whom there is much
to fear and dread, are men who make a fine
appearance, whose apparel is faultless, who
are well-behaved, and to all outward ap-
pearances are real gentleman. But oh! if
we could only have a view of the inner life,
what would we discover? A fiendish de-
sire to destroy that which is pure and chaste;
a heart susceptible only of the basest pas-
sions, black as midnight, and callous with
continued disipation and vice, until con-
science has forgotten her duty, and is for-
ever silenced. And is it possible that such
fiends in human shape, fit inmates for the
lower regions, and to become subjects of
his Satanic Majesty, the lowest of the low,
have led astray some of the daughters of
Zion? Yea, verily. And how have they
accomplished their wicked designs?  Often
by subtle hipocrisy; by polite attentions
and studied consideration; by flattery and
vain praise, and in various ways; thus be-
traying gradually and dragging them down
to misery, degradation and sorry, from
whence it is hard for them ever to rise; and
never can they attain their former position
of purity. I believe this, in many instan-
ces, is caused from a lack of proper training
and good counsel and watchcare by parents;
and often, no doubt, from a vain and fool-
ish desire to be noticed and flattered.
	O my sisters! is it for this our parents
have left their homes in far-off countries,
traversed the trackless deserts, and suffered
the privations and hard strife incidental to
the settling this rugged, barren country? to
raise daughters who will disgrace and dis-
honor their gray hairs? And will we, the
young women of the South, who have had
every advantage of being taught better,
who are blessed with a beautiful and holy
Temple in our midst, a sacred buillding 
reared to the name of the Most High God,
a house into which we are privileged to en-
ter and receive blessings which we can re-
ceive no where else on earth, shall we fall
from our high estate and forfeit these great
blessings, and receive condemnation? God
forbid. Rather let our bodies be laid in an
untimely grave, with a spotless, unblem-
ished name, and our spirits return to our
Father, pure as when they came. For what
would life be without virtue? Only a bur-
then and a curse. Then let us, my young
sisters, shun the very appearance of evil,
be very circumspect in our conduct, and
never allow any familiarity, or impropriety
in the demeanor of our associates; let us
hold our name and reputation so sacred that
no spot or blemish shall tarnish them.
	But we know not what we may do until
we are tried by circumstances. But my
prayer is that we may take warning by the
experience of others, listen to the good
counsel which we receive, and remain firm
and true to the gospel of truth, never swerv-
ing from the path of virtue and integrity,
truth and honor, until the end.
	St. George, Oct. 27, 1877. 	KATE.


HER OWN EMANCIPATOR.

	I do wish wish papa wouldn’t frown so every
time I ask him for a little money, I haven't
got a single new article to wear at Mrs.
H-----s reception and it will be such a bril-
liant affair I know, for Judge Harcourt’s
daughter is going to make her first appear-
ance in an elegant satin just imported from
Paris, and the Lancott’s displayed their
new silks expressly made for the occasion,
just to mortify me. Oh dear, what am I
put into this world for if I can’t enjoy it as
others do!"
	Such and similar exclamations find ut-
terance with our gay young girls at every
little disappointment. To them life is a
ceaseless round of sensation, pleasure, gos.
sip and vexations. Their tireless brains
are forever employed in devising new plans
to outrival others in apprearance. "What
will I wear? What will they say? Will
I be admired?" Are the three absorbing 
questions on her shopping tour, during her
social visits, and in her privte apartment 
before the looking glass. Even her lover 
knows how best to possess himiself of her
favor, for he never feails to supply himself
with an abundant store of flattery, so pleas-
ing to such a woman's ear.
 But in justice to those whose idea of life
is this suggested, I must add that in many
this is but the external, the false substi-
tute of that inner life which contains no-
bler thoughts, higher aspirations that the
world dreams of. That inner life is secret-
ed in the recess of her heart undeveloped;
its owner is scarecely aware of its presence
unless occasion calls forth its hidden pow-
er and makes woman what she should be,
strong in her own right, independent and
self-reliant in her undertakings. But those
faculties rarely make their presence felt in
her actual life, for public sentiment frowns
severely on any woman who dares venture
out of her supposed sphere. They are not
allowed to think or act for themselves; how
could they if htey figured as mere slaves
and toys in the eyes of men? And if for-
merly a solitary wall or protest agaisnt this 
injustice was heard, and known to strike
the chord of sympathy in the kindred
hearts of their sister sufferers, it was quick-
ly smothered by the overwhelming voice of 
public opinion that despotic power which
has so long held women in subjection.
     But a new era has dawned for women.
What was years ago whispered in privacy
under the seal of secrecy, is now publicly
discussed in society; womans inner, nobler
life is coming to the surface, and, in spite
of every obstacle, developing itself with
marked rapidity. Emboldened by the con-
sciousness of their ability they are gradu-
ally rising one by one to appreciate and de-
mand their rights. The doors of universi-
ties and colleges are yielding to the per-
sistent demand for admission; the fields of
industry and progress are open for the ex-
ercise of their ingenuity and skill and wo-


man, single handed and alone, can in every
avenue of labor work her way with profit
to an independent, comfortable position.
T' is true, a woman's first attention should
always be directed to the home circle, but if
she has no domestic affairs to attend to,
should she then in that case be supported
in idleness by the industries of others?
She has a right to engage in business where
her talents may be a source of benefit to
the community; and being in business,
paying taxes, ought to give her an equal
right to the privileges of the ballot.  Of all
the wrongs against which women have bat-
tled, taxation without representation is the
greatest and most difficult to remove.
	But, patience! the hour of deliverance
is near.  The early advocates of womans
rights are slowly but determinedly filing
away at the last link of that long chain of
difficulties which has been their work for
half a century to remove.  When that link
is severed, and those fetters mingle with
the dust at her feet, woman will stand be-
fore the world emancipated.  But to her-
self, and not to men, must she look for the
result.		BERTIE A. WINKLER

DISSIPATION.—There are three kinds of
dissipation in the world—white red, and
black dissipation. White, dissipation is the
waste of nerve and excessive use of the
brain; there are pious dissipation, scholarly
dissipation, business dissipation, the disi-
pation of the men who, with salutary shud-
ders, “thank God that they are not as oth-
er men are.” Bed dissipation is the dissi-
pation of increase of blood by luxurious
food, or the waste of blood by all those pass-
ions and indulgencies which come of luxu-
rious living. Black dissipation is that of
the grosser indulgencies which criminal
men seek. The first named is increassng
rapidly and taking the place of the lower
grades. The peculiar folly of our age is the
waste of nerve force. In all directions we
see the sign of brain dissipation. The dis-
eases of virtuous men are no longer what
they used to be—blood diseases. Paralysis
is beating up in every community and find-
ing recruits. Good men are living too fast.
Each man is swept up against his will into
the tumult of life. The great army of in-
somnists is increasing, and such men have
a call to lunacy.


HOUSEHOLD HINTS.

	New Mode of Washing.—In the “Moniteur
Scientifique” Dr. Queonville describes something
new in washing, whereby our linen may be saved
from the destructive effects of soda and other
washing-powders. The plan has been extensively
adopted in Germany, and introduced into Belgium.
The operation consists in dissolving two pounds of
soap in about three gallons of water as hot as the
hand can bear, and adding to this one tablespoonful
of turpentine and three of liquid ammonia; the
mixture must then be well stirred, and the linen
steeped in it for two or three hours, taking care to
cover up the vessel containing them as nearly her-
metically as possible. The clothes are afterwards
washed out and rinsed in the usual way. The
soap and water may be re-heated and used a sec-
ond time, but in this case half a tablespoonful of
turpentine and a tablespoonful of ammonia must
be added. The process is said to cause a great
economy of time, labor and fuel. The linen
scarcely suffers at all, as there is little necessity
for rubbing, and its cleanliness and color are per-
fect. The ammonia and turpentine, although
their detersive action is great, have no injurious
effect upon the linen; and while the former evapor-
ates immediately, the smell of the latter is said to
disappear entirely during the drying of the clothes
