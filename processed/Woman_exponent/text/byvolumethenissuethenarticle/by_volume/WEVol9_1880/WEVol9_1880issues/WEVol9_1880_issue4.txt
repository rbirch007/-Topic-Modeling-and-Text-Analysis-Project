DREAMLAND.

How often doth the tir'd and weary soul
Long to burst the care-chains which control
Its every impulse. When worn with grief,
What brings to weary hearts that pure relief
Which springs untainted from affection's fount?
Moments of sweet joy o'er which we count
As counts the greedy miser o'er his gold?
And on the crowded Bark of Life are rolled
Adown the dim and winding stream of years,
Freighted alike with pleasures and with tears.
What tones the harshest feelings of this life
To kindness? Forbids unceasing strife?
Softens pain? pillows sickness into sleep?
While o'er the senses there doth gently creep,
A nameless calm—a sweetness of repose,
Which but to feel we'd bear unnumber'd woes?
'Tis Dreamland—That oasis in Life's waste;
That freedom of the soul in which we taste
Again the pleasures and the joys of youth—
Unblemish'd love of beauty and of truth.
In dreams the soul, unburden'd of its care,
Floats on the wings of pleasure through the air.
With what unbounded joy it takes its flight,
And soars into those blissful realms of light.
Where the mind, untrammel'd, unconfin'd and free,
May revel in its own wild ecstacy;
And with a mother's fondness lingers o'er
Each infant joy (now lost forevermore)
As it lies cradled in the lap of Time,
In innocence and beauty half sublime.
In dreams, perhaps, the spirit views again
The little stream that wander'd through the glen;
The dear old cot, round which the woodbine clings,
And through long, weary years its fragrance brings;
The grassy spot, fair fields, the shady nook
Close seated near some softly murmering brook;
Moonlight bowers, bright skies, and waters clear;
The hum of birds, and songs it loved to hear;
And lonely walks, through woods and by the sea,
And happy meetings full of mirth and glee;
While at each turn it takes it seems to meet
The well-known faces that it loved to greet,
And deeper joys fill up each place
Like bright stars midst the dim in heaven's space;
And dreams, desires, and aspirations high,
And visions of bright hopes flit swiftly by—
All mingled in one glad, one joyous throng,
That bears the soul in ecstacy along.
Thus like the little honey bee that sips
Its garner'd sweetness from each flowr'ets lips,
The s[-]ul culls from the joys of by-gone years
A tenderness which this frail life endears.
*	*	*	*	*	*	*
O, Dreamland! Thou sweet haven of pure joy—
The only one man knows without alloy—
To thee we turn when sorrow bows the heart—
When willingly with this poor life we'd part—
To thee we turn, with many a weary sigh
And taste of pleasures that can never die.
Joy follows joy—each in succession gleams,
And from thy fountain well life's dearest dreams;
In thee we find what life can ne'er attain—
We feel a joy unmingled with a pain.

MINERAL SOUVENIRS.—Mr. and Mrs. Wiliam
C. Folger have received from Mrs. Elmira P.
Tufts, a lady friend in Salt Lake City (to whom
they had sent a piece of Plymouth Rock and
other New England relics,) a collection of miner-
als, including a gold lump weighing 1-2 ounce,
dug from a mine in Tiutic District,Utah, a lump
of silver ore, specimens of gold quartz from
mines in Park City, Utah, crystalized salt from
Great Salt Lake, and salt obtained from the
mountains, iron ore, &c. &c., and a piece of gra-
nite rock from the Mormon Temple. Mrs. Tufts
has discovered that she is a descendant from
Tristram Coffin, and is very enthusiastic on the
re-union matter, sending the membership fees
for herself and entire family.

    BY HELEN MAR WHITNEY.
	On the 14th day of September, 1839, Presi-
dent Brigham Young left his family at Mont-
rose, and was brought by Brother Israel Barlow
to my father's house, where he remained sick
until he started with father on their mission to
England. He left his wife with a babe only ten
days old, and all his children were sick and
unable to wait upon each other. On the seven-
teenth Sister Young got a boy to carry her up
from the river in his wagon to our house that
she might nurse and comfort her husband to
the hour of starting. My mother’s babe was
three weeks old, and she was sick with chills
and fever. The day before they started my
father had two very heavy shakes of the ague,
and was very sick through the night. On the
morning of the eighteenth Brother Charles
Hubbard sent his wagon and span of horses with
driver, and their trunks were put into the
wagon by some brethren who had come to bid
them good bye. Previous to starting, while they
were taking breakfast, father got Brother Hub-
bard and another brother to cut down an old
hollow tree, which hung over the house, it had
worried him so that he could not bear to leave
till he saw it felled to the ground; when he
heard it fall he said, at the same time rising
from the table, "Now I am ready to go.” The
parting scene is best told by himself. He says:
"I went to the bed and shook hands with my
wife, who was shaking with the ague, having
two children lying sick by her side; I embraced
her and my children, and bid them farewell; the
only child well was little Heber Parley, and it
was with difficulty he could carry a two-quart
pail full of water from a spring at the bottom of
a small hill to assist in quenching their thirst.
It was with difficulty we got into the wagon and
started down the hill about ten rods; it appeared
to me as though my very inmost parts would
melt within me; leaving my family in such a
condition, as it were, almost in the arms of
death; it seemed to me as though I could not
endure it. I said to the teamster, "Hold up." 
Said I to Brother Brigham, "This is pretty
tough, ain't it? Let's rise up and give them
a cheer." We arose and swinging our
hats three times over our heads, we cried
Hurrah! Hurrah! Hurrah for Israel!" Vilate
hearing the noise arose from her bed and came
to the door; she had a smile on her face and she
and Mary Ann Young cried out to us, "Good
bye, God bless you." We returned the compli-
ment and then told the driver to go ahead.
After this I felt a spirit of joy and gratitude at
having the satisfaction of seeing my wife stand-
ing upon her feet, instead of leaving her in bed,
knowing as I did that I should not see them
again for two or more years." Although too
young to sense the deep anguish which our par-
ents felt yet we children wept bitterly when our 
father came to bid us farewell, not knowing that
we would ever see him again in the flesh. Both
he and Brother Young were going away so sick
they were unable to get into the wagon without
assistance. The scene is so vivid before me that
my eyes are blinded with tears as I try to write;
but words fail to describe it. Our grief for a
time was very great, but the knowledge that
they were messengers of the Almighty to carry
glad tidings to those who were in darkness that
they also might be partakers of the blessings of
the Gospel of salvation, sustained those who
were left. The hymn containing these lines
was often sung by us, and appropriate words
they were in our desolate condition and they
brought sweet comfort:
	"In every condition, in sickness, in health,
	 In poverty's vale, or abounding in wealth;
	 At home or abroad, on the land or the sea,
	 As thy days may demand, so thy succor shall be.
	 When through the deep waters I call thee to go,
	 The rivers of sorrow shall not thee o'erflow,
	 For I will be with thee thy troubles to bless,
	 And sanctify to thee thy deepest distress."

The following sketch will show how the Apos-
tles went forth to preach the gospel. Father
says, "we were without purse or scrip, and were
carried across the prairie, about fourteen miles,
to a shanty near the railway, where Brother O.
M. Duel lived. We were unable to carry our
small trunks into the house; Sister Duel, seeing
our feeble condition, assisted the boy to carry
them in; we were very much fatigued. As soon
as we got into the house she made us a cup of
tea, which revived us, and prepared a bed for us
to lie on.	* 	* 	*	 *	* Next morning
Brother Duel took us to Lima, about twelve
miles. When he left us, he gave each of us a
dollar to assist us on our journey. Brother Bid-
well then carried us in his wagon to John A.
Miksewell's, near Quincy, about twenty miles.
The fatigue of this day's journey was too much
for our feeble health. We were prostrated and
obliged to tarry a few days in Quincy, when we
began to recover. The brethren preached a few
times in a meeting house close to the Congrega-
tionalist church. They commenced their
meeting at different hours from the brethren,
therefore they took a notion to disturb us by ring-
ing their bell furiously after we had commenced
our meeting. At one time Elder John E. Page
preached so loud as to drown the noise of the
bell, and thus brought some hundreds who
otherwise would not have come to meeting. I
was prostrated with the chills and fever, and
stayed most of the time at the house of Sister's
Laura and Abigail Pitkin, who bestowed every
kindness upon me they possibly could. Dr. Or-
lando Hovey, Sister Staley and her daughter
were very kind to me, and administered to me
in my feeble state.
	September 25th. We left Quincy at 11 a.m.,
feeling considerably better. My sorrow was great
to see so many of our brethren sick and dying,
in consequence of being driven from Missouri,
and exposed to hunger and cold. Brother Ly-
man Wight took us in a one-horse wagon and
carried us to Brother C.C. Rich's,at Burton,where
we stayed through the night. Brother Wight
predicted many things, and left his blessing
with us when he bade us farewell. May God
bless him and save him in His kingdom.
	26th. Brother Rich carried us to Brother
Wilbur’s; while on the road the chills came on
me again, and I suffered much pain and fatigue.
	On the 27th Brother Wilbur took us in a
buggy about 25 miles, to the home of James
Allred, in Pittsfield, Ill. 
28th.—Father Allred carried us to the place
where Brother Harlow Redfield lived, where we
preached to a small branch of the Church on
Sunday 29th.
	30th.—Brother Rodgers carried Brother Brig-
ham to Brother Decker's, and myself to Mr. Ros-
well Murray's, my father-in-law, they were liv-
ing within a few rods of each other, near Win-
chester, in Scott County. Here we also found a
few brethren in the church, who had been
smitten and robbed of their property in Missou-
ri; they were once more in comfortable circum-
stances, rejoicing in the Lord.
	October 1st.—We were carried to Lorenzo D.
Young's, a brother of President Young, where
we stayed and recruited our strength until the
4th, when he carried us to Jacksonville, where
we stayed the night. A sister in the Church
hired a horse and buggy to carry us to Spring-
field, and Brother Babcock drove us there, a dis-
tance of 35 miles, where we were kindly received
by the brethren, and nursed. Brother Brigham
was confined to his bed by sickness; Brother
Libeus T. Coon, who was practicing medicine,
attended upon him. Here he found Brothers
George A. Smith, T. Turley and R. Hedlock. I
went from house to house strengthening and
comforting the brethren, and teaching them the
things of the kingdom. I was so far recovered
that I preached on the Sabbath, which caused a
great feeling of love towards us; they got a two
horse wagon and harness, for which they paid
fifty-five dollars, and collected thirty-five dollars
in money for the company. Judge Adams, one
of the Judges of the Supreme Court, of the State
of Illinois, took me to his house. I stayed
with him three nights and the most part of
three days. He gave me five dollars when I


[Column 1]
left. I think he will come into the Church soon.
My father-in-law went with me to visit his
family and friends in the East.
	October 11th.—Resumed my journey in com-
pany with the brethren. We exchanged horses
in Springfield; and with the assistance we re-
ceived from the brethren living there, we suc-
ceeded in obtaining one horse and a two horse
wagon, in which the sisters fitted up a bed for
Brother Brigham to ride on, as he was unable to
sit up. We traveled eight miles with the three
horse team, and put up at the house of Father
Draper.
	October 12th.—We pursued our journey to-
wards Terre Haute, traveled all day; most of
the brethren being very sick, I walked most of
the way, at night I slept in the wagon with Fa-
ther Murray and Brother Hedlock and caught
cold; next morning I had to go until twelve
o’clock before I had any thing to eat, and then
it was transparent pork and corn dodger; the
wagon broke down twice, and the chills came
on me about two in the afternoon, and held me
till night, and then the fever held me all night.
I had the chills and fever three days, and lost
my appetite.
	The third chill was so severe that it seemed
as though I could not live till night. We arriv-
ed at Terre Haute about dusk on the 17th. Bro.
Young and I put up at Dr Modisett’s. Brothers
Smith, Turley, Hedlock and Father Murray
staid at Milton Stowe’s, who lived in one of the
Doctor’s houses. In the evening the Dr. went
to see them, as they were quite ill in health, and
Brother Stowe very poor. The Doctor expressed
great sympathy for them when he returned to
the house, relating over the poverty of Brother
Stowe and the brethren’s ill health, and seeing
them lying on a straw bed on the floor, he shed
many tears to see the brethren going under such
suffering circumstances upon such a long, mis-
sion; but he did not have quite sympathy enough
to buy them a chicken to make them some
broth, or even give them a shilling, although he
was worth four or five hundred thousand dollars;
he said his taxes amounted to over four hundred
dollars a year. 
	In the evening I became very ill. The Dr.
said he could give me something that would do
me good, that would relieve me of my distress,
and I would probably get a nap, but the old man
was so drunk that he did not know what he did,
and he gave me a tablespoonful of morphine; his
wife saw him pour it out but dared not say a
word, although she believed it would kill me.
In a few minutes after I took it I straightened
up in my chair complaining of feeling very
strange and wanted to lie down, on my attempt-
ing to go to bed I reeled and fell to the floor.
There was hardly a breath of life in my body.
Brother Brigham rolled me over on my back and 
put a pillow under my head and inquired of the
Doctor what he had given me and then learned
that he had given me morphine. I lay there
for a long time; when I came to, Brother Brig-
ham was attending to me with a fatherly care,
and manifesting much anxiety in my behalf. I
said to him “don’t be scared,for I shan’t die." In
a short time he got me on the bed and nursed
me through the night.			
It was through the closest attention of Brother
Young and the family that my life was preserv-
ed through the night. I was scarce able to speak
so as to be understood. In the morning the
brethren and Father Murray came to see us.
The brethren laid their hands on me and prayed
for me. When they left me they wept like
children. Father Murray felt very sorrowful,
said he, "We shall never see Heber again, he
will die."  I looked up at them and said, "never
mind brethren, go ahead, for Brother Brigham
and I will reach Kirtland before you will."
Brigham gave them all the money we had ex-
cept five dollars, and told them to take good care
of the team and make all possible speed to Kirt-
land.  They started that day.  In about an hour
after they departed I arose from my bed.
	October 22nd. Elder Babbit and Dr. Knight,
an eminent physician, came from Pleasant Gar-
den to see me.		*	*	*	*	Brother 
James Modisett took us in his father's carriage
twenty miles to the house of Brother Addison
Pratt, from thence we were carried to Plea-
sant Garden. We found a few brethren,
and remained there three days preaching to
the brethren, and those who wished to hear.
*	*	*	*	*	Dr. Knight and some
others gave us some money to assist us on our
mission.

wife giving an account of her sickness since I
left, also of the children William and Helen. I

	26th.—Brother Babbitt took me in his buggy
twelve miles, to the house of Brother Scott; they
were very glad to see us, and we tarried with
them through the night.
	27th, Sunday.—Brother Scott sent his little
son John, who carried us to Bellville, fifteen
miles, several miles of the journey in a rain
storm, which obliged us to put up at an inn for
the remainder of the day and night.  Brother
Brigham was very sick and obliged to go to bed.
I sat up to wait upon him, and spent the even-
ing with the landlord and his lady preaching
to them; they received our testimony and were
very kind to us.
	28th.—The landlord rose up very early and
talked to the citizens abou the travelers who
had staid with him the night previous, and what
he had heard us say concerning the Gospel. The
neighbors flocked in, made many inquiries and
were very anxious that we should tarry and preach
in the place. The landlord said several times he
hoped the stage would not come, that we might
stay and preach, as the people were very
much excited by having had a great discussion
between two popular religious preachers recent-
ly. The stage, however, came along about 10
o'clock, and we started on our way towards Kirt-
land, and we left the landlord in tears. While
in Pleasant Garden we obtained some money, so
that with the five dollars we had left when the
brethren left us on the 18th, now amounted to
$13.50.  When we got into the stage we did not
expect to ride many miles. We rode as far as
Indianapolis, Ind., paid our passage, and found
we hd sufficient means to carry us to Rich-
mond, Ind. When we arrived at Richmond, we
found we had means to take us to Dayton, to 
which place we proceeded and tarried over
night, waiting for another line of stages. We
expected to stop here and preach until we got
means to pursue our journey. Brother Brigh-
ham went to his trunk to get money to pay the
bill, and found that we had sufficient to pay our
passage to Colombus, Ohio, to which place
we took passage in the stage and tarried over
night. When we paid the bill, we found he had
sufficient means to pay our passage to Wooster.
We tarried until  after part of the day and
then took passage for Wooster. When we ar-
rived there, Brother Brigham went to his trunk
again to get money to pay our bill and found
enough to pay our passage to Cleveland. When
we got to a little town called Strongsville, about
twenty miles from Cleveland, towards evening,
Brother Brigham had a strong impression to stop
at a tavern when we first came into the town,
but the stage did not stop there, and so we went
on.  We arrived at Cleveland about eleven
o'clock at night, took lodgings, and remained
until next evening. November 3d, Sunday, in
the morning, we went to the Episcopalian
Church. While returning to the hotel, we met
my father-in-law and learned that Elders Smith,
Turley and Hedlock had just arrived in Cleve-
land. Father Murray was as as much astonished
to see me alive as though he had seen one risen
from the dead. I don't think I ever saw a man

and met the brethren, who were in good health,
compared with what they had been, and in fine
spirits. We learned that they stopped at the

the prediction made on my sick bed. Brother

﻿FAITH AND FRIENDS.

Life's troubles are transient, only
	They are often a needful test,
Nor long will the brave be lonely
	Who with faith and with friends are blest.
God knows that we need refreshing,
	For sorrow He makes amends;
And He thus bestows the blessing
	Of faith and of thoughtful friends.

Oft the roughest roads lead upward,
	And the heavens they're nearer too;
So we'll trust our Father's purpose—
	For 'tis always kind and true.
We'll trust Him ever and always,
	Though to death He brings us nigh;
Though by ways unknown He leads us—
	Nor shows us the reason why.

From the crown of the hill, Experience!
	Are views that are always bright—
They always are wise who climb it,
	But oh! 'Tis a rugged height
Our God we must needs confide in,
	He'll lend us a helping hand,
We'll see as we're upward climbing
	A glimpse of "the holier land."

The way for the weak and weary—
	Is shorten'd and so 'tis best.
We know if we trust God's guiding
	We'll enter his promised rest.
If long or if rough life's journey—
	He is safe who on God depends;
He is rich who has yet the blessing—
	Of faith and of thoughtful friends.

					EMILY HILL WOODMANSEE.
S. L. City, July, 1880.

MARY CHAWORTH.

BY HANNAH T. KING.

	Annesley Hall was the paternal home of Mary
Chaworth, it is a short distance from Newstead
Abbey, the ancient possession of the Byrons;
both are situated in Nottinghamshire, England;
the estates join each other, and have been made
famous by the graphic pen of the poet. Mary
Chaworth was an heiress, and is described as
having much personal beauty, and a disposition
the most amiable and attaching. Lord Byron
had been introduced to her family in London,
and on taking up his residence at Newstead,
and after coming to the title, he renewed his
acquaintance with them. He was then only
sixteen, and the young heiress two years older.
His visits were frequent and familiar; a room
being appropriated to him whenever
he thought proper to make use of it.
A few happy weeks were passed riding,
boating on the lake, shooting at a given
mark, and such like, in which Mary always
made one, Byron being through all her devoted
Esquire. This short period of about six weeks
was probably the happiest, the sweetest, and the
least mixed with alloy of his short, but full life;
In them he imbibed a deep-seated passion for his
young hostess, that was to color all his future
life, and only bring to him bitterness and disap-
pointment. He writes in his diary of this time:
"When I was fifteen we formed a party to visit
Matlock, in Derbyshire; when in that most re-
markable cavern, where you have to cross a
stream, only two persons can occupy the boat at
the same time, and that in a reclining position,
this stream flows under the rock, with the rock
so close upon the water as to admit the boat only
to be pushed through the chasm by a ferryman,
(a sort of Charon) who wades at the
stern, stooping all the time; the
companion of my transit was M. A. C.
with whom I had been long in love, and never
told it though she had discovered it without. I
recollect well every feeling of those moments
but cannot describe them,let them pass. There
were five besides myself,and my M. A. C. Alas!
why do I say my? Our union would have sealed
feuds, in which blood had been shed by our an-
cestors! it would have joined lands, broad and
rich, and two persons not ill-matched in years
(she is two years my elder) united two hearts,
and perhaps strengthened one head, which by
the bye has never been quite right since! and
what has been the result?" These are his own
words.
	It seemed the lives of these two persons were

[Column 2]
mysteriously interwoven, yet destined never to
culminate; Byron has converted all the scenes
of these times into that beautiful, sad, but
graphic poem.—"The Dream"—That is the
strongest, the most thrilling and the briefest
biography ever written in the English language!
her name would not now be known but for the
immortality that this boy-lover gave to it, "even
now she loved another" and it seems she had no
prescience of mind to see, or comprehend the
brilliant destiny awaiting him; but prefrred the
associate of grooms and jockeys, to the poet-boy
who was hereafter to stand before the world the
cynosure of all hearts and eyesl aud alas! the
sequel was the old, old tale, neglect, and deser-
tion, by him to whom she gave her wealth, and
above all herself, who squandered her riches in
reckless extravagance, with horse-racing and its
usual concomitants. She awoke from her fal-
lacious dream to find the gay "John Musters"
over whom she had so fondly thrown the
mantle of her early love, was merely the Hero of
the steeple chase prominent on the race course,
the idol of grooms and jockeys, and men of ques-
tionable character, Yclept "Gentlemen"!—while
"the boy" she had seen no glory in, had become
a nation's glory associated with the royalty for
which mental wealth forms and bestows the
diadem.
	She sent for him and requested an interview!
but to his honor be it spoken, he declined that
which must have been distressing to both: No!
his love had been to him no child's play, no
dalliance with coquetteries, but a passion that
had drugged his soul with bitterness, thrown
his life under a cloud, so pure, as never
after to be trifled with so sacred, that it
seemed the very wedding of the spiritual, and
the eternal. He had passed from out that old
Hall never again to enter it.
	It is perhaps but just to offer excuses for her—
she was an heiress—and the petted, child of
adulation, which wealth engenders—she was
two years his senior which at the early age
they met, making a girl far ahead of a boy of
sixteen or less, he is then merely a being for her
to patronize; to trifle with, and this was her
course with Byron, severe was the penalty; her
mind, never a strong one, became by her sorrow-
ful change, "The Queen of a fantastic realm.
Her thoughts were combinations of disjointed
things, and forms impalpable and unpercived of
others sight familiar were to hers."
	Byron's name alone gives her half the laurel
which o'er shades his grave.


A FEW OF MY THOUGHTS.

	While reflecting upon the present state of so-
ciety and the prevailing sins and vices of the
world, we are sometimes ready to exclaim, with
the Prophet, "How is the gold become dim;
how is the most fine gold changed." How
often are our ears made to tingle and our hearts
to grow sad at the fearful accounts of crime and 
misery that abound throughout the length and
breadth of the land. While we read of the reve-
lations made by a Talmage and are shocked by
the horrible crimes prevalent. While dens of
infamy and debauchery flourish under the pro-
tection of the law, and the vile seducer of inno-
cence walks abroad with unblushing front
amongst the rich and honored of the land, while
the poor victim of his flattery and deception,
whose only crime was "loving not wisely, but
too well," and who has yielded to him all that
woman holds most dear (her purity) is forsaken
and cast out to be shunned and scorned by her
own sex and only sought by the opposite for the
gratification of the basest of passions and be cast
aside till she becomes a loathsome and degraded
thing unfit for the society of man or woman,
till in her agony and despair she is frequently
driven to commit the fearful crime of self-de-
struction, or perhaps thinking to hide her shame
to cover her guilt, she herself tramples on the
best law of our nature and becomes the destroy-
er of her hapless offspring, or vice robed in
splendor displays her gaudy garments at the
corners of the streets. While industrious and
suffering virtue is too often left to suffer and die
uncared for and unknown. Truly we are ready
to veil our faces to shut out the fearful picture
and to cry out in anguish of spirit;
Is there no balm in Gilead? Is there no physi-
cian there? Why, then, is not the health of my
people recovered? Surely the Lord will find a 
remedy, or the nation that is guilty of these
things will be followed by a strange punish-
ment, and be cut off; a fit reward for their deeds.
But who are they who are the first to cry out
against the revelations and institutions of the
Lord? Is it not the seducer, the debauchee, the


vile libertine, who panders to the lusts and pas-
sions of men? But these are not all. How
many of those whose elevated position, and su-
perior advantages of education and refinement
makes them a power in the land, who should
be the guardians of purity and virtue, and many
who profess to be followers of Him who is of purer
eyes than to behold iniquity; in whom was found
no sin, yet do not even they, the teachers of a
false system of religion, cry “Peace, Peace,”
when there is no peace, calling light darkness,
and darkness light, putting bitter for sweet and
sweet for bitter.
   "These are they who creep into houses" (hypo-
crites,) who, under the guise of religion, gain the
confidence of the unsuspecting, and taking ad-
vantage of the influence thus acquired, by the
basest of arts, "lead captive silly women."  Oh,
beware of them; trust not their promises; the
poison of asps is under their lips; the garland is
cankered; there is death in the cup that they
offer; the pillow is burning. May the day speedi-
ly arrive when woman shall understand her true
destiny; when she shall dare arise in the
strength of her purity, and say to the vain seek-
er of the pleasures of an hour, get thee gone! I
have nothing to do with thee. Then would the
libertine and profligate in vain seek for a victim.
Hasten the day of the Lord when righteousness
shall cover the earth.
			P. C. Y., 2d Ward, S. L. City

over the allotted three score years and ten.

the most efficient and courteous committees

Rock (via) the Utah Western Railway to spend
the day in social converse etc., on Thursday
July 8th, inst. They were gathered from all the 
various parts of the city, and the local regions
roundabout, in carriages, wagons and street cars.
They were accompanied by Pres. John Taylor,
Apostle Wilford Woodruff our aged veteran sisters
Elizabeth Ann Whitney and Eliza R. Snow
Smith, some other invited guests; a few news-
paper reporters and some musicians were also
members of the party.

M. There were in addition to all the passenger
cars that could be obtained eight flat cars all
crowded.

respect, nothing occured to mar the enjoyment
of any, and the weather was most propitious.

wagon cover and materials from Tooele. Re-

ade were prepared and all who wished were
furnished with hot water for tea. The 7th,ward

SAN FRANCISCO, June 18th, 1880.
Editor Exponent:
	There has been nothing of late of special in-
terest at present merely the local news of a large
city which does not make a lasting impression.
Local news consists of murders, suicides, acci-
dents, embezzlements and scandals, of which we
have enough to sustain us from day to day;
sometimes we have an extra like the late awful
railroad accident. Each one of you who long
for a view of the world had best remain at home,
where you are comparatively safe and where you
can never dream the half that's being done in
the great, busy world. For the lack of other
amusement, I watch the children in these nar-
row streets, where children are so numerous,
and I reason within myself why nearly all of
them turn out bad, and I have arrived at the
couclusion that it is the lack of youthful amuse-
ments of which the children are deprived. Boys
in particular; girls can amuse themselves in the
house, but boys must be out and have breath-
ing room. All, or nearly all, of the games and
pastimes are prohibited by law. They are not
allowed to fly kites because they get entangled
with the telegraph wires; no ball games, for it
breaks people's heads; no hide-and-go-seek, for
there is no place to hide; no whistles, for there
are no trees; no sleds or skates, for there is no
snow or ice; no place to make a wagon, for there
are no boards, and for my part I sympathize
with the much abused boys; there is no place for
them to play but the street. If they go into a
park, there is a man to keep them off the grass,
and if they sit on the steps of the house, some
one throws water on them.
	The great cry is, "What shall we do with our
boys?" I think that in large cities, some suita-
ble place should be provided for them to play
beside the street. If the city should hire some
wagons to let the boys have a ride for once in
their lives, or provide some suitable amusement,
it would be cheaper than boarding them at the
State's expense. If they had something to oc-
cupy their minds, they would not get into so
much mischief. The depravity among the
children here is frightful. Boys six years of age
smoking and learning different vices from older
boys. Every day we can see little boys, seven,
eight, nine and so on, of age, taken to the pri-
son. They are initiated in crime so young that
the State prison has no terrors; and some of you
mothers who are so anxious about your own lit-
tle ones should feel content and thank God that
you are so far away from such wickedness.
	Everything seems to be on the change politi-
cally, financially and domestically, and we are
all living in a suspense; something seems to
threaten us, but what, time only will reveal.
	Respectfully,					NELLIE.

CURIOSITY.

	Woman's curiosity has become proverb[-]al, and
with me the question arises, what is  curiosity?
I  think it is nothing more or, less than an in-
stinctive desire for  knowledge, and I ask myself
again, is there any thing worth  man's knowing
that woman should not know?  Woman is an in-
telligent being and has her part to act in the 
drama of life  (and  no  very  unimportant  part
either) and should feel interested in all that per-
tains  to  the welfare  of  the  human  race;  then
why should she be kept in ignorance of  existing
facts, that are proper  for  man  to  know.  For
ages [-]ast it has been considered  unpopular, and
almost  disrespectful, for woman  to  know any-
thing outside of her household affairs;  but shank
God things are changing, women are  beginning
to   understand  their  rights,  to  indulge  their 
curiosity,  and  look  and  learn  for  themselves;
they are no longer satisfied to remain in igno-
rance and stupidity.
	Knowledge when rightly  used  hurts  no  one
but tends to exalt,  none  need  be  afraid  that
woman will know too much: the more she knows
the better able she  will  be  to  act  wisely;  and
with the spirit of the Lord to help her she can
more easily and better  perform  the  duties  and
labors of life.  It will make her tasks lighter  for
she will not have so many blunders to rectify so
many wrongs to make right, she will not need
to do the same things over twice or thrice when,
she knows  where,  and how  everything  should
be done and therefore is  enabled  to  avoid  much
unnecessary  labor.  Then  let  woman  as  well
as man strive  for  knowledge, and  let  none  be
ridiculed for being curious.
	Let old  fashioned  notions  be  buried  in  the
past and let women emerge  into  the  light and
bring forth  children  of  intelligence,  who  will
help revolutionize the world.
	No wonder the world is in a  deplorable condi-
tion when women, the mothers and teachers are
not to know only the few things that man thinks
proper.  It is sad to know that so much  corrup-
tion exists in the world.  And I  do not  wonder
that man's sensitiveness is disturbed  when such
gross [-]vils are exposed to the eyes  of  the ladies,
as it has been of late in the  Museum of wax fig-
ures;  but I saw nothing there more unfit for the 
ladies to see than  gentleman, in  fact  I think  if
either is excluded it should be the men.
	By  women  seeing,  and  understanding  the
causes and effects, as mothers, they may, by pro-
per teachings prevent much of the evil that now
exists in the world.  So let no more ridicule and
reproach be cast upon woman for  being  curious.
I say success to  "Women's  cu[-]osity."
	C. DOW.

THE "WORD OF WISDOM."

	The Word of Wisdom, as given in the revela-
lations, includes many th[-]ngs beside the use of
tea, coffee, or tobacco. But as those are desig-
nated there, and I often find myself thinking
about the great demand for tea and coffee by the
sisters among the Latter-day Saints, and how
few of them ever refuse the cup of tea, and that
is always presented at our social gatherings in
some localities at least, I thought I would write
a few lines upon it. That it is a growing evil
amongst us is evident from the fact that many
of our young sisters require the cup of tea to help
them to perform the most ordinary duties of life,
and without it feel themselves totally unfit for
exe[--]on of any kind. And the majority of our
young mothers not only use it constantly them-
selves, but give it to their little ones, thought-
lessly no doubt, but to their injury nevertheless,
as helping them to imbibe habits calculated to
retard their capacities for future usefulness,
which every mother should always keep in mind
in the rearing of her children. How blessed
would we be could we only keep in our hearts
that first love of the Gospel so many of us expe-
rienced in those by-gone days, when we first
embraced its principles, when with light hearts
we not only left off the use of what the Lord
said was not wise, but left comfortable homes;
many of us left fathers, mothers, sisters and bro-
thers also for the Gospel's sake,how small in com-
parison is the abstaining from the use of tea and
coffee.
	If we could only be united in all our efforts
and learn to live by every word that proceeds
from the mouth of the Lord, we would then be
blessed indeed.					AUNT MARY.

WOMAN'S CONVENTION.

	At the concluding session of the N. W. S. A.
Convention, held in Indiaanapolis, Indiana, on
the evening of May 27th, there was a very large
and intelligent audience, and the exercises were
particularly interesting.
	First on the programme was Mrs. E. T. Housh,
of Louisville, Kentucky, who read a pointed and
witty poem, entitled "Scrub up, for the Women
are Coming." Mrs. Carr, of Louisville, reported
woman's work in Kentucky. Mrs. Mary E.
Haggart, of Indiana, made an effective address
and was warmly applauded.	*	*	*	Mrs.
Belva A. Lockwood said:
	"I would not, if I could, add anything to the
eloquent arguments of the speakers who have
preceded me, but I will say that the question
that seems most important to me is which of the
great parties of this country will put in its plat-
form the plank of woman suffrage. That party,
whatever it may be, will be the party of the fu-
ture, for as sure as I stand here to-night, not a
decade will pass, before every woman iu this
country, black and white, will have the right to
vote. The mouth of woman is no longer closed,
the fear has been lifted, and we must become
citizens. It has been said, "When the women
of this country ask for the ballot they should
have it," and as sure as truth is truth, so sure
now is the time to ask for it. Already in many
States they are voting on the school question,
and we need only to have that suffrage extend-
ed. But will the women in their new political
condition render the body politic no better and
purer? They certainly will, and that quickly.
In other words, before we attempt to govern
others we must show that we have a capacity
for self-government. If the laws are bad, we
must be willing to obey them until we can make
them better. If the ballot is given to the women
of this country, let them show the people that
they are fit descendants of the forefathers who
planned the revolution of 1776. Revolutions
make and unmake men, but no single mind
can control the impulse that drives them for-
ward. Slowly but surely in the last half century
has woman been coming to the front, and soon
she will be placed side by side with man as his
equal in everything. The era of woman is now.
She has only to put forth her hand to grasp the
forbidden fruits of the past. What she wants
is thorough and liberal education. The sole ob-
ject of the women of the next generation will
not be to get married, but they will fit them-
selves for self-support. The girl must be educat-
ed like the boy to trades and professions, and the
world is gradually waking up to this fact. In
Europe, as well as in America, the universities
now practice co-education, which gives a new
impetus to artistic and literary pursuits. When
men and women stand equal in everything,there
will be but one code of morals for man and for
woman. Hitherto, men in marrying women
have demanded of them that virtue which they
themselves could not return. See ye not, oh,
woman, that the harvests are ripe and the la-
borers few? Then, indeed, it is time for you to
be up and doing. Show your individuality and
that you are necessary for the perpetuation of
the government. There can be no elevated wo-
manhood without a noble manhood, and equal
and exact justice for both. We want great states-
men in this country rather than great generals,
and intelligent and thoughtful women rather
than leaders of fashion. If women are to have
the ballot, as I believe they are, they should ac-
cept it as a sacred trust, and use it accordingly.
If we want the ballot, now is the time to strike
the blow, but we must have united and concert-
ed action all along the line. Already have Eliza-
beth Cady Stanton, Susan B. Anthony and Lu-
cretia Mott seen the promised land, and I pray
to God that they may be allowed to enter in.
(Continued applause.)
	The speaking was concluded by a lengthy and
carefully-prepared address by Miss Phoebe Couz-
ins. Lack of space prevents a full report, and a
synopsis would do a decided injustice to such a
literary and logical effort."

was that at the election of corresponding sec-
retary the first day. The committee on nomina-
tions reported in favor of the choice of Rachel G.
Foster, an intelligent and earnest young lady of
Philadelphia, and on the motion to amend the
report by substituting the name of Mrs. Sara
Andrews Spencer, of Washington, who has held
the office for some years, a spirited discussion

ensued. The committee claimed that they had
decided upon the change because they under-
stood Mrs. Spencer's health was not good, and
they did not desire to impose upon her further.
That lady pertinently suggested that when she
wanted to be relieved of the office she would let
the convention know it, and not before, besides,
her health was as good as anybody's. She was
enthusiastically supported by "Father Adkin-
son, the prehistoric suffragist," and a number of
the older members of the association. Miss Fos-
ter, who had done nothing to secure the nomi-
nation, very sensibly said nothing, and left her
claims in the hands of her friends, and there, as
is usually the result they remained, as she was
defeated by a decided majority. Mrs. Spencer
is known to be earnest and self-sacrificing in the
work, and has made an excellent officer, but her
opponents claim that she is almost too arbitrary
in her management of affairs.
	The re-election of Elizabeth Cady Stanton to
the office of President of the Association is a de-
served compliment, although it was done over
her protest. In her letter to Miss Anthony she
stated that she was in favor of the choice of Mrs.
Z. G. Wallace of this city, for president of the
Association, as she possessed all the qualifica-
tions of a first-class executive officer, besided be-
ing thoroughly in earnest in the work.
	Mrs. Belva A. Lockwood, the distinguished
woman attorney of Washington City, arrived in
time for the night session Wednesday. In conver-
sation with a journal reporter last night she
said that she was detained in Washington by
important legal business and could not get here
sooner. When interrogated concerning the
Hill-Raymond case she said after attending the
Chicago convention she was going to Georgia
to collect testimony for the case. What the ul-
timate result of the trial will be she could not
say, but believed that it would be against Hill.
She said she had no interest in the matter ex-
cept that of an attorney, and as such she was
determined to press it to the end. Although,
she said, Miss Raymond had somewhat com-
promised herself by accepting money from Hill,
yet the case would be carried through all the
courts.
	In personal appearance Mrs. Lockwood is
rather large, with an excellent face and gray
hair, which is rolled above her forehead. She
was dressed in black silk and satin, and wore a
large gray felt hat, which gave her a decidedly
masculine appearance. In conversation she is
fluent and entertaining.

			BEAVER REPORT.
	The first Sisters' Conference held in the Bea-
ver Stake of Zion, in May last, had a good re-
presentation from every settlement in the coun-
ty. Sister Lucinda Howd presiding. Singing
by the choir, prayer by Elder Robertson. There
were many representative brethren and sisters
on the stand from the different settlements.
President Howd first stated the object of the 
Conference and then the reports were read from
the different places, representing all the societies
in a prosperous condition, temporally and spirit-
ually. President John Ashworth, Elder Wm.
Fotheringham, M. A. McDonny and L. Skinner
spoke of the many good principles pertaining to
our religion. D. F. Cox spoke of the
the many advantages the young people of the
present time enjoyed for improvement  compar-
ed to what we older ones had in our younger
days; exhorted the sisters to faithfulness in per-
forming all the duties enjoined upon them as
Latter-day Saints. Bro. Robertson spoke very
instructively and edifyingly, being filled with
the power of God. A good spirit prevailed
throughout the entire meeting, and every one
who spoke, both men and women, testified to

[Column 2]
the same. Bro. Blackburn spoke upon Celestial
marriage, said we should teach this principle
to our children. Many of the sisters
gave much good and glorious instruction and
bore powerful testimonies of what they had seen
and heard, which corroborated the truth of the 
everlasting Gospel. Sister Mary E. Lightner
went back in her remarks to the days of the
Prophet Joseph, and related many things plea-
sant to hear. Sister L. B. Pratt, our aged veter-
an of the cross, spoke of many interesting inci-
dents of her life; and said she was living on bor-
rowed time, and at her advanced age she had
learned not to fear man but to put her trust in
God. I will just say there is not power in my
pen to write the many good things that were
said in our Conference.
	Brothers Ashworth and Fotherignham said
they never enjoyed themselves better than dur-
ing this conference. They would not say the 
sisters had done some good preaching lest they
should not like it, but they would say they had
done some very good talking. And the presi-
dents of the young ladies gave some very good
and intelligent instructions. 
	Jane Gillis, president of the Primary Associa-
tion, and her counselors, L. Shepherd and Irene
Hills, presented their little intelligent band with
instructions. The secretary called the roll, and
the minutes of the P. A. were read by Edna
Farnsworth, a little girl 11 years of age. The
young are improving greatly in qualifications
in Beaver. The sisters are much united in
keeping their covenants and living up to their
duties. Sister Howd presided with great dig-
nity. 			                     RACHEL.

	Mrs. Woodruff said she was truly pleased in
having the privilege of meeting with the sisters
of Utah County. She had listened with interest
to the account given of the faithful labors of the 
sisters of this Stake. We had been organized
for a noble work.  Let us not yield to the powers
of darkness by neglecting  our  prayers,  but  with
all  our  energies assist in  our small  way  to  roll
on  the glorious work of building temples that we
may have the privilege of entering in and doing
a work for our dear friends, who are waiting the
other side of the vail for us to assist them.  Mrs.
Woodruff said through faith and prayer she had
received a testimony of  the truth of  the gospel,
and urged the necessity  of  all  having  the testi-
mony of Jesus within them, that their faith fail
not.  Let us arrange our home affairs to meet as
often as possible, that our spirits may be fed by
the spirit of  God,  then  we  will  rejoice  in  the
trials we are called  to pass through.  The expe-
rience we gain here will be of untold value to us.
May God bless my dear sisters,  and  I  earnestly
ask him to bless  Sister  Smoot.  I  have known
her many years as a tried and faithful friend.
	Mrs. Lucky W. Kimball presented the compli-
ments of  Miss  E.  R. Snow, S. M. Kimball and
E. B. Wells, who had been invited to meet with
us.  Through press of business  they could not
come, but desired  to  extend  their sincere love,
and felt that they would be with the conference
in spirit.  Mrs. Kimball made an earnest appeal
in behalf of the EXPONENT.  Was confident that
all present could testify  that the spirit of God
had been with us during our conference.  Sister
M. T. Smoot also spoke in behalf of our Woman's-
Paper.
	Adjourned until 2  p.m. Singing, benediction by 
Bishop Paxman. 
	Two o'clock p.m. After the opening  exer-
cises, the  president  said  we  will  now  hear
from Brother Woodruff.  "My sisters and moth-
ers in Zion—I am glad to meet with you to-day.
The Relief  Society  was  organized  by  Joseph
Smith by revelation, and  has  for  its  object the
building up of the Kingdom of God.  It does not
all depend  upon  the  Elders  of  Israel  by  any
means.   You  are  like  ministering  angels; you
are aids to the bishops, and a great many worthy
people would suffer were  it  not  for these organ-
izations.  The responsibilities of building up this
Kingdom  rests  alike  upon  the  man  and  the
woman.  While the Elders  are  among the na-
tions preaching the Gosepl, the mothers have the
guidance of the children.  When I contemplate
the positions of you mothers in bearing the souls
of men, and your future destiny, it is far beyond
the  honors  conferred  upon  the  queens  of the
earth, who are looked upon  with honor.  When
they die  there is an end of their glory.  Your
promise is eternal.  You cannot know the result
of your labors until  you pass behind the vail.
You have no thrones here, but when  you have
faithfully performed the work you were ordained
to do on this earth, your exaltation will be eter-
nal, you  will  inherit  kingdoms forever. It is
only a step to the other  side, it  is  like  passing
from one door to another, and what little time
you remain here  do  all the  good  you can, train
your children aright, teach them the principles
of life and salvation, try to  stem the tide of evil
that is gaining a foothold among our young peo-
ple, teach your children to honor the  principles
of marriage as  revealed  through  the  Prophet
Joseph, without this order of marriage we cannot
be exalted; instill these eternal  principles in the
minds of  the  rising  generation, that they may
control their actions through life.  Let me  say
to the sisters of Zion, we cannot do without your
assistance in accomplishing this great work, our
sons and daughters are to bear off this kingdom,
and it devolves upon  the  mothers  to  prepare
them for the "work before them."  The speaker
said he had never been at home but one summer
in his life, of course the training of children  was
entirely out of the question.  One of his children
was over a year old  before  he  saw him.  "Be
faithful my sisters, that when  the  trump shall
sound, we may come forth and inherit eternal
lives."
	Brother Teasdale  made  some  excellent  re-
marks in regard to the vast amount of goed done
by the Relief Societies,  and  the  object of their
mission. Brother John said he had been highly
 edified and he hoped the sisters would give heed
to the advice of Brother Woodruff.
	Conference  adjourned  until  August  27th.
After singing.  Prayer by Brother Teasdale.

	KANAB.
	The Quarterly Conference of  the Relief Soci-
eties of the Kanab Stake was held  at   Glendale,
June 4th, 1880, Counselor Mrs. Harriet  Bunting
presiding.  Present on the stand:  Counselors H.
Bunting and L. A. Johnson;  Sister L. Spencer,
president of the  Orderville  Ward,  and  Sister
Foot, of the Glendale Ward.  Of  the  brethren:
Presidents H. C. Spencer and Jas. L. Bunting,
Bishops  W.  D.  Johnson, Jr.,  Royal Cutler, T.
Smith, and Elder Carpenter.  The brethren and
sisters made  some very  interesting  remarks in
reference to home manufature, the responsibil-
ities of parents in  rearing  their children;  urged
them to give the little ones  good  educations, to
teach  them  politeness,  and  impress  on their
young minds the importance of  prayer and obe-
dience to the priesthood; wished  the  sisters to
do what they could to help gather the poor.  In
fact, the whole meeting was replete  with  good
counsel  and  instruction.  The  conference was
adjourned till September 3d,  to be  held at Ka-
nab. The Societies  in  the  Stake have loaned
their wheat to the needy, as desired by President
Taylor.  The sisters are trying  to  live their reli-
gion and  to do the best they can, in the absence
of President Elizabeth Nuttall, whom  we  hope
will soon be with us again.  Wishing success to
the EXPONENT and peace and prosperity to Zion,
	I remain, your sister in the gospel,
	M. ELIZABETH LITTLE, Secretary.

	The Quarterly Conference  of  the  Y. L. M. I.
Association of the  Weber  Stake  of  Zion  was
held in the Tabernacle, Ogden, June 18th, Presi-
dent Sarah Herrick presiding. Meeting opened
by singing, "Now  let  us  Rejoice."  Prayer by
Bishop Hart.  Singing, "May we who know the
Joyful  Sound."  Minutes  of  last  Conference
read  and  accepted.  Reports  were  then  read
from thirteen associations, all being reported ex-
cept one.  President  Sarah  Herrick  addressed
the meeting.  She was  pleased with the reports
and was satisfied the  young  ladies were awake
and trying to  perform,  to  the  utmost of their
ability, the  duties  assigned  them.  Felt inte-
rested in the great  work  in  which we were en-
gaged,  and  was  desirous  of  doing  more good.
Hoped we would all profit  by  the good instruc-
tion given at the R. S.  Conference  yesterday,
and ever strive to do  the  will  of  our  Father in
heaven.
	Bishop Sanford Bingham  exhorted the young
ladies  to  try  and  fully  appreciate  the  great
blessings they receive and improve every oppor-
tunity to gain  wisdom  and  knowledge,  that in
after years they might  have  nothing  to regret,
President Sarah Richards, Summit Stake, spoke
on the training of children, necessity of prayer,
liberality, and the despicable practice of tattling.
	Bishop Hart,  West  Weber,  gave  much good
instruction in regard to our duties as Saints, and
was sure if we  lived  our  religion  all  would be
well.
	Prest.  Jane  S. Richards  followed,  endorsing
what had been said, and  impressing  upon  the
minds of the young ladies the necessity of hav-
ing great  faith  and  of  being  united.  Advised
mothers to teach their children when young the
principles of the gospel, and  the  importance of
attending to their prayers and other duties faith-
fully.  Spoke on various other subjects and was
listened to throughout  with  marked  attention.
Meeting adjourned  till  2 p.m. Singing, "Do
what is right."  Benediction by  Elder Thomas
Morley.
	2 p.m.  After singing  and  prayer, Mrs. Mar-
tha Critchelow, president R. S. First Ward was
called upon to address the meeting.  She bore a 
faithful testimony to the  truth  of  the  religion
she had embraced, had been  richly  paid for  at-
tending this conference,  and  hoped  all  present
would try and carry out the  good counsel which 
had been given.
	Mrs.  Martha  Bingham,  president  R.  S.  of 
Riverdale, said many  good  things  encouraging
to all who are striving to  do  right, gave much
excellent counsel and asked the Lord to bless all
in their efforts to do good.
	The presidents of  the  Young Ladies Associa-
tions present each spoke, expressing  a feeling of
deep interest in the associations over which they
were placed, and a desire to perform their duties
acceptably, relying upon their  heavenly Father
for  strength,  knowing  they  were  incom-
petent  to  fill  such  responsible  posi-
tions  without  his  spirit  to  guide   them.
Interesting and instructive remarks were made
by counselors,  Mrs.  Emily  S.  Richards, Mrs.
Josephine R. West and Mrs. Letitia Peery, which
were calculated in their  nature, to  inspire the
young ladies to  greater  dilligence and renewed
efforts in the  cause  of  mutual  improvement.
Bishop McQuarrie gave  a very earnest address,
speaking upon many principles of  our holy reli-
gion, pointing out clearly the importance of ob-
serving the Word of Wisdom, and setting an ex-
ample worthy of  imitation.  Prayed  the bless-
ings of heaven to rest upon all  who are interest-
ed in building up the kingdom  of  God  on  the
earth.  President Sarah  Herrick  had  enjoyed
the Conference, and trusted all felt blessed and
 benefitted by the good counsel they had received.
Exhorted the young ladies to  persevere in good
works,  and  perform  faithfully  every  known
duty.  Conference was then adjourned for three
months.
