THUS sang Isaiah, the great Jewish prophet, more than seven hundred years before the birth of the Savior, and an American prophet several centuries later took up the refrain. Indeed it has been a theme of the prophets down through the centuries.
The Master of the world both by example and precept confirmed, in a superlative way, the truth of this message, and is appropriately referred to as the Prince of Peace. At the beginning of his ministry, he called to his aid twelve men, designated apostles, to declare the gospel of the kingdom to the world, first to the Jews and then to the Gentiles. All but a handful of the Jews rejected their Lord and his message of peace and "nailed him to a tree." It seemed that Christianity was doomed to perish from the earth. But out of Tarsus of Cilicia was to come forth a man of the tribe of Benjamin who was destined to go down in history as one of the greatest missionaries of all times. This character was of such potential worth to the cause of Christianity that the risen Redeemer paid him a visit following the Lord's resurrection and rebuked him saying, "Saul, Saul, why persecutest thou me?" And he said, "Who art thou, Lord?" "I am Jesus whom thou persecutest. ... Arise, and go into the city, and it shall be told thee what thou must do." Saul was obedient to the heavenly command and Ananias baptized him into the kingdom following a visitation of the Master, who said: "For he (Saul) is a chosen vessel unto me, to bear my name before the Gentiles and kings and the children of Israel."
