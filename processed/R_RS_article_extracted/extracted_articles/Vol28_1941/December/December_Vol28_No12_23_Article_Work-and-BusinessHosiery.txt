No Rights Without Responsibilities All that has been said in previous discussions about the importance of a proper attitude toward marriage applies to this lesson as well, for unless the married feel an attitude of individual responsibility in the adventure before them, they will exert little or no effort to make it successful, and the whole thing will likely collapse. There are too many people who enter marriage unprepared for the adjustments, sacrifices, and genuine work that it involves. There are many married people who act emotionally as children or adolescents, and who fail to assume the adult responsibilities demanded of them. They look at marriage in terms of rights rather than responsibilities, and therein lies the secret of their failure. Being emotionally imma- ture, they are quick to blame and to complain, but slow to cooperate or admit faults within themselves; they expect the other person to go three-fourths or all of the way. There are too many of these selfish individuals who are more willing to receive than to give Emotional immaturity has reference to a type of childishness frequently manifest in adult behavior. Sometimes it is referred to as "infantilism," for it makes people act much like babies. Chronological and biological maturity do not necessarily result in emotional maturity; sometimes people grow up physically without growing up psychologically; sometimes people have a lot of birthdays without ever learning to act like adults. Emotional immaturity shows itself in patterns of selfishness, jealousy, et cetera. Crying is often used as a weapon with which to injure the other person or to get one's own way. Sulking, pretending to be hurt, doing and saying little things to hurt the other person, are ways of showing oneself to be emotionally immature. They are also ways of destroying love and breaking up a happy home. Marriage, like life, is a game of cooperation, a process of give and take. He who tries to do all of the taking and none of the giving may get by for a while, but sooner or later he will find his marriage a failure. The love that is kindled in courtship may be either smothered in marriage or fanned into something that is eternal and glorious. Exploitation in courtship often carries over into marriage. The boy who will use and abuse his sweetheart for personal thrills will, as a husband, usually do the same thing, with little regard for the rights and personality needs of his wife; and the girl who is a thoughtless "gold digger" before marriage will usually be an expensive and irresponsible wife after the ceremony. But whether the pattern of exploitation showed itself in the period of courtship or not, its existence in marriage is a sign of marital decay. Students of the family recognize this problem when they speak of the "parasitic wife," whom they describe as a selfish and pampered woman who applies the "gold digging" philosophy to marriage, and who seeks all of the advantages without making any real or worth-while contribution to it.' She is called a parasite because she is selfish and fails to carry her share of the responsibility. Sometimes this selfishness shows itself in an unwillingness to have children, sometimes in excessive demands upon the husband's pocketbook, and sometimes in other ways; but always a wife of this kind is a parasite. And there are parasitic husbands as well. Whenever any person is unwilling to play the game or to do his part in life, he is a social parasite. How can persons 'such as these hope to make a success of marriage?
