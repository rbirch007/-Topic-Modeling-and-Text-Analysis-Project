+h Social Science Foundations Of Successful Marriage Lesson 4 The Start And The Art Of Marriage (Tuesday, February 24, 1942) Lesson Aim: To emphasize the importance of a good start in marriage, and to show that the art of successful marriage hinges to a large degree upon the attitudes of its participants. THE start of any race has a lot to do with its outcome, and life's race is no exception. The intricacies and difficulties of modern living are many, and he who gets off on the wrong foot or makes a poor start will find himself severely handicapped all the way through. Who, When, and How to Marry Marriage commences with a definite ceremony, performed at a given time, and in conjunction with a chosen companion. The start of marriage for anyone involves these three things: another person, a time selected, and a ceremony performed; and if any one of these is not wisely decided upon, the marriage itself will likely get off to a bad start. We will not say more about the choice of a companion than has already been said in previous lessons, except to reemphasize the importance of rational judgment and serious consideration of such factors as chastity, dependability, health, disposition, industriousness, similar backgrounds and interests, et cetera. If one has used his head during courtship, and has chosen his mate intelligently as well as emotionally, that is the one he should marry But the time of marriage should receive careful consideration as well. The time of year is of little or no importance, to be sure, but the time of marriage in relation to the ages and the preparation of the bride and groom is very important. Personalities and situations vary so greatly, however, that even here it is difficult to lay down any rules. It is known, for example, that marriages are usually more successful if husband and wife are about the same age, or if the husband is a few years older. Matches in which the wife is older than the husband, or in which the husband is considerably older than the wife are not usually good marriage risks. But even so, there are cases in which this rule is broken, and happiness found in spite of it. It is also known that to delay marriage much beyond the time of biological 792 maturity and psychological preparedness is to put rather serious: strain upon the persons involved, and to decrease the chances of marriage in the first place, and successful adjustment in marriage where it is consummated. Marriage at a reasonably young age is desirable from a number of points of view. But here there are many exceptions, for young marriage may also mean hasty marriage based upon immature judgment. Furthermore, it may mean' lack of economic preparation; the young man of today should not hope to win his fortune before getting married, but certainly he should have something lined up in the way of a job or specific training for a job. The average ages for first marriages in this country for 1930 were 25.6 for males and 22.4 for females. We cannot say, of course, that these are the ideal ages for marriages, for many people have been successful in marriage who have deviated considerably from this average. What we can say is that the ideal age for marriage will depend to a very large extent upon the particular couple involved and the peculiar circumstances governing them. It is impossible to describe the ideal age in terms of years. The best time for marriage is when the persons involved are prepared?biologically, psychologically, economically, and socially. And where all of these conditions cannot be completely satisfied, the best time is when the most satisfactory balance can be found among them. In other words, religion is able to make the marriage contract sacred, and in this way cement and vitalize its relationships. The sanctification of marriage through the church makes it mean more to those who enter, makes them more firmly resolve that -it shall not fail. And there is no sanctification so deep and profound as temple marriage. Parents can do much to spiritualize the marriage preparation of their children. Home atmosphere is quite normally transplanted into the hearts of the children, and if this atmosphere is one of reverence and prayer over matters of courtship and marriage the children will naturally have a better chance than if it is not. Mothers especially can impress their children with the sacredness of the marriage covenant and the importance of entering into it with the right attitude and with a partner well chosen. Children should be guided during courtship, both as to conduct and as to their choice of associates. If one has chosen a good companion, married at an appropriate time in life, and married under the sanctifying influence of the Church, he has made a good start. The "who," and the "when," and the "how" of marriage are important. Early Adjustment in Marriage Life is a series of adjustments, and the event of marriage is simply one episode in this series. But it is an extremely important episode, for upon its success depends the happiness of not only the married partners themselves, but of children and grandchildren to come. If one has adjusted satisfactorily to the problems of childhood and youth before marriage, he is well on his way to successful adjustment in marriage, although he has no guarantee of such success. Marriage is a continuous process of adjustment; the start ls important to be sure, but the power and the will to carry through are even more important. Successful marriage is a process, not an achievement. Even so, the early months and years of married life are usually loaded rather heavily with problems which need adjustment. To those who have come into marriage somewhat unprepared and heavily freighted with romantic ideas unsupported by stern reality, the period is one of painful disillusionment. Their mistake has been the so-called "romantic fallacy" discussed in the pre- 793 vious lesson. 'To some, adjustment is relatively easy because of the foundations they have laid beforehand; to others, it is hard for the same reason; but to all, regardless of preparation or attitude, the start of marriage presents its difficulties. This is so because marriage requires such a great change in the lives of those who enter it; in one sense marriage is a major life crisis. The more or less highly emotionalized associations of courtship must make room for more practical considerations; couples must "come down to earth," so to speak, and learn to face things a little more realistically. Furthermore, the young married couple must learn to cooperate and get along with each other on a much more continuous and intimate basis than formerly, and this for anyone means adjustment. Much of the success or failure of marriage depends upon the success or failure of this early period; the stresses and strains of these first adjustments between husband and wife may either be the birth pains of a deepening love or the death pains of an immature infatuation. It is important to realize also that conflicts between husband and wife are very likely much more common today than ever before, and that marital adjustments are therefore much more difficult. Social changes have disturbed the traditional roles which men and women have played in the marriage relationship, and new roles have not yet been thoroughly established. It used to be that husband and wife upon entering marriage knew fairly well what society expected of them, and therefore what their role was to be in respect to each other. But this is 794 not so today; the emancipation of woman has disturbed all of this, and as a result, the early part of marriage is usually a period in which husband and wife maneuver and struggle for positions of advantage and control. This maneuvering, although a necessary part of the adjustment, is bound to lead into conflicts of one sort or another. Realizing this, parents and friends will do well to show a normal interest in the lives of the couple, but at the same time to avoid all interference. Needless to say, whenever possible the young married couple should live by themselves rather than with their parents. "What about the honeymoon?" many people ask. The purpose of the honeymoon is to permit the first adjustments of marriage to take place in a restful atmosphere and in isolation from all interference. When this purpose is realized, the honeymoon is a good thing; but too often it is not realized. 'Too often the honeymoon trip is expensive and fatiguing, and is therefore a hinderance to successful adjustment rather than a help. A long, hurried, or expensive trip isn't necessary. Very often the conditions favorable to successful adjustment can better be obtained right at home. Many young people today are wisely delaying their honeymoon until after they have been married, for a year or so. The advent of a child into the family is an experience that requires adjustment on the part of husband and wife. They must now make way for a new personality in their hearts and in their lives, and they must plan together for its welfare. The Art of Mutual Enrichment Success in marriage depends so much upon the attitude of those who understake the adventure that we make no apology in referring to the point frequently throughout this series of lessons. Attitude is the key to maturing and enduring love. He who enters the marriage gates unselfishly and with a determination, to succeed is the one who will succeed. Successful marriage is an art; and an artist is one who feels, one whose soul is set in tune with the infinite. The art of successful marriage is the art of mutual enrichment, growing out of genuine love and devotion. The selfish and the selfcentered are seldom able to make others happy, and as a result are seldom truly happy themselves. Happiness is something that must be shared in order to be realized, and happiness sharing is the art that makes marriage successful. We have already defined love as something more than either physical attraction or emotional involvement. True and enduring love involves the physical and the emotional, to be sure, but it is something more?it is the spiritual and intellectual companionship that grows out of years of sacrificing and working together. True love is that which LESSON DEPARTMENT makes a person put the welfare of someone else above his own; it makes him happy and anxious to share his happiness; it is mutually enriching. But the sad thing is that very often sentimentality is mistaken for genuine love?even in _ marriage. Sentiment has become so commercialized and the modes of expressing it have become so_ standardized around various holidays and special occasions that it is really difficult at times to distinguish true love from its shell. Anniversaries are likely a good thing, but the wife or husband who becomes personally insulted when forgotten and who makes it a matter of marital conflict, is not the wife or husband who is really in love. True love runs deep, and it feeds on mutual understanding and respect rather than sentimental superficialities.; Another factor in the mutual enrichment of married partners is the division of labor and responsibilities. Too often, in this modern day, young women show a dislike for housework and an unwillingness to accept the responsibilities of mothethood. They are career minded, but they apparently fail to realize that homemaking is the greatest career that woman can have. The growing equality between men and women has made too many women feel that to be equal with men they must be like men. Too often we train our young people before marriage to compete with each other, and then after marriage we expect them to cooperate. But equality doesn't mean identity; women can be equal with men and still be unlike them. The biological roles of men and women are necessarily 795 different, and so why shouldn't the social roles be also. Women who are the most womanly and men who are the most manly are the men and women who are best able to successfully play their roles in marriage to the satisfaction and mutual enrichment of all. Chart for Happiness Professor Hornell Hart of Duke University has recently published a study dealing with the measurement of human happiness, and since the theme of our lessons all along has been the attainment of happiness in marriage, it may be well to conclude the present one by giving reference to his book. Mr. Hart's purpose in making this study was to discover the general areas of human happiness and the various factors associated with it, so that individuals can be helped to better understand themselves and thereby improve the happiness of their own lives. By use of a scale, called a Euphorimeter, he was able to get an approximate measure of the relative happiness ratings of various groups of people under various conditions. He found that the major fields, or areas, in which happiness or unhappiness are experienced are: play life, work life, love life, and home life. He found also that the following general factors are conducive to happiness in any field: (1) health and vitality; (2) attitudes of courage and enthusiam toward the difficulties one encounters; (3) attitudes of friendliness and comtadeship toward other human beings as shown through religion and the spirit of unselfishness.
