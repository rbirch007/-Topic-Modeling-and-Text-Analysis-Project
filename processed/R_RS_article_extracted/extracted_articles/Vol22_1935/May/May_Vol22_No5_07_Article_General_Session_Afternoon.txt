GENERAL SESSION Thursday Afternoon
Counselor Kate M. Barker
"Nothing is too wonderful to be true."
When research began to reveal the wonders of the universe, Michael Faraday, the physicist, said, "Nothing is too wonderful to be true." Today, as the radio brings us opera from thousands of miles away, we cannot help but say as he did, "Really, nothing is too wonderful to be true." But marvelous as the world is, it was planned for man and his development. Our Father in Heaven planned a world where character could be developed—a world of rigid law where human beings have freedom of choice, a world in which people have to live and work together. The strength of character which our Father in Heaven wishes us to acquire because it leads to greater joy, can only be gained by living and working together. So our big problem today is, as it has always been, one of human relations. The outcomes of our efforts to solve the problem are brought to us daily and, since the unusual is news, we hear more of the failures than of the successes. They are proclaimed by radio and by the press. They receive undue emphasis by "debunking" biographers. They become the prevailing topic of conversation; and we develop the tendency to decry the faults rather than to appreciate the virtues of our fellowmen. Our vision of the divine possibilities of the soul has been dimmed. With short-sighted, imperfect ideals, our realization too has fallen short. Human relationships have become discordant. The sin of the majority is not one of delinquency but of being satisfied with mediocrity. We need a sublimer outlook on the possibilities of humanity. Our education is of value only so far as it increases our consciousness of the divine potentialities of our souls. We marvel at the beauty of nature, but the most wonderful thing in the world is the capacity of the human soul for friendship, kindness and loyalty. We have allowed the world to become poor in these virtues which make life so fine and beautiful, because they make for happy human relations. And yet these things which the world most needs are in the power of every human soul to give if he but will. The Relief Society holds a strategic position. It is the society of the mothers who instill ideals in the children, mould character, and thus shape the whole of society. Let each one of us resolve for the coming year to get a better vision of what we may be as revealed by the life of our Savior. Let us each be a little more honest in our thoughts and actions, take a little more time to be friendly, be a little more kind, a little more loyal to friends, leaders, and principles. If every Relief Society woman would do this in the home and inspire those in the home to practice it in the community, what a great spiritual force we could be, what a wonderful beginning of building a world where the best and highest in man's life would rule. And, from our efforts, the world might get the vision that in the realm of character and human relationships nothing is too wonderful to be true.
BUILDING FOR BETTER LIVING BY MEETING DAILY LIFE THROUGH THE DEVELOPMENT OF PERSONALITY Hazel H. Greenwood—Member of General Board
"Personality is used to describe almost everything from the attributes of the soul to those of a new talcum powder. It means the individual as a whole his height and weight and loves and hates and blood pressure and reflexes, his smiles and hopes and bowed legs and enlarged tonsils. It means all that anyone is and all he is trying to become."— Menninger. Personality is made up of an inherited nucleus that is acted upon by various powerful social and economic influences that mold and shape into behavior patterns. The situation is the thing to which the personality has to adjust itself. The phase of life as it is presented at any particular moment. The rules of life are made up of laws that become more and more complicated as the demands of civilization increase. If we might always meet the same situations, life would be less difficult, but new situations are constantly arising that call for new adjustments. "Life sets the conditions, man must do the fitting. Fortune is the great scene shifter and from one act to another in the drama we call life, the actor must adjust to his part. Nothing endures forever, times change, conditions alter. Life is not one voyage but a succession of ventures. The Greek philosophers say we never dip in the same stream twice." This process of adjustment to life is the art of living. A well integrated personality is able to meet life's changing fortunes. It may not be easy. He may have to change the situation or he may change himself and he can do both. By one means and another he can manage successfully. We hear little of the person who meets his difficulties with faith and courage. He says little about it. We see mostly those who have failed to meet their situations. The ability to carry on in spite of discouragements and misfortune demonstrates strong healthy personalities. We know that no one can have a mind and a mental life that is completely in harmony. We are always experiencing conflicts. The mind becomes a battlefield with emotions and forces drawn against each other. Conflict means struggle and friction. The happy person is one who has these conflicts reduced to the minimum, for conflict is painful. The successful individual is the one who has his life so adjusted that instead of wearing himself out by anxiety over personal problems he

THE RELIEF SOCIETY TODAY Emeline Y. Nebeker Member of General Board
Believing that "The Glory of God is Intelligence," the Relief Society from its earliest days encouraged study; however, it was not until 1914 that the General Board outlined a uniform course. In this year the Bulletin was issued, twelve lessons being printed for the year. A comparison of the first month's lessons in the Bulletin, and the lessons in the Relief Society Magazine for March, 1935, shows, in the main, a remarkable similarity. In the Bulletin we have: First Meeting— Current Topics, Local, National and International; Second Meeting— Genealogy; Third Meeting—Home Ethics: Civic Pride, 20 minutes; Home Gardening for women, 20 minutes; Fourth Meeting—Literature, 30 minutes; Art and Architecture, 10 minutes. The lessons in the last Magazine are: First Meeting—1:15 p. M. Teachers' Topic; 2 p. M. Theology. Second Meeting —Work and Business. Third Meeting—Literature. Fourth Meeting— Social Service. Both stress the importance of the work of the day. Both have the underlying thought that if we are to gain knowledge "the greatest of all time is today; it is the algebraic sum of all our yesterdays, and holds the promise of all our tomorrows." But always both emphasize the fact that the association with Relief Society members strengthens our faith and helps us to strive harder to keep the two great commandments, the first: "Thou shalt love the Lord thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy mind;" and the second: "Thou shalt love thy neighbor as thyself."
 
VISION Mary C. Kimball—Member of General Board
Joseph Smith had vision as to the inner urges or impulses of women when he organized the Relief Society. He said, "This charitable Society is according to your natures, it is natural for females to have feelings of charity. You are now placed in a situation where you can act according to these sympathies which God has planted in your bosoms. If you live up to these principles, how great and glorious. If you live up to your privileges the angels cannot be restrained from being your associates. * * * This Society is not only to relieve the poor but to save souls. * * * And I now turn the key to you in the name of God, and this Society shall rejoice, and knowledge and intelligence shall flow down from this time." On that prophetic and inspirational utterance and with divine power, the organization was effected and has continued to go forth fulfilling its great destiny. Saving souls is one of the most important works God has given to His children. In the Doctrine and Covenants He tells us that if we labor all our days and save but one soul that great shall be our joy with that soul in the kingdom of our Father. In the Relief Society souls are saved by teaching correct principles, by feeding spiritual food in prayer and testimony and study of the Word of God, and by giving opportunities to serve. Each officer and member needs vision to carry on her work. Each needs that inner light that shall guide and direct her into doing the best possible thing in the best possible way. It was said of old, "Where there is no vision the people perish," and Relief Society organizations, if not officered by women having inner light to guide and direct them, will not fulfil their destiny and will lag behind the other organizations. Every officer and member needs to keep in touch with the fast changing events of her day. She should duly value the past, give to the present its meed of attention, and be ready to make needed adjustments for the future. Visiting teachers require vision to see and to understand the ones they visit that they may minister to each one according to her needs. It is necessary for the presiding officers not only to meet the present opportunities but they should seek for the possibilities greater than they are able to attain to today. Every officer and teacher should cultivate the seeing eye and the understanding heart that she may ever see the best way of doing the work assigned to her and see greater possibilities in the future for achievement. She who lives up to the light she has received today, will tomorrow have greater vision. To the one who has vision, nothing can discourage or daunt for long and although few see great distances ahead, to everyone is given light for one or more steps ahead. Just as the inventor, the landscape gardener, the architect, in his vision sees what he is trying to invent, how the garden will look, how the building when completed will appear, so everyone who has anything to do with saving souls must see the possibilities of each of God's children and with this light she will not be discouraged even though the achievement seems small and progress slow.
 
THE CHURCH IN A CHANGING WORLD Counselor Amy Brown Lyman
We hear a great deal of discussion today about the great changes which have recently taken place in the world; about changes which are now taking place; and about changes which should, or must, take place in the future if the world is to recover itself. We hear about how the world is changing; how Government is changing and must change; how the social and economic order is changing and must change. In fact we hear so much about these things that we almost wonder at times what there is in life that is fundamental, permanent and unchanging; what there is we can depend upon; what there is we can hold to. In a single day recently I noted the following headlines on the editorial pages of several magazines of the current month: "Our Changing World;" "Our Changing Social Order;" "The Old Social Order Has Gone;" "A New Deal for Capitalism;" "Youth in a New World." In all probability many of the changes and many of the proposed changes in world affairs are necessary, and normal people, with a normal outlook on life, and with a long view of things, do not object to changes if such changes would mean development and progress in world affairs; if they would mean more spirituality, more righteousness and more social justice. But questions naturally arise—Why are so many changes and such radical changes necessary? What is wrong with the world? What is wrong with Government? What is wrong with the social and economic order? Without going into present-day conditions and difficulties in detail, I believe we can answer that. Much of the trouble in the world today, and much of the need for changes is due to wrong methods of operation in world affairs based upon wrong ideas, all of which have developed as a result of human selfishness, greed and love of power; secondly, to the ignoring of those basic religious, moral and ethical principles and standards which are fundamental. In other words the world has strayed away from Christian ideals and principles, it has strayed away from God and His ways. Then there is our other question— What can we depend upon? What can we hold to? The answer to this question surely is that we can depend upon the Lord, and upon His word as revealed to us. His word will not change. We can depend upon His Church and its teachings. These are eternal and will not change. We can depend upon and hold to truth, to righteousness, to pure religion, the principles and fundamentals of which never change, even in a changing world. Following this plan may not mean that we can help to control the world, but if we can be only a small factor for good in world affairs it is worth while. All good is far reaching, and nothing good is lost. Even the little pebble dropped in the large pool makes its impression. We can set ourselves in order; we can set our families in order and we can help set our communities in order for we have the franchise and should use it. The Savior set a high standard of life when in answer to the question: "What can one do to inherit eternal life?" He pointed out the law: "Thou shalt love the Lord Thy God with all thy heart, and with all thy soul, and with all thy strength, and with all thy mind, and thy neighbor as thyself." In illustrating as to who one's neighbor is, he gave the parable of the Good Samaritan, which indicated that one's neighbor is anyone who is in trouble and in need. If this one law were observed, and individuals and nations loved the Lord with their hearts and souls and strength and minds, they would serve Him and keep His commandments; they would worship him instead of worshiping worldly idols. And if individuals and nations loved their neighbors as they do themselves, the Golden rule would prevail and such destructive forces as greed, selfishness, poverty and war would be eliminated. This spirit of unselfish love and service is the ideal of Relief Society, and is typified in the work of the faithful members of this organization. It is this ideal which prompts the theme of this conference: "Relief Society Building for Better Living." Joseph Smith also set a high standard of living in his life and teachings, and in the Gospel plan of life and salvation revealed through him—a plan which, if universally adopted, would revolutionize the world. I believe we can therefore proclaim with assurance that our hope for the future lies in the acceptance of, and adoption of religious principles and ideals; in revealed truth;
298
in the teachings of the Savior; in the Gospel plan of life and salvation as given through the Prophet Joseph Smith. Our hope for the future lies in faith in God, and in our willingness to accept His teachings and live His laws. These teachings are eternal and are unchanging in a changing world. These we can depend upon and hold to. In the eternities we must trust our Heavenly Father; why not trust Him here and now? We could not do better than to look to Him for the direction of our life-energy. He can do great things with our lives if we but give them to Him in sincerity. He can make them useful, uplifting, heroic. He never wastes anything. He never forgets anything. He never loses anything. Though He holds the worlds in the hollow of His hand, He will yet remember each of us, and the part we are fitted to play in eternal life. As Latter-day Saints we have much to depend upon, much to hold to. The Lord has spoken in this dispensation, and has instructed us how to live; and if we live fully according to His laws and His teachings, we can still be serene and happy and even helpful in a troubled and changing world. In His great cause our efforts will not be lost.
PRESIDENT LOUISE Y. ROBISON
We have had so many wonderful subjects treated during our Conference that I felt I would rather review our work for last year, and possibly outline just a bit what there is in the future for us. I would like our women to realize that our organization is a charter member, the National Council of Women, and through Relief Society we are members of the National Council. We have been favored recently by a visit from our National President—Lena Madesin Phillips, I wish you could all have heard her speak. In this News Letter which went to every organization in the United States she spoke of the honor and privilege she had while she was here of speaking with President Heber J. Grant, and having a visit with him. This was one of the outstanding features of her visit here. Quoting her words: "These are peripatetic news notes, prepared as I have traveled through the West. They should bear the fragrance of sage and balsam, the everlasting strength of silent mountains and the vigor and freedom of the plains. The problems which so perplex and trouble us in the East seem absurdly unnecessary and out of place in the natural beauty and grandeur of the West." Have we had this vision? A woman who was here speaking a few years ago said, "Women who can look up to these mountains must have high thoughts and a glorious future." When I know that we have these, we have been baptized and had hands of the brethren who hold the authority of God placed upon them, giving them the gift of the Holy Ghost, added to these elevating things which God has given us, I feel we should never have an impure or selfish or ugly thought. Recently a President of the Relief Society came into the office. She had been a Stake President and now, with just as much love for the Gospel and the Relief Society, is working as a Ward President. She said: "Sister Robison, why cannot women be sensible instead of sensitive?" When we have the wonderful possibilities that have been pointed out to us during this Conference and the many opportunities for service, for building ourselves up by helping others—how can we be over sensitive! How we can have our feelings hurt! We had a very lovely example of what I mean within the last week. The sisters of the General Board were bringing in to the Chairman of our Conference Committee the topics they would speak on during Conference. There was one sister, who is one of God's noble women, through some misunderstanding she did not get her title ready, and the program was printed without it. This was our beloved Sarah M. McLelland, when she found the
299
program printed and her name not on it she said: "It is all right, it does not make a bit of difference. I have my notes ready, but it is all right." Can you carry this spirit in your own homes? Can you show it in your Ward organizations. If we want to live up to the blessings and opportunities our Father has given us we must have it. We cannot have the Spirit of the Lord with us unless we put selfishness and jealousy and envy out of our hearts. I am sure the majority of the sisters do this, but it is one little fault for us to overcome this next year. We should appreciate the natural beauty of our country and help to preserve it. In your Relief Society Magazine for this month there is an article by Rufus D. Johnson on the heritage that we have from our parents in the trees they planted. When they came here it was a barren country, only choke cherries and scrub oak here, and now to look over these valleys, not only Salt Lake but wherever Latter-day Saints live, you see beautiful trees. He calls attention to the fact that we have been rather inclined, in our lives, to plant quick growing trees, and not something that will last a lifetime. Let us have the satisfaction that we have added to the beauty of our lands by planting some fine tree. Why not plant a tree when each child is born, and name it, and let the child know it is his tree, and when he is fifty years old it would bring back the memories of home life with its thrills. Plant good trees. They cost very little. Beautify your homes and make your land wherever you are, a land of Zion. Now, sisters, in going home, take the spirit of this conference with you. I am praying that the spirit of the Lord's prayer will be carried in your hearts, then the Lord will give you the power to inspire others with His glorious Spirit. May the Lord bless you in your homes to sense the great things there are to do, and the wonderful things that are awaiting you. If you feel your work is hard, remember it is not your work, it is the work of the Lord. Go to Him—for He will bear our burdens if we will only take them to Him in the proper Spirit. May the Lord bless you and help you in your work. May you remember that little motto to welcome the task that makes you go beyond yourself if you would grow. One of the speakers said the same thought today, that if you live to the very best you can today you will have added strength tomorrow, and if you will do more than you feel that you can do today, God will give you strength to do more tomorrow. May the Lord bless you. I am so thankful the Lord gave you the means that you could come here, and I
