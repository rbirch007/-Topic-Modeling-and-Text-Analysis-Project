Why Make a Budget? Jean Cox The homemaker who said to the automobile salesman, "We haven't made a budget yet, so are not ready to investigate any automobiles," had the right standard for financial success in the home. This home, by the way, represented a close partnership, as the wife felt some definite responsibility in helping to meet necessary payments on land, machinery, new home and furnishing. Her responsibility as a member of the firm extended to personal expenditures as well, since she was just as careful of what she spent as if she were entirely responsible for meeting the bills and notes necessary for an expanding farm business. The Home-Maker's View It may be interesting to note that this homemaker had earned money for several years before her marriage and knew not only the value of money but also the importance of accounts and budgets before her salary check became past history. Her own financial independence had convinced her that money has only a limited purchasing power, and her experience had convinced her that if the dollars are used to satisfy one want another desire will go unsatisfied. In her home, limited by conditions imposed by a new country, this wife and mother continued to spend the income so as to satisfy the greatest possible number of family needs. Success in satisfying needs is largely dependent upon careful planning. It is said that the woman of yesterday thought and thought and then spent, while the woman of today spends and spends and then thinks and thinks. Giving thought as to how to spend after the purchase is made is parallel to locking the gate after the horse is gone. Neither worry nor regrets will make money available for the comforts that should have been purchased with the part of the family check frittered away with large and small extravagances. Attempts to justify this kind of spending frequently accompany the off-hand remark, "I should worry; Dad has to pay the bills." This is, to say the least, characterless conversation. What is a Budget? One of the big reasons why the average person does not radiate much enthusiasm for budgets is due largely to the fact that the term budgets and accounts are used interchangeably. WHY MAKE A BUDGET? 599 Accounts are only records of expenditure, they are memorandums of expense, but budgets are like the blue prints of a building; they are the plan ahead of time for spending the income. Making a budget is merely a device for thinking out the division of the income so as to obtain major satisfactions and pleasures. It helps to divide the weekly, monthly or yearly income so as to cover the needs of the individual and family. Another reason why budgets are apt to be in disfavor is due to the fact that the budget cannot be imposed upon the family or individual. When this is attempted vexation results. A plan ahead of time for spending your money is necessarily different from the division best suited to the needs of your neighbors. The right division for the individual family is only possible when it is based on actual record of expenditures. With this record at hand, careful analysis is necessary in order to determine what expenditures are to be definitely classified as Must Haves or May Haves. How Budgets Must Differ Miss A., in her bachelor quarters, spends more than the ordinary fifth of her salary for rent, but she is convinced that in order to be reasonably happy and comfortable she must have the apartment rather than a room. In order to keep within her living expense allowance, she spends less for food because she prepares a part of her meals in the small kitchenette and also does her personal laundry. Miss B. Has only a room at less cost than Miss A's apartment, but necessarily spends more for food and running expense than Miss A. They both receive the same salary and are able to make practically the same investment saving each month. Each one is spending in accordance with the plan she has worked out, in order that they may have peace of mind in their living arrangements. Budget specialists, in banks and investment hvuses, have worked out different devices to help their patrons decide whether or not it is possible to make readjustments of their opinions as to how many wants they must or may satisfy on their income. Determining the best expenditure of funds is dependent upon judgment. As a device for determining between essentials and non-essentials, it may be wise to list the "must haves" in one column and the "may haves" in another and then divide the income proportionately between the two. MustHaves and May-Haves Miss Agnes Donham, special lecturer and writer on household budgets, divides family and personal expenditures into two big classes?Must Haves and May Haves. The must haves deal 
 with stern reality such as minimum essentials in shelter, food, ciothing and running expenses and advancement on the same income. Different families have different standards as to minimum requirements for health and happiness. The Jones', for instance, feel that considerable sacrifice is commendable in order that the Jones' children may have the opportunities afforded by a better neighborhood than the ordinary budget division allows for rent. The Browns, in contrast to their old friends the Jones', are anxious to trim their expenses for shelter and chothes and incidentals in order that William Jr. May have opportunity for musical education. The following Budget Wheel worked out by Miss Donham is a good device to help analyze and classify expenditures. Where the income is low, more than a fifth of the income is spent for food. This extra amount is usually taken out of the fifth allotted to development and clothing. Representative of Needs One of the biggest arguments for systematic household accounts and budgets lies in their value in character training for the younger members of the family. Children should be taught appreciation of what is done for them by their parents. This should help them to understand what they cost their parents in terms of dollars and cents. Frequently, unruly boys and girls have never been made acquainted with the amount of income the family has available for its needs, and only where there is understanding among all of the members of the family does the budget become a family budget, representative of the needs of the different members of the group. Unlimited and uncontrolled spending is not conducive to good morale in the home. Let the carefully worked out Children Number in family 
 budget determine the amount of spending money each member of the group should request. Stabilizes the Family Budget planning gives stability to the family. Social workers find that 9/10 of the broken families break down on the financial rocks. One well known attorney says that 95% of divorces are directly or indirectly dependent upon questions of family finance. Much of this failure in homemaking is due to the fact that either one or both of the contracting parties have either failed to think or failed to face financial facts that needed careful consideration. Careless thinking in terms of income and outgo result in the accumulation of more bills than available funds can pay. The result usually is financial disaster, unhappiness, loss of confidence and legal separation. A budget helps you to know how you are using your life. Record of plans for and expenditure of your funds help to tell the kind of a person you really are. Comparatively few people have had the opportunity of buying all that they desired. Very few of those who have achieved have been able to develop in their chosen field, without sacrifice and careful planning. If your expenditures show many expenditures that mean little or nothing in lasting satisfaction, it is possible that your present living is not purposeful and worth while. To Think in Terms of Budgets It takes considerable discussion to help people think in terms of budgets. Budget experts are often of the same opinion as the old darkey who says: "T tell 'em what I'm going to tell, I tells 'em and den I tells 'em what I've done tole 'em!" Many people get the idea that accounts and budgets are merely devices for saving. That is not the case. The economist does not stand for pessimistic saving, for life is not made for saving. People who have only money enough to get along with should not be asked to save. The thought behind the saving is to make life more abundant. The idea of planning before spending is to help people live up to better ideals for deferred purchasing power means more abundant life. Working to save and saving to live more completely represent two definite ideals. Consider Relative Values Another use of the budget idea is to show the relationship between income, house value and furnishing. Where the income is WHY MAKE A BUDGET? 603 $3,600 a year a fifth of it may be spent for shelter which should include heat, taxes and depreciation as well. The value of the home is partly determined by its actual renting value. An apartment, for instance, rents theoretically for 10% of its total value. A person living in an apartment where rent is $75 a month is living in the equivalent of a house valued at about $7,000. Few people have any idea of the total value of their house furnishings. This, of course, is difficult because different articles need more frequent replacement than others. Then, too, hangings and furnishings are sometimes disposed of before they are really worn out. Even with these difficulties the average family should know approximately how much their furnishings are worth. If you queston this, make an estimate as to the value of your furnishings, then sit down and list the furnishings you have in each room. The next step is to try to duplicate at a furniture store or a second hand store what you have in your home. Probably considerable surprise will result from the grand total. The question of "How much should be spent for furnishings?" should be carefully considered. Home economists who have made a careful study of this feel that not more than 25% of the house cost should be spent for the furnishing of the home. This percentage should be taken into consideration in planning a home for the total investment of the house, lot and furnishing should not be more according to Dr. Andrews than the family income for three years. The Family on Economic Bases Is your family on a staple economic basis? What determines economic stability? Does spending money for auto, theatres, new house, expensive furnishing, elaborate clothing, travel, etc., give assurance of certain financial freedom? This freedom is short lived, however, if there isn't proportionate savings and reinvestment for the rainy day ahead. If the income is sufficient to warrant expenditures for Iuxuries then some savings are necessary, if the plan for spending is approved by budget specialists. Dr. Benjamin R. Andrews thinks that the family ambitious for an auto should have an equal amount in the savings bank for a rainy day. The most accepted reasons for saving are the emergency, dependence of old age, care of dependents, building a home and education of the younger members of the family. The most generally accepted views for living within a budget are to live more completely, to be safeguarded from financial disaster, to live harmoniously with the other members of the group.
