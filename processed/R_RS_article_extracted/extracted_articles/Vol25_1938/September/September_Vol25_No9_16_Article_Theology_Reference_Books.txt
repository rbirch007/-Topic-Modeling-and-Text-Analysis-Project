RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE
Literature
THE ADVANCE OF THE NOVEL
LESSON 3
ROBINSON CRUSOE (Concluded)
NOWHERE does Defoe reveal his mastery of drama better than in the finding of the footprint in the sand by Robinson Crusoe. Crusoe has been upon his island for years and has been left entirely to his own devices. He has built up a "town house" and a "country estate" and is really the master of his entire island, happy in his possessions, safe, and reconciled to his fate. Then suddenly when all is going smoothly and he has property to lose, he discovers that he is not the only inhabitant of the island.
Defoe makes use of this incident to picture Robinson's terror and his dread. The religion which he had constructed for himself in which he had his God, all-knowing, all-powerful, and sympathetic, went upon the rocks with a crash. Crusoe was human, after all. He was like us all who, when our hopes are shattered and real dangers loom, wonder if God does really know and if he does really care enough to save us. After hours of fear, Crusoe, like us, returned to prayer and to his Bible for solace.
"One morning early," says he, "lying in my bed, and filled with thoughts about my danger from the appearance of savages, I found it discomposed me very much; upon which those words of the scripture came into my thoughts, 'Call upon Me in the day of trouble, and I will deliver, and thou shalt glorify Me.' Upon this, rising cheerfully out of bed, my heart was not only comforted but I was guided and encouraged to pray earnestly to God for deliverance. When I had done praying, I took up my Bible, and opening to read, the first words that presented to me were, 'Wait on the Lord, and be of good cheer, and He shall strengthen thy heart; wait, I say, on the Lord.' It is impossible to express the comfort this thought gave me, and in return I thankfully laid down the book, and was no more sad; at least, not on that occasion."
Crusoe so comforted himself that he, like all of us might do, began to wonder if he had seen a footprint at all. He returned to the place and to his dismay found that it was not only a footprint, but that it was much larger than his own. Furthermore, it could not have been his own footprint. "Both these things," said he, "filled my head with new imaginations, and gave me the vapors again to the highest degree, so that I shook with cold like one in an ague." Defoe, however, did not allow Crusoe to find another person on the island at once. He used this experience of the footprint as the motivation of a great deal of activity on the part cf his hero. Crusoe set about strengthening his position on the island by building "barbed wire entanglements" or what amounted to the same thing. Years passed, this single footprint remaining a nightmare in the mind of the man who did not know what to fear or whence his danger might come.
