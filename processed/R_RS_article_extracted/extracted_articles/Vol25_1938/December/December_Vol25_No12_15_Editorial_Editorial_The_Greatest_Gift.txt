12 Editorial The Greatest Gift EARLY two thousand years ago in Bethlehem of Judea a stable offered refuge to a mother and her babe. Humble shepherds watching their flocks by night were shaken by the light and the words of the angel, "Fear not for behold I bring you good tidings of great joy.... Glory to God in the highest and on 'earth peace, good will toward men." And wise men came from Chaldea and knelt before the Babe. Thirty-three years intervened between the birth and death of Christ. His life was a continual and uninterrupted life of sacrifice, perfected by a supreme sacrifice on Calvary. It gave to mankind the greatest of all gifts, the promise of eternal life. Yet even as He made the supreme sacrifice "the people stood beholding, and the rulers also with them derided Him.... "And the soldiers also mocked him, coming to him and offering him vinegar." Eighteen hundred and five years. After the birth of the babe in Bethlehem another babe was born in Sharon, Windsor County, Vermont, to Lucy Mack Smith, under circumstances and surroundings of the most humble. This babe was destined to be the Prophet of the Last Dispensation, to be an instrument in the hands of God to restore again in all its purity that same Gospel, giving promise of eternal life, for which Christ was sacrificed. Like Christ Joseph Smith's life was short? thirty-nine years. It was one of self-denial and unselfish zeal for the salvation of mankind. Yet his life culminated in a supreme sacrifice at Carthage. A record of the Martyrdom says, "It seems unaccountable that any generation of men could have been so utterly cruel and barbarous to one preaching so pure a doctrine and laboring with such self denial for the good of all mankind." The Christmas season brings to Latter-day Saints a remembrance of the lives of these chosen ones of the Father. It brings an appreciation of the Gospel for which both made the supreme sacrifice in order that men might have the greatest of all gifts. The Gospel is the source of all that is best in the world. It is the influence that turns men to one another with a spirit of love and good will. Stephens are largely for advertising purposes. Therefore the sponsors of the programs, since they are spending fabulous sums of money in this way, present the type of program that appears to attract the most listeners. But how can they tell who listens? Obviously, only by the number of letters, telegrams and phone calls they receive complimenting or disparaging the programs. Mr. Lennox D. Murdoch, Director of Station Operations at KSL, estimates that less than one out of every thousand listeners writes to the station or sponsor about a program. This means that if thirty letters are received, all complimenting a program, the sponsor thinks that thirty thousand listeners like his presentation. This assumption is often untrue. There may be many among that thirty thousand listeners who would prefer a program of an entirely different type. The desires of these people are not considered, because they are not known by those who make up the programs.
Now, all Singing Mothers must like good music?otherwise they would not be members of our organization. The only way we can increase the amount of good music on the radio is to write to our radio station, condemning the bad and praising the good. If a large number of our Singing Mothers were to write in, saying, "The chant of the auctioneer gets on my nerves, and so does the jazz music that usually accompanies it. Why aren't there more radio programs in this country like the Ford Sunday Evening Hour?" we might be instrumental in raising the standard of radio music throughout the country. Let us do this. Not once, but regularly, every time we hear something of which we particularly approve or disapprove. Keep a little pile of penny postcards on your radio and send several each week, praising a good program or objecting to a bad one. It is little trouble, and over a period of time will raise the cultural value of radio entertainment.
MANY have asked for a reprint of the list of numbers for next April Conference. Here it is: Religious numbers: "Sanctus," (Gounod); "O Morn of Beauty," (Sibelius); "O Bread of Life," (Franck); "The Lord's Prayer," (Gates); "Silent Night," (arranged by Epperson). All these except "Silent Night," have already been sung by the Singing Mothers. We must have two or three new numbers for April, so that we do not repeat so much. One will be taken from the new hymn book soon to be issued by the Relief Society. At the end of the "Sanctus" (Gounod) you will find a short piece called "Benedictus." This will be the second. Another may be announced later. Secular numbers: "Dedication," (Franz); "By the Bend of the River," (Edwards); "Annie Laurie;" "In the Time of Roses," (Reichardt); "Song of May," (Asper).
