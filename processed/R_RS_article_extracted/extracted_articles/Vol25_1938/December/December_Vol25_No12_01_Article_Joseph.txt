The interesting little Jewish settlement of Talpioth is located on the Plain of Rephaim and here lives the celebrated Hebrew scholar Joseph Klausner. A short distance beyond Talpioth, on the left-hand side of the road, there is a cistern known as the Well of the Magi. According to tradition the three wise men, after leaving King Herod, stopped at this spot and stooped to draw water. While doing so they saw a star reflected in the well. They followed the star which led them to the place where the Christ was born. There are other traditions to the effect that the well never dries up and that the Virgin Mary stopped near it on her journey to Bethlehem. Soon we came tothe Tomb of Rachel, a relatively modern building with a dome on top. The present structure dates back only to 1841 when Sir Joseph Montefiore made certain additions to the old monument. The tomb was revered in Christ's time and when the traveler Arculf visited it in 670 A. D. He described it as an unadorned "Pyramid? 794  Many eminent scholars believe that the "tomb" does not represent the burial place of Rachel at all. Dr. Gustaf Dalman holds that the original burial place was near Rama, north of Jerusalem, and that later the "tomb" was erroneously transferred to the present spot. He says, "One can understand that when the original place of Rachel's grave, the Benjaminite Ephrat, disappeared, the Ephrata of Judah could not refrain from appropriating to herself the grave of Jacob's favorite wife, especially since all the other wives of the Patriarchs rested in Judah." If one will take the trouble to read and compare Gen. 35:19 with I Sam. 10:2 there will be found certain difficulties to reconcile respecting the location of Rachel's tomb.
