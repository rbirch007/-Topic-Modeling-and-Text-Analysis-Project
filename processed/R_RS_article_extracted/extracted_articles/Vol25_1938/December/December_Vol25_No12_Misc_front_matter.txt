2 A Visit to Bethlehem On Christmas Eve By Sidney B. Sperry T this season of the year our thoughts turn more than customarily to the Holy Land. We associate the birth of the Christ Child with the place of his nativity, Bethlehem. The reader may therefore be interested in following an account of the experiences and observations of the writer who spent Christmas Eve in that little city a few years ago. There was no particular glamor or allurement attached toa visit to Bethlehem?I had been there a number of times before?but there was a thrill at the prospect of spending a few pleasant evening hours in the open fields outside the city. Not only this, but the trip was to be made in the company of archaeologists and Bible scholars who had planned to rejoice in a way befitting the announcement of "good tidings of great joy." Our party left the American School of Oriental Research in Jerusalem late in the afternoon. The weather was cool and pleasant and the sky clear. Our Arab chauffeurs drove at a very leisurely pace, for Bethlehem is only a short distance south and west of the Holy City? about five and a half miles as I recall. We left Jerusalem by the Jaffa gate, descended the Valley of Hinnom and then in succession passed many interesting historical spots: the supposed country house of Caiaphas the High Priest, the tree on which Judas hanged himself (so tradition), and the Valley of Rephaim where David defeated the Philistines (2 Sam. 23: 12, 13).
