Relief Society Conference April 1 and 2, 1938 By Julia A. F. Lund, General Secretary THE Annual Conference of the Relief Society was held April 1 and 2, 1938, in Salt Lake City, Utah. President Louise Y. Robison presided. The following sessions were held: Officers' Meeting, Social Welfare Department, Work and Business Department, Relief Society Magazine Department, Secretaries' Department, Music Department. Two general sessions were held in the Tabernacle. The reception for the Singing Mothers, the one for the Stake Officers, and the President's Breakfast were delightful events. The attendance was very gratifying, and included enthusiastic workers from 116 stakes and 12 missions. The following is the attendance record at the Officers' Meeting held in the Assembly Hall, Friday morning, April 1, at 10 o'clock: Mission Presidents, 12; Stake Presidents, 108; Counselors, 135; Secretaries, 67; Board Members, 404; Total, 726. The ushers were from the Salt Lake OFFICERS' and Liberty Stake Relief Societies. Among the outstanding features of the Conference were the magnificent music, and the launching of the Membership Campaign. In connection with this, special attention must be called to the membership song, "One Hundred Thousand Strong," composed by Beatrice F. Stevens of the General Board of Relief Society. The "Rallying Song" was introduced by a double trio from the Ensign Stake, directed by Ruth Jensen Clawson, and a talented group representative of Relief Society members with Jean H. Wesseman as accompanist. Others whose talents in music added much to the meetings were Lily Priestly's organ prelude. Inez Robinson Preece's quartet, and the beautiful solo by Eliza Bringhurst. The combined choruses of Relief Society Singing - Mothers, directed by Charlotte O. Sackett, with Alta B. Cassity and Frank Asper as accompanists, made Relief Society history. MEETING GREETINGS AND OFFICIAL INSTRUCTIONS By President Louise Y. Robison MEETING with the devoted leaders of Relief Society is a continued source of joy and inspiration. REFERRING to the ruling on donations received by Visiting Teachers, we feel more strongly than ever that all donations collected by Visiting Teachers belong in the Charity Fund. If members of Relief Society or others wish to contribute to the General Fund, we shall be most grateful to them if they will give or send such contributions to the President or Secretary of the Relief Society. WE would like to emphasize the request that officers refrain from using Relief Society for advertising purposes. The fine project for better methods of laundering clothes brought many offers from soap manufacturers for demonstration in Relief Society. We are quite opposed to having any commercial article featured in our meetings. WE have received many compliments from the General Authorities of the Church, morticians, and from Relief Society Presidents on our suggestion that funeral flowers remain undisturbed until after the mourners have gone from the building. We were advised to take this action by members of the General Authorities. IN a few of our stakes, a charge is made for luncheon served on Work and Business day. We hope you will find some other way of obtaining finances. Many of our sisters would be happy to pay for their luncheon, but in each ward there are fine, faithful members who cannot afford this expense and who will not feel comfortable in attending these meetings. "THERE are occasionally Ward Presidents who do not work in harmony with their Stake President. They do not attend Union meetings and feel that they need no advice in the conduct of the Relief Society work. This is a serious condition. There must be complete harmony between stake and ward officers or the work of Relief Society suffers. Before any woman accepts an office in any department of Relief Society she should know what the requirements are and be willing to live up to them. Unless ward officers give loyal support to stake officers, they cannot expect loyalty from their own group. Remember always that the spirit of the Lord is with us only when there is harmony and a desire to cooperate for the good of the whole group. WE have had a wonderful response to the hurried request sent from our office a week ago for  a report on what the Relief Society had done for Church Security, The Presiding Bishopric asked us to get the report for the First Presidency to use at Conference. Within a week, ninety-nine stakes and missions had reported. Although the Southern States Mission is so far away our Mission Mother, Sister Clayson, had her report in the office on time. N making the survey for the clothing requirements of our people Relief Society has discovered some families in urgent need at the present time. In a few cases the bishops have requested the Relief Society to supply those needs from their funds. Evidently these bishops are new in the work, as Relief Society has not now, nor ever has had, sufficient funds to care for rent, light, food and clothing. Relief Society offers service. Our funds are to be used in cases of emergency and to supplement the work of the bishop. HERE are some very fine organizations outside of our Relief Society who are working for the benefit of humanity. We are eager to have our Societies cooperate in spreading useful information but we are opposed to having regular Relief Society class work interfered with or having dues to any other organization collected in our meetings. To'the extent that individual members can subscribe to these helpful movements we are in hearty accord, but this is entirely outside of the Relief Society. WE advise Relief Societies to cooperate with the Agricultural Colleges, with the county agents and demonstrators in any state in which they reside. These trained people have valuable information on the needs of the body and the kinds of 326 food which supply those needs. Deficiency diseases, which result from improper diet, are as great a menace as the contagious diseases. To understand the chemical requirements of the body and to know the foods which supply those needs is of great importance to mothers. WE are making a change in the Relief Society Magazine Honor Roll awards. Instead of all Relief Societies competing on the same basis, there will be three groups. One group will include all wards and branches with membership up to 49 inclusive. One group will be those Societies whose membership is from 50 to 99 inclusive, and a third group will include those with a membership of 100 or over. We feel this is more just. Presidents will please appoint a member of the Board to be responsible for the activities of ward Magazine Agents. One recently reached us from Sister Nibley of the Northwestern States Mission with some very fine reports. The Northern States Mission sends them to us frequently. They are most helpful. Sister May Green Hinckley of the Northern States Mission gave us the very fine idea of placing useful Relief Society information on the back of our membership cards. FROM CORNER STONE TO KEYSTONE By Rae B. Barker. ONE sweep of the mind carries us from March 17, 1842 to March 17, 1942. In these two dates, and the two significant events associated with them, we find the impetus for our present Relief Society Membership Campaign. On that original Relief Society birthday a little handful of women gathered to hear the plan conceived by the Prophet Joseph Smith. With him they built the foundation and laid the corner stone of our great organization. That is now history?we are still making history. The momentous event of the one-hundredth, or centennial, birthday is but a short time off. We are anticipating that date with eager interest for it is our keen ambition to be one hundred thousand strong by that time. The time is ripe to launch a big membership building program. There is an underlying purpose in this campaign which is much deeper than the mere enrollment of numbers. That purpose is to build Latter-day Saint women, and_ to strengthen our organization so that it can serve in ever wider fields. The strength and power of Relief Society is measured by the number of women it serves. How great can be the force of an ever increasing membership of spirited workers, positively charged with a desire for intellectual and spiritual growth, with high moral standards, and with the desire to serve others! The magnitude and scope of such a force not only improves the morale and fiber of our Church, community and nation, but it strengthens the individual so that with greater assurance she may meet the ever changing conditions of this interesting age. In 1842 we numbered only eighteen. By the end of the first year our membership had increased to about one thousand. By the end of the 75th year (1917), our numbers had increased to approximately fifty thousand. Then came the war period with its increased demands for welfare service. The result? Our rate of increase was about four times as great as it had previously been. During the past fifteen years, our growth, even though it was not as pronounced as during the war period, has continued steady and sound. What of the next four years? Spotlight of Attention RELIEF SOCIETY wants to do big things. In the next four years we want to definitely accelerate our rate of growth. The present tempo of living is much faster than it was a century ago. Consequently, we expect big things to be done in a short time. Interest usually follows attention; so we are turning the spotlight of attention upon the challenging problem of increasing our membership. Shall we be One Hundred Thousand strong by 1942?. Let us emphasize the strong, for we must continue to build substantially, always maintaining and elevating the high standards that have formed the foundations of this enduring structure. We must build for permanency. General Plan THAT shall be the general approach to our problem? Growth and building seem to connote the same thing. The membership campaign is in. Every sense a building program. Down through the years many, RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE many women, each finding personal satisfaction and each making a personal contribution, have in the aggregate been building a serviceable "living monument". The arch has been chosen to symbolize this monument, and we will set out to complete this particular membership arch. Significance of Corner Stone and Keystone A LITTLE about the construction of an arch may be of interest. Two terms used to designate important features of the structure are the corner stone and the keystone. The tradition of the corner stone is common knowledge. Often within the corner stone are sealed historical information, resolutions and ideals. The significance of the keystone may not be so commonly known. We often hear of a key man?one who is so important that he is indispensable to his organization; or a key situation?a situation upon which other conditions hinge. The keystone is just as vital to the arch. It is the center of the crown of the arch, and, being the last member to be set in place, is regarded as binding the whole together. Thus the strength and solidarity of the arch depends upon the keystone. Use of the Keystone F, in the next four years we can average a 3314% increase over the 1937 enrollment of about 75,000, we can reach the 100,000 goal. For example, if a stake with a present enrollment of 300 increases to 325 the first year, to 350 in two years, and to 400 in four years, it will have reached its objective. Any stake, ward or mission making its 3314 9% increase in four years will have achieved "keystone success." 329 Plan WE are immediately concerned with a plan that will help us reach our objective. Since we are looking ahead to 1942, a four-year program suggests itself. Our four-year theme is "Building the Membership Arch." The slogan, which we hope will be lavishly used to make our people Relief Society conscious, is: Members Old, Members New, One Hundred Thousand by 42. For psychological reasons, short plans are best. So, the four-year plan will be broken up into one-year plans. Fundamentals in any Campaign HERE are certain fundamentals which are important to the success of any campaign. First: A name, or theme can serve a real purpose. Second: A short time for an intensive drive is most successful. Third: Competent leadership is necessary. Fourth: The use of showmanship is decidedly important. Fifth: Follow-up work is essential to success. Sixth: There should be a simple and effective system of awards. Theme THEME can transform an uninteresting matter-of-fact subject into something of special interest. An idea becomes concrete enough to attract attention. It identifies. A theme serves also as a motif which lends itself to featuring. A theme appropriate to this year's membership activity is ""New Construction." We leave it to you as stakes and wards to develop this idea of new construction in your own way. We might keep in mind that, 330 as always, the ward is the actual building site, and that quality materials and skilled labor are of utmost importance to the success of the project. Short Time Drive INTEREST fades to the vanishing point if a drive continues too long. The 1938 campaign will be launched with the opening of Relief Society next fall, and the drive will end on December 15th. All reports can and should be in the general office with the regular annual reports. Competent Leadership NO drive can be very successful without competent leadership. More will be said on this point under the heading of "Personnel." Showmanship SHOWMANSHIP is the art of dressing up an idea. By this means, a point may be stressed in a more effective way than by the use of words alone. Everyone likes a show, enjoying the play atmosphere. Often we are willing to work hardest at our play. "We are visual minded. Eye impressions stick." An old Chinese proverb says, "One time seeing is worth one thousand times hearing." You will find many opportunities for the use of showmanship: in publicity features, stunts, displays, progress charts, etc. For instance, to visualize the progress being made, one suggestion might be to use an arch designed as a picture frame, and mount on it snapshots of new members as they join your group. Much interest and fun might be stimulated by selecting "catchy" snapshots where possible. Hart WHEN something has been accomplished everyone is ready to declare it was easy, but before it is begun they say it is impossible. This was somewhat the feeling many of our Relief Society women had when our Work and Business Project was launched Jast April. All the difficulties of such an enterprise were seen at once, yet, a thing well begun is half done, and moreover, the surmounting of: the first barrier gives courage and strength for those following. This proved to be the case with our project. In order to get definite and tangible information as to the contribution our organization is making in its legitimate field of economics and family relationship we asked that a survey be made with two objectives in mind, first, to supply factual data to verify or challenge general statements about present day family life; second, to help women to understand and evaluate their own work and problems. The following is a brief summary of that survey: No. Of stakes reporting, 99; No. Of stakes participating in project, 94; No. Of stakes reporting more than 50 per cent of membership participating, 35; No. Of wards reporting, 849; No. Of wards participating, 720; No. Of women making bread, 84 per cent, 25,459; No. Of women doing sewing, 75 per cent, 24,485; No. Of women doing launering, 92 per cent, 28,832; No. Of women carrying on project in bread making, 64 per cent, 5,652; No. Of women carrying on project in sewing, 61 per cent, 4,342; No. Of women carrying on project in laundering, 76 per cent, 5,836. The figures show that more women do their own laundering than other activities. Only 50 per cent of women were interested in the project at first, but as it progressed more became active. Many liked the discussions and the actual work, but did not want to keep records or file reports. Demonstrations, lectures, discussions, exhibits, contests, debates, visits to flour mills and laundries were some of the methods used to put over the project. To the question of what definite value the project was such answers as these were given: It has created an interest in women's problems, and a desire to do more efficient, scientific and professional work. More wholesome and better bread is being made and used than ever before. Non-members have become interested with the result that they want to know more about Relief Society work. All the women participating agree that it is more economical and satisfying to create and do things at home. While our objectives have not been fully realized, the project has been very much worth while, and is more far reaching than we know. Since many women feel they are just home makers and these activities too commonplace to be studied, Work and Business leaders have a big opportunity to help them to be the very best home makers. This department is comparable in its cultural value to the other prescribed courses, and requires thorough preparation in making it one of the best departments in the Relief Society. It can be done!  SUGGESTIONS FOR WORK AND BUSINESS FOR 1938-39 By Donna D. Sorensen FROM the beginning of our organization sewing for the needy has been, and probably ever will be, the most important obligation of the Work and Business department. This activity should never be neglected. However, in response to repeated demands which have come from the stakes for additional help in planning for this day the General Board will print in the July Magazine nine topics for discussion for the coming year on the "Selection and Preparation of Food for Proper Nutrition." Under each of these topics will be given the names of various pamphlets and addresses where these may be obtained. No lessons will be written and the way the stakes and wards utilize this material in the Magazine will be largely left to them. However, the General Board suggests:
1. That the use of this material be optional in the wards and may be used in connection with the traditional activities of the day.
2. That this matter of nutrition is worthy of serious consideration by the wards of the Church as it is timely and worth while.
3. That if any additional material or any other subject be selected by the wards it must be presented by the ward Work and Business leader to the stake for its approval and at any event any subject selected must be on some phase of homemaking. Book reviews or any program on any subject other than home making are out of order on Work and Business day.
4. That the stake Work and Business leader will be responsible for obtaining the pamphlets for her various wards and obtain them early enough to distribute at the Union meeting preceding the day they will be utilized.
5. That if this material on nutrition is used in the wards that the treatment of it be considered in an informal manner with the idea of having an interchange of the thoughts and opinions of all the women present, rather than presenting the material as a formal lesson.
6. That in wards where this material is considered the ward counselor in charge of Work and Business and the ward Work and Business leader consult, and conjointly choose a woman to lead in this informal discussion on Work and Business day. A different woman will be chosen to lead the discussion each month, thus distributing the responsibility and bringing more women into activity. We suggest that each of these women chosen be asked to attend the Union meeting where the pamphlets on her subject for discussion will be distributed.
7. That if the wards in a stake choose to discuss this subject of nutrition the stake leader of Work and Business give it some consideration at Union meeting and the time and emphasis be left to the discretion of the stake leader.
PLANNING FOR SOCIABILITY
Nellie O. Parker
Sociability is an important gateway to an abundant life. The more we use this gate and extend our friendly contacts the more we enlarge our world.
"The world stands out on either side No wider than the heart is wide."
RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE
Friendship is one of the sweetest things in the world. The intellectual and spiritual things we share with others make us richer. The more we give away of this spiritual wealth the richer we are.
Social recreation of any type that helps us enjoy fellowship with one another and gives an opportunity for growth, opportunity to be and become one's self, and to have joy in living is very desirable. It is fundamental to our nature to crave companionship. We get pleasure from doing things together. Even hard work is enjoyable when done in a group?for example, a ward dinner or community fruit canning. We experience recreation of spirit in singing and playing together, and when we share food with one another we obtain a completeness of sociability that nothing else seems to give.
We should aim to make our Relief Society contacts as friendly as possible. Let us think about what others are interested in and try to make them happy. This is a vital element of true etiquette. Assign certain members to welcome everyone who comes to meeting. Strangers and visitors should be given special attention and introduced at least to those by whom they are seated. Let someone look after their comfort and see that they enjoy themselves.
We recommend that Work and Business day be made a happy social day, a sewing circle linked to a party spirit with an atmosphere of informality, insuring free and easy discussion and a feeling of friendly cooperation. Secure constructive conversation by directing it in proper channels to correlate with the project in hand. See that everyone feels she is a vital part of the organization. Plan the social hour so that everyone takes her turn in helping when luncheon or refreshments are served. Give thought to planning the social hour; draw on your originality; vary your menus; have little surprises and glorify the common things, but keep them simple and within the reach of the frugal purse. Keep abreast of the times.
If members, especially leaders, will radiate their enthusiasm and give their best thought to Relief Society, planning ahead and looking forward to it as one of the big things of their lives, they will stimulate interest and enthusiasm in others. Everyone will want Relief Society; it will gain in prestige. Those who work in the organization love it enthusiastically. Our problem is to give everyone something to do. The good leader will divide work, getting as many to participate as possible. Make an important occasion of the installation of new members, and also the welcoming back of inactive ones into active service again. Let someone sponsor the prospective member and make a little talk in her behalf, telling of her interests, talents and achievements. During the social hour the new members can be the honored guests. Everyone should meet them and extend a friendly hand in welcoming them into the Society.
For festive days plan decorations that are typically "our own." We have great ideals and traditions, we have only to work out our own symbols. Let us encourage originality and artistic talent. Let us make Relief Society very alluring and desirable, remembering that those who serve Relief Society most, love it best.

SERVICE AND SELLING
By Vida Fox Clawson
A good magazine representative must first have the ambition to place the Magazine in the home of every Latter-day Saint. She must think about it. Why should it go into the home of every Latter-day Saint? In the first place, the very fact that it is there testifies that the home is a home of culture and the mother discriminating in her taste. The fact that it is there increases the faith of the children. Even if they don't read it, it is good for them to have the Magazine in the home.
This is a day when the good and the bad are so closely intertwined that it is difficult to choose the right. For example, we listen to a glorious radio rendition of Aida or some grand opera, and we are uplifted, then we realize that the sponsors are perhaps cigarette advertisers. This advertising is very subtle but very effective. Few of us realize the effect of it. After months of such advertising, we become used to the thing which at first shocks us. We say, "Well, I guess it's all right. I guess it isn't as harmful as we thought it was." We grow less and less rebellious.
The same thing happens to the boy who learns to admire the editor of a magazine through reading his editorials, and then some day perhaps he sees a picture of this man with a cigarette in his hand. The boy, if young and brought up according to Mormon ideals, is shocked. But he begins this process of thinking, "Well, I guess it's not so bad after all," and finally gets to the point where he thinks it is not so wrong to smoke.
The more magazines advertising tobacco, liquor and things of that kind which we have in our homes the more we need the Relief Society Magazine as a supporting anchor, for it comes to us absolutely unpolluted, not only in its articles but in its advertising.
It is difficult these days to choose the right from the wrong. Right now you and I would have no definite convictions about this life and the life to come if we didn't feel that we are being led by divine leadership, inspired leadership. Those who have the publication of this Magazine in charge are inspired women. ACHIEVING THE GOAL
By Clara B. Wright?Salt Lake Stake
As a group in Union meeting we have studied the pamphlet, "Instructions To Magazine Agents." We have spent several Union meetings in intensive study. By our first Union meeting we have prepared and handed to President Williams a year-round program. In February our Stake Secretary gives us the entire individual ward enrollments. By carefully watching subscriptions we are able to secure large percentages.
A yearly report is compiled by the Stake at the close of the year and on the day that we make our awards in Union meeting this report is handed to the ward Magazine Representative who in turn takes the report to her President. We have found this has been a stimulus for effort the next year.
To illustrate: The Seventeenth Ward has an enrollment of 175 for 1938. Their total 1937 Magazine subscriptions were 123. Since October 1937, they have secured ten new subscriptions which makes a total of 133 subscriptions. 175 total enrollment minus 133 subscriptions equals 42 subscriptions to obtain in order to reach 100 per cent. From March to October is six months, and 42 divided by 6 equals 7 new subscriptions to be obtained each month. Of course we must not lose one active subscription. NUTRITION AND ITS RELATION TO CHILD WELFARE
By Dr. William McKay
As public health workers we are delighted to know that the mothers are devoting so much of their activities to the study and solution of vital health problems. By giving place on your program to the subject of nutrition and child welfare, I think I can safely assume that you, as representatives of the Social Welfare Department of the Relief Society of the Church, are convinced that throughout our state there is ample evidence among our children of the lack of proper nutrition. A study of the reports of the many health agencies and dental clinics showing the numbers of physical defects, the prevalence of malnutrition, and the very high percentage of our child population with carious teeth should be sufficient to convince anyone that education on the subject of nutrition is urgently needed.
The first question which naturally arises in our minds is, "What is the meaning of the word nutrition?" One of the best definitions I have found is that given by Sir Robert McCarrison who defined nutrition as the fundamental function on which the condition of the body, that is to say, health depends. It is not merely food, nor that which nourishes. Food is the instrument of nourishment, nutrition is the act of using it?the series of coordinated processes whereby the nourishment of the body is effected. A primary purpose of the function of nutrition is: thus, to establish and to sustain the structure and function of all organs and parts of the body?to keep, in short, the mechanisms of the body in perfect running order.
Since body development and function begin months before that body is born into the world it is of the greatest importance that our expectant mothers have a clear understanding of the importance of diet during pregnancy. Sherman of Columbia University has shown the very marked physical differences in the offspring from mothers afforded an adequate diet as compared with the offspring of mothers with an inadequate diet. Sound healthy bodies with strong, well built bones, and teeth are the birthright of every child. The foundation for these is laid long before the child is born. Every expectant mother develops a mental image of the sort of boy or girl she would like to bear. Naturally, she hopes, and may I say?prays, that her child will be strong, sturdy, well shaped, of sound frame and have beautiful teeth. Not every mother realizes that the care she takes of herself during pregnancy to ensure a safe delivery is also a vital part of the planning necessary for sound bones and teeth. Regulated diets, under competent medical supervision, mean sufficient quantities of all the essential elements needed for proper growth and development of the body and the proper formation and calcifying of the teeth and bones. Systematic schedules of rest, sleep, exercise, and fresh air are essential for the mother, and these in turn play a part in the development of the expected child. If we accept the report of our dentists that fewer than twenty-five per cent of our children have sound teeth, I feel sure that this number might be greatly increased, if our expectant mothers realized that the formation of their babies' permanent teeth begins as early as the fourth month of fetal life.
The problem of adequate nutrition becomes even more complex after birth. If the mother elects to nurse her baby and has an adequate supply of milk which the child can tolerate, the problem for the first few months is comparatively simple. She must not, however, forget the importance of the protective elements found in cod liver oil, orange and tomato juices. Felding, a German investigator, reports that of 86,000 infants in Germany in 1935 who died before reaching one year of age, one-half succumbed to dietary disturbances, caused in very many instances by lack of breast feeding.
The importance of diet as the child develops is aptly expressed by Dr. W. McKim Marriott: "As the pediatrician deals with the body during the growing period, he may be compared with the builder of edifices; the internist and surgeon with the repair men. Given a solidly constructed structure, it will last well, and the minimum of repair work will be required. We cannot alter the general plans of the architect, but we can see that the proper materials go into the construction. Two buildings of the same size, and outward appearance may differ greatly, depending upon the care taken in construction; the one may approach perfection in structure and wear well, the other, because of the use of poor materials will not withstand wear and tear. It is so with the child's body; the architectural plan of nature may be excellent, but with skimping on materials a poor structure may result."
Adequate nutrition implies far more than the attainment and maintenance of a certain body size and weight or a certain thickness of adipose tissue. A child may be of average stature for the age and have an average height-weight ratio, yet, the nutrition cannot be considered as satisfactory if the child is anemic, has weak, flabby muscles, poor posture, carious teeth, has but little resistance to infection, and is incapable of sustained physical or mental effort. The growing body must be supplied with necessary building materials to perfect its structure and to provide adequately for functional activity. If the total fuel or caloric value of the food is inadequate to provide for the energy exchange, the laying down of body tissues is of necessity interfered with, or else tissues already formed are destroyed to meet the energy demands.
What are the materials necessary for proper and adequate nutrition? They are oxygen, water, and the digestion products of proteins, fats, carbohydrates, mineral elements and vitamins. These may be derived from the following food stuffs:
(1) A good whole-cereal grain or mixture of whole-cereal grains or a good whole-meal bread. (2) Milk and the products of milk?butter, cheese, buttermilk, etc. (3) Eggs. (4) Green leaf vegetables. (5) Root vegetables such as potatoes, carrots, etc. (6) Legumes?such as peas, beans, etc. (7) Fruit. (8) Meat. (9) Water.
That these provide all elements and vitamins needed for normal nutrition is evident from the fact that they are the ingredients of the national diets of certain races of northern India whose physique and health, when they make use of their national diets in their entirety, are unsurpassed by any other races of mankind.
Detailed information on adequate diets as well as suggestions on how to make them appetizing to children can be secured from circulars and pamphlets furnished by our own State Board of Health as well as by the U. S. Public Health Service and Department of Agriculture.
The appetite serves, in general, as a good guide to the total amount of food needed, and providing a suitable dietary mixture is offered there is little danger of under or overfeeding when the amount of food taken is regulated by the appetite of a normal infant or child. Normal infants and children, given all they wish to eat of a well balanced diet, are not likely to become overnourished.
It is often stated that the instinct serves as a fairly reliable guide in the selection of foods, and examples are cited of primitive people with no knowledge of nutrition who instinctively choose well balanced diets and even of young children who, given free selection, choose diets which meet their nutritional requirements. While in general the instinct may lead the individual to select a complete diet this cannot always be relied upon. In most families, mothers must be constantly on the alert if every child in the family receives an adequate supply of all the necessary elements of a balanced diet.
When President Brigham Young said, "This is the place," he probably little realized that the land of Deseret could be made to produce every necessary nutritional element for his people. That being true, it is a reflection on our intelligence when we must admit that there are large groups of children in almost every community in the state suffering from some degree of malnutrition. It is an interesting fact that children in many of our rural and farming areas are actually malnourished from the lack of milk and vegetables. Then too, we must not overlook the economic factor in our present social set-up. It is tragic, but true, that in many of our poorer homes, the diet consists almost wholly of bread and potatoes. Under a proper functioning of our Church Security program such conditions ought not to exist.
The problem of proper nutrition of our children resolves itself largely into one of education. I know of no organization which, in cooperation with our state and local health agencies, is in a better position or better qualified to carry on such an educational program than the Relief Society of the Church! What greater objective could any relief organization have than the development of generations of normal, well developed, healthy children? While spiritual growth is not dependent upon proper physical development, I feel sincerely that the Creator intended that His children should be physically sound and well in order that they might be mentally and spiritually strong.

ADEQUATE PERSONALITY DEVELOPMENT?ITS RELATION TO CHILD WELFARE
By Ora W. Chipman
"For every child understanding and the guarding of his personality as his most precious right."?The Children's Charter.
One of the major objectives in the field of child welfare is that of attempting to secure for every child the development of a personality and of a character capable of meeting every stress and strain of life with confidence and eventual happiness?a personality which will enable him to realize his highest possibilities.
A good concept of personality was submitted at the White House Conference by Dr. Bronson Crothers, who defined it as, "the individual with all his emotional and intellectual peculiarities, trying to realize happiness and efficiency in the environment in which he lives."
Everyone, then, has personality; everyone?even he who seems to fail?is trying to create a successful life. The fortunate individual with the healthful mental attitudes of an adequate personality will succeed.
It is now the belief of many psychologists that adult behavior is the direct result of seeds of behavior planted in early childhood. The opinion of experts at the White House Conference was that factors that distort personality development exercise their greatest effects during the first six or seven years of life, and that those are the best years for development of desirable character traits and for modification of undesirable traits already established.
It has been estimated by authorities that there is a vast number of mentally ill people, not under treatment, who are suffering from serious but wholly avoidable distortions of personality probably caused by faulty training in childhood. There are, also, vast numbers of children who are seriously delinquent and of children and adolescents who are inmates of mental hospitals. One child out of every three is considered by child welfare representatives at the White House Conference to have personality difficulties which are serious enough to require the attention of a psychiatrist. These estimates help us to realize the significance of the development of healthful mental attitudes in childhood.
Conclusions from surveys to discover qualities generally considered as attributes of an attractive personality indicate that the most outstanding are: attractive physical appearance, intelligence, pleasing temperament, and sound character. Of these temperament and character are the most important. It is a common belief that temperament is inherited, but it now seems probable that this assumption is incorrect. "Temperamental reactions are learned habits formed in the early years. Much of the fundamental nature of temperament has been determined by the end of the first three years." The child's temperament consists of his attitudes toward the various phases of life. These attitudes are developed, not born. "Children who are gloomy or optimistic, suspicious or trustful, timid or courageous, loving or hateful, have been taught these modes of feeling, or they have stumbled into them, for these characteristics are not inherent in child nature." Regarding character much has been written and detail will be unnecessary here. The most admirable

A question has been presented to me which I have been requested to answer. The question is: "The Relief Society has been given the responsibility of making investigations, and finding the needs of the families. When this is done and the report given to the Bishop, and he ignores the report and says the family doesn't need the help suggested, what is the Relief Society President to do?"
To answer that properly I should like to present it to the particular Bishop concerned. But I will say that all should appreciate the importance, as they have been requested to do, of having the Relief Society officers who are trained in the essentials of social service, investigate for them all cases who desire relief, and recommend the necessary help. Also they should have the Relief Society administer the relief under their direction.
The Bishoprics have a great variety of responsibilities. Besides, they do not have the facilities at hand to investigate for themselves. To conserve their time, and to employ the means available through the Relief Society Social Service, this work will be more effectually done. Except in very urgent cases the Bishopric should not expend money for relief on any cases without having the investigation and the recommendation by the Relief Society officers. If that is done I am sure that in every case greater justice and fairness will be shown.
There are people who will try to take advantage of the Church. I had a telegram from the Branch President of one of the Eastern cities only yesterday, in which a man who had just arrived from the West, asked for relief upon his arrival there. How could he travel all the way to that Eastern city and then have
349
spent all his money and be in need of help? There may be conditions where people might be short of means temporarily. But for people to travel across the continent and ask for help immediately upon their arrival, looks rather suspicious. It is rather surprising to me. It happened that we had in our office a reliable report from California that this man had been for years going from ward to ward begging from various Bishops, depending on the Church, without doing anything in return. We suggested if he were able to get to Washington he should find a way to get back to his home, that we do not encourage those in need to travel from place to place. They should remain in their own communities, because there they have established residence, and they are entitled to help either from the County, Government or Church where necessary. But when they come as strangers, it is not fair that they should call upon the Church organizations for help; and they certainly cannot get it from the Government or County relief sources.
The Relief Society Social Service workers are trained in the essentials of social welfare. They are generally women of mature experience, possessed of the spirit of kindness, sympathy and good judgment. When they make a report to the Bishop as to whether or not any families need help, I feel, and I have told the Bishops, it should be their pleasure to accept the Relief Society recommendations. The Relief Society is generally better able to get at the real conditions and to distribute relief fairly and sympathetically. If the Bishop refuses to do that, I can only say the Bishop, under the revelation of the Lord, is the judge in Israel and he must be responsible. As a judge, he should accept the information about what is needed by those

We are so concerned to see that the Bishoprics avail themselves of the fine social service of the Relief Society in properly determining and caring for the needs of members in distress, that we shall call this important matter again to their prompt attention.
I need not say how important it is to try and bring about conditions which will enable the people who are on relief to obtain employment. It seems there are some conditions prevailing at present throughout the nation which are interfering with economic improvement, and the employment that should be available. There are many factors which appear to be favorable toward increased industrial activity throughout the nation. We must all realize that the way to increase employment must come through fostering private industry. Yet we must take conditions as they are and try to make the best of them by promoting employment and furnishing relief wherever necessary among the members of the Church.
I would like to say a few words about what I think the women of Relief Society can do. This proposition of developing handcraft is one that will offer possibilities. For example, in one of the hotels in this city, the draperies for the windows were woven and made in a little backwoods section of West Virginia by a group of women in that locality. There is much in the nature of handicraft being fostered by Relief Society that may develop into very worth while industries, and there are many more, as suggested by Director Peterson, that can be promoted throughout this State.
Another thing I would like to mention is the importance of civic and home improvements. By advancing cleanliness and neatness and by tree

STANDARDS OF LIVING
By Counselor Kate M. Barker
Today when there is so much revolt against old orders, many of the standards which we thought were fixed and stable are being lowered. Some youth are asking, "Can one succeed in a business way, and be honest?" Someone must carry these banners forward.
There are certain standards of living which are basic to which we must adhere. The future depends on the willingness of today's leadership to carry these banners, and to awaken and arouse youth. It is not resources we lack, either material or human. It is the will to be and to lead. Surely our Father in Heaven has the right to expect that the members of His Church will assume this leadership. "For unto whomsoever much is given, of him shall be much required."
In our country we have mass civilization, schools, libraries, science in service for all, and we are now talking of security for all. No amount of emphasis upon education, opportunity or security can obscure the basic necessity of the fundamental qualities of honesty, loyalty, industry, cooperation, absolute integrity of mind and heart. Culture is only a veneer unless there is this integrity of heart. This must be built in the homes.
But most people are spiritually lazy. It is easy to send a child to school to learn history, etc., but it takes time to watch the child in his play and all the acts of everyday life to see that he plays fair, that he is loyal to what he knows is right, that he is learning to cooperate, to get down to hard work and to do his work thoroughly. The same characteristics that are developed in little things will carry over to the bigger things of life.
We must build such a sensitiveness to truth and beauty that anything that is false or vulgar is repulsive. This is our only safety. We want our boys and girls to fulfill fine missions. These must be planned for. The Gospel is truth and beauty, and unless a person is fundamentally honest and clean he will not appreciate the real beauty of the Gospel. We cannot have moral integrity without mental honesty.
We must learn to think; we must not pick up an opinion here and a carelessly thrown scrap of information there, or fill our minds with ready-made statements and imagine we know how to think. The world needs straight and honest thinking.

There is no doubt that the world is progressing in social thinking?relief for the needy, make-work for the unemployed, old age assistance. But this type of civilization calls for a higher type of individual?higher in those fundamental qualities of honesty, industry and cooperation. These projects are for those who need. Any attempt on the part of parents and grandparents to evade the law by taking what is not absolutely needed, by not giving an honest day's work, by concealing their property or turning it over to children, is dishonesty. It cannot be concealed from the children and grandchildren.
Standards are not lowered suddenly. A certain mental attitude is developed in a few, and unless counteracted by the active influence of the majority, develops slowly into a general lowering of standards, a generalized mode of thinking. A definite, positive effort must be made to counteract this growing tendency of wanting something for
RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE
nothing, of doing only enough to get by. Think of the mothers, to say nothing of the youth of the next generation, depending on our success in maintaining a high standard of integrity.
SOCIAL ETHICS
General Secretary Julia A. F. Lund
The term social must be understood in its first meaning?companionship or mutual relationships with others of one's kind; group life in other words. Ethics is the science which deals with conduct in so far as it is considered right or wrong, good or bad. In group life there must be organization and effort. This subject, naturally, has a great appeal for Relief Society as it emphasizes the ideal of the organization, the animating spirit of our Church and the spirit of sisterhood, to be willing to serve each other and share burdens and privileges, and to develop the spirit of self-help so vital to success in life.
We stand today facing one of the great crises of the world. We need clear thinking, great courage, firm faith to guide us. Our heritage is one of traditions which have been developed through a study of the past.
We must teach them to realize that liberty is a great ethical ideal, and cannot exist without justice, and the highest aim of justice is the protection of liberty, which always must depend upon self-discipline and self-control.
As we regard the world today, deep gratitude fills our hearts for this land of "choice above all other lands," where we live under the finest pronouncement of social ethics ever to be given of a government, guaranteed under the Constitution of the United States. During the current year we have witnessed great epic in history, Lewis and Clark, and William Clark, that daring woman Sacajawea, have told the heart and soul of America's story.

Of all the civilizing agencies of the human family, the Bible is therefore the greatest treasure house of social ethics. Our art, literature and music, laws and government, our family and
354
community life, the mold of our civilization and all that it implies are shaped by this greatest contribution from the ancient to the modern world. From beginning to end it is full of priceless stories and examples of all our highest ideals. We know that the New Testament is the most ethical book ever given to the world. It is the source of the only perfect social order. Every miracle performed, every parable uttered is a perfect example of applied social ethics. True neighborliness and brotherhood were the cornerstones of Christ's teaching. This is one way of expressing the great value of every human personality which is so precious in the sight of our Father in Heaven. "If you love me, feed my sheep." Is this after all not the source of all good manners?the very flower of our civilization? What we need is more application of the principles in our daily life. Historic and spiritual examples are of no value unless we can use them in solving our problems. A very RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE beautiful and a very useful lesson is expressed in The Log Cabin Lady. It was an eloquent plea for appreciation of the real values in life, yet it called attention to many of our shortcomings and demonstrated how one may be just as manly and just as womanly and yet pay due respect to the refinements of life. This is not a reverent age and lack of reverence often leads to a Jack of courtesy. I believe we are all shocked at times by the lack of respect shown to elderly people, to women, and oh, so often to things which others hold sacred. These offenses against good manners just couldn't happen if we applied the rule of conduct given by the greatest Teacher who ever lived. If we exercised the same care in public buildings and places of worship that we do in our own highly prized homes, the vandalism so common, would be abolished. If we could only live the two laws upon which hang, "all the law and the Gospel,' we would insure the observance of a perfect system of social ethics as well as realize fullest spiritual development. ADDRESS By President Louise Y. Robison REVIEWING the activities of Relief Society during the past six months, one is amazed at the amount of work which has been accomplished by the members of the organization. In many avenues of service women are endeavoring to raise life to its highest level. Spiritual security is found only in action, which like the plane's onward motion is necessary to preserve buoyancy and balance. In understanding, in love, in usefulness, the opportunities for service are boundless. Spiritual and intellectual development are given to Relief Society members in the meetings held each week. Here lessons are studied in Theology, Literature, Social Service or Child Guidance, and household arts are taught. Rich experience comes to 1,918 class leaders who prepare lessons each week, and faith is strengthened through the opportunity of bearing testimony. At these meetings the Relief Society Singing Mothers are a source of real pleasure, bringing a spiritual as well as a cultural influence to the group. Visiting Teachers are also a RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE mighty force for good. Their labors never cease, summer or winter; whatever the weather the work goes on. It would be interesting if we could compute the miles and miles these sisters walk each year in performing their service of love. Aid to the Bishops in caring for the sick and needy, especially in rehabilitating families, has been one of the Society's outstanding activities. What tact, what love and patience are required to understand the heartaches and sorrows of those whose load is too heavy to be borne alone. In order to do all in our power to strengthen Church Security, an industry, under the name of Mormon Handicraft, has been established. Thousands of dollars have gone into homes from this enterprise to supplement family incomes. The Bread-making Project has been helpful. Hundreds of demonstrations have been given and reports of the interest revived in this important household art has been most gratifying. Improved methods of laundering have created great interest. The skill in remodeling used clothing has been developed to a stage of perfection that is truly amazing. Cooperating with the State Board of Health, Relief Society women have assisted in holding clinics, and much remedial work has been accomplished. The Magazine drive which closed in November was _ outstanding. While the Australian Mission carried off the laurels with 338% subscriptions in the Hurstville Branch, we remember with gratitude each Magazine Agent for the excellent service to Relief Society. Even if the goal of 100% was not always reached, the fine missionary work accomplished in wards and branches was of great worth. Relief Society work is not confined 355 to the United States alone. Lessons are given in many tongues, but the spirit of the work is the same. Our records show that 18,290 days were spent with: the sick and lonely since last Conference. These were not leisure hours. Home work was not neglected, but women testify in all seriousness that they are given added strength when in service to humanity. Has not our Father in Heaven always lifted our burdens to the extent that we have diligently sought Him? Although Relief Society has accomplished much, there is still the call for service. As long as there are ill, malnourished children, as long as there are poorly managed homes,' as long as there are lonely hearts and people who have lost faith in God, there is work for women of Relief Society. Would we have the power to do these things? The secret of power lies in willing obedience to the small requirements, the things we sometimes feel are too insignificant. The burden of the Savior's message, when He dwelt among men, was, "I do not mine own will but the will of my Father that sent me." Could any principle of the Gospel or any advice given by the Prophet of God for our good be too simple to win our attention? At heart we are all religious but following our own ideas or desires often deprives us of great blessings. If we could only realize that the things the Gospel stands for are the things of most value in life! Do human beings ever get so much on a small investment as on the little actually invested in willing obedience to the word of the Lord? This is the simple cost of spiritual power. More than anything else, our world needs spirituality. Multitudes have lost faith and vision and courage. These are inside values. They are qualities of the spirit. 356 How can Relief Society women help to restore this spirituality? How can they develop such faith as the Centurion exhibited when he asked the Lord, Jesus Christ, to heal his servant? He said, "Speak the word only and my servant shall be healed." We have two avenues given to us in which to use our influence, the home and the community. We have been given the injunction, "'to correct the morals and strengthen the virtues of community life". To develop faith in our homes we should have cleanliness, order, industry, and love. We should establish the practice of family prayer, keep the Sabbath day holy, and cheerfully perform acts of kindness for our families. The Relief Society Home Scriptural Reading Project offers subject matter for spiritual development. How many of our children know the names of the Church Authorities and the offices they hold? Are children taught to take care of their bodies and that health depends very largely on what one eats? In community life we can lead in reverence for houses of worship, respect for public property, health through cooperation with county nurses. Conduct depends largely upon health, and health upon the food one eats. In our Membership Campaign the best way to get new members is to show by our lives that Relief Society is a benefit to us. I count it an asset to be a member of Relief Society. One's strength is in the righteousness of the cause, the worthiness of its purpose, the nobility of the work. They nerve us for effort, sustain in defeat, inspire to further endeavor. The Gospel gives us the power of patience, endurance in the long wait and assurance of final victory.
