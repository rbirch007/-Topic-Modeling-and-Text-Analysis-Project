Widtsoe Home Economics Department?University of Utah HERE is no chapter in the field of nutrition so dramatic as the one dealing with the subject of vitamins. The science of nutrition met many difficulties in its development. For years the chemist could not make a complete analysis of foods because of imperfect methods and apparatus. Finally in 1906 a diet was worked out by the chemist which was thought to be complete in every respect. This diet was made up of pure protein, fat, carbohydrates and the essential minerals and was fed to experimental animals. They not only did not grow but soon sickened and died. The addition of a small amount of whole milk to this diet resulted in normal growth and development. What was there in whole milk that made such a dramatic change? Zealous research of many earnest workers over a period of years gave the answer to this question. This experimentation resulted in the knowledge that there are a number of important organic substances found naturally in foods that are essential to life and growth. These substances are now known to be the vitamins. In 1913 it was determined by a number of our nutrition research workers that there were two general characteristics of these unknown vital substances: One was that certain of these substances were soluble in fat and others were soluble in water; hence, they were called by Dr. McCollum "fat soluble A" and "water soluble B". Another research worker, Dr. Funk, called them vitamines A and B. Since more has been learned concerning these substances, the names have been changed to "vitamins". Methods of Investigation There have been two methods of approach to the study of the vitamins, the chemical and the biological. The latter is the older method and served to show the effects of the presence or the absence of these naturally occurring vitamins in our foods when fed to experimental animals. In 1913 McCollum, Davis, Osborne and Mendel, in experiments made independently and at about the same time, found that white albino rats fed on a diet containing all the known nutrients would grow and thrive when butter was in the diet, but when lard was substituted as the only source of fat they would cease growing and become sick and soon die. Many other fats were used in numerous experiments, and it was found that the fat in butter, egg yolk, fish-liver oils, in thin, green leafy vegetables and the yellow fruits and vegetables and the germ of cereals either contained these fat soluble vitamins or substances that are changed into vitamins in the animal body. Most of the vegetable and commercial oils resemble lard in their nutritive value. Chemical Investigation Chemical research has now established the identity of six vitamins. Others are being investigated. It has been determined that no single chemical description applies to the vitamins as a group. Each vitamin is a distinct chemical substance. In regard to their functions in nuttition, they may be grouped as a single class of nutrients. As a group they have a regulatory action on the utilization of foodstuffs and in the control of body functions. But each vitamin has specific values. Research has determined that most of our vitamin values are formed by Nature in our foodstuffs. We have always known that we eat to live, but we are fast learning that what we eat has an important bearing upon how long we shall live and the degree of health we shall enjoy. The Fat Soluble Vitamin Vitamin A is one of the most interesting and one of the first vitamins to be discovered. The experiment cited above called attention to the fact that something soluble in the fat of milk was necessary to the life of the experimental animals. With butter in the diet, the animals grew normally; with lard as the sole source of fat, they sickened and died. In due time all of the important food fats were worked with and their vitamin A value determined. The original source of vitamin A is built up by Nature in certain growing plants and is called carotene. When foods containing carotene are eaten they are changed into vitamin A. Certain foods of animal origin such as milk fat and egg fat contain both vitamin A and carotene, the RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE, APRIL 267 latter being changed into vitamin A when eaten. Carotene is of a distinctly yellow color, while vitamin A is colorless. All green vegetables contain carotene, but the yellow color is covered up with the green color of the chlorophyll. Hence any colored vegetable, cereal or fruit?red, yellow, or green?is a good source of vitamin A. Vitamin A Values in Foods Since carotene is the chief source of vitamin A, we look to the colored vegetables and fruits and to the milk fat and its products, whole milk or cream cheese, eggs and fish oils as our best food sources. The carotene content of a plant increases up to maturity. One must remember that the yellow color may be hidden by the green color of the chlorophyll. - When we eat these colored foods, our bodies convert the yellow carotene into the colorless vitamin A. Cows and chickens feed upon green grass, hay and other forage crops, thus making use of food sources of vitamin A that man cannot use. This vitamin value is passed on for human use in the form of milk and eggs. Measurement of Vitamin A Values Vitamin A values of foods are now measured in units in two different types of experiments: first, by feed-. Ing known quantities of carotene or foodstuffs containing vitamin A to experimental animals and determining the rate of growth; second, by feeding known quantities of vitamin A in its synthetic form and noting the rate of growth. The chemist now knows the chemical nature of vitamin A and can synthesize it in his laboratory. There is no period in life when this vitamin is not needed by the body. It plays a very important part not only in the growth and development of the body but also in the efficient regulation of the body processes at all ages. When the intake of vitamin A is insufficient in experimental animals, growth ceases, the hair becomes rough and shaggy, the characteristic eye trouble develops, respiratory diseases are apt to occur and a general weakened condition of the body results. Lack of Vitamin A Tends to Cause Eye Infection Susceptibility to eye infection is one of the most outstanding characteristics of vitamin A deficiency. The eyes become dry as the natural secretions fail. Such fluids naturally Spinach 21,000 keep the eyes clean by washing out bacteria and other foreign particles. The eyelids swell, become sticky and sore and the membrane becomes inflamed. If this disease progresses far enough, blindness results. This condition is found among children much more frequently than among adults. During the World War the poorer children in Holland suffered this eye trouble due to vitamin A poor dietaries. When whole milk was introduced into their diet, the trouble cleared up. Lack of Vitamin A Tends to Cause Respiratory Diseases Respiratory diseases are much more frequent on vitamin A deficient dietaries. The cellular structure of the respiratory (bronchial tubes and lungs) and alimentary (stomach and intestines) tracts become so weakened that infection readily attacks them. Again the loss of the natural secretions which tend to cleanse such tissue, makes it possible for germs to attack them and cause colds, sinus trouble, ear infections and bronchial trouble. While such deficiency diseases are much more readily observed in experimental animals, the same effects are possible in the human body but in a less spectacular manner. It is due to this fact that any dietary deficiency in human nutrition is so serious. The effects are so slow and so insidious and are usually not accompanied by pain, so that serious damage is done before one recognizes the evil. The difference in the life span of the experimental animal, usually the white albino rat, and the human being is great, one day in the life of the rat RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE, APRIL - 269 being equal to one month in the life of a human being. Chemical Nature of Vitamin A Vitamin A and Carotene are organic substances-soluble in fat. 'The chemist now knows the composition of these two substances and is equipped to test their comparative values in food. Such chemical tests will hasten the development of our knowledge of nutrition. Vitamin A is insoluble in water, so that we do not lose any of this nutrient in cooking processes. Vitamin A is not destroyed by heat up to the boiling temperature of water, nor by the pressure, cooker, unless the pressure is exceedingly high and the cooking is extended over a long period of time, a condition seldom necessary in the ordinary cooking processes. The canning of food does not destroy its vitamin A value. If vegetables are stored for a period of several months, they lose considerable of their vitamin A value due to the oxidation of the air. Rancidity in fats destroy the vitamin A value. How Much Vitamin A Does the Body Need? Intensive research is in progress in an attempt to determine the quantities of vitamin A, measured in units, required daily by the growing child and the adult. No definite standards are yet available, but Mrs. Rose and her co-workers have suggested that 100 units per 100 calories of food for the average adult and 200 units for 100 calories for the child would be a good guide. By using the table of vitamin A food values one can plan a diet that will supply these amounts. Any excess of vitamin A will do no possible harm as the body has the power of storing it. This-is a blessing as one can eat liberally of green growing vegetables in the season thereof, and the excess will be stored for future use. One cannot depend upon this storage exclusively, but must supplement it by feeding food sources of vitamin A daily. In planning menus refer to the vitamin A food value table and select the available foods rich in A. Use as many of the green foods uncooked as possible. The green outer leaves of lettuce are thirty times richer in A than the inner white leaves; green cabbage is better than white; spinach, parsley and all green leafy vegetables, carrots, yams, sweet-potatoes, yellow corn meal, yellow fruits and tomatoes are all excellent sources of vitamin A. Butter, whole milk, cream cheese, fishliver oils, eggs and the vital organs of animals are also rich sources of A. If thought is given to the building of the family dietary, there need be no deficiency of this important food nutrient.
