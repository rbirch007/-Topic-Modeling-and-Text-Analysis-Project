Our Viewpoint
A SURVEY of the activities of Relief Society for 1938-39 reveals a year characterized by unusual growth and achievement. Over 5,000 women have joined our ranks, adding strength to the Society; accomplishments in the field of welfare are a source of satisfaction, while the educational program has never been more ably carried forward. However, as the year draws to a close the inclination of most of us is not to focus attention upon achievements but rather to view in retrospect the difficulties encountered, the problems met, and the great amount of effort which performance of our duties has entailed. So easily wearied, we are prone to feel that it has all been so much work. We rationalize: "In former days before we assumed this responsibility we walked whither we would. Shall we continue to be burdened with the heavy load; obliged to continually think of others and not to please ourselves? Shall we go on?" Such thoughts are both natural and common in view of responsibilities. Perhaps no one ever put his hand to the plow of an enterprise without indulging for at least a brief moment in such thoughts. But natural as it is to want to relinquish responsibility it is unwise. Only as we accept it do we grow. The fundamental road to advancement is individual effort?work. Latter-day Saint women, through divine inspiration, have been given definite assignments; the fulfillment of these brings development. When we assume the attitude that too much is required of us we not only stand in the way of our individual advancement but we also retard the work of the Organization. The Relief Society program properly interpreted, with work intelligently delegated, does not unduly burden any woman. If we will erase from mind the idea that we are overworked, that we are making sacrifices out of proportion to what should be required of us, we will find maximum enjoyment, advancement and success in the work of Relief Society.
