Layton Member, Relief Society General Board HE objective of every Relief Society class leader is to convey to her class members ideas, knowledge, and basic information of the lesson in the time allotted to her. It is her earnest desire that learning will take place that the purpose of her hours of preparation will be accomplished. She, therefore, welcomes a teaching aid which will add to the effective presentation of her lessons. Learning is experience centered. The more vital the sensory experiences that are provided in the lesson presentation, the easier it will be for class members to learn. To see as well as hear helps us feel more completely the reality of the experience shared through lesson subject matter. The Master Teacher shows us the value of appeal to the eye as well as the ear:... Bring me a penny, that I may see it. And they brought it. And he saith unto them, Whose is this image and superscription? And they said unto him, Caesar's. And Jesus answering said unto them, Render to Caesar the things that are Caesar's and to God the things that are God's. And they marvelled at him (Mark 12:15-17). Well might they marvel. When was a message more simple or more forceful? When was a visual aid used more effectively? Values of Audio-Visual Aids There are many audio-visual aids which we may use today. Books, still pictures, motion pictures, film strips, maps, posters, graphs, charts, blackboard chalk illustrations, records, and many others are readily available. But before we can make effective use of them we must understand some of their teaching values. They save words and so save time. They stimulate interest. They focus attention. They supply concrete material. They give correct impressions. They aid in retention. They help recall experiences. They substitute for real experience. They provide emphasis. They enrich and clarify. Oe oe ee _ However, as you study their values, you also become aware of possible dangers in their use. For example, if a picture shown does not apply specifically to the lesson and must be explained, neither words nor time are saved. It is true that interest will have been stimulated; attention will have been focused; the class may remember the incident; but the lesson certainly will not have been clarified and a correct impression will not have been left by the use of the aid. Selection of Teaching Aids Our selection of teaching aids must be carefully made. Relief Society is a Church auxiliary, and every picture, chart, graph, map, or Page 27 28 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JANUARY 1953 book used, must teach truth in such a manner as to forward Relief Society and Church objectives. We must be sure that the aid we plan to use: 1. Will have direct relationship to lesson subject matter. Z. Will supplement and enrich lesson subject matter. 3. Will have definite and apparent place in lesson presentation. 4. Will forward lesson objective. 5- Will be suitable for Relief Society members. If the chart or picture has no direct relationship to the lesson, it will distract from the smooth, wellintegrated unfoldment of the lesson material and will serve no real teaching purpose. Relief Society members are women of broad and varied experiences. An abstraction or generalization attains a richness of meaning only to the extent that concrete experience is wide and varied; also the value of visual aids depends upon the nature and extent of the person's previous experience. It follows, then, that visual aids prepared for a child's level of experience would not be suited for use in teaching an adult level. Cautions in Choosing Teaching Aids: We must not use: 1. Too much material for one lesson. When you find it hard to discard an interesting picture and are tempted to use too much material, think how the effectiveness of the lesson of the coin would have been lessened by elaboration. 2. Material unsuited to the teaching facilities. You must suit the amount of material you plan to put on a blackboard to the size of the blackboard that is available.. Light projected pictures demand a room that can be darkened and a source of power for the light. 3. Material poor in quality. A picture that is poor in quality, and detracts from the ideal, does not add to lesson quality, such as a line drawing of a Book of Mormon character prepared for children. 4. Inaccurate or confusing material. Any map used must be accurate, with the placements on it correct. Latter-day Saints do not show angels as having wings. Charts that are not simple and definite may confuse rather than clarify. 5. Material not well integrated. If you have a real reason for using a record, picture, chart, or book, you will have no difficulty integrating it in your lesson plan. It will fit as does the right piece in a puzzle and will forward your objective by its use. Ten Ways Pictures Aid in Teaching While all audio-visual aids have a certain common value, each one has a certain definite service it can render. For example, pictures serve in ten ways. A well-chosen picture may capture attention and at the same time introduce a lesson, arouse interest, curiosity, and suspense, and thus stimulate thinking about the lesson. Pictures definitely increase understanding. "You will have to draw me a picture" has become a catch phrase to let a person know you do not understand his explanation. We can read about a place or hear it described, yet find that when shown a picture of it, the thousand THE VALUE AND USE OF AUDIO-VISUAL TEACHING AIDS 29 words have not made us understand as one picture does. Through arousing interest and aiding to a more complete understanding, pictures stimulate the desire of the members to tell the class about their experience, or ask questions, and so they promote class participation. Pictures create mood. They can arouse in us definite feelings of response. Literature leaders will be able to use this to advantage in the lessons on poetry. To be able to create the mood of the poet's word picture will aid in an appreciation of a poem, as a stage set does a play. Pictures aid memory. They help us re-live experiences. One picture can serve as a quick review of an entire lesson, and thus accelerate learning. We never become too old to appreciate a book containing pictures. They enrich reading, they stimulate the imagination, and help us go beyond the mere words of the text. Use of Pictures in Literature Lessons "Imagination is the soul of poetry," Coleridge says. The right picture may stimulate our imagination and add to our enjoyment of a poem. In the lesson on Shelley, "My soul is an enchanted boat, which like a sleeping swan doth float," or "deep autumnal tones" or "drive my dead thoughts like withered leaves," call for pictures which will help us imagine the mood and beauty of the lines. Pictures of the countryside the authors knew and loved, help us re-create the experiences they had. The rugged highlands to Scott typify the stark courage of Jeannie Deans and her rugged honesty which provide the theme for "The Heart of Midlothian." We follow her by map and picture on her journey from Edinburgh to London to see the queen. Fortunately for us, another woman took that same walk, and we have pictures and a map illustrating it in the National Geographic Magazine of August 1950. Teaching Helps for Theology At the present time there are no pictures illustrating the Book of Mormon which can be purchased, and so for help in these lessons we must look to other teaching aids. The blackboard is the common medium of visual instruction, and is readily available. Its use also encourages attention, adds interest, and brings active class participation. The class leader can list, describe, or sketch ideas as the occasion demands. This season both our theology and social science courses have lessons that are doctrinal in nature. Doctrine does not lend itself to visual presentation; however, there are many concrete and_ factual things in the lessons which may be illustrated on a blackboard to aid in understanding, to clarify, to add emphasis, and to help us remember. In lesson 13 of the theology course, "Separation into Nephites and Lamanites," we could list the differences between the Nephites and the Lamanites. We could write 30 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JANUARY 1953 down the answers given by class members in answer to the question, "What industries did Nephi encourage among his people?" 'The principles of progress applicable to our nation which are found in the development of the Nephite civilization, might be written on the board. 'Aids for Social Science The social science lesson 8, "Modern Trends in Government," has some factual material in it which could add interest and make the lesson more forceful, if listed. The abundant knowledge which has been given in specific fields, the agencies being debased today, the signs of warning we have been given already, will all provide lesson emphasis if placed before us by the class leader. Collecting Teaching Aids for Future Lessons As soon as your Relief Society Magazine comes, read over the lesson, and become aware of the possibilities contained in the lesson for audio-visual aids. It will be sur- ' prising to you how many ideas come to you for lesson enrichment. You may start to collect aids for future lessons, as well. Many magazines are a fruitful source for tear 'sheets (pages torn from a magazine or book). The magazines of the past year have had a wealth of good material on the early history of the United States Government, the Constitution, and reproductions of historical documents. These are always valuable, so look over your magazines carefully before you discard them, and start a file of good tear sheets. Use of Hectograph and Opaque Projector When simple line drawings are needed for a lesson presentation, it is possible to reproduce them | with a hectograph. A hectograph helps us prepare study sheets in quantity when we wish to give excerpts to all members of our class. Hectographs can be obtained from any good school supply house or you may make your own. To display pictures that are too small to be seen by all the class, or that you do not wish to remove from a book, an opaque projector can be used. 'These can be made quite inexpensively and are very simple to use. Directions for making your own may be found in the New Home Workshop Encyclopedia, Popular Science Publishing Company, page 439. The Sunday School in your ward may own both an opaque projector and a _ hectograph. Directions for making both were given in the Sunday School Visual Aids Clinic held in connection with the Sunday School general conference of 1952. You can trace the enlarged drawing or map from the projection or use the projection if facilities are available.
