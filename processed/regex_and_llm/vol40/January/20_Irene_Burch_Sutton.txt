She kept it polished a shiny black and the fire glowing cozily. On the front door of the stove above the grating it said Home Comfort, and on the oven door it said Marvel. In the summertime Anne burned dry chips in her stove, and in the wintertime she burned thick sticks of pine and mahogany wood brought down from Logan canyon by her husband John, with his team, old Duke and Baldy, hitched to the running gears of his wagon. John was a man a little over medium height, and he had wavy light brown hair and merry blue eyes. Sally Anne's hair and eyes were dark, and she was as slender and lithe as a willow. Now Sally Anne wanted a summer kitchen. She was bound and determined to have one. You perhaps know what a summer home is, even if you have to live in your home the year round. But you cannot know what a summer kitchen is unless you are about my age and remember when they were in style. All Sally Anne's neighbors had summer kitchens. Reeses' had one built out at the side of their house, connected to the back porch by a little runway. Jorgenson's summer kitchen didn't fasten on to their house at all, but stood clear away from it, and some planks were laid down from the back steps of the house to it, for Mrs. Jorgenson and the family to walk on (especially handy in rainy weather). Davis' summer kitchen was much on the same order, except no planks. And so I could go on and on from farmhouse to farmhouse all up and down the river in Jonesville, proving that Sally Anne's neighbors nearly all had summer kitchens. But all that doesn't say what a summer kitchen was. Well, it was a place to put the kitchen stove in the summer and not "heat up the whole house," as Sally Anne put it. One hot summer day while Sally Anne was cooking dinner, the kitchen (which was kitchen and dining room combined), kept getting hotter and hotter. The bread was baking in the oven, and kettles of vegetables were boiling on top of the stove. The meat for dinner was frying, and so was Sally Anne. The children were all out playing in the swing under the trees, and the "men folks" (John and the big boys) were up in the meadow hauling hay. Sally Anne was alone in the hot kitchen. She leaned over the woodbox to get another stick of wood to put in the stove, thinking to herself, I feel like putting the stove out instead of putting this wood in! But she put the wood in—she had to or the bread and the dinner would have been. Ruined. She wouldn't cook supper that evening. She simply fed the fam ily something cold. There were no warmed up potatoes with fresh gravy; no rice pudding or pie. There wasn't anything but the bread that had finally cooled, freshly churned Page 39 butter, green onions and _ lettuce from the garden, and milk to drink. She was on strike for a summer kitchen.
