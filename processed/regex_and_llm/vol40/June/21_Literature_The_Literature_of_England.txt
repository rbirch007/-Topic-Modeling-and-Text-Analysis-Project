Jacobs T' was during the "most glorious reign" of their beloved Queen Victoria that Englishmen achieved a wealth and power never known to them either before or since. This complex period of titanic change challenged all alert men to readjustment, to vigorous affirmation and impassioned denials, even to shiftings between doubt and faith. This year we are to have the privilege of beginning a study of this immensely varied, fruitful, and powerful Victorian literature. Just as Victoria was the first queen to be photographed, so a study of Victorian England first enables us to see mirrored in retrospect the problems of our modern world. In studying the Victorians we return often to that which we know, for in the Victorian period we find the confusing, challenging, but great preface to our own day. "Brittania rules the waves," the proud nation sang; she also ruled more foreign lands and peoples than any other power. The Bank of England was the greatest financial force in the world. England was riches; she was power. This period was one of staggering, dazzling change. Within the lifetime of Queen Victoria England experienced more sweeping changes and reforms within herself than during the thousand years preceding. Before the Great Reform Bill of 1832, true democratic representation in Parliament was hated by entrenched aristocracy as the most hortifying of all possibilities; not until mobs rioted in the streets did the Tories (or Conservatives) relent and vote for parliamentary reform. 'To contemporary workingmen, the achievements of the Factory Act of 1833 seemed incredible, almost too good to be true: children under nine could no longer be employed in mines or mills (save silk mills); children under thirteen could not be forced to work more than nine hours a day; and children from thirteen to eighteen years of age were limited to a twelve-hour day. In 1837, when Victoria ascended the throne, English reformers were still rejoicing over their success at abolishing slavery in the English colonies, yet at home women (rather than mules) still pulled coalcars deep within the narrow mine shafts, and the cheap labor of children so endeared them to industry that they could not be spared from the loom long enough to be educated. Well over four hundred crimes were punishable by death, and public hangings were still exciting spectacles. During this era, progress in science and mechanics was likewise astounding. Fundamental "firsts" laid the groundwork for modern geology, physiology, psychology, physics, and chemistry. In medicine germs of many deadly diseases were first isolated, vaccines first given, an- LESSON DEPARTMENT esthesia was first used in surgery and in childbirth (against the opposition of those fundamentalists who felt woman should not be allowed to escape from the curse of pain given her for sinning in Eden). The steam, later the internal combustion, engines revolutionized manufacturing and transportation, while the cheap steam press, the telegraph and the wireless vanquished _barriers of distance. The Colt revolver, the Winchester rifle, the French 75 artillery, and the Gatling machine gun transformed warfare as did the steel plow and the combine harvester farming. It was Sir Henry Bessemer's process of making cheap steel which made this age of steel possible. Men began worshipping — that which was newest, biggest, most. According to Carlyle, the modern hell became the fear of not succeeding. It was almost the age of inevitable progress; a progress born "'not of accident but of necessity. What we call evil and immorality will disappear. It is certain that man must become perfect.... The ultimate development of the ideal man is certain." 'Thus wrote Herbert Spencer, one of the great prophets of nineteenth-century optimism. But many Victorians protested such an enthusiastic, idealistic statement.
