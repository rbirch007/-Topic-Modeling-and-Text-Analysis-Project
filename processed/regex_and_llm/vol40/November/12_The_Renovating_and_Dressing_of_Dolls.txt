THE RENOVATING AND DRESSING OF DOLLS 751 THE AUTHOR WITH COMPLETED DOLLS Cleaning and Painting Gather together your dolls, not too many at one time, eight or ten are enough at one painting. Take them apart, saving rubber and wigs, if usable. Then wash thoroughly with damp cloth and scouring powder and wipe dry with a clean cloth. Examine for missing fingers, toes, noses, or any small cracks that need mending. 'These may be mended with plaster of Paris, mixed with enough water to work with; let dry, then apply a thin coating of glue to the mended parts. This keeps paint from soaking into the plastered area. Sandpaper the rough spots, making sure the doll is smooth and clean. Now adjust the eyes, those that open and close; or, if damaged, replace with others taken from heads that are no longer usable. The dolls are now ready to paint. Apply the flesh-colored enamel to the body evenly and hang the doll to dry on a clothesline with a string attached to the metal piece on the doll's body. Let dry thoroughly. In the meantime, paint the heads and set them to dry. Paint the faces carefully. Your own creative ability will determine fine shades of coloring. Paint on hair, if no wig is to be used. You may now reassemble your doll. If the doll is all bisque, use the crochet hook to attach rubber to metal bar in the head; pull through the leg slot in the body, and fasten a leg, clamping metal pieces together with pincers. Put into place the other leg, then attach the arms in the same manner. Make sure the rubber is tight. A little rouge may be blended on the knees, elbows, and the backs of the hands, if desired. You may paint fingernails and toenails with red paint. 752 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1953 Assembling Parts of Various Kinds of Dolls There are many different kinds of dolls. The bodies may be made of bisque, rubber, plastic, or cloth, those with hair and those without. So assemble them according to material and size. If a cloth body is used, after the paint is dry, add as much stuffing as needed to properly fill the body, and adjust the head. It is sometimes practical to glue a piece of cardboard or cloth to the bottom of the head, so the stuffing will not go from the body into the head. Tighten wire, which is strung through the neck, with pincers and twist it several times until the head is on tight. Snip off the excess wire with wire cutters, and tuck under. Be sure to mend any holes in the body, so the stuffing will not come out. In assembling, you may use the good parts of different dolls so long as they fit properly and match. Always save all of the good parts of old dolls, as you may be able to use them later. Before putting the wig on a doll, comb it well, as it is much easier to handle then. Glue the wig to the head, tying firmly with a cloth under the chin until dry, and curl with curling iron. Dressing, In dressing dolls study the type and facial expressions, then dress each one accordingly, with full skirts, puff sleeves, bits of lace, ribbon, old beads, flowers, or other odds and ends you may gather from friends or which your own sewing box may provide. Crisp materials are best—organdy, taffeta, nylon, net, or a pretty printed cotton. The tops of small children's wornout socks in dainty colors, or white, provide material for the doll's stockings. Cut off bottom of the foot part and sew the upper part to fit the doll's foot. Shoes may be purchased at dime stores for a nominal sum, usually ten to fifteen cents a pair. They give the doll a finished touch. If, however, you do not wish to go to this expense, a ribbon tied in a pretty bow over the socks and around the ankle is a pleasing substitute. Many individual touches may be added—a necklace of pearls or tiny beads, a flower, or a ribbon in the hair or at the waistline. A suggestion for dressing baby dolls is to cut out a square of outing flannel, a dainty print or plain, and crochet a simple stitch around it with yarn for a blanket, then crochet bootees to match and run a ribbon or crocheted string through them. This, of course, eliminates using shoes and socks. Bonnets do much to beautify the dolls. There are many ways they may be made, but fluffiness emphasizes daintiness.
