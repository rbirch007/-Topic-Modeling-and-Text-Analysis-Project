Whosoever will be great among you, let him be your minister; and whosoever will be chief among you, let him be your servant" (Matthew 20:26-27). NOW: in the month of February, when we remember with everincreasing gratitude, the birthdays of great men whose words of faith and wisdom and whose deeds of magnitude and compassion have gone far beyond our borders to influence the destiny of the world— at this time it is well to consider the qualities of greatness in men. It is well for us to understand why some men may justifiably be called great and noble. It has been said that history is but the lengthened shadow of great men. However, it may also be inferred that the deeds of some men who have greatly influenced the course of world events have cast darkened shadows across the earth, and have left behind them a path of destruction, of broken dreams, of abandoned faith, and_ shattered hopes. Not every man who has wielded power among his brothers may rightfully be called great, however vast the shadow that he casts upon the land. What, then, are some of the qualities of authentic greatness, the attributes which enhance the stature of men and lift them and their brothers to more lofty ideals and more lasting accomplishments? Though all men are born equal before the law and before God, all are not of equal physical strength or mental power; all are not placed Page 98 in equally favorable environments. To some extent, every life is beset by accidents and influenced somewhat by chance. Yet, in any time or place, the great man requires of himself the utmost effort to make the best of his circumstances, to widen the borders of his little world, to rise as tall as innate capabilities and the vista of his surroundings will permit. He does not regret unduly that he may not have been blessed with all the tools of accomplishment perhaps more lavishly bestowed upon another, but he takes the abilities and the insight and the ideals which he has and which he can develop, and makes his own house of life. And who shall say, except our Heavenly Father, in the day of final judgment, who has been the most faithful builder, he whose final accomplishment is greatest, or he who has risen from the most forbidding circumstance? It would seem, if we scan the pages of history, that men of achievement have walked their earthly paths guided by a great purpose to which they have dedicated themselves, and the more this ideal transcends personal ambition and enters into the sphere of the welfare of mankind, the greater has been the uplifting influence of the individual. It was the hope of Washington EDITORIAL to see his countrymen united under a Government to which the wise and liberty-loving of the earth might repair. It was the high resolve of Abraham Lincoln to preserve the Union of the states, and at the same time to give freedom to the slaves. And it is likely that neither Washington nor Lincoln, though they were different in their personalities and in their heritage— it is likely that neither desired the tremendous responsibility that leadership demands. Both men were cast by their innate greatness, their ideals, and their integrity, as well as by the demands of their times, into roles of supreme leadership. 99 Also, they molded the character of their age, placing the welfare of the group. Above their individual advancement. It is well, in considering the elements of greatness in men, to read again the words of the Bible which tell of the mother of Zebedee's children who came to Jesus, asking that her sons might be advanced in their positions with the Master. Jesus said unto her, "Ye know not what ye ask." 'Then he explained the order of the Father's kingdom— "... Whosoever will be chief among you, let him be your servant." —V. P. C.
