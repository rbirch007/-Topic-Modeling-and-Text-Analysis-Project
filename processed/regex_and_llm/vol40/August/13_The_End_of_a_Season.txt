The End of a Season Zera Wilde Earle ROM her kitchen window Helen watched the school bus roll to a stop at the end of the lane and her children alight. A farewell flutter of hands, then the bus moved on. Bitterness welled up within her. Those other children, she thought, all were going home to security, but her poor youngsters The three stood at the mailbox now, ten-year-old Margaret on tiptoe to see what her taller brothers might find there. As usual, Helen thought of the letter they had brought a few weeks ago. It was from Mr. Moore, the agent who had control of this farm now. Even yet she could see that crisp sheet with its curt notice. The farm had been sold, it said. The new owner desired possession in eatly March. Down at the gate now a daily joyous reunion was in progress. Helen watched the dog, Jake, romp from one child to another in frenzied bliss, then, barking ecstatically, tear round and round the group as they started up the long lane. Margaret, she could see, carried a paper bag. That would be the much-needed - anklets she had told her to buy during school lunch hour. How the child loved pretty things to wear! With a sigh, Helen took potatoes from the bin and began to peel them. George and the two little ones would soon be back, too. The whole family would be hungry. She should really fix something good for supper, she thought apathetically, but she had no heart for it. From across the bare March fields drifted the troubled cry of a killdeer. Was the bird, too, she wondered, being driven from his home? George had tried to warn her that this very thing might happen. "Farms are selling like hot cakes," he said. "If Moore gets a good offer for this place, he's not going to turn it down just to give us another chance." But Helen had not let herself think for one moment of that possibility. True, they had not been able to meet their payments, so, last fall, they had been obliged to relinquish legal claim. But they could remain as renters for a year, couldn't they? Then next fall they could begin to buy the place back. They had to stay, Helen reasoned. T'wo years ago they had come here to make this farm their home. Their savings had gone as a first payment on it. George had given up his work down home. Now to go back, beaten She could not bear to think of it. OUTSIDE, now, the dog's welcoming bark heralded the return of the rest of the family. From her window, Helen watched the two little ones scramble from the car while George stopped to gather parcels from the back seat. Dear George, she thought, how tired and troubled he looked. That tapping of remorse became a painful stab in her breast. He would be happy to go back to his old work, if only she were resigned to leaving. For she knew now that George was not a true farmer. He felt no warm satisfaction at sight of a moist furrow falling back from his plow blade, of potato sprouts thrusting their way through the soft loam, of stubble fields dreaming in September sunshine. And, as if in retaliation, his acres only yielded mediocre returns. But his skillful hands and keen eyes had helped him to excel in his 527 old work down home. Why, she wondered, had they both become restless there? "The farmers are surely making a mint of money," George had said, and Helen, remembering childhood vacations at Aunt Mary's farm, had been as eager as he to make the change. Well, she thought now, not every man can be a farmer; and the work to which George will be returning is important, too. One of Annie's small boys, with others who were talented in the arts, were found using berries, roots, and leaves for color, and any material they could find as their canvases or boards on which they recorded scenes of their surroundings. Some of the young ladies made waxed flowers, flowers from wool, cloth, silk, feathers, and sometimes natural flowers were used and treated for preservation. Beautiful bouquets were made, from natural grasses and grains, by Annie's daughters and their friends—some colored, others frosted with alum and _artistically arranged in a vase, then covered with a glass globe. This was a cherished adornment in any home —flowers or fruit under a_ glass globe on a wooden base. Among the needlecrafts carried on by Annie and other women, the most popular were weaving, spinning, knitting, crocheting, tatting, netting, embroidering, and _ crossstitch handwork. Any item of clothing that could be knitted or crocheted was made by the busy, never-tiring hands. Eyelet embroidery was another art of the industrious, competent pioneer women. An example of the type of sewing they did is the handsewn dress that Mary Walker made for her first child in 1866. Annie admired the dress, which was three feet long and had a border three inches deep around the six-foot lower edge of the solid eyelet embroidery. It involved months of patient work. To transfer the pattern from one space to another, Mary mixed flour and soot, then put it on the outside edge of her fist, pressed the fist against the finished embroidery, then stamped the pattern onto the new section. A creative pastime for the women was making hair flowers. Long strands of hair were easily obtained from friends and relatives, different Shades being used. Black hair was for blackberries, blond for lilies, and various shades were blended for petunias, forget-me-nots, daisies, and leaves. Fine stocking needles were used for the fine loops, and larger needles were used when larger loops were needed. A piece of very fine wire was bent in the center, making a double wire. After straw was braided and bleached, it was moistened and run between the rollers of the press to flatten it, then the braids were sewn into hats, belts, or bags. Wool cards were an indispensable item for Annie as a homemaker, for without them she could not comb the wool before it was used for quilt bats or spinning into yarn for fabrics or knitting. Annie soon taught her daughters how to use the cards. They were a pair of wooden blocks about four by eight inches, with handles. 'The surface of each block was covered with symmetrical rows of fine wires or tiny slender nails protruding onefourth inch through a layer of leather over the wood. The handle of one card was attached in the opposite direction to the handle of the other card, and the wool between the two was combed and combed until it was evenly distributed over the wires, then the heel of the top card was used to pick up and push the wool into a tiny roll or layer about two by nine inches, which was then lifted off the cards and placed aside in piles to be spun later, or placed directly upon the quilt top and added to until the bat covered the quilt top, a slow, tedious process. One fabric which was very serviceable and popular was called linseywoolsey.. It was made of two-thirds cotton and one-third wool. Friendly Indians gave valuable information to pioneer homemakers on how to use sand for dyeing and what they called their paint pots. Much experimenting was done, and various methods were used to obtain the desired shades. Native rabbit brush stems supplied yellow dye. Creosote bush gave a rich brown. Wild dock supplied rich brown, not only for material, but also for leather. A plant which grew like a toadstool in the hills and develops a round bulb, was used for making dye. When dry, powder could be 331 shaken out of it, which made a good brown or black dye. The pioneers carefully cultivated the indigo plant for blues, and the madder plant for red, and purple was made from the berries of the plant. Brown and yellow dye was made from the leaves and roots of this plant. Not only did the factories dye, but Annie's family did also, and when the dye pot was on, it was an occasion for people to get together and dye all the fabrics they had accumulated tor some time. For a long time, however, fabrics were so scarce that the canvas from the covered wagons was dyed and made into trousers. Sometimes Annie's boys looked at each other and laughed until tears ran down their cheeks over their appearance. Each child in his turn lay on the canvas while Annie cut the trousers out around him.
