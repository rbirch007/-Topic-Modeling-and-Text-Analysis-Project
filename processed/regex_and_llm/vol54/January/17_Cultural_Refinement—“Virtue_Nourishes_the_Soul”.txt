CULTURAL REFINEMENT Ideals of Womanhood in Relation to Home and the Family Dr. Bruce B. Clark Lesson 6—'"Virtue Nourishes the Soul'' 'Virtue is the health of the soul.' Joseph Joubert Northern Hemisphere: Fourth Meeting, April 1967 Southern Hemisphere: August 1967 Objective: To show the beauty and truth of the statement The lesson for this month covers seven short selections plus one somewhat longer story, all of which are printed, with full comments and questions for discussion, in Section Six of Volume 2 of Out of the Best Books. Class leaders and Relief Society sisters having access to the text should study the selections there because space permits only very abbrevjated treatment in this Magazine lesson. Also, class leaders should not try to teach all eight selections because there are too many to cover in one lesson. Instead, each leader should choose those selections she feels will be most valuable for her group. Probably most class leaders will want to use the Tolstoy story as the central selection and add two or three of the shorter pieces for enrichment material. (Note to class leaders: The only selection in this lesson now under copyright prohibiting your making copies of it is the little poem "Fire and Ice" by Robert Frost. All other selections may be re-copied if you desire.) GENERAL COMMENT "Man flows at once to God "Virtue is the health of the soul.'' when the channel of purity is open." Later in this lesson we will read these words in an essay by Thoreau. Wise thinker that he was, Thoreau recognized, as other wise and inspired men have done, that the cultivation of purity moves us toward God and the allowance of impurity moves us away from God. We have the word of the Savior that this is so, for he said: "Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God"' (Matthew 5:8). Note that the emphasis is on purity 'in heart." "Let virtue garnish thy thoughts unceasingly"' was the similar admonition of Joseph Smith as he prayed and suffered in Liberty Jail (Doctrine and Covenants 121:45). The beginning of virtue is self-control of one's actions, to keep them pure. More difficult is control of one's words, to keep them clean and in good taste. Most difficult of all is control of one's thoughts, to keep them wholesome and_ uplifting. All three controls are necessary for the fully virtuous life. These are beautiful words — virtue, modesty, chastity, purity. Men and women should be chaste and modest at all times—never 73 January 1967 vulgar in action, word, dress or thought. Note that the sentence begins '"'"men and women." There is no double standard among Latter-day Saints. The same principles of virtue and clean living apply equally to men as to women. Moreover, as members of the Church striving toward eternal goals, we should avoid not only evil itself but also the appearance of evil and situations in which we are tempted to do evil. We should in all ways and at all times conduct ourselves with dignity, modesty, and control, remembering that temporary pleasures are always wrong if they endanger permanent joy and peace of mind. The only way to be comfortable with oneself is to be comfortable with one's conscience. This is not to suggest a rusty conscience, but a sensitive, clear conscience. There is no sin so small but that avoiding it will make us better, and almost no sin so great but that one can be redeemed from it through genuine repentance. With regard to chastity and unchastity, however, we should remember that the Lord regards sexual relations outside the marriage covenant as a sin second only to murder in seriousness. One cannot restore life when it is taken, nor virtue when it is taken; that is why these are the two most serious sins in human relations. All of these things are, of course, not new. They are as. Old as the gospel, and as true. Indeed, they are a vital part of the gospel, and as Church members we have heard them over and over. The scriptures and the sermons of our living prophets are filled with beautiful, powerful ad- 74 monitions to let virtue govern our lives and to avoid evil. Our purpose, however, in this lesson is to approach these ideals not through scripture and sermon but through art, letting the art-creators of the world add their insight to the joy of pure living and the anguish of impure living. Before moving to the literary selections, we have just two more general items to mention: The first is a reminder that virtue should be genuine and not just surface or narrowed to selfrighteousness. Because the problem of self-righteousness was treated extensively in Volume 1 of Out of the Best Books, we shall not explore it again here. But we do need to be reminded of the danger. The second item is a brief sampling from President David O. McKay's many writings on virtue, chastity, morality, purity, and motherhood. No one in modern times has commented on these things more than our re- -vered contemporary prophet: Love is the highest attribute of the human soul, and fidelity is love's 'noblest offspring. A woman should be queen of her own body. Chastity is the crown of beautiful womanhood, and self-control is the source of true manhood,. Not indulgence. Sexual indulgence whets the passion and creates morbid desire.... Gentleness and consideration after the ceremony are just as appropriate and necessary and beautiful as gentleness and consideration before the wedding. Chastity is the virtue that contributes to the peace and harmony of the home. More homes are ruined and more hearts broken because of unchastity than by the violation of any other virtue. PROVERBS, CHAPTER 31 The last half (verses 10-31) of Chapter 31 of Proverbs in the 'Old Testament serves as an excellent brief introduction to this lesson on virtue. It identifies the attributes of womanly purity and, like the other Psalms and Proverbs, reflects many poetic qualities in its wording. That is, it is lovely both in its substance and its language. Because the Bible is available to all readers, we will not print any of the verses here but simply suggest that Relief Society sisters turn to the Bible itself. Class Discussion How many specific qualities of a virtuous woman can you identify in this passage? What are these qualities? Search your own soul to see how many you possess. CHAPTER XI, "HIGHER LAWS," OF WALDEN Henry David Thoreau (1817- 1862), with Emerson, comprises the heart of the mid-nineteenthcentury American romanticism, known as_ transcendentalism. Walden (1854) is his masterpiece, and one of the great books to come out of America. At other times during 1967-68 we will explore Walden more fully. Here we present just one small excerpt, a part of Chapter XI on "Higher Laws." Several major points are stressed in this passage: (1) The entire universe is moral, and man must be moral, too, or be in conflict with the eternal laws of the universe. (2) Every person has within him animal desires and divine aspirations.
