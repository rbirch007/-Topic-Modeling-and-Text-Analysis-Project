MEDALLIONS OF ARTISTRY MARK HER YEARS " Eva Christena Otteson Evans, Hayward, California, is ninety-nine years old, and as she says, her handicraft has been a pleasure and an ornament over the years. She is now knitting a skirt, and she has been making quilts in many patterns since she was sixteen years old. Knitting was a familiar craft of her childhood days and very early she learned to make her own designs and to fit her stitches into clothing for use in the cold winters of her native town—Spanish Fork, Utah. She spun the wool from her father's sheep and dyed it in beautiful deep colors. She has made forty-two hooked rugs, fifteen sweaters, ten woolen stoles, six afghans, and numerous table covers, chair sets, pillow tops, and chair throws. Her handwork brightens the homes of her relatives and friends and is lovingly treasured as gifts of artistry. She is mother of eight children, grandmother to thirty-two, great-grandmother to 129, and great-great-grandmother to forty-six. The skills learned in the homemaking program have been applied to the cultures of the respective localities in making a total of 1,361,894 articles of clothing, quilts, household furnishings, and non-sewed handicraft items. Through the social relations lessons which were correlated with those studied by the Melchizedek Priesthood, Latter-day Saint women have been able to see more clearly their responsibility to human relationships that originate in the home and extend into areas of Church and community living. Increasing numbers of sisters have responded enthusiastically to their study of the new cultural refinement course dealing with literature and fine arts which reflect and motivate the development of desirable attributes and characteristics to be emulated in the life of every Latter-day Saint woman. The greater percentage of sisters attending regular Relief Society meetings is indicative of their appreciation and acceptance of the enrichment available to them through the varied courses of study which are carefully coordinated and guided under the direction of the Church Correlation Committee. Bounteous rewards of enlarged abilities, talents, and testimonies have also come into the lives of 196,259 Relief Society members who have served as leaders in carrying on the program of the organization, in 837 stake, district, and mission organizations and 5,972 ward and branch units throughout the Church. 1966 Marked progress has been made in the quantity and particularly the quality of visiting done by 130,301 visiting teachers, who, as emissaries of Relief Society assigned in pairs, have made 4,704,379— nearly five million—visits to families carrying with them a message of spiritual uplift and with a charge to observe any instance of need in the homes where they visited. Services given by Relief Society to the distressed and the aged have been far-reaching. A total of 753,371 hours of sympathetic, loveinspired service was given at the direction of Relief Society presidents, in addition to 395,181 visits to the sick and homebound, and also other services such as bedside nursing care and assistance to families at the time of death. At the direction of the Priesthood, 56,212 devoted women contributed 765,086 hours of service on Church Welfare projects, such as assisting at the canneries in processing vegetables, fruits, and meat products, and also in making clothing and household articles for the needy, As an aid to the bishops, Relief Society presidents made 99,512 visits and 86,812 other contacts to families needing assistance. Music continues as a vital aspect of the program of Relief Society. In addition to the development gained by its members through congregational singing in the regular meetings, 50,561 sisters have participated in 3,601 Singing Mothers choruses whose refining influence has, in turn, been carried over into their homes. This marks a historic step forward in making available to this second largest language group in the Church the Magazine in their own tongue.
