Sixty Years Ago
IN the State of Utah there is a long, narrow valley that winds tortuously for nearly two hundred miles between mountain ranges. In 1865, there were no fertile farms, no luxuriant vegetation, nothing green except a thin ribbon of cottonwoods and willows along the winding course of the river. Beyond this, on either side, a wide strip of gray-green indicated the greasewood area, which ended at the foot of the silver-blue, sage-covered benches. Perhaps at that time the valley possessed a measure of beauty, but such as it was, it was cold, fierce, and inhospitable, even forbidding. Like the Utes who roamed over its bottom lands and benches and camped by the river and the mountain streams, it had rugged elements of beauty, but, at heart, it was quite cruel.
Late in August of that year, as the sun sank behind the Pavant Range and the shadows lengthened and chased the sunlight out of the valley onto the bench and up the mountainside, there to linger and play in golden glory for a few splendid moments, herders were driving home a motley drove of cattle, the chief wealth of the pioneer settlement. These were to be kept for the night in the corral. Three sides of this enclosure were made of cedar posts, deeply and securely embedded in the earth; the fourth side was formed by the high rock wall of the fort. To the north, and adjoining the corral, lay the small field which had been cleared and broken. It was laid off
in squares, as it is today, but the whole field was golden with ripened shocks of cut and bound grain.
Many things had happened since the time, three years before, when the first camp had been made. The first year's scanty crops had been planted too late to mature. The second year, the crops had been subjected to two scourges, grasshoppers and drought. Long before planting time of the third season, the Ute Indians, incited by Chief Walker and Chief White Horse, had gone on the war path. No conciliatory efforts of the settlers had induced the savages to consider peace. Therefore, much of the time and energy of the men and boys had been required to haul the rock from the bench and build the four thick, high, walls of the fort. That task was now over. It was a homely, but formidable structure of gray cobblestones. Lean-to shanties built against the inner walls afforded a crowded shelter and a minimum of comfort to the inhabitants. Equidistant from the corners, at each end, was a sixteen-foot gate built of hewn cedars pinned together with strong maple pegs.
As yet, the Indians had made no direct attack on the fort. The chief evidences of warfare were the frequent raids on the cattle herders when they ventured too far from the fort, or the theft of such cattle as wandered into the canyons in search of cooling shade or greener feed.
