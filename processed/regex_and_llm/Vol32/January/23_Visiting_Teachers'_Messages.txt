Hinckley For Tuesday, April 3, 1945 "Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God" (Matt. 5:9). Objective: To show that enduring peace between men and nations, in the home and abroad, will come only through the adoption of the precepts of the Master. TO be able to compose differences, reconcile those at variance, promote harmony and happiness is a rare and precious gift, and this is within the reach of the humblest. We may not be able to write a book, accumulate a fortune, distinguish ourselves in the field of statesmanship or politics, we may not startle the world with inventions, but we can all contribute to the peace and happiness of those about us, and, in so doing, we become real peacemakers. Such indeed are the children of God. Whosoever cherishes hate in his heart, whoever makes it easier for men and women to suspect each other, to mistrust each other, that individual, regardless of his profession, is fighting against peace. Whoever is hard to live with in the home, whoever is a promoter of strife between man and man, whoever creates discord, discontent, and misunderstanding, whoever preaches selfishness, whoever fosters prejudice is a disturber of peace. On the other hand, if we are peacemakers, if by what we say, by what we are, by what we do, we preach the "Gospel of Reconciliation"; if we promote peace in the home, peace in social circles, peace between nation and nation, then we are making common cause with the Prince of Peace. His gospel is the gospel of peace. Enduring peace between men and nations, in the home and abroad, will come only through the adoption of the precepts of the Master, and through magnifying these precepts by actually applying them in our daily lives. "Peace on earth" is not to be established by congress and parliament, nor by any group of international representatives, peace cannot be legislated into existence for it comes from within. Driggs For Tuesday, April 17, 1945 IT is somewhat difficult for folk today with widespread mail service, the press, and the radio at their command, to visualize how severely limited were the means of communication in the long ago. In these days, gospel messages may be broadcast with electric speed over all the world. With the Savior and his apostles, gospel teaching was largely restricted to places within walking distance, and to conversing with the few who cared to listen. It was a kind of home-to-home missionary service, with occasional opportunities to give the divine message to larger groups, attracted by the marvelous works and words of the Master.
Later, when Paul was carrying forward in the realms of Greece and Rome, he wrote epistles to help him spread the truth. These gospel letters were far more, however, than ordinary communications. In letters, they carried greetings and cheer and courage to the different branches of the Church and, at times, to individuals. They sometimes dealt also with specific problems that troubled the saints. But more than this, they were masterful interpretations of the gospel of Jesus Christ. As such they hold a high place in the world's great literature.
The epistles found in the New Testament, written not only by Paul, but by other evangelists, have classic qualities. Though addressed for the most part to specific congregations, they ring with truth for people of all time. Outside the gospels, no other religious works have been more often quoted in the Christian world; nor have any had a more profound influence in guiding action along ways of righteousness. Most of the epistles preserved for us in the New Testament were produced by Paul. How many other gospel letters this ardent missionary wrote, we do not know. Through the busy, troubled years of his ministry he was constantly speaking?portraying the gospel with fervor and clarity before varied types and classes of people from common folk to kings. A hint of his eloquence comes to us in the complimentary remark of King Agrippa: After Paul, as a prisoner, had pleaded the cause of Christ before him, the King said, "Almost thou persuadest me to be a Christian. And Paul replies, "I would to God, that not only thou, but also all that hear me this day, were both almost, and altogether such as I am, except these bonds" (Acts 26:28-29). His persuasive eloquence vibrates in the epistles preserved for us. These gospel letters reveal not only a clear mind, but a fervent soul. They are the blossoming and the fruitage of years of devotion to the cause of truth, which, with direct commission from the Savior, he had made an intrinsic part of his own being. It was dearer to him than life itself. Oliver Wendell Holmes once said, in effect, that sermons should not be written until they had been spoken many times?this to round and polish them as pebbles are smoothed and perfected by rolling with the waves on the beach. Paul doubtless often voiced the profound truths, and the reasons for his belief in Christ, before he wrote his epistles. When he did address" one of these gospel letters to saints he had helped to bring into the fold, he was ready for such written expression. He talked to them fervently with his pen; and in doing so, he often expressed great truths with literary finish and force. Following are a few of his many lines that bear out this statement: For I am not ashamed of the gospel of Christ: for it is the power of God unto salvation (Romans 1:16), For the wages of sin is death; but the gift of God is eternal life, through Jesus Christ our Lord (Romans 6:23). The Spirit itself beareth witness with our spirit, that we are the children of God (Romans 8:16). Let love be without dissimulation. Abhor that which is evil; cleave to that which is good (Romans 12:9). And my speech and my preaching was not with enticing words of man's wisdom, but in demonstration of the Spirit and of power (I Corinthians 2:4). Let all things be done decently and in order (I-Corinthians 14:40). If in this life only we have hope in Christ, we are of all men most miserable (I Corinthians 15:19). O death, where is thy sting? O grave, where is thy victory? (I Corinthians 15:55). But I fear, lest by any means, as the serpent beguiled Eve through his subtilty, so your minds should be corrupted from the simplicity that is in Christ "(11 Corinthians 11:3). Be not deceived: God is not mocked: for whatsoever a man soweth, that shall he also reap (Galatians 6:7). 'Taul," says Dinsmore, "had a rare power of condensing much wisdom in a sentence. That is why preachers find him so rich in text.
