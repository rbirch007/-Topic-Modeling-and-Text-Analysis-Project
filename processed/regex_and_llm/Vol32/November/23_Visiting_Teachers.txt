Bennion For Tuesday, February 5, 1946 Objective: To show how charity is tactful and courteous because it is inspired by a reverence for life. Jesus went unto the mount of Olives... And all the people came unto him... And the scribes and Pharisees brought unto him a woman taken in adultery;... They say unto him, Master, this woman was taken in adultery, in the very act. Now Moses in the law commanded us, that such should be stoned: but what sayest thou? This they said, tempting him, that, they might have to accuse him. But Jesus stooped down, and with his finger wrote on the ground, as though he heard them not. So when they continued asking him, he lifted up himself, and said unto them, He that is without sin among you, let him first cast a stone at her. And again he stooped down, and wrote on the ground. And they which heard it, being convicted by their own conscience, went out one by one... And Jesus was left alone, and the woman standing in the midst... He said unto her, Woman, where are those thine accusers? Hath no man condemned thee? She said. No man. Lord. And Jesus said unto her. Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more (John 8:1-11). This incident offers an interesting study in contrasting attitudes. The scribes and Pharisees were thinking of the law, of punishment, of tempting Jesus, and had no feeling for the inner life of the woman. Jesus believed in the law of chastity and even enriched its meaning (note Matt. 5:27-28), but he also had reverence for the soul of the woman. He replaced her shame with hope and faith. He handled the situation with tact and graciousness. Even after the multitude left he asked her no personal questions, but simply, "Where are those thine accusers? Hath no man condemned thee?" Think of the hope, love, and gratitude she must have felt as she left with his gracious words to cherish in her memory: "Neither do I condemn thee: go, and sin no more." (Notice, however, that he did not say, "Thy sin be forgiven thee.") Charity has a deep reverence for life and expresses itself with tact and courtesy so that it will not embarrass nor offend nor give pain to an already wounded spirit. A charitable person gives in such a way that the recipient is hardly aware of the distinction between giver and receiver. There is a gentle, quiet, spontaneous, courteous way of giving which is wholly uplifting in its effect. The secret of developing this type of charity is to keep in mind, not the giver nor the gift, but the need and joy of the person who is to be the recipient of one's love and good will. Driggs For Tuesday, February 19, 1946 ENGLISH drama had its beginnings in religion. After plays and pageantry had been banned for centuries because of the vileness to which they had sunk in fallen Rome, the priests began to introduce dramatic elements into church ritual. A simple portrayal of the story of the resurrection or of the angel and the shepherds and the coming of the wise men, might be given as part of the commemorative ceremonies of Easter and of Christmas. Other significant stories from the Bible also might be portrayed?all to bring the scriptures closer to the common folk. What began thus simply with the priests, gradually developed into more elaborate dramatic presentations. A calendar of holy days set by the church opened the way for remembering various events in the life of Jesus at different times of the year. Laymen, as well as priests, began to take part in these religious plays and many of them were performed outside the church. They offered recreation to help lighten the so-called Dark Ages. With the nearing of the Renaissance came other developments. Miracle plays, depicting legends connected with the saints, began to appear; with them came the moralities, or moral plays, in which the various virtues and vices were allegorically presented. In time, these dramas, intended to impress truth and righteous living, were given in the common language of the folk, and performed largely apart from the church. Then groups of "strollers," or wandering actors, would take the plays from village to village or castle to castle. Such a troupe of players, as many will remember, is employed by Hamlet when he would prove the king guilty of the crime that Hamlet feels he has committed. Naturally, with the gradual separation of the drama from church ceremonies, there came other than just religious themes into it. Expansion and development of the histrionic art, following the Crusades and the discovery of America, brought a "Golden Age" of play writing and play acting, especially to England. During the long reign of Elizabeth, the theatre flourished, and plays unsurpassed in dramatic and literary art were created. Those were days, remember, when the drama to succeed had to stand on its own merits. There were no such accessories as electric lights to flash rainbow effects round the players; no scenic settings nor music to accord with the varying emotions stirred by the play. Theatres, on the contrary, were rather crude affairs with a platform on which the actors played their parts. Below them, standing "in the pit," were the common folk; above, in circular rows of stalls, were the lords and ladies or other people of means who could afford seats. It took drama of gripping appeal to hold such an audience.
One playwright and producer who took the challenge in such a situation and succeeded marvelously was William Shakespeare. Not a great deal is surely known of his earlier life. He certainly must have grown up with the drama?probably taking active part in his younger days with strolling players, and helping to shape the plays themselves as he went from place to place. Some basic training such as this, added to his superb natural talent, gave him the wit and wisdom and the art that have made him the greatest dramatist of all time. That he has held the highest place is attested by the fact that for decades hardly a year has gone by without seeing his name on Broadway; and the further fact that thousands upon thousands of students are taught his plays year after year as the finest examples of drama ever created. They live for all time.
Something even deeper than mere genius must explain this phenomenon in literature. Quests to learn the inner secret of his art?and thousands upon thousands through the years have made the search?are about as conclusive as the efforts to learn how the firefly gives its light, or why the skylark rises to heaven when it sings. This, how-
ever, we do know of Shakespeare: he did create plays, a good many of them, that have brought stirring and uplifting entertainment to humanity through centuries. He has left mankind both thrilled and thinking.
May the writer be pardoned here the telling of a personal experience. His first real contact with Shakespeare came at a Saturday afternoon performance in the old Salt Lake Theater. Julius Caesar was the play. This youth sat entranced for two hours?and came out different. He bought a copy of the play, and for weeks thereafter was studying and reciting its lines. It was the beginning of a lasting love for great drama, and the setting of a helpful standard of appreciation.
Not long after that experience he saw another play in the same old theater, Gismonda, it was called. There were thrills aplenty in the colorful drama, with one of America's leading actresses of the day in the stellar role. It gave stirring entertainment, but not re-creation. There was no running out to buy the play, no reciting of lines ringing of truth such as these:
... lowliness is young ambition's ladder.

Cowards die many times before their deaths;
The valiant never taste of death but once.

Shakespeare had the skill to portray life?outward and inward life; to create scenes that move and stir the heart and leave the mind informed. In Julius Caesar we have Brutus, the man of honor, the idealist, made a prey by scheming politicians. In this, as in all the best of Shakespeare's plays, w6 have situations, scenes, characters that are true to life for all ages. More than this, with unforgettable lines, this master voices truth for all time. There is no preaching of religion, yet in his plays sin does not triumph, but good is the victor even though the story ends in tragedy. Bible influence may always be felt and often seen in his words and lines and allusions to Holy Writ. They reveal sin in its ugliness, but only as a darker background against which virtue and nobility may be more impressively portrayed. His purpose, like that of all high-minded artists, is to inspire a love of the good, the beautiful, and the true. In all this he is in perfect accord with the Bible whether that source of inspiration is revealed openly or not. Successful dramatists since those days of Elizabeth, have followed the basic pattern set by the Bard of Avon. Directly and indirectly their best plays reveal Bible influence. Generally it is to be felt only in the spirit of the play as it vibrates in the scenes and in characters of noble, of lovable qualities. Outwardly it is shown in allusions and quoted lines from scripture. Occasionally, the theme may be taken from the Bible, or, as in the Passion Plays of Oberammergau, it may faithfully portray characters and scenes of Holy Writ. Within recent years the Bible story has been presented rather elaborately in pageantry and moving pictures. One such performance was "The Eternal Road," which was short-lived. Another, ?The Ten Commandments," portrayed the story of the escape of Israel from Egypt, the giving of the Commandments to Moses, and then applied all this in a modern drama. Will Rogers, after seeing this play, remarked, "It is easy to see where God left off in the play, and the movie-picture man began." The spectacle again was short-lived.
