GENERAL RELIEF SOCIETY CONFERENCE 753 It is our earnest desire that it shall be built by the many rather than the few. That every person may be given opportunity to contribute to its erection and enjoy the sense of satisfaction and pride that comes from so doing, we believe that every one of our more than 100,000 members will want to make a contribution and thus become in reality a part owner in this Relief Society home and Latter-day Saint women's memorial edifice. We believe that throughout the Church are many men, as well, who will welcome the opportunity to honor a wife or mother by contributing funds, and that many other friends of Relief Society will wish to show their regard for the Society by assisting financially. Through the united support of all, we believe that the necessary finances will be readily and willingly contributed. We realize, however, that to erect the type of building we have in mind is a mammoth undertaking. We know that if we are to realize our ambition the full support of every enrolled member of Relief Society will be necessary. We would like at this time to know your attitude toward the proposed building program. Therefore, just as our sisters of long ago called for a vote on this identical proposition, so today, forty-nine years later, we too are calling for a vote of this great congregation composed of Relief Society women of our day representing most of the stakes and many of the missions of the Church. Your General Board proposes the erection at the earliest possible date of a Relief Society building that shall be monumental and beautiful, to adequately serve the members of the Society not only for today but in the years to come. Will all those in favor of this proposition please show it by the uplifted hand. (The vote was unanimous in the affirmative.) This is a glorious experience, not only to see your uplifted hands but to feel your support. As soon as plans for the building and its financing are perfected and approved they will be released to you. We feel assured of your support. We appreciate your support in all of the work of Relief Society. We are grateful for your righteousness and your spirit of service. We are grateful for the strength of your testimonies. We are grateful for the women of the past, for their vision and understanding of the needs of Latter-day Saint women, and for their great contribution to us and to the future. We are above all things grateful for the gospel. May the Lord bless you and comfort you and may our righteous and worthy ambitions know fulfillment I pray, in the name of Jesus Christ, Amen. Ash ton First Counselor, Presiding Bishopric SUCCESS to you in this building! May you speedily start, and may you speedily finish it. If we are perfectly honest with your, and sometimes we are, we would say something like this to President Spafford, "Success to you in this building," and, as the host said to those guests he had in the house as he went into the closet to take out their coats, "Here's your hat, but what's your hurry?" Our building over there is crowded. You need a new building and so do we need more elbow room. Success to you! I wish that every building that is financed would be financed as easily as you are going to finance this building; and I hope I have the privilege, and I am sure it won't be denied, to give my pittance in this direction. In closing, inasmuch as President Smith has mentioned the Lamanites, I just want to pay tribute to a Relief Society worker; namely. Sister Flora Hicks, who has had charge, with her husband, of the Lamanites in the Papago Ward, Arizona. She was called down there ten or twelve years ago, and she passed away about eight weeks ago. We had the privilege of standing in that house as those Lamanites came in to pay homage to her. They are supposed to be cold; they are not supposed to be very enthusiastic in their emotions. When those people came in, about three hundred strong, one at a time and in groups, they cried like babies in the loss of that wonderful woman and what she had done for them. And as they put her under the ground, the little Lamanite president of the Relief Society put fifty dollars in the hands of Brother Hicks. They heard that he had had some intention of moving back to Salt Lake City sometime. She said, "Here is fifty dollars, and men have another fifty dollars. That is for a monument to this good woman. You are not going to move her from this sacred soil?" God bless her memory. She gave herself to help other persons raise their standards of life. God bless you. Amen. If we were to continue attending In that day of long ago, the sisters general conference without our sis of the Relief Society went out into the fields after the brethren had harvested the crops and literally gleaned what was left, and from that day on until 1919, the sisters of the Relief Society had been accumulating wheat which eventually amounted to thousands of bushels. The Federal Government, in 1919, proposed the purchase of this wheat, and it was sold to the Government for the sum of approximately $400,000. With the approval of the First Presidency, the money was placed in trust in the Presiding Bishop's office with the understanding that this money would be invested in such a way as to insure its safety and return some interest. Over the years the interest has been paid to the Relief Societies of the Church, who in turn have used it for maternity and child welfare work. But about four years ago, the First Presidency deemed it advisable to invest the money again in wheat to the end that should there come a day of need, some provision would be made to provide for the members of the Church. Hence, a large elevator was erected on Welfare Square with welfare labor, which holds approximately 250,000 bushels of wheat. A small elevator was purchased at Levan, Utah, and another one at McCammon, Idaho. The recently purchased flour mill at Kaysville has an additional storage capacity of 72,000 bushels. At the present time the Relief Society has stored in the Salt Lake Elevator and the one at Levan approximately 215,000 bushels of wheat which has a value at the present market price of over $200,000, and there is a sizable amount of the original wheat funds that has not yet been invested in wheat.
As we contemplate this achievement by the women of the Church, the effort required, it represents to
all of us a grand and glorious achievement. More than that, it represents the principle of generosity and willingness to provide the staff of life for any members of the Church who might be in need. It is further evidence of the divine power that inspires men and women to do those things which are for the spiritual and temporal benefit of the Church.
AND now as you approach a new project in the way of a building for the women of the Church, there will be no question about your success and the final accomplishment of your objective. No doubt when this building project gets underway, the brethren of the Church will be given the privilege to contribute to the fund that will cover the cost of the building as an expression of appreciation and gratitude for the splendid service rendered by this great organization of women.
My mother was a Relief Society president. I say, "God bless her for her devoted service," for out of the service rendered in this great organization, she set her children an example which instilled in their hearts a love for the gospel of Jesus Christ. When I think of these ministering angels of the Relief Society, the words of the song just rendered express best the spirit in which they labor: "Peace I leave with you; my peace I give unto you, not as the world gives, give I unto you." The spirit of the Master motivates this great organization of our sisters to render a service which causes all of us to say, "God bless them."
May God bless you in all of your endeavors, I humbly pray in the name of Jesus Christ. Amen.
