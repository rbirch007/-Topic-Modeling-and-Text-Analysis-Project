Potted Plants Complete a Picture Eva Willes Wangsgaard HEN the planting time rush is over, the workshelf and the garden house make an ideal area for caring for and displaying potted plants. 'The perennial favorite is the geranium. Geraniums offer a generous variety of color, florets, and even leaf form and fragrance. 'They may be purchased from abundant supplies of well-started slips, many already in bloom, in nurseries, florist's shops, supermarkets, and in many department stores. Or you can start slips yourself, if you have a room where plants can get plenty of light. Another favorable trait of geraniums is their tolerance for shade. It is amazing how well they will bloom if given light, fertile soil, at least monthly feedings of fish emulsion, and good circulation of air. Fuchsias, fibrous-rooted begonias, and tuberous begonias make good potted subjects, but must be chosen according to the conditions of sunlight and shade an area offers. Borders help tie a garage or workshop into a garden. Fibrous-rooted begonias offer a wide variety of kinds and colors, differing chiefly in heaviness of leaf, stalk, and blossoms. The B. Semperflorens is the favorite variety. In choosing a border a more striking effect is secured by choosing plants of the same variety and color. Some fibrous-rooted begonias have red-tinted leaves. Others are a rich, almost translucent green. Some blooms are deeply red, and they range from there down through luscious pinks to flesh pink and pure Page 288 white. A border of green-leafed begonias with a wealth of deep pink blossoms brings more enthusiastic compliments in a garden than your most exquisite tuberous begonia blooms. 'They are sold in flats or in individual pots, but either way, they are very tiny when set out around Mother's Day in the Intermountain Area, or whenever danger from frost is past. 'They should not be placed closer than twelve inches, however, because at late July or early August they have grown into beauteous mounds of generous blooms, making a compact border. They bloom constantly, from an inch-high start till the snow covers them or the frost nips them. Fibrous-rooted begonias are shade loving and thrive best with only morning sun or filtered sunlight. The begonias in the border in the picture would have been happier and would have made a better showing had they been planted in a raised border. 'The easiest way to keep a raised border raised is to enclose it. If you are handy at masonry or can afford to hire a mason, a stone enclosure is beautiful and most permanent. But 2" x 8" redwood slabs are inexpensive, readily available, light to handle, and easy to nail. 'They hold up very well, too. I order mine cut to fit the needed length and mitred to fit at the corners. 'Then they are easily pushed into position and secured with finishing nails. It is wise to give them two coats of redwood three-in-one finish before using them. If barnyard humus is used, however, it is necessary to water it down thoroughly at least three times before planting, in order to wash away excess nutrients which might burn the tender roots. The soil proportion which I find brings excellent results is one-half black loam, onefourth sand, and one-fourth humus. WO advantages of the raised garden or border are: first, the roots have plenty of room and no robbing or interference from spreading roots of trees and shrubs; second, the plants get a freer circulation of air. An extensive expanse of wall in a garden is cooler and more pleasing to the eye if it is broken by vines. The trumpet vine, with its bunched amber-colored trumpets and pinnate leaves is a beauty in bloom and has the lovely habit of luring hummingbirds to enrich the garden's enticements. It has strong, spreading, tenacious roots, however, which are capable of sending up suckers as far away as twenty feet from the parent vine. The lace vine is another prolific spreader, but its large, shapely leaves and lacy racemes of white flowers are a gracious sight. The clematis is slower growing, usually, but comes in such a variety of colors and types and sends out such a generous supply of blooms that one vine makes a breathtaking coverage and a glorious background 290 for regal and Madonna lilies, as well as other garden beds. Some varieties are Ramona (Alice blue), Cnmson King (deep red), Ernest Markham (red), Duchess of Edinburgh (double white), Henryi (single white), and Jackmani (purple). These flowers are two to three inches in diameter and range from four petals to seven. 'The four-petal flowers resemble dogwood blossoms. All clematis vines need netting or framework on which to climb. The ever-lovely wisteria is slow growing, but a wonderfully satisfying wall and arbor coverage, with its generous pinnate leaves and grapelike bunches of lavender to purple flowers. Leafy vines, like the vetch ivy, give a tropical greenness and lushness, yet cooling effect, all in green glossy leaves. It is an excellent background for lush blooms such as the tuberous begonias.
