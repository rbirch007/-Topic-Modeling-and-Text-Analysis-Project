LITERATURE e@ America's Literature The New Birth of Freedom Lesson 37 — The Literary Lincoln Elder Briant S. Jacobs (Textbook: America's Literature by James D. Hart and Clarence Gohdes Dryden Press, New York) For Third Meeting, February 1963 Objective: To establish a closer interrelationship between Lincoln's character and _ his writings, that we may more fully appreciate the genius of both. O every American, Abraham Lincoln seems as familiar as the five-dollar bills in our purses and pockets and as near as the fourcent stamps our tongues have moistened. Each February we publicly recirculate the worn, yet beloved stories of his walking miles to return borrowed books, of dropping behind his fellow travelers to pull a pig out of the mire, and restore fuzzy, peeping birds to their nest, of saving sleeping sentries from execution, of saving the Union and freeing the slaves. If our modern educators' routine memory exercise is ever ignored, it is only to permit our youths to memorize the Gettysburg Address, which is well worth memorizing. We give his name to schools and cars, logs and insurance, while from year to year the Lincoln stories grow of their own momentum. But often they are akin to the legends we absorbed at the same time we came to "know" Abraham Lincoln; our childhood image of Lincoln dominates our adult years as well, often with their familiar stories, serving, 856 actually, to prevent our adult rediscovery of him. How temptingly easy it is to coast along on our familiar generalizations about him, inevitably true since "we have known them all our lives." Yet few ventures into the American past yield greater insight into our mature selves than does a serious attempt to know Lincoln the man, Lincoln the symbol, Lincoln the man of the people who, in the words of Gamaliel Bradford, "still smiles and remains impenetrable." Actually, instead of thus generalizing we might be discovering for ourselves the mystery and charm of Lincoln, one of America's truly great men. In scarcely a hundred years, more has been written about him than about any other figure in world history except Jesus of Nazareth and Napoleon. But before we begin our pioneering quest for the "new" Lincoln we should pause to define our goals. Is it to know Lincoln as history or Lincoln as biography? Our goal is to know the man; our paths to this goal: history and his own words.
