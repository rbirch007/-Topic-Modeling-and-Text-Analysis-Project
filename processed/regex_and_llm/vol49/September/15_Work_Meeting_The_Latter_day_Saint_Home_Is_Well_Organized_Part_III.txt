WORK MEETING The Latter-day Saint Home (A Course Expected to Be Used by Wards and Branches at Work Meeting) Discussion 3 — The Latter-day Saint Home Is Well Organized (Part III) Dr. Virginia F. Cutler For Second Meeting, December 1962 Objective: To help us understand the value of organizing our time. WHEN you have achieved the goal of making your home convenient, of having each item located where you use it first, of having duplicate items, such as cleaning materials, in different parts of the home to save backtracking, you are ready for attacking the next goal, which is organizing your time. You should start looking at time from the standpoint of a daily, weekly, monthly, and yearly sched- WORK MEETING ule. There are some folks who look down life's path for a five or ten year stretch and try to make each day's activities add up to meet their longtime goals. A prominent educator tried this idea and was determined to complete her master's degree before reaching age thirty, her doctor's degree before age forty, and to have her sons well on their educational path, with missions accomplished, before reaching midcentury. She has other goals for each decade before becoming a centenarian. 'There have been interruptions and minor changes along the way, but the big goals have been accomplished. The time schedule of each day, if carried through to completion, can give one the satisfaction that comes with accomplishment. A calendar with large squares that would give one an opportunity to make notes for each day, would be an invaluable aid in making timeplanning easier. Go through your yearly calendar and make a special note of all of the birthdays, special celebrations, and significant events for your family. Then, with a colored pencil, block off the time that will be required for getting ready for special events or for certain types of work that must be done. When you see the over-all picture for the year, you will be able to space out your work to avoid overloading the daily and weekly schedule. Now, look at your schedule for the week (the blackboard is used to demonstrate the method). Rehef Society is written on one square; Church events are scheduled in their proper places, and family hour, the highlight of the week, is starred. After all the items important to you and your family are on the schedule, then it is time to think of how you are going to get your work done in between all of these other obligations. No two people organize and plan their daily work in exactly the same way. 'The essential thing is that you do organize, that you do have a plan, that you do get up early in the morning and get yourself ready for the day's work. This means wearing suitable clothing, not a house robe and bedroom slippers. A careful planner and organizer can accomplish more in three hours than another person who works aimlessly can accomplish in a whole day. Scientific planning requires that you know what is to be done, who is to do it, when it is to be done, and how it is to be carried out. Your day's work may include these jobs: meals to prepare, lunches to fix, beds to make, rooms to clean, walks to sweep, animals to feed, darning to be done, a hem to turn up in a dress, and a visit to the dentist. Just listing the jobs will help you to plan who should do them. Homemaking is a_ co-operative enterprise, and each member of the family should share the responsibilities as well as the benefits. Rotating assignments to keep interest going is an asset; teaching the method for doing the work is the managers task; accepting the work done without redoing it is a must; checking and teaching ways to improve is part of the follow-up. Very young children can be taught to put their clothes away and will later welcome the oppor- 700 tunity to make their beds. Give them praise and encouragement for what they do and lead them into greater responsibility. The efficient home manager does not do all the work herself, but considers the home a training center for improving home and family life and sees to it that every member is part of the plan. Here are some tips for streamlining some of the jobs to be done and for finishing work on schedule: Plan meals well in advance. Pack the lunches the night before. Pick up misplaced items and straighten the living room before bedtime. Develop the "while" technique — while the bread is baking have the darning handy. Learn to be ambidextrous. In all this planning, don't forget to save some time for self-improvement. Have you heard about the homemaker who complained to Louis Agassiz after hearing one of his lectures that she never had a chance to do any of the things he suggested? She was always busy and never finished her work in time to do any reading. She said that one of the main jobs she had to do was to peel the vegetables and get the food ready for dinner. "Where do you sit while you peel the vegetables?" he queried. "I usually sit on the back step," was the answer. "What is the step made of?' he asked. "Tile," was the answer. He asked her what kind of tile and she said, "I don't know. "How many years have you been sitting on that step?" he asked. "Fifteen" was the answer. "Lady, here is my card," said he, "find out everything you can about tile and send me a report." This experience changed her life. She started planning her work so that she would have time to study about tile. She read about it in the dictionary and in the encyclopedia. She borrowed some books from the town library and she found something made of tile everywhere she looked. She wrote a twenty-seven page report of her findings and sent it to Professor Agassiz. He was very pleased with the information and used some of it in a report he was making. He sent her a check, and asked her what was under the tile. She replied, "Ants." He sent word back that he would like to have her make an investigation about ants. 'This study took her to the country, to the museums, to the science laboratories of the university, and she wrote a lengthier report about ants than she had written about tile. Professor Agassiz had her report published and she received enough money to buy several books and to enroll in some classes that would help her with her new interest. She eventually earned enough to travel extensively and to enlarge her understanding of the world and _ its people. Every homemaker should feel that she at the end of the day has learned something new — that she is a better person than she was the day before. She must continue to build herself if she is to be an interesting member of the family group and be able to inspire others to greater accomplishments. Thoughts for Discussion 1.
