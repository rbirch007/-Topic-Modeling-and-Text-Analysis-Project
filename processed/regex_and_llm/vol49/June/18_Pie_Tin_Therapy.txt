Pie-Tin Therapy Pauline L. Jensen T is strange how differently people react to various situations. Some people, when wrestling with a problem, take long walks, weed a garden, or even scrub floors until they have settled the issue. With me, I bake pies! Whenever I am beset with a day filled with frustration, or some problem that is bothering me, I cast everything else aside and whip up six or eight pies. I figure that in the thirty odd years of my marriage I have baked a total of at least eight thousand pies. That is only an average of twenty pies a month, and counting eight pies to a baking, that means I have settled one thousand problems with a pie-tin. The peculiar part of all this is that, although these _pie-baking sprees were intended for my benefit, the end result has been twofold. For the minute I see those luscious looking pies standing end to end on the kitchen counter, I immediately begin figuring out how many I am going to give away, and just whom I'm going to give them to. The Page 446 latter actually isn't a problem at all, since it isn't hard to figure out who needs a pie the most. Yesterday, for instance, I went on a pie-baking jamboree. I had been fretting all morning over the disloyalty of a friend — something that none of us likes to have happen, so at noon I got out the pie-tins and the necessary ingredients and got busy. I had just taken the first pie from the oven — a cherry one with juice oozing out around the edges, when the telephone rang. It was Lou, my neighbor. "Do you have a can of fruit or something like that?" she asked, ""Don's folks have just arrived in town, and they will be out for lunch. I haven't a thing — not one single thing in the line of desserts or sweets." "Cherry pie, coming right up," I assured her, and heard her gasp of pleasure. I took the pumpkin pie from the oven and set it aside. That one was for Dr. Anna, for it is her favorite. Besides, she is bedfast, and seldom PIE-TIN THERAPY gets homemade pie. I paused a minute and smiled. It is wonderful to see her smile when I take a pie to her. She smiles so seldom these days, that anything that cheers her is well worth the effort. As I glanced out the window I saw Ruth, another neighbor, coming home. She had had a busy day with her Primary class. Right then I was in sympathy with her, since I am not so far removed from those days myself. Since Ruth was giving her time and effort to a worthy project, why not send the lemon pie to her? I pushed it back to cool a bit before delivering it. The mince pie filled the kitchen with its fragrance. That one I would put in the freezer, I decided. Mince pies keep so well. Then I remembered my friend Min, and what she had said the other day. "I'm starved for a pie with real honest-to-goodness mincemeat filling.' Oh well, why not give this pie to Min? I had several more jars of mincemeat in the storeroom. After all, where could I find a better friend? I thought of all the nice things she had done for us, and this I thought was but small payment. The apple pie must go to Marjorie, for I had planned it especially for her. Chained to a wheel chair, a gift of home-cooked food was the most welcome thing she could receive. She had said so, many times. I glanced at the two pie crusts standing on the table. Chocolate 'pie would taste good, I thought. We hadn't had one for a long time. As I lifted it, with its golden brown meringue, from the oven, Bobbie, the boy next door came in 447 with a package of chicken giblets for our cat. His eyes lighted up. "Chocolate pie!) Uhmmmm! My favorite!" He sniffed ecstatically. With a sigh, I slipped the pie onto a baking tin for him to take home. After all, hadn't he just brought our cat a special evening meal? Quickly I stirred a custard, filled the remaining shell and put it in the oven. How lucky, I thought, that I have a husband who indulges me in this luxury of pie giving. Not once has he objected, for he has his own philosophy regarding this. "No matter how much we give, it is but small payment in exchange for all our blessings," he says. I looked over the array of pies with a feeling of satisfaction. From long experience I knew they would be well received, since through the years, I had learned the technique well. For my pies had gone everywhere — to Church, to PTA, to Primary and MIA affairs, and bake sales. But best of all, eight thousand pies had taught me far more than culinary skill! I remembered once again the problem that had sent me on this therapy. I had almost forgotten it! Had my friend been disloyal, or had I taken offense without thinking it through logically? All flesh is weak, including mine. Was what she did so important after all? It was over — done with. Why not forget it now? The afternoon was gone, but it had been well spent. Enough piecheer for seven families, and an extra bonus — my worry disposed of neatly with pie-tin therapy.
