Care, and Storage Ruth P. Tippetts Consumer Marketing Agent Utah State University Extension Service 66 T'S the Food You Eat That | Counts." Good nutrition for your family is your goal in buying and caring for your food supply. Through rain or through shine, in winter or summer, you have the big job each week of buying your family's food supply. You have many items to choose from. 'This complicates the picture for most shoppers. In some areas many foods are found in fresh, canned, and frozen forms. 'Though offering greater variety, more opportunity for satisfaction, and moneysaving possibilities, the multiple forms impose on shoppers a greater responsibility for making a_ wise choice. For most of us, improved transportation has extended the season for fresh food beyond that of the locally grown products. Many canned and frozen products are available throughout the year. So the homemaker's choice is less clearcut than it once was. The shopper's decision to select a fresh, canned, or frozen food de pends on many things. Shoppers usually evaluate several factors, such as cost, nutritive value, use, convenience, storage, and family preference. Prices for fresh foods change more during the year than their canned and frozen counterparts. 'The price of the fresh form is generally lowest during the local growing season. Remember that markets often have specials on all three forms of a food. With all the complications of shopping, food specialists say there are three keys to success in food shopping. 'These keys are Wise Planning, Intelligent Selection, and — Proper Care of Food. Wise Planning considers what you are going to buy before you go to the market. Make a shopping list and prepare a week's menu in advance. Use the following daily food guide to keep your family well fed. 'This guide is a pattern of choices based on what is known about our needs for vitamins, minerals, proteins, and calories. Here is that guide: Milk Group: Children need one quart per day; teenagers 1-1'% quarts; adults 1 pint or more. (Cheese and ice cream can replace part of the milk.) Meat Groups: 2 or more servings of beef, veal, lamb, pork, poultry, fish, eggs, with dried beans and peas and nuts as occasional alternates. Fruit-Vegetable Group: 4 or more servings, including a dark green or deep yellow vegetable at least every other day; a citrus fruit or other vitamin-C-rich fruit or vege table daily. Bread-Cereal Group: 4 or more servings. Or restored. Page 90 Other fruits and vegetables, including potatoes. These should be whole grain, enriched, FOOD BUYING, CARE, AND STORAGE Survey your cupboard and refrigerator to be sure of what is on hand. Know how much money you can spend. 'l'wenty-five per cent of the food budget is generally allowed for meat, twenty-five per cent for dairy products, twenty per cent for fruits and vegetables, twelve per cent for bread and cereals, and eighteen per cent for sweets, fats, and oils. Keep your food list flexible and list alternate choices in case one food is too expensive, such as the kind of meat for the main dish, or fruit for the dessert. By knowing what is needed before you go to the store, you won't be spending money for something that you don't need. You can take advantage of quantity prices if you need large amounts. Check the food items that are plentiful. The wise food shopper needs to know the time of the week and time of year when various foods are available in the largest volume. These are the ones that are most likely to give you the most value for your money. Acquaint yourself with the grades and different qualities of foods that are on the markets. Lesser known brands often provide just as good quality, and, for many uses, the top grade is not necessary. FTER you do your planning, then, it's time to use the second _ key Intelligent Selection. If you are wise, you will stick to your shopping list and buy the protective foods first — dairy products, fruits, vegetables, and meats. Let the other items come after these. Then you won't be giving in to impulse buying too much. A shopping list that is well made out, having related commodities together, will save you 91 time, and you need only shop once or twice a week to take care of the family's needs. When buying meat, you can approach your market with confidence if you arm yourself with information. First, let your eye be your guide. The best beef comes from young cattle. Meat from such ant mals is dark red, the fat is firm and white, the bones are pink, porous, and soft-looking (gray or white bones are those of an older animal). Good pork will look lean, firm, and pink, with a firm white covering of fat. Look for veal which is juicylooking, and light pink in color, with little fat covering. Choice lamb will be fine-grained, pink, and firm, with bones showing red where cut. It is wise to buy on the basis of cost per serving instead of cost per pound, especially when you buy meat. Bone and fat must be figured in the cost per serving. Beef short ribs may cost less per pound than hamburger, but it gives you only one-third to one-half as many servings. OMETIMES, according to the use you're going to make of the food, you can buy a second or third grade instead of the top grade. Grade "B" eggs are good for baking and mixing in scrambled eggs and omelets. Spend money for Grade "A" eggs only when you're going to use them for poaching, frying, or cooking in the shell. The same is true for canned goods. Casseroles and stews don't need whole tomatoes. Buy a second grade of these. Using judgment in this way can save money. Take advantage of "'specials, watch food prices. " but In finding a 92 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—FEBRUARY 1962 bargain the first thing to do is to compare prices. And that means to compare prices no matter what special sales offers may be fluttering from banners, hung in the store, posted on the windows, or featured on cans and boxes. 'Those "cents off" legends on the labels of so many brands these days are not reliable. Look for the actual selling price on the item. Learn about manufacturer's brands and retailer's brands. Here you may find equal quality at a savings. Your best guarantee of quality is to read the labels. They contain helpful information as to weight, size, brand, grade, number of servings, cut, measure, price, ingredients. Buy in quantity only if you can use the product or have adequate storage space. If your family is small, small size jars and packages may be wiser buys than giant sizes, even at a low price. NCTM: here is the third key, Proper Care and Storage of Food. Many homemakers overlook the importance of good care and storage for the food items they buy at the store. There isn't any point to doing a good job of planning and a good job of buying, only to let the nutritional value slip away, simply because the food was not put where it would keep its quality until it was used. Wash fruits and vegetables and store them in the vegetable compartment of your refrigerator or other cool place to keep them fresh, attractive, and full of value. Leaving them in the heat is one sure way of losing the very things you paid for when you bought the fruits and vegetables. Keep eggs clean, cold, and covered. Why covered? The shell of the egg is porous. It can lose moisture by evaporation and absorb odors from other foods. If you use the convenient built-in racks in the refrigerator, be careful to cover odorous foods and use the eggs within one week. If covered, the storage life is from two to six weeks. Keep milk in the refrigerator below 45°F. Storage time for pork cuts is somewhat shorter than for other meat cuts. Use ground meat in one day, variety meats in two days, fresh meat cuts in three to six days. If you don't plan to use ground meat the day you buy it, keep it in your freezing unit. Ground meat spoils quickly. Wrap cold cuts, sliced, in semimoisture proof material, such as waxed paper, and store at 38°F'. Use within six days for best quality. Store cured meats, bacon, and ham at 38°F. Use half ham within seven days for best quality; whole ham within ten days. Probable storage life of whole ham, four weeks; of half ham two to three weeks. Store fresh fish, loosely wrapped, at 32°F. Use within one day for best quality. Remove the tight wrappings from poultry. Rewrap loosely and store in the coldest part of the refrigerator. Use within two or three days. | A loose wrap on poultry or other fresh meat permits the air to circulate around the surface of the meat which helps to retard the spoilage. While the tough shell of nuts would lead most of us to believe that nuts are one of the best keep- FOOD BUYING, CARE, AND STORAGE ers, the opposite is true. Nuts have a high fat content. They require refrigeration. When the oils get rancid, the nuts will be stale. So, store nuts in airtight containers in the refrigerator or freezer. Unshelled nuts keep better than shelled. Unsalted nuts keep better than salted, because salt spreads rancidity. In an automatic refrigerator with the control set for normal operation, the center storage section of the cabinet will probably run between 38°F. And 42°F. The area just below the freezing unit is colder, for storage of foods such as meats, poultry, and fish, needing the lower temperatures. The bottom of the cabinet is somewhat warmer than the center, for less perishable foods. If in doubt about your refrigerator, take the temperature in different locations with a thermometer. More and more frozen foods are being used. "Zero 'Temperature"' is the most important factor to highquality frozen foods. Once food damage has been done because of higher temperatures, nothing can 93 restore quality. A frozen turkey held for one week at twenty to twenty-five degrees is likely to darken. Orange juice will separate. Fruits and vegetables will lose color and flavor. Other storage care should be given to some fruits and vegetables not regularly stored in the refrigerator. Potatoes, for instance, are a hardy looking vegetable, but they are really quite perishable. Light causes greening which results in off flavor. 'Too high temperature hastens sprouting. Too low temperatures may convert some of the starch to sugar, giving the potatoes an undesirable sweet taste. So, keep potatoes in a cool, dry, dark place with good ventilation. Store onions in slatted crates in a cool, dry, well-ventilated room. Pumpkins need dry, cool temperatures. Place on slatted shelves one layer deep. Store apples in a cool, humid place. 'Temperatures close to 32°F. Are best for apples. We are not even sure how it is pronounced! What if it is one of those squeezed-in, smoggy, coal mine places?" The pick-up did not answer, but went along faithfully pulling the loaded trailer. More desert! More dried-up vegetation. "What if Paonia is just some more of this desert!" I exclaimed, wildly. My husband assured me that 5,600 feet altitude did suggest "in the mountains." We were both worried whether there was a trailer park with modern hookups. 'Thinking of the supermarkets and laundromats, I said: "We could hookup at Grand Junction!" Lynn replied, laconically, "Too far!' I tried again: "Delta?" He replied again: "Too far!" The monotony of the desert does tend to make one irritable sometimes. AlPage 108 though I am unusually kind to my good husband, I said: "Oh, heap big Indian Chief, far too wordy!" We had planned to get to Paonia before dark, but taking two hours rest brought us in when it was getting late. My husband inquired at a filling station about trailer parks. The considerate man got his motorcycle and went ahead of us to a trailer park among some cottonwood trees a mile out. There were six neat-looking mobile homes, widely spaced. And there was our patient "spot," waiting for our arrival at the far left, with a patio pavement! The next day was Memorial Day. We spent it getting orderly and clean. 'Towards evening, Lynn telephoned the president of the branch, who lived about twenty miles away. When Lynn came back, I said, "What did he sound like? I hope he is not the glamorous sort!"' My husband said in astonishment, "Well! What do you mean?" I recovered with, "Oh, I want him to be ordinary like me." He said with surprise, "You are not ordinary!" Which was flattery, indeed! Lynn said, "He's dropping by after Mutual to meet us!" I did not see how Lynn could watch TV so calmly after supper, knowing this touchy meeting was coming up. So much depends on a building superintendent and _ his wife getting along smoothly with the people they come among. He told us that there was a full time carpenter available, and a good project clerk, also. These two facts were an immediate comfort! He and Lynn talked for some time about the project and possibilities, and then Lynn said, "Let's go see some of those people you mentioned, if you have the time." Fullness of Life and Exaltation Elder Ariel S. Ballif For Tuesday, May 22, 1962 Objective: To stress the importance of obedience to law in attaining the blessing of exaltation and to summarize woman's place in the gospel plan. The goal of the L. D. S. Family is to "... Bring to its members such lives as will enable them to return to the inner circles of that celestial home from which they came, — a dwelling with the Heavenly Father and Mother throughout the eternities"' (President J. Reuben Clark, Jr., 808). "T, the Lord, Am Bound When Ye. Do What I Say." BEDIENCE to the commandments of God is prerequisite to all the blessings promised to mankind. In Section 130 of The Doctrine and Covenants, verses 20-21, we read: There is a law, irrevocably decreed in heaven before the foundations of this world, upon which all blessings are predicated — And when we obtain any blessing from God, it is by obedience to that law upon which it is predicated. There are many promises in the scripture to the "chosen people," but in each case to realize the blessings they must hear the word of the Lord, know his mind and will, and apply it in their lives. Marriage is a command of God. We are forcefully reminded by revelation that there is a particular kind of marriage (temple marriage) for us if we would receive the fulness of his blessings. 'The privilege of going to the temple to be martied is dependent upon conformity to the highest standards of human behavior, such as living the law of Page 146 The Relief Society Magazine, December 1940, page chastity, which is purity in body and mind; and possessing a firm testimony of the divinity of Christ and his gospel plan. It would seem that right living prepares the way for divine guidance in our lives. Also, that assistance or blessings do not come to us without a concerted effort on our part. 'There are two verses, 18 and 19, of Section 130 of The Doctrine and Covenants that should be quoted: Whatever principle of intelligence we attain unto in this life, it will rise with us in the resurrection. And if a person gains more knowledge and intelligence in this life through his diligence and obedience than another, he will have so much the advantage in the world to come. Then, our attention is called to the fact that blessings are all predicated upon the fulfillment of the law. This is an appeal to the intelligence of men and women and the use of intelligence to the realization of their greatest possibilities. The Lord is willing to help where intelligent effort is made. LESSON DEPARTMENT Fulness of Life Essential to Exaltation Any person building a house will set down some specific plans according to which he hopes to achieve the objective. The more important the physical structure and the more costly, the more detailed and elaborate the plans and the blueprints. This, then, gives direction to the structure. 'There may be necessary changes and adjustments but, in general, the house will be like the plans. Wi5uth good plans and good artisans, together with carefully selected material, it becomes an actuality. It will be no better than the planning, the craftsmen, and the material used to make it. 'This is, to some degree, analogous to the process of developing a successful family and qualifying for the blessing of eternal life. The big task comes in training and developing the personality under divine principles. 'There is the never-ending responsibility of developing self-control, unselfishness, and in making adjustments to the ever-changing relationships of personalities in a house. It is the difficult task of developing the technique of "we" and "our" in meeting problems and satisfying needs; the maturing of like interests into common interests through the sharing of common experiences in every phase of life; in general, it is reducing to a minimum the basic differences and increasing the individual capacity of making adjustment to each other. Nothing worthwhile is attained without personal effort. We don't find perfection, we make it. In ideal family relations there is no substitute for the art of adjustment which depends upon the maturity of the 147 individuals, which, in turn, is dependent upon a life developed from good, wholesome living. To the couple who have lived fifty years together, through hardships and success, sharing in full the realization of their religious ideals and goals, there is an important significance of "'at-one-ness' referred to in the idea of "one flesh," quoted in 'The Doctrine and Covenants, Section 49, verse 16. 'They have not only become sure of each other, but have become so much a part of each other through their sharing, working, planning, and adjusting that certainly, to them, there is full realization of the "at-oneness" idea. 'This "at-one-ness" is the product of successful living together. It is a growing feeling of indispensableness, knowing no end. The above type of experience is an explanation of how the Lord can be bound. The blessing is theirs in the full realization of each other's value. It is the value of good living expressed in a degree of perfection. The harmony of their lives together is the preparation for eternal marriage. The plan of life and salvation might be compared to a magnificent organ with all the perfection in sound known to the skill of organ makers. If a baby presses the keys one may get squeaks, discords, and chaos, so far as music ls concerned. A person with a few organ lessons and a little knowledge of music can produce limited harmony and melody. The true value of the organ and its possibilities to make beautiful music increase as the knowledge, training, and artistry of the player increase. So when the truly great organist plays upon the same instru- 148 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—FEBRUARY 1962 ment, there is a full expression of the tone quality and harmony, and the great possibilities of the organ are fully realized. So with life, particularly with married life. In the wisdom of the Creator, each individual has the potential greatness that can only be fully realized in successful marriage. It can be enriched by knowing what to do and doing it. 'The instructions are thorough and complete from the great Designer. In this way God is bound. He has defined the rules or life and _ life eternal, pointing out the way to accomplish them. If we apply these principles, the results cannot fail, the promised blessings are ours. The degree of success varies with our individual capacities to understand and to do. The Eternal Nature of Marriage As the statement at the opening of this lesson by President Clark points out, the goal of the family is so to organize the lives of the members that they return to their Heavenly Parents for eternity. 'This statement implies the importance of the here and now. The blessings of the everlasting covenant of marriage are not all for the next world. In reality the blessings and promises of the next world cannot be attained unless we are living the laws here. Man is that he might have joy here and now, and have it more abundantly. The whole program of the Church is to insure its members a rich, full life. If this is attained here, then the promises of eternity have real meaning. There are blessings in store for all who keep his commandments. From the writings of President Joseph Fielding Smith, special encouragement is given to the righteous women of the Church. He says: Now, just one more thought. You good sisters, who are single and alone, do not fear, do not feel that blessings are going to be withheld from you. You are not under any obligation or necessity of accepting some proposal that comes to you which is distasteful for fear you will come under condemnation. If in your hearts you feel that the Gospel is true, and would under proper conditions receive these ordinances and sealing blessings in the temple of the Lord, and that is your faith and your hope and your desire, and that does not come to you now, the Lord will make it up, and you shall be blessed — for no blessing shall be withheld. The Lord will judge you according to the desires of your hearts when blessings are withheld, and He is not going to condemn you for that which you cannot help ("Elijah, the Prophet and his Mission," Utah Genealogical and Historical Magazine, January 1921, page 20). The promise of the future life is glorious beyond our power to understand. "Eye hath not seen, nor ear heard, neither have entered into the heart of man, the things which God hath prepared for them that love him" (I Cor. 2:9). Summary 1. The Eminence of Woman As a summary of the first two lessons, may we point out that, like man, woman is a creation of God. She was given as a helpmate of man in a partnership responsibility for subduing and populating the earth. In the plan of creation, God gave to woman a place of exceptional eminence. 'The importance of the responsibility given her complements the assignment given to man. Marriage is ordained of God. In fact, it is vital to the fulfillment of the plan of life and salvation. The fulness of the Priesthood can only LESSON DEPARTMENT be attained through successful temple marriage. 'This places woman in a most strategic position — a position of honor, glory, and vital importance, and with all this, is the responsibility of being a successful partner. There is in the relationship a unique element of equality, meaning evenly balanced or proportioned; having competent powers, ability, or means. While Adam was designated as the mouthpiece of God, Eve was designated as the mother of men. This set up a balance and a division of responsibility which provides a basis for sound family organization. Only with her can man obtain exaltation in the celestial kingdom. 'Truly, she is the leading lady in the drama of life. She holds equal responsibilities for the accomplishment of the purpose of life, "to bring to pass the immortality and eternal life of man." Together, and only together, can the full realization of the destiny of the human family be achieved. 2. Service, the Mission of Motherhood Childbearing is a woman's unique service. Providing a clean, healthy body and a sound mind are most essential; however, being well-born is far more inclusive. It is being born into a home and family where the parents are qualified to provide healthy stimulation to the growth and development of each child spiritually, intellectually, and temporally., As the mother exercises judgment, wisdom, and care in selection of food for her family, she must also use judgment, wisdom, and care in selecting the spiritual and mental 149 stimulation of her family. So also must the greatest care be used in providing and directing the social contacts of the child. The major part of a mother's service is developing the child's judgment so he can learn to choose for himself. Child development is the constant challenge of the mother. Discipline is bringing one's mental powers under control] and directing them into useful channels. The ideals, values, and objectives of the society in which one lives can, through discipline and obedience, become a part of the child in infancy and remain for life. The person who is most free is the one who knows the law and obeys it. Moral values represent the wisdom of time-tested behavior; moral values represent the best judgment of man in tune with the mind and will of God. 'There is and must be for us a divine tone in the moral values of our society. Homemaking is largely the creativeness of the mother. It is, in reality, a joint father and mother responsibility, yet it is the artistry, personality, and industry of the mother that predominate. Besides the beauty of the physical setting, she is responsible for the things in her home and elsewhere that stimulate the mind and spirit of her children. What they, the children, read, think, hear, and see, they become. To meet her challenge, mother must be constantly alert to her own mental stimulation.
