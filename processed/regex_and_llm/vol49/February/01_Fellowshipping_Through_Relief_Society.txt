Fellowshipping Through Relief Society Elder Mark E. Petersen Of the Council of the Twelve [Address Delivered at the Officers Meeting of the Annual General Relief Society Conference, September 27, 1961] T is wonderful to be with you sisters. It was thrilling to watch the roll call and see women from all parts of the world arise. It surely impresses us with the fact that the Church is now a world Church, as it never has been before, and surely the prophecies are being fulfilled, wherein the Lord has said that the little stone cut out of the mountain without hands shall roll forth and fill the whole earth. We have had a demonstration here this morning of how that little stone is really rolling forth, and how truly enough the world is our field. It is a great inspiration to be associated with the Relief Society. Every year I am more impressed with the excellence of its work and the devotion of all you wonderful people, and with the outstanding leadership of your great Presidency and General Board. I am constantly amazed at the ingenuity, the inspiration, the strength, and the leadership of these wonderful women. They have been most cooperative with us in every way, as we have tried to carry out various phases of our part of the program, which has touched theirs. We are so grateful for the way in which all of you have responded. This morning, for a few minutes, I would like to talk to you about one of the subjects mentioned by Sister Page 76 Spafford in her excellent address and report. She mentioned that we are reaching about half of the women of the Church through our Relief Society, and she also mentioned the importance of reaching the good women of the world who are not members of the Church. May I talk to you about those two things for just a moment. We believe that Relief Society is so important that it ought to be taken to every woman in the Church, that we should not be satisfied in any sense with reaching half of them. We know, too, that another lesson Sister Spafford taught us this morning ls all important, we cannot wait for the women to come to Relief Society. We must go out and seek them. We must ask them before the Rebeccas or some other organization asks them. Let us be solicitous about the women of the Church first, then about the good women not of the Church, so that we may bring them in. One of the most important things about reaching out for these people is that we exhibit real friendliness and neighborliness in doing so. We are trying to promote in the Church, through our missionary program, a good neighbor policy whereby every Latter-day Saint will be a good neighbor to every other Latter-day Saint. We would like to have every FELLOWSHIPPING THROUGH RELIEF SOCIETY vif Latter-day Saint woman be a good neighbor likewise to every non-Latter-day Saint woman. Isn't it but keeping the commandments? Isn't the second great commandment "Thou shall love thy neighbor as thyself"? We cannot fulfill that commandment if we are exclusive at all. We must have the missionary spirit with respect to enlisting the women who are already members of the Church. If we are reaching only half of them, then let us set our goals to reach the rest of them. Relief Society may be used as a marvelous re-activation organization. We may reach out to the younger women, the new mothers, and to the working mothers. Who needs Relief Society and its work more than working mothers to help them enrich their lives and their homes? And we can reach out to the wives _ of inactive brethren. We can reach out to inactive women themselves, and we can reach out more to sisters who are active in other organizations, but not so much in our own. UT how are we going to do it? We are going to do it by being friendly, being neighborly, by visiting, by soliciting their attention, and by giving them a demonstration of what Relief Society can do for them. I once heard an address by one of the great salesmen of the business world. 'This man, after talking about the great value of friendliness in making sales, said that the next great point was that people who are selling a product must show the customers what the product will do for them. I think that is very applicable to Relief Society, don't you? If we, through friendliness, can build up confidence in the minds of those who do not come, and then if we can follow up on that friendliness by showing to them what Relief Society can do for them, then we will, if I might use the business expression, sell them on Relief Society. I firmly believe that many women do not come to Relief Society because they do not know what Relief Society is. I do not believe that all of them understand what Relief Society has to offer them. How can they know, unless we tell them? Is it out of the way, for instance, for us to go into the home of a new mother and give her a demonstration right in her home of what Relief Society can do by way of teaching her home nursing, child care, child psychology, making home a more attractive place, building up the high standards that ought to be in every good Latter-day Saint home? May we not go to the working mother and show her how Relief Society can fit into her needs and her time? May we not go to the intellectual, yet inactive woman, and actually give her a demonstration in her own home of what the literary lessons can do for her? And may we not bring the message of Relief Society by actual demonstration to all others in the same way? We need to go out and demonstrate by actual live demonstration what our work will do for them. Then there is this great missionary program referred to by Sister Spafford. President McKay has announced that every Latter-day Saint is to be a missionary, and part of that means that the existing 78 organizations, including the auxiliaries, will participate in that program. That means that we are going to continue this friendliness, this neighborliness, and by this neighborliness and friendliness, build confidence in the mind of the nonmember neighbor, and let her know what Christian goodness there is in a good Latter-day Saint woman. First, build confidence, invite them to social groups, then invite them to Relief Society, and when the time comes, arrange, as Sister Spafford has said, to co-operate with the missionaries. T would be a wonderful thing if every good Latter-day Saint mother would be willing to invite to a cottage meeting in her home her good non Latter-day Saint neighbors and permit the missionaries to come in and give the missionary lessons to these women so that they may understand the gospel doctrinally, as well as by merely observing us. Every one of us can be missionaries by extending the hand of fellowship to nonmembers, by encouraging them to be friendly with us, by inviting them to our cottage meetings, by co-operating with our stake and foreign missionaries. I was glad that Sister Spafford invited us to use the people, lest we lose them. I am confident that if we can properly fellowship the women of the Church, and the good women not of the Church, and hold out to them a hand of friendship, we can bring in many of the Latterday Saints who are not now coming; we can use our work as a means of conversion for many who are not yet members of the Church. Will you be willing to look at this matter from the standpoint of a missionary? We must save the Latter-day Saints, as well as the nonLatter-day Saints. We must convert the Latter-day Saints, as well as the non Latter-day Saints. If half our women do not come, there must be many among them who need conversion to our way of life, to our organization. 'here are many nonmembers who are willing to come. Let us use our facilities to convert them, so that the light of the gospel may come into their homes that they may then help to build Zion. That we may all work together in this way, is my humble and earnest prayer in Jesus' name. Amen. Oie A T
