ONE LITTLE CHRISTMAS GIFT I was thinking of the first Christmas, and about giving gifts. Mama had always said that only the people who sacrificed to give really knew the meaning of giving. "Can I get anything else for you, Polly?" Mr. Van Wagoner asked, as he turned from the scales and held out a piece of peanut brittle to me. "The red bandana in the window — how much is it Mr. Van Wagoner?"' "Oh, that?" He took out a box from under the counter. "A lot of them in here," he said, lifting the lid, "and they're just ten cents each. Bet you're thinking of a present for your Papa." I nodded. 'My Papa," I told him, "needs something to cover his face on a cold morning. He hasn't a scarf. And I have ten cents that I earned myself." "Well now, isn't that nice?" Mr. Van Wagoner approved. "Not every man gets a present from his daughfer." "I think I'd like to buy one," I heard myself saying. Mr. Van Wagoner beamed. "I'll wrap it up. Let's see." He disappeared into the back room for a minute, and returned with a small piece of green tissue paper and a length of red twine. "Em going to make your Papa's present look really festive," he said. With wide eyes I watched him, and when he handed it to me I thought that I had never seen a package that looked so beautiful. Thanking Mr. Van Wagoner, I turned and went out of the store, but I didn't trust my eyes to look at the window where the little doll was still standing. Mama didn't mention the doll when I got home, she was so busy I guess that she forgot, and I was glad because I wanted Papa's present to be a surprise to everyone. Johnny had made a stand for the tree, and he had brought it into the house. "Let's decorate it now," cried. So Mama let us start with the apples and popcorn strings. When we had finished decorating it, our Christmas tree was an unforgettable sight, with ropes of popcorn and rose berries, apples, bright red, and cookies cut in all sorts of shapes. Mama put the candles on and promised that we could light them for a few minutes before we went to bed. When supper dishes were done we gathered around the organ and, while Mama played, we sang "Silent Night," and "Away in a Manger," and all the other carols we knew. When the last candle was lighted Papa blew out the lamp, and the wonderful Christmas tree stood glowing in the darkened room. All too soon it was time for bed. I watched for a chance, and when no one was looking, I laid Papa's present under the tree. Then I went to bed with my wonderful secret making me feel warm and happy inside. In the early Christmas dawn we trouped downstairs to see what Santa Claus had left. My old doll, Samantha, and Susie's Mary Jane were sitting proudly under the tree all dressed up in new clothes. The 902 clothes almost made the dolls look new, too. 'There was a harmonica for Johnny, and some _ building blocks for little Tim. And, wonder of wonders, somehow Santa had managed to leave a pearl necklace for Susie and a blue bracelet for me. Papa and Mama stood by smiling at our exclamations of delight. But when everything else had been examined, one little gift was still under the tree. "Papa," I said, "you forgot one present, there under the tree." "Well, so I did," he exclaimed, stooping down to pick it up. "I wonder whom this is for?" "It's for you," I said quickly, remembering that I had forgotten to write his name on it. With starry eyes I watched as he untied the red string. The whole family was watching, too. And Mama looked at me and gave me such a wonderful, sweet kind of smile that I knew I would never forget it. As Papa held up the red bandana, his voice got kind of chokey. "Polly," he said, "it's the nicest Christmas gift I've ever had in my whole life." "It will make a perfect scarf for your face on these cold mornings," Mama told him, and she tied it around his face to show us. My heart was singing, I hadn't known until then just how wonderful it was to be a giver. I was proud and happy, happier than I had ever been on any Christmas before. I think I knew a little how the Judean hill folks felt when they brought presents to the manger on that first Christmas morning. Later, when I was helping Mama clear up the breakfast dishes, she looked at me with glowing eyes. "Polly," she said wisely, "you have made your Papa and me so happy and proud, and this is the Christmas you will remember the longest, for today you have learned what Christmas really means."
