Holmes the Man Oliver Wendell Holmes was born near Harvard Yard in Cambridge in 1809, just 100 years after Dr. Samuel Johnson, his neo-classical ideal whom he revered so _ highly that he reread his works each year. His father, Abiel Holmes, was pastor of the First Church in Cambridge for thirty-seven years, and a prominent historian of Puritan New England. Although his father's rigidity and coldness contributed in large measure to Oliver's later repudiation of religious creed and the Calvinistic definition of man as a depraved animal, he never lost his belief in a benevolent Deity. His mother was a smiling, cheerful soul whose outlook was far removed, indeed, from that of her older husband. In 1840, Oliver married Amelia Lee Jackson, a woman with a fine mind and of unusual charm. After graduating from Harvard in his twentieth year he studied for the law, but to him, it was so dry and boring that for relief he wrote the poem "Old Ironsides" which aroused sufficient public indignation to save the American Revolutionary War frigate The Constitution from being destroyed. He studied medicine in Paris for two years just as vital medical discoveries were being made, and returned home after extensive touring in Europe to become the "whip which drove back the objectors and_allowed the modern scientific concept of medicine to gain entrance." Most abhorrent to the young doctor was the old idea that God caused sickness in corruptible men as punishment for their sins — sin itself being regarded as an inescapable mortal frailty resulting from Eve's yielding to temptation. The more Holmes studied science the more he found God's benevolent handiwork in men and nature. For him science was "piecemeal revelation" of God's truth. "There is no conflict between science and _ religion; science represents the thought of God discovered by man." For Holmes, God and nature could never be separated. When a wound healed "God's presence and power and knowledge are there healing it." Educated, intelligent, cultured Hi) 780 through travel and a proud and dominant family tradition, he was well aware of the great transitions his age must face. As a most beloved teacher at Harvard Medical School for thirty-five years, he exalted his faith in the scientific method and in the reality of that which is knowable through the senses, almost to the level of a religion. But as poet he played, dashing off witty puns in conversation and formal speeches, both rhymed and in prose, to amuse himself and his many warm friends. And, if in his Boston, the "statehouse is the hub of the solar system," then Boston's great public halls and the homes of his friends were the revolving planets which carried him sparkling through the brilliant social whirl which he loved and esteemed so highly. Each year from 1851 to 1889 he carefully wrote verses for the annual reunion of the Harvard class of '29 and he won several prizes for his publication of medical papers. He also wrote three "medicated" novels: Elsie Venner, The Guardian Angel, and A Mortal Antipathy, none of them distinguished. They were written to popularize his psychological and environmental theories. But as for his verse, he spoke truly when he wrote: I'm a florist in verse, and what would people say If I came to a banquet without my bouquet?
