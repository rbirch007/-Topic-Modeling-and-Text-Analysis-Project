My typewriter, record player, and young, old-maid self have lived a happy, carefree, pine-needle life here until this summer. In late June, a summer ranger friend asked if I would dog-sit with a Dachshund belonging to another seasonal ranger who had come from Oklahoma with his dog, not knowing dogs were not allowed in the park. "No," I answered violently. "A dog has to be fed and watered, curried, and carried. I come and go too much. Absolutely not." "But it would onlv be for two months, and a dog would be good company." Unfeelingly, I interrupted, "Tim here is all the company I need and he can feed himself." Mv dear friend's fourteen-year-old visiting son looked disturbed, not amused. The ranger expostulated, "How can you be so selfish? Kennel life would be sickening for Fritz and his owners. 'The youngest boy cried himself to sleep last night." "Tough," I said heartlessly. Pi prize my independence." At this stony moment, big-eyed, dog-loving Tim began to beg and plead that, while he was around, he would do everything — feed, water, chase, quiet, and bathe Fritz — so that I would have neither care nor complaint. My ranger friend hastened my ungracious capitulation by adding, "Fritz's mistress says she'll be glad to do your washing and ironing in exchange for the dog's board." That did it. The well was low, the nearest laundromat twelve mountain miles away, and I detested ironing. RITZ came, and both he and I complained vociferously for two davs. I didn't like his whimpering, his cold, wet nose, or his barking. He hated his leash, being away from his loving family, and the cold nights in our outdoor "dorm." Poor Tim bore up bravely under my scathing I-told-you-so's and Fritz's heartbroken cries until two AM. When he quieted the dog's shivering and lonesomeness by shoving him down in his sleeping bag. My objections to this unclean bedmate were blotted out bv the blessed quiet. The next: morning I tried to look at the dog objectively. He was a compact, muscled, brown bundle of nervous energy with pretty markings and soulful brown eves. He was so short, the wild daisies hid him and so nervously brave he barked at falling pine cones. Tim showed me proudly that Fritz would sit and beg, but this didn't impress me in view of the time he spent on the couch and beds. He was a spoiled, beloved dog, and losing his family was, obviously, a traumatic experience. Most of the day he whimpered to Page 335 336 go out and, once there, cried to be off his leash. Even Tim seemed disenchanted by his charge, and I thought vainly of ways to rid myself of the pest. Unexpectedly and dismayingly, that was accomplished when I opened the back door in answer to a friend's hail and out shot Fritz. Tim and I spent a hot, breezeless afternoon looking for the escapee. Gnats buzzed, dust rose, tempers heightened, and echoes of futile calls were our only accomplishment. 'Tim worried about Fritz being thirsty, tired, chased by bears, bitten by a snake, and irretrievably lost. More selfishly, I fretted about what his ranger family would sav and feel and lamented how short-lived would be my vacation from ironing. Toward evening, a panting, dusty Fritz dragged in, gulped water, nuzzled Tim, and fell heavily asleep under, not on, the couch. After that, there was no more whimpering, running away, or need fora leash. Fritz was 'Tim's shadow. I had as little to do with him as possible, but was amused at the way his ears flopped as he ran and the way he bounced over the mountainside. Tim taught him not to jump on the beds, but he was still a pest. He cried so we couldn't—or, rather, Tim wouldn't—leave him home alone. In the car, he climbed over and on us and, of course, while we shopped or went sightseeing in Yosemite Valley, he had to be restrained by a leash — a calamity which he bore with the equanimity of a caged bear. Still, his mistress was doing our washing and ironing and supplying the smelly, dog food with so many compliments on our stewardship, I felt as if I were the "Good Neighbor" of 1962. HEN came the day Tim left for home and the whimpering routine began again. I had to see that Fritz had food and water and tried angrily to quiet his barking when friends came to see me. He seemed to realize he was the man of the house and protected me fiercely, if misguided, by barking, growling fairly menacingly, and pawing the ground so that dirt and pine needles flew all over the paths. He followed me everywhere. In the mornings, he greeted me as if he hadn't seen me in days — jumping on me, wiggling, and nuzzling my hands. It was flattering and I knew I had made the team with Fritz. One afternoon, he scurried out from under the couch in obvious distress and tried his best to make it outside before he was sick. He missed, but looked so abject and mournful that I heard myself saving, "That's all right, honey. You couldn't help it." Honey! Fritz had made the team with me. T remained utterly adamant at having him in or on my bed, or talking baby talk to him, but when he stood and begged for loving, I gave it to him and, when I shivered out of my swim in the river, his warm, lovable body felt good. Occasionally, I submitted to his licking; often I played with him, and frequently I talked to him. Our "lovematch" continued until summers end when Fritz left with his real family. Sure, that's I, but I miss the ecstatic morning loving, the floppy ears, yours-but-to-command eyes, 337 and faithful shadow of Fritz.
