Gardner For Tuesday, October 9, 1956 HEN one meal a day must be carried to work in a lunch box, the homemaker has a real challenge to meet, for into that lunch box must go foods that will provide a fair share of the day's needs and be so inviting, satisfying, and healthful, that the worker or LESSON DEPARTMENT school child will keep physically and mentally fit. Whatever the packed lunch lacks in providing a third of the day's nutritional needs should be met in the other two meals. With the use of thermos jars, in which can be carried warm soups, stews, or other main dish foods or _refrigeratorchilled beverages, the task of preparing a tasty and well-balanced packed lunch meal is greatly simplihed. With the improved food wrappings one is now able to include in the packed lunch crisp strips of salad-type vegetables, with the assurance that they will stay refrigerator crisp until eaten. Glass jars or colorful plastic containers with tight-fitting lids—in which can be carried well-chilled fruit, baked custard, or other desserts, are a further help in giving the meals you pack, the same high rating as your other meals. Because sandwiches are so often a mainstay in the lunch box, it is especially important that they be prepared with care, imagination, and a thought of what they will taste like three to five hours after they are prepared. The imaginative sandwich maker today keeps her sandwiches right at the top in appetite appeal. In addition to enriched white and whole wheat, she also uses raisin, bran, French, rye, and nut breads for variety. She uses a wide variety of sandwich fillings and tries always to include two different kinds of sandwiches in each lunch box meal. She combines ingredients for sandwich fillings with a thought for Note: See ' Magazine and "Packed Lunch Suggestions," zine, October 1952, page 669. 483 contrast in flavor, color, and texture. Bland fillings are sharpened with lemon, custard, ketchup, sauces, and pickles. Crunchiness is added by crisp bacon, nuts, cucumber, celery, carrots, or shredded cabbage. Fillings which lack color are enlivened with chopped pickle, pimiento, olives, sweet peppers, or parsley. The — skilled sandwich maker creams, but never melts, the butter she uses; then spreads it out to the very edges of both slices. She is generous with sandwich fillings, making them as thick as one slice of bread. Each sandwich is neatly and securely wrapped to prevent drying out of the bread. She wraps clean, crisp lettuce leaves in waxed paper to be added to the sandwich just before it is eaten. She includes in every lunch an element of surprise — some fresh fruit, a piece of favorite cake, candy, cookies, or other dessert. In many instances, the home freezer is the sandwich maket's best friend. A variety of sandwiches can be prepared and kept in the freezer for two or three weeks, but no longer. Frozen sandwiches wrapped two to a package in moisture-vapor proof wrappings, not waxed paper, thaw in about three hours and are still refrigerator cool. They should be eaten soon after thawing, however, since frozen food deteriorates quickly after thawing. Whether the packed lunch is to serve aS a main meal for a working man, for a school child, or for a dainty refreshment at a social function, it deserves all the care and interest you can give it both for your sake and the one who will eat it.
