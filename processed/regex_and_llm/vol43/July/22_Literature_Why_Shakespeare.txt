To bask in Shakespeare's life-giving zest and gusto, to be quickened by the sheer cleanness and precision of his words, to be awed by his wisdom and justice as he shares with us his probings deep into the human heart and the mysteries of mortality — in these realms of highest adventure Shakespeare is our supreme guide and spokesman, and has been for centuries. 'Time has proved him moral and true; his virtues reveal him as brilliant and just; his selfless humility proclaims him worthy of the sustained love of mankind. None of our writers has received more love or deserved more, than he. Love cannot be commanded or forced, it must be earned, usually by giving oneself to others. Shakespeare has given himself to others so abundantly that rarely are we Page 484 Measure for Measure, I. 1.30-36 fully aware of our debt. For centuries he has been the most widely quoted man in our culture, surpassed only by the Bible. Actually he constantly places more words in our mouths than any of us realize. "Brevity is the soul of wit" (to quote Shakespeare), and well-turned phrases are so rare because so few mortals have ever been able to put the nub of the matter into a few memorable words. Best proof that Shakespeare is the master phrasemaker of the language is to cite a few of his contributions to our everyday language which after centuries still survive. Merely because these now common expressions are Shakespeare's is not to justify them. Actually we should ignore these "old saws" and invent our own. Would that we could! Would we were Shakespeare! Attempts to Know Shakespeare From 1623, when the First Folio edition of Shakespeare's plays appeared, up to the present, at least 120 men have dedicated their mature years to editing Shakespeare's works, criticizing the texts, relating his plays to their sources, determining the order in which the plays were written, etc. The first ShakeSpeare societies appeared early in the nineteenth century; today they are everywhere. Special Shakespeare collections are prized possessions of the wealthiest, largest li- 485 braries in England and the United States; in the United States, the largest and most famous is the Folger Library in Washington, D. C., an entire institution dedicated to Shakespearean scholarship. So thoroughly has Shakespeare been counted, compared, explained, analyzed, paraphrased, quoted, searched by X-ray and ultraviolet light that no one can doubt his greatness, since he has survived this deluge triumphant. When many of our best minds for centuries have dedicated their lives to him, what remains for us to do or claim to do? J. W. Mackail gives us the only sane and true answer: The best approach to Shakespeare is to read him. It is easy, and common, to read too much about him (Approach to Shakespeare, page 5). To be known, then, Shakespeare must be read and heard. Yes, we possess him in some degree, since it is impossible for a literate, attentive person in our society to escape hearing him being constantly quoted. We have studied him at school in our youth, and, occasionally, we may have read him for our own pleasure during maturity. All members of our western culture beyond eatly childhood know of Shakespeare, in varying degree; not quite as many know him. Many of us feel that we would like to know him "if we just had the time." On a more mature level, let us grant the fact that all of us have time for what we most enjoy. Inasmuch as we now have time to enjoy Shakespeare — the next two years of the literature lessons have been dedicated to introducing him — our immediate challenge is to convince ourselves 486 that he yields a rich enjoyment which eminently deserves our attention, our effort, and, most precious, our time. The more we read Shakespeare the more each of us, no matter who we are or whatever our background, must come to realize that in every page of Shakespeare there is joy and truth and insight which will immediately enrich our day-to-day existence, if only we will be among those who will give him ear. If we will each day share with ourselves and our families only one or two of his great thoughts or piercing insights into human frailty, then we will understand as never before why his audience constantly increases in size and loyalty. Why Study Shakespeare? One reason may be that time has proved his greatness, has proved his visions and values to be true. If we study him as the greatest dramatist, because some of our greatest critics have so proclaimed, we know we shall not be wasting our precious time and effort. But, valid and popular as this approach may be, in part it robs us of the privilege and pain of making our own judgments. Let us ask ourselves the following questions: Why have millions of people read Shakespeare of their own choice, and attended his plays? How has he fed the timeless, basic hungers of multitudes of people, from all walks of life? How can anything more than three hundred years old be considered great in our modern, scientific, efficient world? The longer we read him the more we marvel at the aptness and cunning of his words. 'The fecundity of his imagination seems inexhaustible. Sometimes we even come to feel that his ability to portray a soul, or catch an essence of place or mood solely through words reveals almost supernatural powers. 'To witness such word dexterity makes us more humbly aware of man's infinite though mortal capabilities. As an example, consider his effortless skill in using the commonest household incidents to reveal the deeper, hidden truths within us which are often felt but seldom expressed. How very apt is his comparing the spilling of concealed guilt with one's attempt to carry a pan brimful: So full of artless jealousy is guilt, It spills itself in fearing to be spilt. Hamlet, IV. 5.19 Such a comparison provokes us into deep thought, itself its own delight, since it frees us from our constant, exclusive concern with the surface trivia of life. Self-Revelation One of Shakespeare's most substantial rewards is that, more forcefully and intensely than any other writer, he reveals us to ourselves; he "holds the mirror up to nature" and helps us see ourselves as we really are. 'This he can do because he "has the most comprehensive soul," the most searching eye, the sharpest pen. Although he does not provide Copyright 1902 all the answers, he uses the method of poetry and art — intuition, insight, metaphor, rhythm, subtlety — to help us see all things as they really are. Probably his supreme gift is his intense zest for life, possible only if preceded by a probing, recording curiosity about everything in life: nature, society, ourselves, our God. Because Shakespeare felt this straining tug of his own monstrous curiosity and obeyed it, he feels and thus soothes our own. Seeing truth in his created world, we can then better see it revealed in ourselves. As William Hazlitt has observed: Every single character in Shakespeare is as much an individual as those in life itself.... How many other candidates we RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1956 have had for the office of Greatest English Poet, writers sometimes partially blind to the goodness of man and nature, writers calloused by cynicism and despair, or writers who refuse to face life's great spiritual and moral problems but would rather escape in glib shallowness or frivolity or sensation! For how many have illicit love and adultery been cause for jest; how many have seen cruelty and pride and hypocrisy and yielding to animal desire and faithless self-interest, as the inevitable state of man. But Shakespeare is none of these. As Coleridge has seen in all his wisdom: Shakespeare has no innocent adulteries.. No virtuous vice; he never renders that amiable which religion and reason alike teach us to detest; or clothe impurity in the garb of virtue. Shakespeare's characters depict the basic truths of mortality and immortality in and of themselves, without seeming to be pious, sanctimonious, or "preachy." His truths never seem "'tacked-on," or sugarcoated moralizing pills slipped down our throats when he has popped our mouths open; instead, his truths are the very essence of things as they are. Because he is excited by all phases of life, he discusses everything, and holds nothing back. Because he has vowed to hold the mirror up to all of nature he presents every human evil and weakness, but his greatness is that, without posting little moralizing signposts (which would weaken and mar his larger effect), he subtly suggests to us that what he speaks of as evil is evil. For example, when anyone mentions the word luxury but leaves LESSON DEPARTMENT it untagged, without any slanting adjectives, how are we to know whether that person regards luxury as good or bad? But when Shakespeare personifies luxury as "the devil luxury, with his fat rump and potato finger" (Troilus and Cressida, V.2.55), it is difficult for us to refrain from identifying so vigorous an image not only with the immediate character who speaks it, but with Shakespeare's own opinion. To define this as his over-all attitude toward luxury seems further justified when we find him speaking elsewhere of luxury in similar tone; for example, in The Merry Wives of Windsor, Falstaff is caught and chided by the fairies in his attempt to steal a man's wife: Fie on sinful fantasy! Fie on lust and luxury! Lust is but a bloody fire, Kindled with unchaste desire, Fed in heart, whose flames aspire, As thoughts do blow them, higher. V.5.97-102 Again, after reading the following (II.3.153-171) on pride, a revealing aside from 'Troilus and Cressida, how much more subtle and personal does Shakespeare make pride than if he had merely tacked on the wall such a proverb as, "Pride goeth before destruction," a widespread custom in Elizabethan homes. Ajax and Agamemnon have been lamenting Achilles' pride. Note particularly the cunning Shakespeare employs to tell us that Ajax is a proud man, and about as awkward in selfanalysis as the rest of us. Speaking of Achilles, Ajax says, "Is he so much? Do you not think, he thinks himself a better man than I am?" 489 Agam. No question. Ajax. Will you subscribe this thought and say he is? Agam. No, noble Ajax. You are as strong, as valiant, as wise, no less noble, much more gentle, and altogether more tractable. Ajax. Why should a man be proud? How doth pride grow? I know not what pride is. Agam. Your mind's the clearer, Ajax, and your virtues the fairer. He that is proud eats up himself. Pride is his own glass, his own trumpet, his own chronicle, and whatever praises itself but in the deed devours the deed in the praise. Ajax. I do hate a proud man as I hate the engendering of toads. Nestor. (aside) And yet he loves himself. Is it not strange? How superbly Shakespeare's tone herein accomplishes what might well have been his two objectives: first, to give a body or form to his own emotional attitude toward pride; and second, by Ajax's smugness ("How doth pride grow?'') and by Ajax's own vigorous image of pride ("as I hate the engendering of toads'') to illustrate in Ajax the very quality which he condemns. Shakespeare subtly forces us to realize that pride is not a vice which occurs only in others. He does not strongly denounce it in Ajax, as Ajax resembles the rest of us. And Shakespeare seems to say, "Inasmuch as we are all in this business of life together, let us then act as brothers, and tolerate each other in our human weaknesses." Shakespeare has been many things to many peoples; so he must be to each of us. But whatever else he may or may not be, read him, that he may then be allowed to remind you that all men become brothers when, in compassion, they share 490 the great binding experiences of life: pain, sorrow, laughter, death. It is in these tender times that all men shrug off their surface badges of distinction and understand one another, and this great unifying truth does not come to an end with the termination of either mortality or death.
