Harrison, Harcourt, Brace & Company, 1948) For Tuesday, October 16, 1956 Objective: To understand the reasons for Shakespeare's greatness and appreciate his contribution to our lives. Thyself and thy belongings Are not thine own so proper as to waste Thyself upon thy virtues, they on thee. Heaven doth with us as we with torches do, Not light them for themselves; for if our virtues Did not go forth of us, 'twere all alike As if we had them not ERE English literature to be compared to the universe, Shakespeare would be the sun, about which all others circle to feel the warming influence of its outstanding glow. To bask in Shakespeare's life-giving zest and gusto, to be quickened by the sheer cleanness and precision of his words, to be awed by his wisdom and justice as he shares with us his probings deep into the human heart and the mysteries of mortality — in these realms of highest adventure Shakespeare is our supreme guide and spokesman, and has been for centuries. 'Time has proved him moral and true; his virtues reveal him as brilliant and just; his selfless humility proclaims him worthy of the sustained love of mankind. None of our writers has received more love or deserved more, than he. Love cannot be commanded or forced, it must be earned, usually by giving oneself to others. Shakespeare has given himself to others so abundantly that rarely are we Page 484 Measure for Measure, I. 1.30-36 fully aware of our debt. For centuries he has been the most widely quoted man in our culture, surpassed only by the Bible. Actually he constantly places more words in our mouths than any of us realize. "Brevity is the soul of wit" (to quote Shakespeare), and well-turned phrases are so rare because so few mortals have ever been able to put the nub of the matter into a few memorable words. Best proof that Shakespeare is the master phrasemaker of the language is to cite a few of his contributions to our everyday language which after centuries still survive. For how many of the following have you ever expressed gratitude to Shakespeare? There's something in the wind (Comedy of Errors); play fast and loose, out of the question (Love's Labour's Lost); single blessedness, the course of true love never runs smooth (Midsummer Night's Dream); halcyon days (I Henry VI); paint the lily, elbow room, cold comfort (King John); what's in a name?
