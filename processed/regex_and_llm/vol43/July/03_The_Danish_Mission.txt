The Danish Mission Preston R. Nibley T the October Conference of the Church, held in Salt Lake City in 1849, Erastus Snow, thirty-one years of age, a member of the Council of the 'T'welve, was selected to open a mission for the Church "in the Scandinavian Countries." Elders Peter O. Hansen and John E. Forsgren were called to accompany him. Elder Hansen was a native of Denmark and had begun to translate The Book of Mormon into the Danish language before leaving America. When these brethren reached England, in April 1850, Elder Snow requested George P. Dykes, an elder laboring in that country, to join them, as he had some knowledge of the Norwegian language; the missionaries then continued their journey and arrived in Copenhagen, Denmark, on June 14, 1850. In delivering their message to the Danish people, they met with almost immediate success and, on August 12, 1850, less than two months after their arrival, Erastus Snow baptized fifteen converts "in the clear salt water of the Sound." Other elders arrived in the mission and the work of proselyting was gradually extended to all parts of Denmark. Andrew Jenson, Assistant Church Historian and a native of Denmark, computed in 1930 that 26,656 of his countrymen had joined the Church since the introduction of the gospel in 1850, eighty years previously. During that time, also, he stated, 13,984 of these baptized had "emlgrated to Zion." Denmark remained a part of the Scandinavian Mission until 1920, when a separate mission was formed with Elder Carl E.. Petersen as the first president. In March 1956, there were 1,875 members in the Danish Mission, located in sixteen branches. Holger P. Peterson is the present president of the Danish Mission. Seventeen Relief Society organizations were reported in December 1955, with 359 members. Ora Irene H.
