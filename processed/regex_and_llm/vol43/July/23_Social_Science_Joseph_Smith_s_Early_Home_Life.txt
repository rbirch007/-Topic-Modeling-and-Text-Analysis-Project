Social Science—Latter-day Saint Family Life Lesson 1—Joseph Smith's Early Home Life Elder John Farr Larson For Tuesday, October 23, 1956 Objective: To show the influence of the Prophet's early family life upon his teachings to the Latter-day Saint women. Poe Smith established a pattern, both of opportunity and of obligation, for Latter-day Saint women which has been followed and built upon by the succeeding Presidents of the Church. His teachings regarding Latter-day Saint women came as inspiration from the Lord, after long years of preparation for his calling. 'The Prophet's early family life undoubtedly played a most important role in influencing this preparation. In the life of the Prophet's mother we find exemplified the virtues which should characterize a true Latterday Saint. A glimpse into the Smith household will help us to appreciate more fully the development of the Prophet's lofty concepts regarding woman's sphere, which are to be discussed in the lessons to follow. Family Background Joseph Smith, Jr. Was born the fourth child in a family of eleven. His parents, Joseph Smith, Sr. And Lucy Mack Smith, descended from substantial New England stock, the Smiths having been "honorable farmers" and the Macks "industrious land owners." His parents were chosen to fill very important muissions in helping to establish the Church in this dispensation. The parents were well-to-do at the time of their marriage, and for a few years following, but, because of the fraud of a trusted agent, a large indebtedness was imposed upon them, which not only wiped out their entire holdings but burdened them for many years to come. Through it all, the parents seemed sustained by a deep spirituality and absolute trust in God which had characterized their families for generations and which were readily absorbed by their children. Their trials and hardships resulted in even greater humility, strength of character, and family solidarity than they had heretofore known. The Smith children learned at an early age to be industrious and dutiful, sharing alongside their parents the responsibilities of their pioneer life. In this superior home Christian virtues were taught and_practiced.. Family prayer, Bible reading, and thoughtful meditation by the fireside were the order of the day. Kindness and consideration were their watchwords. 'The parents instructed the children in reading and writing, since they "had, in great measure, been debarred from the privilege of schools." So impressive were their evening family gatherings, that on many occasions neighbors joined to partake of their spirit. On one of these evenings, soon after the family began singing, a couple of little boys came in, and one of them stepped up to Samuel and whispered, "Mr. Smith, won't you pray pretty soon? Our mother said we must be home by eight o'clock, and we would like to hear you pray before we go" (Ibid., page 189). Often repeated in the family circle were the prophetic declarations made by members of both the Smith and the Mack families prior to this time, which were to be fulfilled by the Prophet's mission. Lucy Mack's brother, Jason Mack, had believed: 491... That by prayer and faith the gifts of the gospel, which were enjoyed by the ancient disciples of Christ, might be attained.... He was also of the opinion that God would, at some subsequent period, manifest His Power as He _ had anciently done—in signs and wonders (Ibid., page 9). One of the Smith ancestors had been impressed to say that a Prophet would be raised up within the family to perform a great work on the earth. The familiar accounts of other spiritual manifestations to family members combined to produce a spiritual sensitiveness and belief in divine power, which were undoubtedly a preparation for the family's acceptance of the Prophet's mission. Family Acceptance of the Prophet's Message The assurance that his father would accept his story was given Joseph by the Angel Moroni on the morming of September 22, 1823. This was the morning after the angel appeared to the Prophet in his bedroom to instruct him regarding the sacred record he was to translate. He was sent from the field by his father when he seemed ill. As he went home, the angel appeared and asked him why he had not told his father that which he had been commanded to tell. Joseph replied: "I was afraid my father would not believe me." 'The angel answered, "He will believe every word you say to him." And, just as the angel had predicted, his father not only believed him, but charged him to follow every instruction given him by Moroni (Ibid., page 79. See also Pearl of Great Price, Joseph Smith 2:29-50). The interest of parents in the 492 worthy activities of their children and faith in their accomplishments are real factors in attainment. The support of the Prophet by his parents, and by his brothers and sisters, stimulated him in his calling. The entire family openly received Joseph's message. In fact, they were so eager to hear more that the eldest son, Alvin, who always exhibited great anxiety over the plates, suggested they arise early, complete their work, and leave time for long evenings to listen to Joseph. 'The Prophet confided what he had been permitted to reveal, cautioning them to keep his words within the family circle. The Prophet's mother beautifully describes the family during the days which followed: From this time forth, Joseph continued to receive instructions from the Lord, and we continued to get the children together every evening for the purpose of listening while he gave us a relation of the same. I presume our family presented an aspect as singular as any that ever lived upon the face of the earth—all seated in a circle, father, mother, sons and daughters, and giving the most profound attention to a boy, eighteen years of age, who had never read the Bible through in his life: he seemed much less inclined to the perusal of books than any of the rest of our children, but far more given to meditation and deep study. We were now confirmed in the opinion that God was about to bring to light something upon which we could stay our minds, or that would give us a more perfect knowledge of the plan of salvation and the redemption of the human family. This caused us greatly to rejoice, the sweetest union and happiness _pervaded our house, and tranquility reigned in our midst. During our evening conversations, Joseph would occasionally give us some of the most amusing recitals that could be RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1956 imagined. He would describe the ancient inhabitants of this continent, their dress, mode of traveling, and the animals upon which they rode; their cities, their buildings, with every particular; their mode of warfare; and also their religious worship. This he would do with as much ease, seemingly, as if he had spent his whole life among them (Ibid., pp. 82-83). Prophet's Dependence Upon His Parents Each child derives great strength, ability, and character from the love, advice, and close association of his parents. The Prophet Joseph Smith was no exception. He leaned heavily on the support and counsel of his parents, both before and after the organization of the Church. The bond of affection between them grew stronger as they shared the many responsibilities involved in his mission. One instance is cited to indicate that Joseph desired to include them in his joys as well as his anxieties. Upon completion of the translation of 'The Book of Mormon, Joseph asked his parents to join him at the Whitmer home. The evening was spent in reading the manuscript, and the following morning "after attending to the usual services, namely, reading, singing and praying, Joseph and Oliver Cowdery, David Whitmer, and Martin Harris, went to a nearby grove to engage in prayer, after which they were shown the plates by an angel. (See D. H. C. I, pp. 52-57.) When Joseph returned to the house, he threw himself down beside his mother and exclaimed: Father, mother, you do not know how happy I am: the Lord has now caused the plates to be shown to three more besides myself. They have seen an angel, who has testified to them, and they will have to bear witness to the truth of what LESSON DEPARTMENT I have said, for now they know for themselves, that I do not go about to deceive the people, and I feel as if I was relieved of a burden which was almost too heavy for me to bear, and it rejoices my soul, that I am not any longer to be entirely alone in the world (History of Joseph Smith By His Mother, Lucy Mack Smith, page 152). The Prophet always showed deep gratitude for the influence of his parents. 'This is shown by the following entry in his Annals made in October 1835, when the Prophet visited his father for several days, attending to him during an illness: Waited on my father again... All this day with my heart raised to God in the name of Jesus Christ, that He would restore him to health, that I might be blessed with his company and_ advice, esteeming it one of the greatest earthly blessings to be blessed with the society of parents, whose mature years and experience render them capable of administering the most wholesome advice.... We called on the Lord in mighty prayer in the name of Jesus Christ, and laid our hands on him and _ rebuked the disease. And God heard and answered our prayers — to the great joy and satisfaction of our souls. Our aged father arose and dressed himself, shouted, and praised the Lord (D. H. C. II, page 289). "Mother Smith" There were many sterling qualities in Lucy Mack Smith which endeared her to her family and the people about her. She was by nature refined, cultured, and dignified. She was kind and courteous, and, because of her sympathetic understanding, she was of great comfort as she ministered among the sick and distressed. 'The Prophet had ample opportunity to see her strong leadership in action, first in the home, then in the community, and afterwards among the women of the 493 Church, and must have felt the need for others to emulate her spirit of service and her deep spirituality. Her life was not an easy one, but at no time did she question the hand of the Lord. In fact, she seemed to have a talent for turning adversity into a blessing. At one time the entire family, except the parents, was struck with typhus fever. It was at this time that Joseph developed a leg infection and underwent a most painful operation. After these experiences, she wrote: Having passed through about a year of sickness and distress, health again returned to our family, and we most assuredly realized the blessing; and indeed, we felt to acknowledge the hand of God, more in preserving our lives through such a tremendous scene of affliction, than if we had, during this time, seen nothing but health and prosperity (History of Joseph Smith By His Mother, Lucy Mack Smith, page 58). Early in her married life, when seriously ill, she had promised the Lord if he would restore her to health, she would devote the remainder of her life to him and her family, and this she did consistently. She had great faith in the power of the Priesthood and was always ready to sustain her husband and sons in their respective Church assignments. On many occasions when the men of the household were absent, she adroitly handled intruding enemies to prevent their obtaining The Book of Mormon manuscript. Mother Smith was an effective missionary, not only by means of her good life, but in actually expounding the principles of the gospel. Her years of experience were invaluable to the younger mothers of the Church, and they looked to her for wise counsel and sound judgment. Her capacity to love was unlimited. Not only did she shower her own children and husband with affection, but she extended the same devotion to those who married into her family; she welcomed the Phophet's wife, Emma, from the time he first brought her to the Smith home as a bride. There was always a strong affinity between them. Not every mother is called upon to be the mother of a prophet, but the challenge of establishing a righteous home life, one that will carefully develop those traits of character so well developed in the Prophet, is an incentive to Relief Society members everywhere. Every child, if properly nurtured in the home, will be better equipped to respond to calls of leadership which may come to him—whether in the Church, the community, or the nation.
