EDITORIAL 9 VOL. 43 JULY 1956 Fro ntiers T has been said that frontiers are now but half-forgotten realms in the world of history, that their eager, spiritual beckoning, their call to adventure and far places, their promise and their fulfillment will never be known to another generation. It is true that the geographical aspects of the world are fairly well known, having been viewed from the skies, and having been explored even to the lonely outposts by the brave and the venturesome. Nearly all the habitable regions are occupied, and, although some areas are still in various phases of the pioneering stage, still the noontide of seeking the unknown frontier is past. Today we are not privileged to experience the sacrifice, the wonder, and the rejoicing of the quest of a far frontier. 'lhe wagons are gone, the trails but faintly marked upon the prairie; the mountain peaks are named and mapped, the course of the rivers long discovered. We may feel that in our generation we have lost something of that fearless facing of the future, that undeviating purpose, that great fulfillment that led the vanguard westward. And turned the desert into gardens. Still we need never be without the heritage of the frontier. It is our great legacy from the past to be for us a source of courage and buoyant inspiration, for, though the geographical frontiers have vanished, Page 442 the frontiers of the future forever remain — the undiscovered realms of mind and spirit — individual development and group accomplishment. Along these pathways, there are still mountains to climb and wide and verdant valleys to be cultivated. Much of life is like an ever-beckoning frontier. Take the morning, for instance — dawn is forever a frontier, the light of day on a far peak, the joy of waking with the golden hours unfolding. No one knows in the morning what treasures of enlightenment and understanding a day may bring. Its hours are all to be discovered — "'if I take the wings of the morning c A new type of work is a frontier, or a new way of doing a familiar responsibility. A homemaker walks each day in a frontier land, for almost every hour brings some new adventure on the trail of time. It may be only the planting of the garden, the glory of first bloom, the ripe fruit on a tree. It may be an adventure into designing or home decorating, or perhaps the making of a gift or the planning of a birthday dinner — or something more prosaic. Perfection may be in small units, and often a valley view of life may exceed in contentment the expanse that some far height reveals. How privileged is the woman who sees her children and her grandchildren embark on the frontiers of life. The mysterious world lies all around EDITORIAL the infant — to be explored — the texture of a petal, the satin lining of a quilt, the windowpane — and then all the further. Knowledge breaking upon his consciousness. Wonderful is the frontier of childhood and privileged those who may Share and influence that journey which reaches through family patterns into the far eternity. Each picture first seen, all the music heard and learned, are pathways of satisfaction where, in the mind and spirit, one may feel the joy of discovery. Who has fully discovered the treasures along scriptural highways? Each reading and re-reading may become a frontier of spiritual heights. Recall the Proverb, speaking of wisdom: The Lord possessed me in the beginning of his way, before his works of old. I was set up from everlasting, from the beginning.... When there were no depths, I was brought forth; when there were no fountains abounding with water. 443 fields, nor the highest part of the dust of the world (Proverbs 8:22-26). For us in the Church, our own Relief Society, established when Nauvoo the Beautiful was a frontier city, for us the world-wide sisterhood offers a broad and shining pathway into new interests and accomplishments, a journeying into regions of the mind and spirit not known before. For all of us there are illuminating and satisfying experiences in every meeting, every activity — new friends, new thoughts, new knowledge, further enlightenment, and the ever-increasing unity of the sisterhood. Once a poet in reverie asked, "What canst thou find with seeking which hath not long been found?" There is much to be found with seeking which we have not yet experienced, and the place to find our beckoning horizons is still along the far and near frontiers.
