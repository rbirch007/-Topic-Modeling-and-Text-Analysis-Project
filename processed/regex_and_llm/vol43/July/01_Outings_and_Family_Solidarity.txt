Outings and Family Solidarity LaVern W. Parmley President, The Primary Association RECENTLY at a stake conference, one of the leaders said to Brother Oscar A. Kirkham, "After the morning meeting I wish you would take the opportunity to walk home with my son. He is not going to his Sabbath School, and I wish you would have a chat with him." Brother Kirkham, after the meeting, took the chap by the arm, and they started down the street towards home. He thought a very good approach would be to say: "You know, you have a great father. He is a fine man." 'The boy immediately turned and said, "Brother Kirkham, I wish you would talk to my father for me. In all my life he has never been with me to a movie. He has never taken me with him anywhere. I haven't even had a game of checkers with him. I would appreciate it." There are many adults who seem to have attained the dignified state of parenthood without having first passed through the undignified state of childhood. Childhood memories dim with the passing of years, until, finally, all that are left are those of the somewhat idealistic side. Through doing things together, parents learn to understand their child's point of view, and to deal more sympathetically with his natural urges and desires. What all parents really need is a bridge between themselves and their children Page 420 to shorten the gap and bring about family solidarity. Business men, educators, and _ religious leaders agree that the early home experiences of a child are important factors in his life. The question has been asked — "Can a child fail in his life's work because parents have provided him with poor home training and environment?" Executives and personnel experts of leading American corporations declare a unanimous "yes." United States industry says this to parents: "We wouldn't want your son or daughter, if you have failed to train him for successful adulthood." Most industrial leaders believe members of a family should pull together to develop their standards of living. According to Lawrence A. Appley, President of the American Management Association, Inc., "There seems to be a direct significant relationship between early home environment and on-the-job SUCCESS." In an article "Your Children Need Family Recreation,' written by Dr. Rex A. Skidmore of the University of Utah, and published in the November 1955 issue of the Children's Friend, we read: It has been said that "the family that prays together stays together." Some experts have added to this that the family that "plays together grows together." Case studies show that both of these activities have great possibilities sound family relationships. Some values of the family playing together are particularly important. First, the family usually gets better acquainted with each other. 'Too many families live at home in name only and are away from each other most of the time. Family recreation can be "loads of fun." Playing with those who are near and dear to one is enjoyable in many respects. Participating in recreational activities establishes a deeper bond, and opens the doors of communication among family members, in developing 422 A picnic seems to be one type of outing suitable for families. Young and old enjoy taking a lunch to a park, canyon, seaside, or other appropriate place where there is an abundance of fresh air and things to do, where the children can romp and expend some of the energies which build up within them. Parents who nag their children at home usually find it easy to let them run, yell, and sing to their "'heart's content" in a picnic area. Parents who do not take their whole family on a picnic at least several times during a year are missing a golden opportunity for having fun and _ strengthening family bonds. Elder Ezra Taft Benson, at a general conference, said: Our homes need the stabilizing influence of a closer relationship between parents and children. Every boy and girl needs the safeguard which an intimate association with mother or father will provide. 'This relationship must be built upon love and mutual confidence. Such an atmosphere can be created as parents plan for recreation and diversion together with their children. An evening spent in wholesome activity; a picnic in the mountains or nearby park; or a project for fathers and sons and mothers and daughters — these will all tend to increase filial affection and love in the home, and strengthen parent-children relations. Time thus spent yields big dividends. Parents in this close association have no difficulty teaching virtue, honesty and industry, the basic principles of our American way of life. There can be no genuine happiness separate and apart from the home. More recreation and more devotion in the home will result in greater family solidarity. Solidarity, as defined in the dictionary, means: "An entire union of interests and responsibilities in a group; community of interests, standards, etc." Children, themselves, feel the necessity of family solidarity, of RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1956 sharing, working, and playing together in a family group. The question, "What do you think makes a happy family?" was asked of more than 1500 school children. Every grade from the second grade to the last year in high school was represented in this study conducted by the New Rochelle Guidance Center. The following are some of the answers given: A thirteen year old boy summed up his answer in these words, "A happy family reminds me of a baseball team, with Mom pitching, Dad catching and the kids fielding with everyone taking a turn at bat." The seven and eight-year-olds focused their answers on the concrete satisfactions of family life: a picnic in the park, the "surprise" daddy brings home, holiday celebrations, the family pet. One said, "Our family is happy because we help each other a lot." The nine-year-olds put their emphasis on relationships in family life: 'They frequently mentioned family parties and outings, playing games with the family, talking about pleasant things at meals. 'To them happiness meant togetherness. The ten - year - olds generally reached this conclusion: Do your chores, have fun, and go places together. The eleven and twelve-year-olds continued to underline the ideas of the younger children but did so more maturely. The happy family is the united family. 'The most important thing in a happy family is being together a lot. OUTINGS AND FAMILY SOLIDARITY Family solidarity was what each group wanted. 'These young people, so varied in age and_background, agreed with startling unanimity that being together in the family and doing things with all of its members is the very essence of happiness. ESTABLISHING play habits of the right sort is perhaps one of the most important things in character development. 'The president of a stake once said, "Oh, if my father had only let me _ play when I was a boy. If I wanted to _ play ball or go on a hike, he would say, If you've got more energy than you need, there's the hoe and there's the cornfield.' His attitude towards play activities was that they were a waste of time. Now I feel myself all cramped and bound into a limited space of action. I can't reach out easily for social contacts." There is a great vacancy in that man's life. In the book Recreation in the Home, we read: It is generally recognized as true that right living is not transmitted by merely telling children to be honest and brave and true. Rather it is developed in the individual as a phase of other activities and through the example of parents and children working, playing, and carrying on social life together. Perhaps there is not the same degree of pleasure to the boy who blisters his hands on a hoe handle or exhausts himself in running a mile to chase the cows out of the lucerne patch, that there is when he blisters his hands on a baseball bat and exhausts himself in making a home run. But there can be pleasure in both if the proper attitude is fixed for both. In any program of family activity 423 there must be socialized co-operation. The tasks allotted should take into consideration the age and abilities of the children. 'They should give opportunity for individual success and excellence of performance. Individual initiative should not be crushed and the child should be given due credit and praise when the task is completed. Boy Scouts in the family may be given the joy of building the fire, and even cooking the meal for a family outing. The girls may plan and prepare a picnic supper for the back yard. They can set the table and arrange flowers for the centerpiece. If the outing is held in the canyon or park, the younger set can be given the "clean-up" assignment. Scouts, again, can use their "know-how" in seeing that the fire is completely out. Fortunate is the family that works and plays together. A family will be surprised at the enjoyable time that is to be had by spending a vacation at home. Many families fail to explore their own communities and the facilities available for recreation. 'They never visit places of interest that tourists travel miles to see. 'They do not realize that parks and museums are established for their interest and pleasure. 'There is a world of enjoyment and education to be had in such places. Backyard camping and cooking can be most exciting. A trip to the zoo affords a lot of fun. A bicycle or hiking trip can be exhilarating. Family picnics in the park, or neatby canyons can be real family outings. No one has more time than someone else. 'The difference is, some use it wisely, others waste it. Parents should take time to lay up a store of good family relationships. In a recent issue of a weekly periodical a mother summarized her thoughts about time: My biggest mistake as a mother was a mistaken perspective of time. While our children were growing up, I was under the impression that the major part of my life was being lived while I had them with me. Looking back, I well understand what my beloved grandfather meant when he used to speak of "those few weeks we had the children with us." It is such a short, short time. I don't mean it seems brief; it is brief! For a birthday present a father once gave his son a piece of paper with the following written on it: "To my dear Son: I give you one hour of my time each day, to be yours, and to be used as you want to, without interference of any kind whatsoever. Your Father." Realizing that this father could make a very costly gift to his son, a friend asked, "How did you happen to hit upon the idea of giving such an extraordinary present?" His face and bearing carried the tell-tale marks of idleness and dissipation. 'Robert!' I exclaimed in amazement, 'to see you like this — and you with such a fine father!' Parents must realize how much of the mature years of their children will be spent in seeing the things memory preserves for them. 'They must guard the space in a child's memory storehouse, so they will have stored up a mighty collection of mental movies which will depict happy family associations. In Elder Mark E. Petersen's book, For Time or Eternity, he says, "It is our faith that the family is a basic unit in heaven. We should be saved as families." Family outings are some of the best tools to use in building family solidarity. Family solidarity built upon this earth will continue throughout eternity.
