Gardner 'HIS is the second year of the course of study on "Food Preparation and Service." Too often in our day, women look beyond the walls of their homes for opportunities and experiences that offer challenge and contribute toward cultural growth. The fact is that the world's greatest laboratories for such experiences are our homes. Our homes are gold mines of opportunities, but the mining is not successfully done without effort. It requires planning and an earnest and continuous search. The important thing is that each wife and mother approach her task with a feeling of pride in her work as a maker of homes and a builder of characters, and that she allow nothing to blind her vision nor discourage her efforts. One of the richest shafts in this gold mine is in the area of meal preparation and service. If the mining is carefully and thoroughly done, the rewards are incalculable. The physical health of family members is kept at its best, and ties between mother and children are strengthened through their united relationships in the family kitchens. Since the best foods may be served at a nominal cost, the choosing, preparing, and serving food becomes a real art, satisfying the creative urge that is within us all and turning would-be boredom into deep satisfactions. Because food preparation is a daily activity, it can be a source of great, creative joy. Everyday duties, when done with competence as well as with love and devotion, enrich us spiritually; spiritual satisfaction is the finest fruit of all endeavor. 'The same experience results in boredom when we fail to develop skills that assure us success and a growing love for our work. The objective of these lessons is to teach the basic rules for success in choosing, preparing, and serving food, so that the experience of food preparation will become a real art and furnish recreation as well. What activity does more to re-create one's enthusiasm and love for life than to prepare and serve a delicious food that lifts the spirits and gives comfort and peace to the ravenous appetites of growing children? The full objective of these lessons will be reached only if each presentation includes a discussion on how each individual food fits into the entire meal picture. The fact that foods are good is not enough; they must also be good with the other foods with which they are served. OLiterature—Shakespeate in Our Lives Preview of Lessons for 1956-57 Elder Briant S. Jacobs HETHER we will or no, Shakespeare is always with us. Few of us are even mildly aware of the many actual words he places daily in our mouths; fewer still have taken the time to trace these daily phrases back to the plays themselves. And scarcely any of us have ever said aloud one word of thanks or appreciation to this greatest writer in English literature for the warming, cheering, enlarging contributions he makes constantly to our lives. The purposes of our study of Shakespeare are to understand more fully: (1) how near we are to Shakespeare in our daily lives; (2) how much our awareness of the human and natural worlds about us is quickened through reading him; and (3) how deeply indebted we are to him for his words of universal truth. Realizing full well that the high qualities of respect and love can never be forced nor dictated, our supreme objective is to understand Shakespeare more fully that we might enjoy him; respect and love then follow inevitably. Controversy over Origins or influences is not our major concern; nor are we interested in analyzing him as do scholars or critics. We are interested in finding within his lines those qualities which have made him immortal; we do want to become intimate friends with his characters and to feel at home in his stage settings. 'Then his words which we hear constantly ringing about us will gather richer meanings. 'Then his high poetry will sing more strongly within us, and lift us, sadden us, baffle and tease us, just as does life itself when most truly, keenly seen. 'Then in his speaking more intimately to us we shall realize in simple awe why his works have only increased in use and vitality throughout the centuries. More fittingly than of any other writer it might be said of Shakespeare that he is "all things to all men." His world of literary truth LESSON DEPARTMENT is adorned with the delights of the imagination, with poetry, music, beauty, fantasy, suffering, terror, wisdom, compassion. It is his people who come more fully alive on the page than do our neighbors and business associates in so-called "real" life. With him leading us and putting words and visions before our inner eye, we shall plumb depths of wisdom and human insight elsewhere and heretofore unknown to us. And best of all, gradually we shall come to realize that the unifying force of all his plays — Shakespeare himself — has seen life as truly as he has seen it fully. He never cudgels us into accepting any prejudiced, partial view of life as the whole and only one; he never preaches nor poses. Instead, he seems to say, "Here is a version of truth, of life as it has come to me. Consider it as you will; reject or accept it if you must; yield up to it your enjoyment and sympathy if you can." Our text is G. B. Harrison's Shakespeare Major Plays and the Sonnets, Harcourt, Brace & Company, 1948, $6. It will be used for the entire course. It is imperative that each class leader have a text, one which is competently edited, with full footnotes at the bottom of each page. Lesson titles and objectives for the first year follow: Lesson 1. Why Shakespeare? Objective: To understand the reasons for Shakespeare's greatness and appreciate his contribution to our lives. Lesson 2. Shakespeare's World Objective: To present the customs, 409 beliefs, and conflicts of Shakespeare's life and world, which were ready to be given harmony and artistic form by his emerging genius. Lesson 3. Shakespeare's Poetic Power Objective: To feel more profoundly the peculiar power and mystery of Shakespeare's poetry that we might read his works with enlarged understanding and hearts. Lesson 4. Lesson 5. The Merchant of Venice Objective: To learn more fully that the two-edged sword of mercy cuts those who do not extend mercy, just as it cuts those to whom mercy is not extended. Lesson 6. A Midsummer Night's Dream Objective: To show that in A Midsummer Night's Dream, Shakespeare touches our lives through fantasy and poetry; through qualities we all approve, such as love, delight, joy, and humor. Lesson 8. Julius Caesar Objective: To realize that idealistic men of good will may destroy themselves in bold but imprudent attempts to destroy the evils they feel exist.
