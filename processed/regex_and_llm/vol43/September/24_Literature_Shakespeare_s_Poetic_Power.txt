Harrison, Harcourt, Brace & Company, 1948) For Tuesday, December 18, 1956 Objective: To feel more profoundly the peculiar power and mystery of Shakespeare's poetry that we might read his works with enlarged understandings and hearts. New approach to Shakespeare has been the major contribution of 20th century scholarship. It holds that plot and character are important but secondary: the dramatic intensity of the play itself is best sensed in the dense verbal texture of the words as spoken by Shakespeare's characters. 'The emotion lying deep within the situation is evoked by the words, not by plot or character, which themselves come to the reader through Shakespeare's poetic concentration and imagery. We cannot remind ourselves too often or earnestly, then, that Shakespeare the poet, is the very heart of Shakespeare the dramatist. 'To understand Shakespeare we must constantly work downward from the surface of his poetry, phrase, image, and symbol. In his words lies the key. The Nature of Poetry Macaulay says, "By poetry we mean the art of employing words in such a manner as to produce an illusion on the imagination, the art of doing by means of words what the painter does by means of color." Page 630 Hallmarks of poetry are brilliance, freshness, power, condensation. It has a high singing all its own which can no more be defined than denied, as in: When the sweet wind did gently kiss the trees And they did make no noise. Merchant of Venice, V. 2.3 These beautifully simple lines catch the tranquil silence as only lovers of nature can hear it. Likewise it is the words which reveal Macbeth's fears that Duncan, whom he is to murder, had lived so meek and clear a life as king that:... His virtues Will plead like angels trumpet-tongued against The deep damnation of his taking-off. And pity, like a naked newborn babe, Striding the blast, or Heaven's cherubin horsed Upon the sightless couriers of the air, Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye That tears shall drown the wind. Macbeth, I, 7.
