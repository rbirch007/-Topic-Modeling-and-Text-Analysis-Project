New approach to Shakespeare has been the major contribution of 20th century scholarship. It holds that plot and character are important but secondary: the dramatic intensity of the play itself is best sensed in the dense verbal texture of the words as spoken by Shakespeare's characters. 'The emotion lying deep within the situation is evoked by the words, not by plot or character, which themselves come to the reader through Shakespeare's poetic concentration and imagery. We cannot remind ourselves too often or earnestly, then, that Shakespeare the poet, is the very heart of Shakespeare the dramatist. 'To understand Shakespeare we must constantly work downward from the surface of his poetry, phrase, image, and symbol. In his words lies the key. The Nature of Poetry Macaulay says, "By poetry we mean the art of employing words in such a manner as to produce an illusion on the imagination, the art of doing by means of words what the painter does by means of color." Page 630 Hallmarks of poetry are brilliance, freshness, power, condensation. It has a high singing all its own which can no more be defined than denied, as in: When the sweet wind did gently kiss the trees And they did make no noise. Merchant of Venice, V. 2.3 These beautifully simple lines catch the tranquil silence as only lovers of nature can hear it. Likewise it is the words which reveal Macbeth's fears that Duncan, whom he is to murder, had lived so meek and clear a life as king that:... His virtues Will plead like angels trumpet-tongued against The deep damnation of his taking-off. And pity, like a naked newborn babe, Striding the blast, or Heaven's cherubin horsed Upon the sightless couriers of the air, Shall blow the horrid deed in every eye That tears shall drown the wind. Macbeth, I, 7. 18-25 Again, this passage itself embodies Macbeth's eloquent fear in exalted, LESSON DEPARTMENT 631 A Perry Picture BIRTHROOM, SHAKESPEARE HOUSE, STRATFORD impassioned imagery. Note the similarities between such a passage and one of Shakespeare's best sonnets: Let me not to the marriage of true minds Admit impediments. Love is not love Which alters when it alteration finds, Or bends with the remover to remove. Oh no! It is an ever-fixed mark That looks on tempests and is never shaken. It is the star to every wandering bark, Whose worth's unknown, although his height be taken. Love's not Time's fool, though rosy lips and cheeks Within his bending sickle's compass come; Love alters not with his brief hours and weeks, But bears it out even to the edge of doom. If this be error and upon me proved, I never writ, nor no man ever loved. Sonnet 116 Here Shakespeare blends together into a perfect oneness—concise statement (first sentence); play on Copyright 1903 words (love-love, alters-alteration, remover, remove); simile Love is mark, or star; not a fool); personification (Father Time with his sickle); intellectual wit-turn (last couplet). So skilled is Shakespeare's craft that we are scarcely aware of what blends the whole together; namely, rhythm (it is the star to every wandering bark); and thyme (at the ends of alternating lines: minds-finds, love-remove, mark-bark, shaken-taken, etc). Yet each blender, each part, though definable on the surface, speaks to something deep within us, something which can be expressed in no other way. In Shakespeare's sonnets we find some of the best and purest poetry in our literature. Yet, for those familiar with these poems, careful attention is required when they are 632 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—SEPTEMBER 1956 Copyright 1924 WILLIAM SHAKESPEARE A Perry Picture reread. For those experiencing them for the first time, they may seem difficult. Here the standard recipe holds: read and reread aloud, then to oneself, relating each difficult line or image to that which precedes and that which follows, until its relation to the whole becomes apparent and the poem as an artistic unit "jells'" into completeness. At the risk of stating the obvious, let us then paraphrase the above sonnet, realizing as well that the entire poem is the only acceptable summary of itself. The theme of the poem is love's constancy despite adversities and the ravages of time. "Marriage of true minds" makes love a spiritualized blending of like forces rather than a physical union. 'That love is not true which changes when it finds in the lover changes or flaws not at first apparent, or which— instead of resisting one who belittles or removes charm or beauty from the beloved—actually joins with the remover in the very process of taking away. By contrast, true love cannot be shaken by tempests; it is the star which guides every small craft at sea as the mariner sights the star and records its height in appreciation and _ trust, disregarding the detail of whether it is a star named or unknown. Though Time's sickle harvests rosy lips and soft cheeks, it can never harvest love, which is not Time's lackey or fool, because true love is not changed by Time's passing, but remains unchanged until judgment day. Should any skeptic succeed in proving this view of love false, he must also disprove the obvious fact that I (Shakespeare) have just written this poem, or, more impossible, prove that no man has ever been in love. Equally famous, and _ perhaps somewhat easier to understand on first reading is sonnet 55: Not marble, nor the gilded monuments Of princes, shall outlive this powerful thyme. But you shall shine more bright in these contents Than unswept stone, besmeared with sluttish time. When wasteful war shall statutes overturn, And broils root out the work of masonry, Nor Mars his sword nor war's quick fire shall burn The living record of your memory. 'Gainst death and all-oblivious enmity Shall you pace forth. Your praise shall still find room Even in the eyes of all posterity That wear this world out to the ending doom So, till the judgment that yourself arise, You live in this, and dwell in lover's eyes. LESSON DEPARTMENT Herein the theme is the immortality of love, preserved not in the beloved, nor in stone monuments, but hereafter in the sonnet itself as well as in all lovers' eyes. Shakespeare's beloved will be immortalized in bright memory by the sonnet, while gilded monuments or monuments scarred by time's neglect will have passed on. Neither war nor the fires of war shall destroy the loved one who shall prove victorious: over death and indifferent fate, to be praised by all posterity. Note the accumulative power Shakespeare achieves by repeating the letter s in the following lines: But you shall shine more bright in these contents Than unswept stone, sluttish time. Besmeared with The image in the last line is irresistible, largely because Shakespeare chose exactly the right words working in harmonious combina-- tion of visual and aural image until it registers forcibly on our inner self as the bulky, awkward paraphrase or a more literal rendering never could! Note also how much force is contributed to the following line by the key word wasteful, which, in turn, blends in alliterative harmony with When and war; likewise in the next line the two strong words are the noun broils and the verb-adverb root out. "Living record of your memory" is a combination far more powerful than any of its key words spoken separately. Thus poetry gives a charm, a delight, and a penetration to intuitive, perceptive depths of the mind such as can come from no other experience in life nor from any other art- 633 form. 'These preceding examples are poetry, all containing the same material—words—and appealing to the same object. 'They speak of the deep-smouldering fires and unconscious realities within us all which are so rarely acknowledged and fed as only the greatest poetry can feed them. As we have just proved, the texture and resonance of true poetry is undefinable, yet two tools are common throughout: condensation and imagery. Often these are the same. Poetic Imagery Just as Shakespeare is useful on the common level of our normal small-talk, so he becomes indispensable whenever a larger emotion or conflict or characterization is to be communicated. 'To effect these miracles in words, Shakespeare uses imagery, composed of metaphor and simile, which are two forms of comparison. Metaphor simply says one thing is another: "Our bodies are our gardens'' (Othello I. 3. 324). Simile is a figure of speech in which like or as is always present: "His nose was as sharp as a pen" (King Henry V. II. 3.17). Here lie some of poetry's most powerful tools. The poet's most vigorous, creative impulse finds expression in metaphor, which not only gives fresh expression to his inner self, but, by his new association, he endows dead objects with life, animals with human traits. Conversely, on every hand _ his images reveal truths or likenesses which ordinarily lie dormant beneath the shallow levels on which we live. Metaphor deals in wordmagic, for it goes beyond or below our logic or reason to appeal to other realms and values in us, we know 634 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—SEPTEMBER 1956 not why or how. As L. C. Knight states in his Wheel of Fire: The Shakespeare world is not the world we see, but it is... The inner world we experience, the world we live and fear. Excerpts From Plays To illustrate Mr. Knight's point, let us examine various images from the plays, reminding ourselves that, literally, a play is only a certain combination of words which _ is unique within itself because it obeys its own inner laws; for reasons not to be expressed, a metaphor within a play is "right"; and this "rightness" occurs frequently in Shakespeare. Analyze and communicate, if you will, the following famous image from Troilus and Cressida: Time hath, my lord, a wallet at his back Wherein he puts alms for oblivion. TIT. 3. 145-146 The first thing we learn about experiencing this image is that, in order for it to speak to us, we must give it our complete concentration. Here Shakespeare's words mean so much more than they say: is he telling us that hypocritical Time will accept our bribes which we childishly believe will guarantee our being remembered, while actually only darkness lies ahead? Regardless, we'll agree that such. A marvelous image provokes us to project our thoughts into realms as difficult to acknowledge and solve as our own personal tensions. Food is useful to Shakespeare in defining moral qualities, as when he has a character in As You Like It point out that, though "honesty coupled to beauty is to have honey a sauce to sugar," yet "to cast away honesty upon a foul slut" is "to put good meat into an unclean dish" (III. 3). Such a comparison sings in Our memories; we see it in our mind's eye. Again his comparison deftly sums up the heart of truth. But three seem most piercingly to communicate Lear's defiant frustrations: a) "You sulphurous and thought-executing fires." Who can react to raw, open blaze other than emotionally? B) "Smite flat the thick rotundity 0' the world!" This image gives to Lear's wrath the stature, the vast cosmic power of thunder so potent as to flatten the curved, thick earth; c) "Rumble thy bellyful!" Rumble contains within itself the very sound it defines, while bellyful is not so much vulgar as expressing a rude, healthy awareness of reality which no synonym can begin to approximate; the two words together in their aural and visual images crescendo to the point of fury and contempt which Lear felt. Spit! Spout! In their staccato brevity further drive home the climax-nail with Thor hammer-blows. All in all, this passage contains the essence of ravaging, naked fury—not done with scenery, nor costume, nor brassy sound-effects, but with words. Shakespeare's Songs To end on a calmer note, we should recall that Shakespeare lived in the golden age of English music when almost everyone played an instrument, just as all of us now play the radio or T'V, and hired musicians played for patrons who were at the barber's: The man that hath no music in himself, Nor is not moved with concord of sweet sounds, Is fit for treasons, stratagems, and spoils. The Tempest contains almost enough music to justify likening it to a symphony. For evoking a mood of eerie mystery nothing can surpass: Full fathom five thy father lies, Of his bones are coral made, Those are pearls that were his eyes.
