Sixty Years Ago Excerpts From the Woman's Exponent, September 1, and September 15, 1896 "For THE RIGHTS OF THE WOMEN OF ZION AND THE RIGHTS OF THE Women or ALL Nations" RELIEF SOCIETY IN CANNON WARD (SALT LAKE CITY): Sister E. B. Wells spoke briefly upon the educational benefits of the Society, morally and spiritually, and what she realized it had been to the women in these valleys, and the vast work that had been accomplished for mankind through the Society. Exhorted the young women to take hold of the work and help carry the responsibility, while there were yet some of those living who had labored so diligently in laying the foundation. President Horne gave some further instruction about the duties of the members, and told them that beside the record kept here the angels were silent notes taking, and they were recorded in heaven —KEditorial Report ELECTIONS: Let us have honorable men and women for our officers, and see to it that we get them. Don't let us shirk our duties. See that all women are registered and vote, and also attend the Primaries. Keep abreast of the times; stand for our rights and do not be daunted. —Editorial THOUGHTS On the Death of Mrs. Ellen C. Clawson Sing, dear one sing, and shout for very joy! In the world above the stars, where strife cannot annoy; The incense from this lower world, of work so nobly done. Will rise and bless thee ever, while countless ages come. —Lydia D. Alder WOMAN KNOW THYSELF: In these days when schools, seminaries, colleges and universities are being established to propagate the weal and wisdom of the world, let us not overlook a school for women wherein they can obtain a knowledge of themselves and the great scientific laws that govern their physical organization. 'This, indeed, should be made a point of every woman's education to fully qualify her for the crowning mission of life, that of motherhood. —Ellis R. Shipp, Md. CO-OPERATIVE STORE: The Woman's Co-operative Store has been removed from Main St. To the Nauvoo Building, on First South St., West, where they have commodious sales-rooms, and have also been purchasing new goods, and are very desirous of the patronage of the ladies. 'The store makes a specialty of its millinery and burial clothing. The sisters of Relief Society and others would do well to call before purchasing elsewhere, as it is hoped the store will be a success even though the times are a little hard. —KEditorial Note OUGHT WOMEN TO WANT TO VOTE? There has never been a time in all our history when the American Republic so much needed the help of all who are interested in the perpetuity of this nation; women as a part of the nation ought to feel bound to give their best thought and work to its support —Ex. Page 590 TWENTY women are Members of Parliament in India's lower House of the People. In India a bill has recently been passed _permitting wives, widows, and daughters to inherit family property and to dispose of it as they see fit. 'This is a great forward step in the area of women's rights. Many men fought the proposition, but Nehru said, "I believe that if any great advances are coming to India, they will come largely through the women of India." ERMAN women still live up to their reputation for industry. All spring and summer, grandmothers, wives, and daughters have been working in the fields. Many young women are employed as translators in business firms. Many act as guides for foreign-speaking people. As a rule their English is excellent. Then, there are teachers and journalists. A surprising number of physicians' and dentists' signs bear women's names. 'Two German women physicians are on the staff of the United States Army in Frankfurt. WHEN the medical school in Vienna first opened its doors after the war, a large number of Viennese women were among those who gained admittance.
