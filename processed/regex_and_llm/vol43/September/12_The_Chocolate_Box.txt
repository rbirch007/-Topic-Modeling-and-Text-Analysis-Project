The one with the picture of the laughing Gibson Girl on top. It was Mama's habit of economy that made the night of the chocolate box such a shining memory. It was Saturday might. Mama had put on her good broadcloth coat, her plumed hat, and her inherited fur piece. "You may go to the store with me and carry the shopping bag," she told me. We left the other six children playing cylinder records on an old music box. I can see Mama now, walking down the boardwalk of the mining town in her high-buttoned shoes. The general store, a two-story rock structure in the middle of town, stood with smoke-darkened houses huddled around it like fightened puppies. Whenever Mama entered the store, the miners would pause in their loud laughter and remove their caps. Mama was very pretty. I thought she looked like Pearl White standing there with her plumed hat held high above her slim, straight shoulders. She bought our Saturday night supplies, carefully inquiring into the price of each item. My tongue felt dry when she asked for the regular package of Kickapoo Indian worm lozenges. Mr. Graham put everything in the shopping bag and said the bill was two dollars and fifteen cents. Mama started to pay him, when she saw the box of chocolates on the shelf. It was a big box with a pink bow and gold letters. 'here was a picture of a beautiful girl on the top. Mama just kept looking at it. It seemed as if she were remembering the days before all the children, when Papa could afford to bring home candy and flowers. It seemed as if she wanted to forget for a moment, that Papa's job was long on work and short on pay and that the forty-hour week was far in the future. She kept staring at the box of chocolates. Mr. Graham said, ""That's a right nice box of candy for only a dollar." Mama stood fingering the coins in her small knitted purse. She looked around the store. I knew she was figuring how much flour or calico a dollar would buy. Suddenly, her pretty cheeks were crimson to her hairline as she saw the miners watching her. She laid the money on the counter. "Tl take the candy," she said, with a sidelong glance at my shining face. QUR feet had wings as we carried the groceries up the incline to our house. "Wait until you see what Mama's got," I called as I bounded through the front door. Out of the mesh shopping bag came the shining box. Large-eyed children whispered, "However did you get it, Mama?" It moved with us from one small town to another through Papa's years of railroading. It always went with "the careful things," like Mama's heirloom dishes.
