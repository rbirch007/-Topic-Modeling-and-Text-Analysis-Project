Little Boy Reading a Book Evelyn Dorio S there a more wonderful sight anywhere than a little boy reading a book to himself? Listen to the chuckling sound he makes! It isn't included in the musical keyboard, because no one ever captured it long enough to place it properly. It isn't quite a sharpened flat sound nor yet a flattened sharp sound; it isn't in proper fifths or ninths; yet it is a most natural sound — that reading chuckle! The reading chuckle is different from the shout of football or baseball; different from the "peanut crackling" of the Wild West matinee, or the "roof-raising ha-ha"" of television. No, indeed, the reading chuckle is rather elegant in parts, an educated sound! Close observation reveals that the reading chuckle begins with a mild "H'm, h'm," and then adds up to a slur of "h'mmmmm, h'mmmmm," as a boy keeps chasing words across the page. Then the whole chuckle may disappear except for the background of bubbles that float out of his open mouth. The boy now turns the page and opens his eyes wider the better to devour the words! "'Hey! Heyeyeyey!"" he warns the hero who is scrambling from paragraph to paragraph. The boy adds a piccolo whistle and "Yeeeeeow! Yeeeeowowowow!" This means danger to the hero, and the boy switches on a little red counterpoint beneath his voice. 'He, he! Ho, ho!" he pipes from the top of his head (where little boys always hoard their favorite squeaks!) and then a broad and yellow-colored "Ahahahah!" which blows directly out of his relaxing lips, tells you everything came out all right. Notice, next, the reading position a little boy assumes while he's reading a book to himself! It's a jackknifed and two-story-sprawl position! First the book is over his head, then his head is over the book. Or the book's forward, no, it's backward, no, it's two-way animation! Now the book's an incompleted forward pass over his ears, and then it's a straight pitch, unless it could be behind the catcher! Now he slaps down the book or maybe slaps his own tummy in the tumult! So that when he groans "Oh, chohoh!" you can't tell whether it's for his tummy or for his book! 'Then he'll surprise you by settling down to the forward look: elbows anchored, book anchored, mind anchored. But don't miss that special star-spangled face a little boy wears while he's reading a book! You can observe this best, if you speak to him. "It's bedtime, Son!" you say. But he doesn't really hear you. He may look at you, all right, but through a pair of clear and sparkling marbles, and he doesn't see you at all, even in the electric light. You feel as though you're talking to a star; as though you're disturbing a new Star. NOTHER objection: We Have an Excess of Magazines. How can we arouse this person's interest? First of all, be interested in the publications she now has in her home. Compliment her on the fine choice of reading material she has selected. 'Then let her see your Magazine. People like to see what they are buying. Be enthusiastic about it. Point out some of its fine features. For example: the attractiveness of the cover, the quality of the paper, the clear and readable print, and the limited but high type advertising it contains. All mothers want their families to enjoy the best books and magazines so they can cultivate good reading habits, and so we know she will want to include our Magazine in her collection. 605 I was talking to a young mother last week, and in the course of the conversation she told me that her son had come home from school with the assignment to bring a copy of the Preamble to the Constitution to class next morning. It was too late to get to the library; she had looked through all the current magazines she had, but was unable to find it. Suddenly she remembered attending a Relief Society class and the lesson was on the Constitution. She recalled that they all recited in unison several times the Preamble. She immediately gathered her Magazines together and in just a few minutes she found what she was looking for. She hadn't been too interested in the Magazine before, but this little incident aroused her interest enough so that she began reading the material contained in the Magazine. She was delighted at what she found, and I know that The Relief Society Magazine will have preference above the other magazine from now on in_ her home. Another objection: Why Not Have One Church Magazine? This might be a fine idea, but think how hard it would be to carry this large book to our meetings and, inasmuch as the Relief Society lessons are in the Magazine, it would be necessary to take it. Don't you think our present Magazine is a much better size? It is small enough so that it can be easily tucked into our purses, and wherever we go, we can always have good reading material at our fingertips. How privileged and blessed we are to have a Magazine of our very own, edited and published by 606 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—SEPTEMBER 1956 inspired women who bring to us the gospel ideals along with the counsel and advice we need in our daily living. Think how difficult it would be for a child to handle such a large book. If he has to thumb through several pages to find the section he is interested in, naturally the pages become soiled and possibly torn; some could even be torn out and lost, and then the magazine would lose some of its value, and, as most women take pride in keeping their Magazine clean, so it can be bound for future reference, it would lose its attractiveness and value. Another drawback would be the expense of compiling and mailing such a book. It could be possible that we would not be able to afford this type of publication. Another thing, three or four members of a family might want to use the magazine at the same time. Father might need it to get a little information for his Priesthood lesson; Johnny might need it because he must give a talk in M. I. A.; Mother would just have to have it to prepare her Relief Society lesson! You can see this could present quite a problem. Can't afford it is another objection we hear from time to time. Have we ever stopped to consider just how little the Magazine actually costs? For twelve and one-half cents a month we can have the finest reading material available delivered directly to our homes. Where else can one find such value for so little? With a little planning, we can save Our money very easily. We could obtain a small dime bank and each month place ten or fifteen cents in it, and in this way our money would be ready for our Magazine representative when she calls for our renewal. When the Magazine was first published the price of the publication was $2. This was really a lot of money to those women, but they worked and struggled to earn the price of the subscription. Today, we receive our magazine for $1.50. How wonderfully blessed we are! One sister keeps a little glass jar handy, and every time she finds a penny or a nickel on the floor, into the little jar it goes. In a short time, with little effort, and no extending of her budget, she has the money for our Magazine. We must have a goal and our goal must be high. We must labor diligently to reach it. If we are successful in reaching our goal, let us not stop, but always have the incentive to do just a bit more. We must never be satisfied, but always have the desire to climb higher and higher. Again, let us consider our calling an important one, and as we go from door to door, our first interest should be to make a friend for the Magazine and a friend for Relief Society, and always keep uppermost in our mind, that we buy our Magazine to read.
