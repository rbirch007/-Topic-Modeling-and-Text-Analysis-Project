Poetic Imagery Just as Shakespeare is useful on the common level of our normal small-talk, so he becomes indispensable whenever a larger emotion or conflict or characterization is to be communicated. 'To effect these miracles in words, Shakespeare uses imagery, composed of metaphor and simile, which are two forms of comparison. Metaphor simply says one thing is another: "Our bodies are our gardens'' (Othello I. 3. 324). Simile is a figure of speech in which like or as is always present: "His nose was as sharp as a pen" (King Henry V. II. 3.17). Here lie some of poetry's most powerful tools. The poet's most vigorous, creative impulse finds expression in metaphor, which not only gives fresh expression to his inner self, but, by his new association, he endows dead objects with life, animals with human traits. Conversely, on every hand _ his images reveal truths or likenesses which ordinarily lie dormant beneath the shallow levels on which we live. Metaphor deals in wordmagic, for it goes beyond or below our logic or reason to appeal to other realms and values in us, we know 634 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—SEPTEMBER 1956 not why or how. As L. C. Knight states in his Wheel of Fire: The Shakespeare world is not the world we see, but it is... The inner world we experience, the world we live and fear. Analyze and communicate, if you will, the following famous image from Troilus and Cressida: Time hath, my lord, a wallet at his back Wherein he puts alms for oblivion.
