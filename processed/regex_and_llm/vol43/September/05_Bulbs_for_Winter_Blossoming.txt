Bulbs for Winter Blossoming Dorthea N. Newbold Garden Editor, Deseret News - Salt Lake Telegram HE dreary days of winter can be brightened with a display of colorful, fragrant blooms of bulbs. Forced into flowering ahead of their usual blooming period, an early springtime can be enjoyed at close range by the apartment house dweller, or by the occupant of city or country home. Hyacinths, tulips, daffodils, crocuses, and paper-whites can be forced into early flowering in your window garden, providing a few basic rules are followed. For example, on daffodils you may have seen the leaves and stems form beautifully, and the flower buds begin to swell, but there the growth stopped, and the flowers never developed. This condition is termed bud blast or blasting. The main cause is lack of enough roots in the pot to enable the bulbs to carry out the full possibilities of flowering. Diseased bulbs will act in the same manner; but it is possible to buy excellent quality, disease-free bulbs. Early planting, to allow time for the proper development of a root system, is of prime importance. This period varies somewhat with each different kind of bulb. Simplest to grow are those bulbs which will flourish in pebbles and water. Possibly the best known of these varieties are the snowy paperwhites. The yellow Soleil d'Or and the white and yellow Chinese sacred lily are also good subjects for Page 586 growing in water. 'These are all classed as polyanthus daffodils, and successful flowering should be the rule, but unsatisfactory results are seen too often. First, secure firm top-size bulbs, as soon as they come on the markets. Store in a flat in a cool room until the last of October. Use containers deep enough to allow the bulbs to be set firmly in pebbles with two inches or more of space below for roots. Fill the bowls with water, room temperature, to one-half inch above the bottom of the bulbs. Thereafter keep the water level fairly constant at the base of the bulbs. You will need to add water twice a week. Add a piece of charcoal to keep the water sweet. A shaded shelf, either in a basement or utility room, where the temperature does not rise above fifty-five degrees Fahrenheit, is the best place to keep daffodils while the roots are forming. The main items to watch during the rooting period are to keep the water at the desirable level, to keep the bulbs in a dark, cool place, and to provide ventilation. _ After three weeks, use a pencil carefully to push aside the pebbles to see if the bottom of the bowl is well filled with roots. When the bow! Is full of roots, the daffodils can be brought into light. Avoid direct light until the foliage turns green. For longer lasting blooms, remove from the sunlight as the flowers open. HE tender French Roman hyacinths can also be grown in water. 'These bulbs produce blooms that are a blue, white, or rose-pink. The bell-shaped blooms are not as closely packed along the central spike as are the bells of the Dutch hyacinths, but they are a_ lovely addition to the indoor garden. Allow them to make roots for about eight weeks in the same cool, dark place you have provided for the paper-whites. The familiar Dutch hyacinths are easily grown in special hyacinth glasses, but are difficult to manage in bowls of pebbles. The hyacinth glass is quite tall, and has a cupshaped top which holds the bulb. 587 Only enough water should be used to keep it touching the base of the bulb. Refill the glass as needed. Dutch hyacinths must be stored in a cool dark place, and usually it takes about twelve weeks for the bulb to fill the lower part of the glass with roots. Then it will be three or four more weeks growing in light before the large bloom opens. To be sure that the stems and leaves are sturdy and strong, place them in strong sunlight as soon as the foliage has turned green. While there are only a few bulbs that can be brought into flower by growing in water, there are many that can be forced in soil. Regulation-type clay pots, bulb pans, and glazed pots may be used successfully to grow the bulbs. Whatever the type of container you select, the soil and method of growing the plants remain the important part. You will need two parts good top soil and one part leaf mold or compost. Mix in enough sand so that the mixture crumbles in your hand when moist. Place an arching piece of broken flower pot over the drainage hole. For drainage material, use about a one-inch layer of small pebbles. Fill in with the potting mixture to the level at which the bulbs will rest. Of course this will vary according to the size of the bulbs. After the bulbs are placed, add more soil, making it firm as you work. Leave about one inch of space at the top to allow room for watering. HE early spring bulbs, grape hyacinths, crocuses, and snowdrops can be forced into blooming indoors. Provide a moist, cold, storage place for tulips, daffodils, and hyacinth bulbs while they are filling the pots with roots. Place the pots in either a well-drained cold frame, or in a shallow trench. Dig the trench twelve inches deep and place a four-inch layer of cinders in the bottom for drainage. Place the containers in and fill in around the pots with peat moss or sharp sand. Add a covering of sharp sand, and cover with leaves or straw. C This mulch makes it easier to get the bulbs out during the winter. Twelve weeks is a safe period to allow for the root-making processes. 'Then bring the pots into the house and place in a temperature of about fifty degrees. Remove any soil which may be clinging to the pale new shoots. Water plants enough to keep the soil moist, and, as the growth increases, more water will be needed. Water should be at room temperature. As soon as the foliage has started to spread, and the stems are well started, move the plants into bright sunlight. If you can keep the room temperature at sixty degrees, you will have much better results with the bulbs you are forcing.
