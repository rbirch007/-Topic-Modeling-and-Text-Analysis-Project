EDITORIAL 9 VOL. 43 SEPTEMBER 1956 And Shey Shall Seach Sheir Children a O one can rear a child today without often despairing over the little time that the child is in the home in his waking hours, especially after he enters school. Activities of all types are continually pulling and diverting his attention from the home. Section sixty-eight of the Doctrine and Covenants gives some instructions from the Lord to Latter-day Saint parents to guide them in the proper rearing of their children. 'The commandments are focused on training children before they reach the years of accountability, emphasizing the vital import of those first eight years. By the time children are to be baptized their parents are expected to have taught them " the doctrine of repentance, faith in Christ the Son of the living God, and of baptism and the gift of the Holy Ghost by the laying on of the hands " (D. & C. 68:25). A love for his Lord and Savior should be a life-sustaining force rooted deep in a child's soul, never to perish. He should be carefully taught so that he understands that the Savior died for him and he should know the love Jesus showed in dying for him. The added knowledge of the nearness and dearness of his Heavenly Father to him at all times, as exemplified by his sending his Beloved Son into the world to die that everyone may go back to his heavenly home, may be recognized and expressed by the child in his prayers. Page 592 The soul of a child is so perfect that to enter the kingdom of heaven one must become as a little child. Childish faith, as one grows older, however, may shrivel if a mother does not continually see to it that it is constantly nourished. Family and individual prayers, family hours, reading of the scriptures together, attendance at sacrament meeting, at Sunday School, Primary, and, later, the Priesthood quorums and M. I. A., foster faith. Where a mother encourages her children's attendance she is nurturing their faith. Her own acceptance and example in supporting Church activities foster a kindred spirit in her children. Sometimes a mother fails to evaluate blessings at their true worth. 'This may be illustrated by the value placed by one mother on that great blessing to be married for time and all eternity in a temple of the Lord. She seemed to weigh eternal progression in a balance against the wearing of immodest clothing; and the clothing outweighed the eternal balance wheel. Her daughter had not been taught a true perspective, and she did not have a temple marriage. Another mother found it pleasurable to fritter away her time rather than to devote herself to the Church organizations. Her knowledge of the gospel shrank as she filled her mind with non-essentials. Another mother gave her services only to the community, which EDITORIAL crowded out and became pre-eminent over any effort on her part to build up the kingdom of God. Her daily living pattern shadowed the way for her daughters, who gradually lost interest in the ward activities and became inactive. Nevertheless, no woman is in a position to judge another. Only an all-wise Father can judge. It behooves every Latter-day Saint woman, however, to seek by loving, understanding persuasion and effort, to impress eternal values on her thoughtless sister and, at all 593 times, to set an example of righteousness in humility. The Lord must be pleased with the mother who always has time for loving attention, painstaking care, and faith-promoting unfolding of the great plan of salvation as a precious gift to her children. The early telling and living of gospel principles nurture the seeds of faith in her children and give to them sustenance until each child grows, by faith, his own tree of saving knowledge. —M. C.
