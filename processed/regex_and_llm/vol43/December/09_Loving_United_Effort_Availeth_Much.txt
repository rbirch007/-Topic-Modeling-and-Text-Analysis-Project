Simonsen [Address Delivered at the Officers Meeting of the Annual General Relief Society Conference, October 3, 1956.] stand before you today with the deepest feelings of gratitude in my heart for the blessings of the Lord unto me, and with a greater realization of these blessings, than I have ever known before in my life. Within the past week the words of one of old have gone through my mind _ repeatedly: "W hat shall I render unto the Lord for all his goodness unto me?" [| know that the most acceptable gratitude is shown in service, so I pray that throughout my life, in whatever circumstances I may be, I will be able to express my gratitude through service. You sisters here today, the leadership of Relief Society, from the amazingly widespread stakes and missions of the Church, as evidenced by the roll call just completed, are here for one purpose, that is, to equip yourselves better spiritually and intellectually, for the positions which you hold, that you may be able to serve your Father in heaven better by teaching his gospel to his daughters and in helping to establish his kingdom here upon the earth. General Relief Society conference is always a time of rejoicing. It is always a spiritual feast. Why? Because you sisters come with this singleness of purpose, united in faith and in testimony. It is a Page 814 spiritual feast because we receive instructions from our general Relief Society officers, counsel from the Priesthood through the General Authorities of the Church, and, above all else, because the spirit of our Father in heaven is with us here. This conference of October 1956 is of special significance to us. It witnesses the fulfillment of a dream which has persisted in the heart of Relief Society for many, many years —the dream of having a functional and beautiful building of its own. The dream began early in Church history, even in Nauvoo, and continued with the sisters through their forced move from the beautiful city of Nauvoo to the uninviting West, where the sisters continued their work of benevolence, unselfish sharing, and effective service in the Lord's work. And the dream to have their own building, like a golden thread, ran through all activities, as is shown by the minutes of the general Relief Society conference held October 3, 1896. Let me read you an excerpt from these minutes: President Zina D. Young, in her remarks, opened out upon the subject of a house for the Relief Society, to be owned by them. Their plans called for kitchens, pantries, banqueting hall, cooking utensils, a furnace and closets and a room for the janitor, and offices upstairs, "with a library and bureau of information, that will be in the best sense educational, and where lectures may be given by distinguished professors in all branches of science, art and literature, and on general topics." UR building, which is now completed, more than fulfills the dreams of all of us. As you view this magnificent building, both inside and out, you will realize the great amount of hard work, trained professional help, and loving and prayerful planning that have gone into the building. You will know that the inspiration of the Lord has 815 been over it all. It was designed and will be equipped to meet the many and varied services which Relief Society offers to the Church membership—and it is beautiful. This building not only meets the specific needs of a great and growing organization divinely inspired; it represents the individuality and love of the Relief Society women throughout the world, through the contributions they have made for its construction and the many decorative and useful pieces which are the gifts of individuals, and stake and mission Relief Societies. 'The Lord knows, and we all know, that many of these contributions were made through sacrifice, but all were made freely and through love. A sister living a great distance from Salt Lake City said to President Spafford, "At first I thought, why, in my lifetime I have given many gifts which I never expect to use myself, and now it gives me ereat joy to know that I have a share in that beautiful building." The first day the building was open for inspection two little sisters stood at the entrance to the lounge room, their eyes shining in admiration. One turned to the other and said, "We didn't know our five dollars: would buy so much, did we?" Surely loving, united effort availeth much. This building is, indeed, a dream fulfilled. Page 816 JITORIAL C VOL. 43 DECEMBER 1956 NO. 12 A Dedicated Home for Relief Society T began as a dream, or even before the dream took form, it was a longing deep-planted in woman's heart, to have a home, to have one spot designated particularly and set apart around which, as a warming fire, Relief Society workers might gather. It was an insistent urge companioning Latter-day Saint women as they were driven from their own hearthsides again and again. And now at last on the third day of October, one thousand, nine hundred and fifty-six, the seeding and garnering are over, and the harvest has fulfilled the dream. It was the practice in early days, for the sisters to provide their own meeting places in the wards. In 1897 President Zina D. H. Young said "she was pleased with the exertions of the sisters in getting houses to meet in." Relief Society Halls—so named—provided homes for Relief Societies in metropolitan centers, in verdant valleys, and in bleak, sparsely settled communities. The sisters had Relief Society homes and, in addition, through obedience to the voice of their prophets, they had granaries guarding life-sustaining wheat. The practice changed. Relief Society Halls were abandoned as room for Relief Society work was provided in meetinghouses. Individual granaries, not always satisfactory storage places, were emptied, and later great Church elevators were filled as the result of thousands of small loads of Relief Society wheat. But while ward Relief Societies were provided with meeting places, what of the Central Board of Relief Society? They had no place of their own in which to meet; they had to borrow a meeting room; and there was no place for the preservation of general Relief Society records. And so the dream of one functional and beautiful home in which the work of Relief Society throughout the world might be conceived and from which it might be directed was born, and is today a reality. A home of the heart of Relief Society of which every Relief Society member is a part. A home containing the loveliest offerings of the world-wide sisterhood. Unitedly, through sacrifice, and with the assistance of the Church, The Relief Society Building stands an ensign to women. In the present and future years this symbol of the devotion of Relief Society women and of _ their lofty destiny in the gospel plan will speak to the sojourners from other countries and remote civilizations: "We, the members of Relief Society of the restored Church of Christ, built this home through sacrifice with the help and under the guidance of the Priesthood of God. No other women in all the world are blessed as are we. Relief Society Page 817 818 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1956 came into being through the inspiration of the Lord to his Prophet Joseph Smith in 1842. Another prophet im 1956 dedicated this our home to the Lord. We are continually guided and sustained by his servants. This Building is magnificent, but its beauty has been fashioned as that of the virtuous woman of Solomon's Proverb. 'Those who labor in Relief Society do so in a spirit of service, of deep humility, thankfulness, and dedication. We are nurtured through the compassion we extend to others. We follow our grand key words, "Said Jesus, "Ye shall do the work which ye see me do.'" Arrogance in possessions has no place in our home. The 'offering reflects the refinement, graciousness, intelligence, and strength of faith possessed by the early sisters, exemplified in Lucy Mack Smith, by the staunch and true pioneer member, and the member of today and tomorrow, the bedrock of whose character has remained the same — selfishness, compassion, charity, and love. Relief Society today through humility and service and with the help of the Lord, will earn the blessing invoked for the Building by President David O. McKay in his glorious Dedicatory Prayer when he asked "May the building and sursoundings radiate a hallowed influence even to passersby, as perfume is wafted from sweet-scented flowers." From this spirit will blossom a rededication to service for the poor both in spirit and means; for the needy, for the afflicted, and sorrowing. Thus the Building may and must be hallowed. —M. C. S.
