GAIN, at the One Hundred Twenty - Seventh Semi - Annual Conference of the Church, held in the historic Tabernacle on 'Temple Square on October 5, 6, and 7, 1956, the saints received instruction, inspiration, and a renewal of their faith. Truth seekers the world over were invited to learn of the restored gospel and its eternal message. That Jesus is the Christ, the power of The Book of Mormon as a witness for Jesus the Chnist, the importance and effectiveness of humility and faith, ways of perfecting the saints, reverence in houses of worship, the Word of Wisdom, tithing, chastity, kindness, patience, charity, honesty, peace for the individual and for the world, and obedience to all the principles and ordinances of the gospel, were teachings given special emphasis. President David O. McKay presided and conducted all mectings. The general sessions were broadcast by radio and television over a wide EDITORIAL network in the Western States, and the Saturday evening Priesthood meeting proceedings were carried on a closed circuit to some eighty assemblages of the Priesthood gathered in chapels throughout Utah and in many other parts of the United States. All members of the General Authorities spoke during the conference except Elder 'Thomas E.. McKay, Assistant to the Council of the Twelve, who, however, was well enough to attend some of the meetings, and Elder Milton R. Hunter of the First Council of the Seventy, who was confined to his home as a result of a recent illness. N his opening address, President McKay reviewed the material progress of the Church, the increase in membership and _ activities, the many new chapels, the great expenditure of means and effort, all directed towards "perfecting the soul." President McKay then asked: "Have we progressed spiritually as well as in these physical and teaching organizations?" He defined spirituality as: Every noble impulse, every unselfish expression of love, every brave suffering for the right; every surrender of self to something higher than self; every loyalty to an ideal; every unselfish devotion to principle; every helpfulness to humanity; every act of self-control; every fine courage of the soul, undefeated by pretense or policy, but by being, doing, and living the good for the very good's sake — that is spirituality. President Stephen L_ Richards spoke of the request from "a distant foreign country" for a statement of the beliefs and purposes of the Church. <A reply was submitted, which included a copy of the thir- 819 teen Articles of Faith, and this statement of the objectives of the Church: The objectives of the Church are to bear witness to the divinity of the Lord Jesus Christ; to teach all men the principles of his Gospel; and to convert and persuade them to follow in his ways and keep the commandments of God, that they may thus advance the Kingdom of God in the earth, to bring brotherhood and peace to men and nations, and earn exaltation for themselves. A warning against the influence of doctrines and cults not in_harmony with the gospel of Jesus Christ was expressed by President J. Reuben Clark, Jr. He spoke of the "Olivet Discourse" of the Savior and its divine implications. President Clark summarized his testimony of the gospel in words of strength and simplicity: Jesus did live. First, there was the great plan in heaven; that did actually occur. The earth was formed, Adam came, the human family followed. We came here to prove ourselves. Finally, Christ was born in the meridian of time. He lived. He taught. He gave instructions. He was crucified. 'Then on the morning of the third day, he was resurrected, thus bringing to each and every one of us the blessings of the resurrection. As members of Relief Society, we feel especially grateful and uplifted by the conference messages which gave inspiration and direction for us in our homemaking activities, in our responsibilities as wives and mothers, and as workers in the auxiliaries of the Church. Thou didst not lay these things to the heart, neither didst remember the latter end of it " (Isaiah 47:7). Be Honest with Yourself ANY women are grieved because do not consider themselves beautiful. When such thoughts come to us, we may be looking at only one facet of the many-sided gem which is our life. For beauty, through the ages, has been a changing value, depending upon the transient preferences of individuals and of groups. It sometimes happens that those who were not thought of as lovely to look upon in their youth, become more beautiful with each passing year, and their thoughts and their attitudes are reflected in radiance on their faces. Some women are as beautiful in their maturity as is the autumn, which must inevitably follow the blossoms of summer. And the testament written upon the faces of the aged often reveals gentleness and courage, enthey durance and serenity, while the youthful face must await the sculpturing of time to give it the lineaments of completed harmony. We must accept our earthly bodies as they have come to us through our inheritance, rejoicing if we are strong and well, and seeking to overcome our incapacities. There is much that can be done to maintain health, to be neat and clean, to wear appropriate and becoming clothing, perhaps to gild and polish. But even when all this is done, we still need the comfort and the wisdom of the saying: "Beauty is as beauty does." Perhaps it has taken some of us many years to realize the enduring verity of this proverb. Gratitude for our own particular gifts is said to be the beginning of wisdom, for truly our Heavenly Father has bestowed a multitude of gifts upon his daughters, and the choicest of these are the talents and abilities which require effort, practice, experience for their full fruition. They are the lovely "provisional" gifts that may be infinitely added upon, that may be polished to a shining luster — music, painting, poetry, sculpture; the particularly feminine gifts of homemaking, the "graces beyond the reach of art"; and those most precious of all talents of the uplifted spirit, which, forgetting self, can direct its energies toward those works of charity and benevolence that are forever beautiful.
