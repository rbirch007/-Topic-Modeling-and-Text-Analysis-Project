I. 1. 196-200 Save for Shakespeare's addition to the nurse and his new emphasis and characterization on some figures, he lifted the entire substance of the plot directly from Arthur Broke's poem, The Tragical Historie of Romeo and Juliet, published in 1562, itself deeply indebted to Bandell, an Italian novelist. But while in his source many weeks of wooing precede the wedding, 'Tybalt is not slain until several months later, and Romeo's exile lasts from Easter through September, Shakespeare achieves his power of impact by condensing all these events into three or four days. It was by thus rearranging this plot and people that Shakespeare first created a wellknit plot which enabled "live" characters to create themselves into reality. This plot has a jewel precision which, through its superb craftsmanship, itself gives pleasure. The chief fault of the play is the posing artificiality of Romeo in the early scenes, and such long dry runs as Friar Laurence's long discourse on herbs (II. 3) or Lady Capulet's rather artificial comparison of Paris to a book (I. 3). Plot The play begins in the streets of Verona, where the flashing swords and malice of Tybalt once more enflame the ancient vendetta feud between the two wealthy houses of Montague and Capulet. The Montague Romeo loves Rosaline, but when he masks and attends the Capulet ball, he instantly loves Juliet, their only daughter. After the ball he secretly enters her garden to hear her vowing her love for him. Out of the shadows he replies, and they agree to be married secretly the next day by Friar Laurence. Soon after their marriage, Romeo meets 'Tybalt, now his relative by marriage. Romeo cannot now dare to defend his honor when Tybalt insults him, so Mercutio, his gallant friend, fights Tybalt and is slain. Seeing his dearest friend killed, Romeo slays Tybalt and is banished from the city for life. Meanwhile, unaware that their daughter is already a wife, the Capulets arrange for Juliet to marry Paris, a wealthy, older suitor. Desperately frightened by his ardor and their approaching marriage, Juliet appeals to Friar Laurence for help, after both her nurse and her mother advise the marriage. 'The priest gives her a potion to drink which will make her appear dead for fortytwo hours. After she is placed in the family vault, Friar Laurence expects to summon Romeo from Mantua to the Capulet family tomb where he is to steal away his drugged bride. But fate prevents the messenger from finding Romeo in Mantua; instead, Romeo learns only that his bride is dead. After hastily buying poison he returns to her tomb, where he is discovered by Juliet's promised husband, Paris. Tragedy vs. Pathos In Romeo and Juliet Shakespeare reveals how heavily he is still influenced by the medieval concept of tragedy, which held that mortals come to disastrous ends because an irresistible Fate has thus decreed. (See "Ordered Universe" Lesson 2.) The Prologue to Act I tells how: A pair of star-crossed lovers take their life, Whose misadventured piteous overthrows Do with their death bury their parents' strife. Prologue 6-8 More than fifteen passages within the play constantly remind us that their love is "death-market" that "froward fortune," "fortune's cruel will" and "false fortune" have predetermined the disastrous end of their love before it began. When, at their first impassioned meeting, Juliet seeks to know the identity of her lover, she says: Go ask his name. If he be married, My grave ls like to be my wedding bed. But such a definition of tragedy is no nearer tragedy than is the play itself. In his Poetics, Aristotle defines tragedy as "a form of drama exciting the emotions of pity and fear. Its action should be single and complete, presenting a reversal of fortune, involving persons _ renowned and of superior attainments." 'Tragedy has always involved man's own inner struggle and a conflict of will. 'Tragedy involves a disastrous end to some great person through his own inner weaknesses. In the highest tragedy, defects may be either of will or moral strength. Romeo and Juliet, therefore, falls short of containing high tragedy, since its disastrous end is produced, not by inner struggle or weakness within the characters, but by a series of unfortunate coincidences. It was just bad luck which brought together Mercutio and Tybalt, as it was bad luck that the messenger to Romeo was sealed up in a house filled with plague. And if only Friar Laurence could have arrived at the tomb one minute before Romeo drank the poison! 'These are ele- 848 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1956 ments of chance, not of tragedy; therefore, they comprise a level of secondary importance within the play. The elements of tragedy, though present, are here dominated by pathos, or the quality of awakening sympathy, pity, and sorrow. Never do we feel a fear resulting from any conflict within anyone. We are carried along on the same currents of hope and despair which alternately carry the lovers to heights and depths—indeed one of the play's strongest appeals is that Shakespeare enables his audience to become Romeo and Juliet. The dominant reality throughout the play is the magnificent irresistibility of pure love. In the suddenness of their passion lies the weakness from which all disaster stems. So suddenly are they met and mated, so impulsively do they disregard all lesser reality than their great love, that suffering is bound to follow. Even Juliet, for a moment, is distressed by their union which is:... Too rash, too unadvised, too sudden, Too like the lightning, which doth cease to be Ere one can say "It lightens." II. 2. 118-120 Father Laurence, in his maturity, sees the impassioned lovers as they themselves cannot. The sweetest honey Is loathsome in his own deliciousness, And in the taste confounds the appetite. Therefore, love moderately, long love doth so, Too swift arrives as tardy as too slow. In our concern over details of plot, definition, and weakness, we must never forget that, despite all, for those of all ages in love with love, this is their best-loved Shakespearean play (for such persons, greatness is not the basis of judgment). In fairness to the play, as a whole, we must then proceed to point out three of its triumphs. 1. Triumph Over Falseness Just as the great theme of the play is the triumph of love, so Shakespeare has filled almost every scene with love's imagery and music. In his complete fairness, Shakespeare's all-seeing eye has defined love as seen from many points of view. Thus through Mercutio's eyes love is "'sex'' and a clever game; for the nurse, love is earthly, bawdy, and a practical means of ensuring property and position; for Juliet's parents, love is a rash, unnecessary pain which, in no way, should be the concern of their daughter, particularly since her father is so eminently capable of choosing for her a husband who can guarantee her prestige, wealth, security. Furthermore, her father himself was a "mouse-hunter' (modern: wolf) in his younger years. None of these understand the world into which Romeo and Juliet have entered, since, in each case, their definition of love is partial or false. 'The lovers are most nearly able to share themselves with Friar Laurence, yet, even he, fills his first speeches with erotic symbols. 'Thus, by all those nearest them, they are tempted to love partially, or for the wrong reasons. 'The great glory of the LESSON DEPARTMENT play is that never do they yield to these pressures. Through both their words and the shape of the entire play, love is cut off from the usual world with its usual definitions of love, and exalted into its own high realm where love speaks of love and thus creates itself. All attempts to debase love into something less than holy are thwarted. Triumph Over Flatness When most of us feel the oc- 849 casional need to break our self-imposed chains and express emotion for a loved one, we smile, hold hands, send flowers or a commercial sentiment, or even say, "I love you." Largely we feel only, because we cannot say. Romeo and Juliet provides invincible proof that great love is created and sustained by using the tongue to capture and enthrall those more direct avenues to the soul: the eye and the ear. As Hazelton Spencer has phrased it so well, "Romeo and Juliet is made of music." It is a_ lyrical hymn to love which bestows exciting magic upon its devotees. Triumph Over Death How much stronger is our conviction that love is good, when we know those involved, as now we know Romeo and Juliet. From one practical viewpoint their love destroyed them. But then we have a right to ask, is that the reason they live forever in the hearts of mankind? We grieve as we read the final act of the play, yet, despite the grip of pain and sorrow upon us, still there glows within us a satisfying warmth at the memory of so great a love which once was. And ever shall be. When Romeo breaks into the Capulet vault to discover Juliet dead, he pictures Death as his rival lover: Ah, dear Juliet, Why art thou yet so fair? Shall I believe That unsubstantial death is amorous, And that the lean abhorred monster keeps Thee here in dark to be _ his paramour? For fear of that, I still will stay with thee, LESSON DEPARTMENT And never from this palace of dim night Depart again. V. 3. 101-108 Thus Shakespeare brings before our eyes his belief that a love made great in life need not end at death. Thus, in his deep wisdom and keen seeing, in his lyrical beauty and his entire fairness, he allows us to see the mysteries of love through his 851 eyes, that we might know greater happiness and security upon returning to ourselves.
