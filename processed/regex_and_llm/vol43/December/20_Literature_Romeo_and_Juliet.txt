S common as it is true, is the old saying, "All the world loves a lover." And no lovers have been more widely known and loved than Romeo and Juliet, surely because of all lovers in literature theirs is the pure love which each of us desires to possess and experience personally and ideally. By the very fact, then, that from its first presentation Romeo and Juliet has been one of Shakespeare's most popular _ triumphs, we may justly assume that in its words and motions we find embodied our own "best" loves, but loves condensed, purified, and exalted through the genius of the poet Shakespeare. Only those who have been initiated know the truth of love's logic as stated in the first lines of his sonnet 138: When my love swears that she is made of truth, I do believe her, though I know she lies. I. 1. 196-200 Save for Shakespeare's addition to the nurse and his new emphasis and characterization on some figures, he lifted the entire substance of the plot directly from Arthur Broke's poem, The Tragical Historie of Romeo and Juliet, published in 1562, itself deeply indebted to Bandell, an Italian novelist. But while in his source many weeks of wooing precede the wedding, 'Tybalt is not slain until several months later, and Romeo's exile lasts from Easter through September, Shakespeare achieves his power of impact by condensing all these events into three or four days. It was by thus rearranging this plot and people that Shakespeare first created a wellknit plot which enabled "live" characters to create themselves into reality. This plot has a jewel precision which, through its superb craftsmanship, itself gives pleasure. The chief fault of the play is the posing artificiality of Romeo in the early scenes, and such long dry runs as Friar Laurence's long discourse on herbs (II. 3) or Lady Capulet's rather artificial comparison of Paris to a book (I. 3). Plot The play begins in the streets of Verona, where the flashing swords and malice of Tybalt once more enflame the ancient vendetta feud between the two wealthy houses of Montague and Capulet. Immediately sensing the danger, Prince Escalus decrees death as the punishment to the next person who RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—1956 starts a street-brawl. The Montague Romeo loves Rosaline, but when he masks and attends the Capulet ball, he instantly loves Juliet, their only daughter. After the ball he secretly enters her garden to hear her vowing her love for him. Out of the shadows he replies, and they agree to be married secretly the next day by Friar Laurence. Soon after their marriage, Romeo meets 'Tybalt, now his relative by marriage. Romeo cannot now dare to defend his honor when Tybalt insults him, so Mercutio, his gallant friend, fights Tybalt and is slain. Seeing his dearest friend killed, Romeo slays Tybalt and is banished from the city for life. Meanwhile, unaware that their daughter is already a wife, the Capulets arrange for Juliet to marry Paris, a wealthy, older suitor. Desperately frightened by his ardor and their approaching marriage, Juliet appeals to Friar Laurence for help, after both her nurse and her mother advise the marriage. 'The priest gives her a potion to drink which will make her appear dead for fortytwo hours. After she is placed in the family vault, Friar Laurence expects to summon Romeo from Mantua to the Capulet family tomb where he is to steal away his drugged bride. But fate prevents the messenger from finding Romeo in Mantua; instead, Romeo learns only that his bride is dead. After hastily buying poison he returns to her tomb, where he is discovered by Juliet's promised husband, Paris. In their encounter Romeo kills Paris, sees Juliet apparently dead, then drinks the poison as he dies in her arms.
