Social Science—Latter-day Saint Family Life Lesson 5—"Unto the Least of These" Elder John Farr Larson For Tuesday, March 26, 1957 Objective: To show that salvation is the ultimate aim in all service to the needy. THE Prophet Joseph Smith clearly enunciated a philosophy of dealing with those unfortunate persons who find themselves in trouble or wanting the essentials of life. His teachings, based upon and consistent with those of Christ, nevertheless were somewhat strange and different for his day. 'They stressed the importance of the individual and his progression, salvation, and exaltation. He urged the sisters to develop certain characteristics within themselves which are now recognized as important in dealing with people who have problems. Basically, the Gospel Plan was taught. He emphasized the importance of mercy, understanding, and acceptance of the human weakness, as well as the necessity of being forgiving and refraining from judgment. He observed, time and again, that the worth of souls is great in the eyes of God. Everything he suggested was calculated to improve the individual. The basic objective of his teachings, that of individual salvation, is a guiding principle in all service. It is applicable whether the service be to the sick, the poor, the bereaved, the troubled, the discouraged, or the spiritually weak. The Prophet's teachings were specific and practical. He seemed to reject the philosophy of the day, 1.¢., that the "poor" were basically criminal in nature, and his emphasis on the individual was at variance with the idea that all should be treated alike. His charge to "seek out objects of charity" represented a change from the desire of most people to avoid responsibility for the poor. Conditions of want and the pinch of poverty were not unfamiliar to the Prophet. His mother describes instances when the family had few of the essentials. The Prophet knew 852 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1956 what it was to be without a home and without food. 'That he had been concerned with the poor prior to the organization of the Relief Society is indicated in his annals. The general conditions of the poor and the treatment of persons involved in crime were undoubtedly known to him. As an astute observer, he had a knowledge of the programs dealing with these matters. His experiences and knowledge, together with his understanding of people, and the inspiration which was his, magnificently equipped him to recognize the errors and injustices and to give advice to correct this service to others. Early Welfare Philosophy in the United States The early philosophy and statutes relating to the poor were based upon the English Poor Relief Society Act of 1601. 'The treatment of the poor by local communities was basic to this system. As a result, needs were met by a makeshift pattern, entirely lacking in uniformity. 'The principal concern of the law was to prevent nonresident indigents from obtaining residence, and provision was made for their removal from the community, as criminals. Many indigents were forced from one community to another, particularly the mentally impaired and the _ chronically ill. The "overseers of the poor' had the power to remove dependent children from their parents and "indenture" them to an employer who would support them in return for their labor. Often this became virtual slavery. In the administration of the law little concern was given to individual differences in need, the attitude being that all should be treated alike. Josiah Quincy, a contemporary of the Prophet, in reporting on the Pauper Laws of Massachusetts, wrote of providing for the poor by letting them out to the lowest bidder. He inserted in his report the following statement which illustrates this method of care:... The average expense of supporting adults and children is about one dollar and thirty cents per head per week; this we consider a large sum, but the poor being sometimes boarded with those who are in want themselves, it is not lost to the town (Massachusetts, General Court, Committee on Pauper Laws, Report, 1821). In the early part of the 19th Century there was a trend towards caring for the poor in almshouses, particularly in large cities. 'These institutions were generally overcrowded, poorly ventilated hovels, containing the human driftwood of the time, unsegregated as to sex or physical condition. 'They were crowded with the mentally ill, the sick of all kinds, the aged, and children. Medical care, if available at all, was available on an emergency basis. 'The food was meager and the clothing was inadequate. From 1837 to 1843 the United States was in a severe economic depression. During this period there was extreme suffering. Horace Greeley wrote in the "New Yorker," on January 20, 1838, that approximately one-third of the 200,000 wage earners in New York City were unemployed. In speaking of their plight he wrote: Some of these subsist on the scanty earnings of former days, some are aided by relatives or personal friends, and some manage to avoid starvation with the few LESSON DEPARTMENT shillings which partial and precarious employment or still more uncertain beggary afford them; but there are still thousands... Who are in utter and hopeless distress, with no means of surviving the winter but those afforded by the charity of their fellow citizens. It was in this period, following a meeting of 5000 indigent persons who listened to inciting speeches, that the "flour riots" broke out, in which hungry people broke into flour storage facilities in New York. This added confirmation to the popular belief that the poor were also criminal. "Unbending harshness" best described the criminal code of that time. There was no "individualized justice" as we know it today. Extenuating circumstances rarely were considered by the Courts. 'The concept of punishment as a deterrent to crime gave birth to the Pennsylvania and Auburn Systems of Prisons. Under the former, prisoners were separately confined throughout their term, while under the Auburn System complete solitary was had during the night and silence during the day. 'The failure of these systems later brought penal reforms. The Individual Recognized Our present enlightened attitude towards social problems developed slowly. Along the road there were valiant pioneers, with Joseph Smith in the vanguard. His teachings on this subject were, in reality, refinements and specific applications of the Gospel Plan. Because of their focus on the individual and _ his spiritual, physical, social, and economic betterment, they stand out as an early unequivocal step in the development of programs and philosophy of human betterment. In 853 1842 John Augustus (1784-1859), a Boston shoemaker, was just beginning his work of reforming convicted criminals. After observing the futility of fines and imprisonment in reformation, he asked and was given the opportunity of working personally with those who were considered hopeless. His success in the middle of the 19th Century founded the professional service of Probation. In New York in 1843, the Association for Improving the Condition of the Poor was organized to urge relief on a personal basis with emphasis on moral rather than material aid. The contributions of Jane Addams (1860-1935) and Mary Richmond (1861-1928) will not soon be forgotten. Jane Addams will be rembered as the founder of "Hull House." She was a crusader, a philosopher who had as her objective a better life for all people everywhere. Mary Richmond was a technician, the pioneer in bringing together a body of principles for use by professional social workers. Each of these contributions brought to light the favorable potentialities of the individual. 'The work of each of these pioneers, Joseph Smith, Jane Addams, John Augustus, and Mary Richmond, was characterized by the following: (1) an awareness of the existence of unsavory conditions; (2) a determination to correct; and (3) sufficient initiative to take the necessary corrective measures. Herein lies a "key" to social progress. The Prophet's Teachings The concluding statement of the Prophet Joseph after he turned the key at the organization of Relief Society was: 854 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1956 this is the beginning of better days to the poor and needy, who shall be made to rejoice and pour forth blessings on your heads (D. H. C. IV, page 607). In giving a general direction to the women of the Relief Society, the Prophet made this statement: Said Jesus, "Ye shall do the work which ye see me do." 'These are the grand keywords for the society to act upon (D. H. C. V, page 20). Nowhere could be found a better foundation for this work than in the teachings of Jesus. 'The forgiving, understanding attitude of Christ, and the lessons he taught, by parables and by his actions, provide a philosophy of dealing with others, unparalleled in history. Its effectiveness has been shown time and again. The objectives of the Relief Society, as previously discussed, made it clear that service to the unfortunate is of major importance to the organization. In one of his talks to the Relief Society, Joseph Smith laid down a fundamental axiom: The best measure or principle to bring the poor to repentance is to administer to their wants. The Ladies' Relief Society is not only to relieve the poor, but to save souls (Ibid., V, pages 24-25). This admonition implies more than a concern for the physical wants — it suggests a higher objective, that of individual salvation. We lift the soul of man by administering to his wants, material and spiritual. 'The first step is that of meeting the basic needs of food, clothing, and shelter. 'This is seen as a simple process, but an important one. 'The way these needs are met often determines whether or not further needs can be met. Some persons in need are difficult to find. They are often too proud, or too ashamed, to admit a need. Consequently, the objective of the Relief Society in "searching after objects of charity and in administering to their wants" becomes meaningful. In this connection an excerpt from the Handbook of the Relief Society, will be of interest: At the second meeting of the Society, on March 24, 1842, the President, Emma Smith, suggested the propriety of appointing persons who might wait upon the poor. No action in the matter was taken, however, until the next year, cases of need meanwhile being reported by anyone discovering them. On July 28, 1843, a committee of sixteen was appointed "'to search out the poor and suffering, to call upon the rich for aid, and thus, as far as possible, relieve the wants of all." They were told that the spirit of the Lord would bless them in their labors and stimulate the hearts of the well-to-do (Handbook of Relief Society 1931, page 29). Thus, the "Necessity Committee" became the forerunner of the visiting teachers. Visiting teachers, 60,639 of them, in 1954 made a total of 2,634,185 visits. It is important for them to continue to "search out the poor" so that needs may be met through the program of the Church. 'The Prophet observed at one meeting that the women had now been placed "in a situation in which you can act according to those sympathies which God has planted in your bosoms" (D. H. C. IV, page 605). 'These, he said, were feelings of charity and benevolence. The Prophet taught that women must be merciful to one another and overlook small things. He spoke of being "armed with mercy" (D. H. C. V, page 19). On another occasion he put it this way: As you increase in innocence and virtue, LESSON DEPARTMENT as you increase in goodness, let your hearts expand, let them be enlarged towards others; you must. Be long-suffering, and bear with the faults and errors of mankind. How precious are the souls of men! The female part of the community are apt to be contracted in their views. You must not be contracted, but you must be liberal in your feelings (D. H. C. IV, page 606). The Prophet always stressed the importance of personal influence on others to deter wrongdoing. In one Relief Society meeting he said: It is the object of this society to reform persons, not to take those that are corrupt and foster them in their wickedness; but if they repent, we are bound to take them, and by kindness sanctify and cleanse them from all unrighteousness by our influence in watching over them Nothing is so much calculated to lead people to forsake sin as to take them by the hand, and watch over them with tenderness. When persons manifest the least kindness and love to me, O what power it has over my mind, while the opposite course has a tendency to harrow up all the harsh feelings and depress the human mind (D. H. C. V, pp. 23-24). Example of Service Service to others, in the Prophet's life, was a daily reality. His mother describes one occasion in the spring of 1839: As the season advanced, the brethren began to feel the effects of the hardships which they had endured, and also the unhealthiness of the climate in which we were then situated. 'They came down with the agues and bilious fevers to such an extent that there were whole families in which not one was able to help himself to a drink of cold water.... Joseph and Emma, seeing the distress, commenced taking the sick into their own house, with the view of taking care of them and making them more comfortable. Teachings Applied The teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith created a surge of brotherly love and kindness that has characterized the Church and the Relief Society from that time to the present. 'The story of service within the Relief Society is a thrilling one. Each chapter has resulted from an application of the wise counsel of the first President of the Church. Instances of service to individuals are largely unwritten because of their personal nature. 'They are nonetheless a part of the Relief Society story. 'The women of the Church, through their organization, have constantly applied the teachings of the Prophet. At their first meeting they commenced to raise funds to care for the poor and to "search out objects of charity." They participated in disaster relief in San Francisco following the 1906 earthquake; famine relief to China in 1907; and during both world wars aided and assisted those needing their help. Since the establishment of the Church Welfare Plan they have continued their service by aiding the Priesthood in effectuating the purposes of the Church Welfare Plan. The leadership of the Relief Society has brought stimulation to the field of social services in nurse recruitment and training programs, the founding of hospitals, from its operation of the Relief Society Home, through the operation of the Social Service and Child Welfare Department. In addition to this they have encouraged social work education and training through LESSON DEPARTMENT scholarships and loans to deserving students. (See pagel of Relief Society, pp. 22, 27, 28, 30, 31, 41, 42, 45-49-) Spiritual Development The Church Welfare Program has provided Relief Society members with endless opportunities for service. 'This service can only be fully effective if the sisters go about their work with the approach suggested by the comments of President David O. McKay, made in General Conference on October 3, 1936. In speaking of the Church Welfare Program he said: The development of our spiritual nature should concern us most. Spirituality is the highest acquisition of the soul, the divine in man; "the supreme, crowning gift that makes him king of all created things."" It is the consciousness of victory over self and of communion with the infinite. It is spirituality alone which really gives one the best in life (107th SemiAnnual Conference, with Report of Discourses, page 103). Supplementary References 1.
