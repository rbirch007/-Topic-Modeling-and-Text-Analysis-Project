Social Science—Latter-day Saint Family Life Lesson 5—"Unto the Least of These" Elder John Farr Larson For Tuesday, March 26, 1957 Objective: To show that salvation is the ultimate aim in all service to the needy. THE Prophet Joseph Smith clearly enunciated a philosophy of dealing with those unfortunate persons who find themselves in trouble or wanting the essentials of life. His teachings, based upon and consistent with those of Christ, nevertheless were somewhat strange and different for his day. 'They stressed the importance of the individual and his progression, salvation, and exaltation. He urged the sisters to develop certain characteristics within themselves which are now recognized as important in dealing with people who have problems. Basically, the Gospel Plan was taught. He emphasized the importance of mercy, understanding, and acceptance of the human weakness, as well as the necessity of being forgiving and refraining from judgment. He observed, time and again, that the worth of souls is great in the eyes of God. Everything he suggested was calculated to improve the individual. The basic objective of his teachings, that of individual salvation, is a guiding principle in all service. It is applicable whether the service be to the sick, the poor, the bereaved, the troubled, the discouraged, or the spiritually weak. The Prophet's teachings were specific and practical. He seemed to reject the philosophy of the day, 1.¢., that the "poor" were basically criminal in nature, and his emphasis on the individual was at variance with the idea that all should be treated alike. His charge to "seek out objects of charity" represented a change from the desire of most people to avoid responsibility for the poor. Conditions of want and the pinch of poverty were not unfamiliar to the Prophet. His mother describes instances when the family had few of the essentials. 'That he had been concerned with the poor prior to the organization of the Relief Society is indicated in his annals. The general conditions of the poor and the treatment of persons involved in crime were undoubtedly known to him. As an astute observer, he had a knowledge of the programs dealing with these matters. His experiences and knowledge, together with his understanding of people, and the inspiration which was his, magnificently equipped him to recognize the errors and injustices and to give advice to correct this service to others. Early Welfare Philosophy in the United States The early philosophy and statutes relating to the poor were based upon the English Poor Relief Society Act of 1601. 'The treatment of the poor by local communities was basic to this system. As a result, needs were met by a makeshift pattern, entirely lacking in uniformity. 'The principal concern of the law was to prevent nonresident indigents from obtaining residence, and provision was made for their removal from the community, as criminals. Many indigents were forced from one community to another, particularly the mentally impaired and the _ chronically ill. The "overseers of the poor' had the power to remove dependent children from their parents and "indenture" them to an employer who would support them in return for their labor. Often this became virtual slavery. In the administration of the law little concern was given to individual differences in need, the attitude being that all should be treated alike. Josiah Quincy, a contemporary of the Prophet, in reporting on the Pauper Laws of Massachusetts, wrote of providing for the poor by letting them out to the lowest bidder. He inserted in his report the following statement which illustrates this method of care:... The average expense of supporting adults and children is about one dollar and thirty cents per head per week; this we consider a large sum, but the poor being sometimes boarded with those who are in want themselves, it is not lost to the town (Massachusetts, General Court, Committee on Pauper Laws, Report, 1821). In the early part of the 19th Century there was a trend towards caring for the poor in almshouses, particularly in large cities. 'These institutions were generally overcrowded, poorly ventilated hovels, containing the human driftwood of the time, unsegregated as to sex or physical condition. 'They were crowded with the mentally ill, the sick of all kinds, the aged, and children. Medical care, if available at all, was available on an emergency basis. 'The food was meager and the clothing was inadequate. From 1837 to 1843 the United States was in a severe economic depression. During this period there was extreme suffering. Horace Greeley wrote in the "New Yorker," on January 20, 1838, that approximately one-third of the 200,000 wage earners in New York City were unemployed.
