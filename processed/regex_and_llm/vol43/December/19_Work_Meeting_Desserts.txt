Work Meeting) Lesson 6 — Desserts Rhea H. Gardner For Tuesday, March 12, 1957 ESSERT originally meant food served after the table had been cleared or deserted of everything, even the cloth removed. In Europe, "dessert" still means fruit served at the end of the meal. Americans are responsible for giving the sweet the place of honor at the end of the meal. The nght dessert complements a meal. That is, it adds what is needed to make the meal complete or give it perfect balance. If the main course is a sumptuous and filling one, top it off with something light and easily digested. When the rest of the meal skimps on calories and protein, you have a chance to do your bit nutritionally by including a satisfying dessert. The easiest of all desserts to prepare is a bowl heaped with colorful fruit which has been washed, dried, and chilled. Cheese and crisp, unsweetened crackers are natural affinities, or crisp cookies are pleasant accompaniments, Cake is one of the most popular desserts. Many would say that no one can claim to be a really good cook unless she can make a featherlight, mouth-watering cake. As the altitude increases the air pressure becomes less. This requires some slight changes in the recipe that basically is still as good as ever. For cakes containing shortening and two cups flour, it is necessary to reduce the baking powder one-fourth to one-half teaspoon for every rise in altitude of 2500 feet. A reduction of about two tablespoons of sugar for each cup and a slight increase in liquid may also be necessary for perfect texture, for each rise in altitude of 2500 feet. When one goes to a lower altitude, additions of the same amounts should be made, if your recipe gave perfect results where you were. All-purpose flour will not yield as fine and light a product as will cake flour. If you use all-purpose flour, remove two tablespoons of 844 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1956 flour for each cupful used and add in its place two tablespoons of cornstarch. Ingredients which are room temperature make a better textured cake than those taken directly from the refrigerator. A uniform temperature near seventy to seventy-five degrees Fahrenheit is considered best. 'This is particularly important when making sponge-type cakes. Egg whites that are room temperature when used give a lighter, more tender cake of greater volume and finer texture. Ingredients that are too warm, however, can also spoil the cake. Warm, oily fat will not cream satisfactorily, will not incorporate air and build up a smooth, creamy mixture and will produce a coarse textured cake. It is almost impossible to beat the shortening, sugar, and egg mixture too much. When cakes requiring these ingredients are being made, thorough mixing in_ these stages is all to the good of the cake. Once you start adding the liquid and dry ingredients, stir gently to blend, but do not beat the batter more than necessary for perfect blending of ingredients. Pie is the real American dessert. Shells which are filled and then baked, should be served as soon as possible after they have cooled to be just warm. If baking must be done several days in advance of serving, there are two alternatives. You may freeze the ready-to-bake pies so they can be removed from the freezer and baked as needed, or you may bake the pastry shells in regular tins or on the back of muffin tins. Pre-baked shells may be kept for weeks, if stored in a covered container in a cool place. Most cream and_ custard-type fillings such as pumpkin or any of the cream fillings may be stored in covered containers in the refrigerator for a week or two. When ready to serve your pie, freshen the pastry by placing it in a warm oven for a few minutes to give it that just-out-of-the-oven freshness. One of the most popular wintertime desserts is steamed carrot and fruit pudding. It is easily and quickly made and is fairly inexpensive. If kept in a tightly covered container in a cool place, it keeps for weeks. When processed in glass jars or cans and sealed air tight, it will keep for a year or more. Prepared in this way, it makes a perfect dessert when unexpected company calls, for a Sunday dinner, and for those extra busy days any month of the year when the menu calls for a nutritious and satisfying dessert. The nght sauce complements the pudding but never dwarfs the delicate flavor of it. Choose the sauce carefully for your pudding by first deciding what the pudding needs to enhance the eating quality of it. One pudding may need a low calorie tart sauce made of a blend of fruit juice; another one, a sauce wherein a pleasing blend of spices predominates; while for others, a rich, hard sauce is a must. In every instance, make sure that the sauce plays a supporting role and not the role of the main character of your dessert. Increase the fun of meal preparation by including a dessert surprise on the family dinner menu at least every week or two. Get off the LESSON DEPARTMENT beaten track dessert-wise, and you will discover more appetizing food to serve your family than you ever dreamed of before. Such a practice, not only with desserts but with all other foods, does much to re- 845 place boredom with adventure and fun in food preparation.
