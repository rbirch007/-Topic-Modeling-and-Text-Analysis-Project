Social Science—Latter-day Saint Family Life Lesson 3—The Key Is Turned Elder John Farr Larson For Tuesday, January 22, 1957 Objective: To show significant steps in the organization of the Relief Society and the opportunities for service found in its objectives. And I now turn the key in your behalf in the name of the Lord, and this Society shall rejoice, and knowledge and intelligence shall flow down from this time henceforth... (Joseph Smith, April 28, 1842, D. H. C., IV, page 607). HE organization of the Relief Society, under the hand of the Prophet Joseph Smith, was in fulfillment of a revelation of the Lord. That its organization was inspired is evidenced by its accomplishments over a century of service. Within its stated objectives are found unlimited opportunities for personal improvement and service. The year 1842 was one of great progress for the Latter-day Saints. It is described, in part, by the Prophet Joseph Smith. On March 1, 1842, he wrote his now famous "Wentworth Letter" (Ibid., page 536). In response to a request of Mr. John Wentworth, editor of a Chicago newspaper, the Prophet sketched the history of the Church, and, in summarizing our beliefs, set forth our "Articles of Faith." After describing the terrible mob attacks of 1838 he wrote:. We arrived in the state of Illinois in 1839, where we found... A people who were willing to be governed by the principles of law and humanity. We have commenced to build a city called "Nauvoo"... We number from six to eight thousand here, besides vast numbers in the country around We have a city charter granted us, and charter for a Legion... For a University, for an Agricultural and Manufacturing Society, have our own laws and administrators, and possess all the privileges that other free and enlightened citizens enjoy (D. H. C.,, IV, page 540). Brief review should now be made of the last lesson in which is described the status of women in the early part of the igth Century. It LESSON DEPARTMENT was in this setting of limited legal, social, and economic position, with persecution and violence resting, that on March 17, 1842, eighteen women were organized as the Female Relief Society of Nauvoo. Shortly before this time, some of the women had expressed a desire for an organization of their own, and some preliminary steps had been taken by them, even to drafting a constitution and by-laws for a proposed organization. None of them, however, had contemplated the breadth of the Relief Society as envisioned by Joseph Smith. Latter-day Saint Women Unusual Early latter-day Saint women were superior women. Nowhere could there be found another group of women who had endured so much persecution and sorrow, who, in spite of this, were so well tempered by the gospel. The calibre of the women is well described in the Prophet's Annals of March 24, 1842: Our women have always been signalized for their acts of benevolence and_ kindness... In the midst of their persecution, when the bread has been torn from their helpless offspring by their cruel oppressors, they have always been ready to open their doors to the weary traveler, to divide their scant pittance with the hungry, and from their robbed and impoverished wardrobes, to divide with the more needy and destitute (Ibid., pp. 567-568). He further writes:. And we are well assured from a knowledge of those pure principles of benevolence that flow spontaneously from their humane and philanthropic bosoms, that with the resources they will have at command, they will fly to the relief of the stranger; they will pour in oil and wine to the wounded heart of the dis- 707 tressed; they will dry up the tears of the orphan and make the widow's heart to rejoice (Ibid., page 567). Characteristic of the Latter-day Saints, complete minutes were kept of the organizational meetings of the Relief Society and much of the information in this lesson has been taken from this source. Significant Observations Three significant observations are noted in the organization of the Relief Society: (1) The importance of authority is shown in its organization under the direction and after a pattern of the Priesthood. (2) Within the circumstances — surrounding the organization we find another principle, that of revelation. (3) The purposes and objectives of the Relief Society, as stated by the Prophet, indicate a keen understanding of the needs of people, and, in light of present-day problems, demonstrate their practical durability. Organized By Priesthood The Relief Society was organized under the direction of the Priesthood of God. The Prophet Joseph Smith, who held the "keys" of the Priesthood, personally conducted the organization. He requested John 'Taylor, who later became President of the Church, to assume - the chair, and appointed Willard Richards to act as scribe. President Smith gave detailed instructions to the women in parliamentary procedures. How strange this would have seemed to other women, not members of the Church, who were seldom recognized in this way. Each person who became a member was voted upon by all other members, thus stressing the importance at- 708 tached to membership and underlining common consent. Emma Smith was selected as President of the Relief Society, and she chose Sarah M. Cleveland as her first counselor, and Elizabeth Ann Whitney as second counselor. Eliza R. Snow was selected secretary. (For further interesting details of the organization see Ibid., pp. 552, 567; A Centenary of Relief Society, pp. 14-18.) Fulfilled Revelation The organization of the Relief Society fulfilled a revelation of the Lord. In addressing the newly organized Female Relief Society— and this was often his forum—the Prophet referred to Section 25 of the Doctrine and Covenants, dated July 1830. This was a revelation of the Lord for the benefit of Emma Smith who at that time was a young woman of twenty-seven years. In the revelation the Lord referred to Emma as an "elect lady" and said that she would be "... Ordained under his hand to expound scriptures, and to exhort the church, according as it shall be given thee by my Spirit" (D. & C. 25:7). The Prophet at the organization of Relief Society said:... I gave much instruction, read in the New Testament, and Book of Doctrine and Covenants, concerning the Elect Lady, and showed that the elect meant to be elected to a certain work, &c., and that the revelation was then fulfilled by Sister Emma's election to the Presidency of the Society (D. H. C., IV, pp. 552- 553)- Other significant words of the Lord are found in this revelation as follows: And verily I say unto thee that thou RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1956 shalt lay aside the things of this world, and seek for the things of a better. Wherefore, lift up thy heart and _ rejoice, and cleave unto the covenants which thou hast made. Continue in the spirit of meekness, and beware of pride. Let thy soul delight in thy husband, and the glory which. Shall come upon him. Keep my commandments continually, and a crown of righteousness thou shalt receive. And except thou do this, where I am you cannot come. And verily, verily, I say unto you, that this is my voice unto all (D. & C. 25:10, 13-16). Although this revelation was given to Emma Smith, note the Lord's word "... That this is my voice unto all." These words and this experience must have given great encouragement to these women. Purpose of Relief Society The purposes of the Relief Society, as stated by the Prophet Joseph Smith, have endured the test of time, and today still stand as worthy objectives. At the meeting on March 17, 1842, the Prophet said the Relief Society was organized: that the Society of the sisters might provoke the brethren to good works in looking to the wants of the poor, searching after objects of charity and in administering to their wants—to assist by correcting the morals and_ strengthening the virtues of the community, and save the elders the trouble of rebuking; that they may give their time to other duties, etc., in their public teaching (Relief Society Minutes, March 17, 1842, Centenary of Relief Society, page 15). Subsequently he mentioned other objectives, including _ self-improvement, that of reforming individuals, and the practice of holiness. LESSON DEPARTMENT It is interesting to observe that the purposes of the Relief Society are stated in the affirmative while a characteristic of most of the early women's organizations, usually reflected in their name, was that of being 'anti' to some thing or condition. Possibly this might explain why so many of these organizations have long since dissolved and one reason why the Relief Society has continued to be a vital organization through the years. In spite of great sociological advances in the 114 years since the organization of the Relief Society, seldom have there been greater opportunities for service than today. Many service areas, _ particularly those of a temporary nature, have changed very little over the years. For example, the necessity for help when death attends, for encouragement when sickness and sorrow are present, and for teaching the gospel to others, are as vital today as at any previous time. Neither has the need for stimulation to good work and approbation for accomplishment abated. Latter-day Saints have made tremendous inroads in solving many of the social problems of the day. 'The Gospel Plan throws light upon the solution of others. The challenge of the Relief Society objectives is shown by a consideration of some problems upon the American scene. The comments to follow are intended only to demonstrate the existence of some current vital problems and to stimulate thought on Relief Society objectives. Some Objectives Applied "In looking to the wants of the poor" are we sensitive to needs other than financial? Placing food in 709 the stomach and clothes upon the back should be only the beginning of our concern. What of character, self-reliance, and. Encouragement? Are all needy receiving attention? If we are truly "'searching after objects of charity and administering to their wants' we will lend our stimulation to the search for knowledge with which to help man. In stretching the life span, new problems have been added to those of the aged. We need to examine their needs in light of our time. The prevalence of mental illness today suggests the need of further research. Little progress has been made in eliminating causes of mental illness, although treatment facilities and technique have made egrcat strides. Disease, as known a century ago, is almost gone, but even a casual observation reveals "gaps" for which knowledge is greatly needed. The development of transportation and 'communication media have had the effect of breaking down the influence of the home and consequently accentuating the need "to strengthen the virtues of the community." Many communities show a distressing lack of interest, to say nothing of the absence of "know how," in changing or eradicating those conditions which give rise to many evils of this day. 'The break-down of family life is at a new high, both in divorce and near divorce, with all the heartaches and misery that attend such tragedies. The high incidence of crime, particularly among young people, is a distressing challenge to all. As a democratic society, we lack an awareness of the power of personal influence, for good and evil. 'The influence of a true Latter-day Saint is more than that of suggesting good conduct, and this is important, but the greater value comes in stimulating inquiry into | the gospel, thus opening the door for others to obtain a knowledge of the truth. Mothers of the Church, who are keeping the commandments, are "practicing Holiness" and personify all of the noble virtues. It is their influence that exerts a tremendous impact "in strengthening the virtues of the community." In a complex society, such as ours, the need for self-improvement ls felt by most people. 'The very essence of the gospel suggests self-improvement. Not only in the sense of doing nght as opposed to doing wrong, but in being able to do all things better. Whether this is in the art of homemaking, of understanding our children and_ their problems, or of improving our skills and knowledge, it is basic in the Gospel Plan. Self-improvement ls consistent with perfection, a goal of the Gospel Plan. Much has been accomplished since the organization of the Relief Society. Much is left to be done. The door of service is open to all who desire to enter. 'The objectives of the Relief Society have been declared by the Lord, through the Prophet Joseph Smith and his successors. For each member, under the inspirational direction of leaders of the Church, there are limitless opportunities for service and personal growth. LESSON DEPARTMENT Supplementary References 1.
