Charity Never Faileth George H. Mortimer President, New York Stake HE apostle Paul would disagree strongly with the oftheard adage that nothing is certain but death and taxes. And rightly so, for he was inspired to instruct the saints in Corinth that "Charity Never Faileth." Charity is a composite of qualities or attributes which everyone who desires to live in accordance with the principles of the gospel of Jesus Christ must earnestly strive to acquire. 'Their acquisition is predicated upon an understanding and proper application of the divine laws governing human relations which must be obeyed in order to obtain this blessing. Conscious, prayerful, and devoted striving toward this goal is one of the most rewarding pursuits in life, for it assures us the most favorable and successful results of all our worthy activities. The approach to all our problems by the path of charity is as Paul states "a more excellent way" than any other. It is particularly appropriate for the sisters in the Church to meditate upon the way of charity, not only because the slogan of their Relief Society is Charity Never Faileth, but more importantly because they, by teaching and by example, exert the predominant influence in shaping the thought and response patterns of young children through their close association with them in the home, the school, and the Church. Page 644 Let us consider the attributes of Charity as Paul lists them. Charity Suffereth Long. The quality of long-suffering is that of long patience of offense. 'There are not many people who pass through life without being subjected to offenses of greater or less degree. Some of them may arise in the home — a wayward child, an inconsiderate parent or brother or sister. 'They may arise in school — an undisciplined pupil, an unfair teacher, cheating classmates. 'They may arise in business — people who take unfair advantage in dealings, an ambitious co-worker who stoops to every device to gain advantage, a tyrannical boss. Instead of meeting the offenses in kind, as most people impulsively tend to do, the more excellent way, the charitable way, is that outlined by the Savior of not resisting the wrong, of turning the other cheek, of giving the cloak to him that sues for the coat, of going the second mile with him who compels you to go the first, of loving your enemies and praying for those who despitefully use and persecute you. 'Thus patience of offense, which leads to peaceful human relations, contrasts strongly with the spirit of avid and aggressive defense of every right and privilege, which leads to contention. "Blessed are the peacemakers: for they shall be called the children of God." CHARITY NEVER FAILETH Charity... Is Kind. Kindness is the quality of gentleness. It goes beyond good will toward others, which is benevolence, and embraces the actual doing of good to others, which is beneficence. It characterizes the compassionate service of the Relief Society. Not only is kindness the quality which endears one to others in this life and motivates giving meat to the hungry, drink to the thirsty, shelter to the stranger, clothing to the naked, and visiting of the sick and imprisoned, but it will be, as the Savior so impressively taught, a factor which determines who is admitted to the kingdom of God and who is cast into the kingdom of the devil. Charity Envieth Not. 'To envy is to feel discontent at another's possessing that which you want. It is not the desire for good things and the mghteous striving to acquire them for use in doing good to others that is inconsistent with the charitable approach to life's problems, but rather the coveting of things, position, and power for the gratifying of pride and vain ambition and the resort to unrighteous means to achieve them. 'The Lord created the good things of life to delight those who use them with thanksgiving. Envy makes gratitude impossible, It precludes contentment and joy. Charity Vaunteth Not Itself. To vaunt oneself is to make a vain display, to be hypocritical. Against no sin did the Savior speak more strongly than against hypocrisy. "Woe unto you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For ye are like unto whited sepulchres, which indeed ap- 645 pear beautiful outward, but are within full of dead men's bones and of all uncleanness. Even so ye also outwardly appear righteous unto men, but within ye are full of hypocrisy and iniquity." He wants us to appear as we really are, to be genuine and sincere. Charity. Is Not Puffed Up. This is to be conceited, to have an exaggerated opinion of oneself. It is the antithesis of meekness and humility. The parable of the Pharisee and the publican who went into the temple to pray contrasts the vanity of a boastful prayer with the justification won by a sincere and humble prayer. It is to the meek, not to the braggarts, the Lord promised the inheritance of the earth. Charity Doth Not Behave Itself Unseemly. A charitable person exhibits the quality of decorum, doing and saying the right thing at the right time and place. It is the foundation not only of good manners, but also of reverence in sacred places. It is a manifestation of consideration of the feelings of others which lies at the basis of good human relations. Charity.. Seeketh Not Her Own. This is the quality of unselfishness, of losing oneself in the service of others. 'The rewards of this attribute are: "He that findeth his life shall lose it: and he that loseth his life for my sake shall find it Whosoever will save his life shall lose it: and whosoever will lose his life for my sake shall find it. Whosoever will be great among you, let him be your minister; and whosoever will be chief among you, let him be your servant." 646 Charity... Is Not Easily Provoked. One who is provoked displays annoyance and vexation. It is a characteristic of a child quickly to display annoyance and vexation at physical pain, when his feelings are hurt, or when he does not get his way. He has a difficult time projecting himself into the position of another person as a basis for understanding the behavior that gives offense. In strong contrast to the immature response of a child, is the example of the Savior between the time of the great intercessory prayer in Gethsemane and his death on the cross. Not only did he curb any expression of annoyance at the unjust abuse which was heaped upon him, he rebuked Peter for using a sword and prayed forgiveness for his executioners. 'The degree to which we are able to respond to provoking situations in this Christlike manner is a measure of our maturity of thought and action. Paul expresses the maturing process of his own life by saying, "When I was a child, I spake as a child, I understood as a child, I thought as a child: but when I became a man, I put away childish things." It is not the times when we curbed the impulse to do or speak spitefully that we remember with regret, but the times when we hastily said or did an unkind thing. Charity... Thinketh No Evil. Those who think no evil are the pure in heart. "Blessed are the pure in heart: for they shall see God." Solomon recognized the power of thought on character when he said, "as he thinketh in his heart, so is he." The importance which the Savior placed on thought is RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1956 seen in these teachings, "for out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh. A good man out of the good treasure of the heart bringeth forth good things: and an evil man out of the evil treasure bringeth forth evil things... For out of the heart proceed evil thoughts, murders, adulteries, fornications, thefts, false witness, blasphemies: These are the things which defile a man." By thinking no evil, and using our minds for good thoughts, the dreadful consequences of evil thinking can be avoided. Charity... Rejoiceth Not in Iniguity. 'The vice of rejoicing in iniquity may manifest itself in two ways. One is in finding pleasure in doing sinful acts; the other in having "pleasure in them that do them." 'There should be little misunderstanding on the part of members of the Church about the importance of finding joy and satisfaction in personal righteousness rather than iniquity. But there are many people who successfully resist the temptation to commit certain sins who seem to find pleasure or satisfaction in those who commit them. It is this trait which underlies gossip, for the gossiper rejoices not only in hearing the account of another's sin but also finds pleasure in spreading the story. 'Vhe quality of charity demands that there be no rejoicing in iniquity on our own part or on the part of anyone else. Charity... Rejoiceth in the Truth. The gospel of Jesus Christ is the truth. The attitude you take toward the commandments and revealed will of the Lord which are CHARITY NEVER FAILETH an integral part of the gospel will determine, quite largely, your response to temptation. If you resent such commandments as thou shalt not steal, bear false witness, covet, or commit immoral acts, or if you view the negative aspects of the Word of Wisdom as encroachments on your personal liberty, you are much more likely to succumb when tempted than if you are sincerely grateful for these principles and regard them as an armor. "Blessed is the man... (whose) delight is in the law of the Lord; and in his law doth he meditate day and night." Charity... Beareth All Things. To bear is to support, sustain, carry. Every man or woman who has had the privilege of working in a position of authority in the Church which involves calling others to serve, knows the importance of the quality some members possess of bearing all things. These are they who accept calls willingly, who are anxiously engaged in learning their duty and in doing much good, and who do not limit their activity when they have done their fair share. 'hey carry on their appointed tasks to the end and lend enthusiastic support in word and deed to the programs sponsored by the local and General Authorities. This quality is of particular importance to the married women of the Church who are instructed of the Lord to "provoke" or motivate their husbands to good works. The wife who gladly accepts a standard of living which makes the payment of tithes and offerings possible, properly supports her husband in obeying these commandments. She can 647 make it easy for him to perform the ministerial duties that take him from the home, such as ward teaching, administering to the sick, missionary work, council meetings, if she adapts her schedule to his and lets him know by word and deed that she willingly sacrifices his companionship on these occasions. On the other hand, so great is her restraining influence on her husband's success in business and Church affairs, if she puts a barrier in his way, that he rarely passes through it. While the wife of an active Latter-day Saint man_ necessarily foregoes much in companionship with him, it is a sacrifice that calls forth the blessings of heaven. No other woman can have quite the peace of mind when her husband is away from home that a woman can have when she knows that her husband is engaged in the Lord's work. Charity... Believeth All Things. This is the attribute of faith—in God, in fellow men, in oneself. It characterizes those who declare with Nephi, "I know that the Lord giveth no commandments unto the children of men, save he shall prepare a way for them that they may accomplish the thing which he commandeth them.' 'Those who possess this attribute meet and deal with others without suspicion and are quick to forgive those who repent of breaches of the confidence that had been placed in them. Charity... Hopeth All Things. To hope is to desire something with the expectation of getting it, with faith that it can be obtained. Hope is the quality that enables people 648 to surmount discouragement, to maintain zeal and enthusiasm in the face of reverses and failures, to make the best use of things at hand, to resist resentment against conditions which cannot be changed. It sustains the mother through the months of discomfort during pregnancy, through the agony of delivery, through hours of anxiety when illness strikes, through bereavement when loved ones go. It is the way to serenity rather than despair. Charity Endureth All Things. To endure is to bear great and lasting hardships and evils. This quality makes it feasible to be in the world but not of the world and to withstand being persecuted for righteousness' sake without resentment. By cultivation of this quality you can rejoice when men revile you and persecute you and say all manner of RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1956 evil against you falsely for Christ's sake. It makes you immune to the scorn and ridicule of the unrighteous. And the promised reward is that "he that endureth to the end shall be saved." These, then, are the qualities that constitute charity which never fails in any situation. While Bible scholars do not agree as to the correct rendition of Paul's thought Charity Never Faileth, we can certainly agree that these attributes are based on eternal principles and therefore never end. They are, moreover, divine attributes which all those who hope to gain the celestial kingdom will have to acquire in this life.
