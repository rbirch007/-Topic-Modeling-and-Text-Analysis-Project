The Function of Art Whenever we yield and tell our children a story, we fulfill the same relation to them as our artists do to us. It has ever been thus, and, to judge from the booming presentday sale of classical records, radios, T'V's, magazines, and books, and attendance at movies and concerts, that need is a consistent one. As modern man's leisure increases, so does his need for entertainment, most of which is concerned with recreating reality in a more personal, condensed, memorable form than our rather monotonous, daily life. Yes, indeed, this is the timeless function of art: to recreate (or to interpret) the shapeless, massive reality which surrounds us, into a new form, which is colored by the artist's own personal perspective and values. Or in Shakespeare's own LESSON DEPARTMENT words, his purpose is "to hold the mirror up to nature, as 'twere." And, inevitably, this mirroring of nature includes his own nature too. A play is more than anything an art-form: it mirrors reality as the author sees it, neither as good nor bad, but as real. Shakespeare is our greatest writer, not because he is our greatest thinker or philosopher, but because he is the fullest, best artist; that is, he understands all humanity deeply, and can express his understanding in living images and language within his chosen medium of expression. But the characters whom he has created are not great because they are true to life: they are immortal because they fulfill their artistic function in the passage or play in which they appear. Most important, the play, as Shakespeare well knew, must be true to its own nature. A great play is "an expanded metaphor," to use Wilson Knight's excellent term. It has a soul, a purpose, a reality of its own which are not the same as those we know in life. In real life we rarely speak soul-to-soul with each other; compared to figures in a drama, real people are quite silent and retiring. And it is the very function of plays to erect this art-work which in fiery phrases, sings out in the language of the soul which most of us conceal. Hamlet, for example, could never occur in real life, yet he is the greatest success on the stage, for there he exemplifies within himself almost all human emotions, bewilderingly yet really condensed. Like all of Shakespeare's greatest dramatic creations, he is the poetic, artistic shape given by his creator to 701 a universal conflict, emotion, or thought. Shakespeare's Sources Perhaps the most popular but distracting hobbyhorse in the study of Shakespeare is the attempt to "explain" him. Since he was relatively unschooled, not a nobleman, and had no power nor influence outside the theater, it becomes a great temptation to point out what influences dominated him, and to reveal the sources of his ideas, skills, and beliefs. In Lesson 'Two a scant introduction into this world was attempted, not in an effort to give Shakespeare sources, but to give ourselves sympathy and understanding. Only after such an introduction can we begin to see that Shakespeare, like every successful artist, cannot escape recording the spint and texture of his day, but he does this secondarily; all his art and power begin in his own emotion and vision. As he succeeded magnificently in recording himself, then, did he record his age? Yes, evidences of Plato, Ovid, Plutarch, Holinshed, Dante, Copernicus, Machiavelli, Montaigne, Chaucer, and the Bible, have been found in his work; he incorporates all these, and he is none of them only. It is worth noting that, of all these books, only Holinshed's Chronicle and Chaucer are "English'; all sources of his knowledge were derived from other cultures, including the Bible. Although more than ten different translations of the Bible had been made into English since William Tindale's first translation in 1526, the Geneva 702 translation (1560) was the one Shakespeare undoubtedly knew best. We cannot know with certainty whether the Bible was taught in Stratford grammar schools, — or whether he came to know the Bible so well through attending church services and travelling cycle plays, which dramatized those episodes from the Old 'Testament which Shakespeare. Used in_ his plays; namely, Dives and Lazarus, Adam and Eve, Cain and Abel, Solomon, Job, Daniel, Herod, Judas and Pilate, the fall of Lucifer, the serpent in the Garden, the Flood, Moses and the Red Sea, etc. Most commentators agree that, as did the average Elizabethan gentleman of his time, Shakespeare knew his Bible well (actually he quoted from or made allusion to forty-two books of the Bible and six of the Apocrypha); they also agree that his images drawn from Biblical history were few in comparison with his extensive use of other image-sources, but that his frequent allusion to Biblical phrases and figures of speech is often passed over by the reader who does not know his Bible as well as Shakespeare obviously did. For example, Shakespeare does not mention Joshua when in King John he alludes to the sun's standing still, or when, in The Tempest, Caliban recalls Jael's driving a nail into the head of Sisera. Richard III prays that the Lord may not hear women complaining with God's appointed leaders in the same tone as Shimei had cursed King David; Richard II contrasts Christ's reception of little children with his advice to the rich young man who asked what he must do to be saved. Shakespeare was also greatly indebted to his predecessors and contemporaries in the history of the English theater—the morality and cycle plays, the interludes, Seneca, Lodge, Nashe, Green, Kyd, Marlowe, Beaumont and Fletcher, but, though he borrowed from some and LESSON DEPARTMENT imitated others, they are not Shakespeare, individually or collectively. His plays are loaded with references to contemporary events and personalities. His largest, most pressing concern was to please a penny-paying audience next and every 'luesday; this he succeeded in doing only by making his plays contain the throbbing heart of Elizabethan England. How else could he appeal through the years to a zestful people who loved laughter, boldness, courage, display, and sensation, who were prosperous, hot-headed, industrious, devout, patriotic individuals? The age gave Shakespeare his materials and themes. Yet it was his own high gifts as poet and _playwright which gave these "things" shapes and identities. As he himself tells us of the function of a poet, "And as imagination bodies forth the forms of things unknown, the poet's pen turns them to shapes, and gives to airy nothing A local habitation and a name" (A Midsummer Night's Dream, V. 1:14). The "Real" Shakespeare Genius speaks to genius. One of the most piercing critical comments on Shakespeare is that of John Keats: "Shakespeare led a life of allegory; his works are the comments on it." Thus the "real" Shakespeare is the sum total of his plays, his poems, and his sonnets. The "real" Shakespeare is the comprehensive, universal observer who saw into all men's hearts and transmuted his living, contemporary values both of personality and of belief, into immortal art. Surely contemporary accounts are 703 "teal." Without exception each recorded description refers to him as "our Shakespeare" or "my Shakespeare." Aubrey, the son of one of his fellow-players spoke of him as "a handsome, well-shaped man, verie good company, and of a verie ready, and pleasant and smooth wit." Ben Johnson disagreed fiercely with Shakespeare's dramatic theories, yet Shakespeare was one of the few friends with whom Ben never quarreled, and he said of him, "I loved the man and do honor his memory, on this side idolatry, as much as any." It is important to know that Shakespeare's plays fall into three groups which reveal much of his personal outlook. 'The first large, experimental period, ending in 1601 when he was thirty-seven, contained Senacan spectacle plays, light comedies and romances, and _histories. 'The second, or dark period, extended from 1602 to 1608, when he wrote but two comedies and all his great tragedies, including Timon of Athens, so bitter in its denunciation of humanity that it is not considered a successful stage-play and is rarely presented. 'The final period, ending in 1612, includes Cymbeline and The Tempest, two of his happiest, warmest works; this, then, is a period of reaftirmation and expanding belief in the goodness of man, nature, and life. That he stopped writing when he was only forty-eight, mght at his peak, when honor, success, and material return were sweetest, ls worthy of note. But we must go one step further. If the "real" Shakespeare is to be found only in his plays, to 704 which real Shakespeare do we refer? The common error in eighteenth and nineteenth century criticism was to create Shakespeare in the image of the critic's own personality and prejudices, which makes much of this criticism worthless. Even in the present day, he is claimed by every group, but, for every claimant, evidence can be found in the plays which cancels out any such slanted approach. For, if the plays are acceptable evidence, Shakespeare knew Catholicism and Anglicanism and Puritanism; he could have been all or none. No one has been more in love with love, yet no writer has so heartily condemned. The shortcomings of woman. Some find in him the stirrings of democracy, yet none feared the undisciplined mob more than he: it merited his entire contempt. His Henry V has been described as the pure epic of English patriotism, yet he uses Falstaff to poke the bitterest fun at all those who honor the military.. If Shakespeare is the characters he creates, then in one creation he is honest, in another lying and treacherous. In one play he is mad, in the next a fool. But why go on? Any and all are his creations; and insofar as each makes his soul-language contribution to the artistic whole, that character should be considered part of the "real" Shakespeare. Often as artist he has been concerned only to present every shade and depth of life without pronouncing moral judgment, yet from his tone and from the imagery which fulfills his unconscious intent, we come to fee] that we can know the inner spirit of the man. Not only could he see more of life's contradictions, pleasures, and pains than other men, but he had courage to present with balanced fairness all phases of life. Often in the most dismal of tragic scenes he uses humor as a means of interpreting life and adjusting to it, for instance, the grave digger dialogue in Hamlet. Often, like the Elizabethans for whom he wrote, he has _ his characters laugh at their own weaknesses. But, in contrast to most of his contemporaries, he is always, in the final analysis, wholesome. Always in Shakespeare: Love surfeits not, lust like a glutton dies. Venus and Adonis, 803 Never is illicit love presented as healthy, nor even as cause for jokes and amusement. Samuel Taylor Coleridge, certainly the most important Shakespearean critic of his time, understood who Shakespeare was, for he wrote: Shakespeare has no innocent adulteries, no interesting incests, no virtuous vice; he never renders that amiable which religion and reason alike teach us to detest. LESSON DEPARTMENT If he occasionally disgusts a keen sense of delicacy, he never injures the mind; he neither excites nor flatters passion in order to degrade the subject of it; he does not use the faulty thing for a faulty purpose, nor carries on warfare against virtue by causing wickedness to appear as no wickedness through the medium of a morbid sympathy with the unfortunate. In Shakespeare vice never walks as in twilight; nothing is purposely out of its place; he inverts not the order of nature and propriety. Shakespeare saw evil in the world and depicted it honestly as evil. He does not blame sinners; instead he pities them, holding their sins, and indeed, all evils as corrupting diseases on the body of the world, which must be stamped out if the world is to remain normal and good. How well he knew that: The web of our life is of a mingled yam, good and ill together (All's Well That Ends Well, IV. 3). Yet in his impersonal artist's approach, he could depict Shylock's cruelty and greed and cunning—all of which Shakespeare hated most— with such sympathy for the man they controlled, with such self-identification with Shylock's suffering that, despite Shylock's brutality, Shakespeare brings us to feel that he was wronged. Such compassionate identification with the vile and evil in man is one of Shakespeare's surest qualities of greatness. Shakespeare thus remains our greatest writer, not only because of his superb word-skill, but because, knowing mankind so deeply, he defined the true moral truths of man and God as the truths he honored; likewise his definition is that of all moral men in the western tradition of man. Had it been otherwise, Shakespeare could never have become the supreme spokesman for the enduring heart of mankind. In truth, his judgments constantly reflect the great moral values of justice and compassion and humility which represent the English Bible at its best. Perhaps the greatest tribute to Shakespeare is that, increasingly, we feel his constant humility before the task of depicting all humanity.
