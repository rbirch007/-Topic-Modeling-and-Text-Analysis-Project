Without doubt, the world of fantasy feeds their deepest, most real hungers, and the chant, "Mommy, tell me a story" echoes down the ages as proof that humankind, at least, in its unspoiled innocence, has no question about the superior values of living in the play-world which is only a miniature of grownup actuality. Why else do little girls cherish dolls and tiny stoves "that really light up," while little boys mirror their fathers almost to make fathers ashamed, demanding saws and hammers, model trains and planes, submarines and _ torpedoes and, now, jets? The Function of Art Whenever we yield and tell our children a story, we fulfill the same relation to them as our artists do to us. It has ever been thus, and, to judge from the booming presentday sale of classical records, radios, T'V's, magazines, and books, and attendance at movies and concerts, that need is a consistent one. As modern man's leisure increases, so does his need for entertainment, most of which is concerned with recreating reality in a more personal, condensed, memorable form than our rather monotonous, daily life. Yes, indeed, this is the timeless function of art: to recreate (or to interpret) the shapeless, massive reality which surrounds us, into a new form, which is colored by the artist's own personal perspective and values.
