So Dear to My Heart Ruth Young revisited you, old chapel, out at West Jordan last might. Fortyone years you have served the people—you dear, rambling, old chapel. Some have called you ugly —with your steep narrow stairways and four stories of classrooms. 'To me you have ever been beautiful and comforting—my Balm of Gilead —with your stone entrance ways thrown wide like arms stretched forth to greet me. My ancestors helped to build you, hauling granite stones by wagon down from nearby Little Cottonwood Canyon for your huge foundation. By the way you were constructed, they must have believed you would endure forever. But now —just a stone's throw south of here—there stands a splendid, shiny, very, very modern new chapel, with stairs designed for easy walking, lower ceilings, and a good heating system. But you have a personality —you dear, old, friendly building. Once you had three huge stainedglass windows. One to the east... One to the north... And one to the south. Their subject was an ebony bowl of sky-blue flowers against an amber and violet and green background. How easy it is to remember long years back when I was a skinny little girl sitting on a bench—feet not quite touching the floor. It had been a Fast Day—and my tummy was being gnawed by hunger pains; but the people kept getting up "bearing their testimonies." I had long ceased to listen, and was eagerly watching the floor beneath my bench. When the Page 676 sun shone upon a certain section of brightly colored green glass in the window it would appear on a certain section of the wood floor. Then it would be time for the bishop to stand up and end the meeting. I had learned this through many Fast Days. It was my own private sundial long before I could read a clock. Then—one Sunday morning just four years ago, we were shocked to discover that some boys had spent the night throwing boulders through your beautiful sky-blue flower windows, absolutely destroying the north and south ones. Next week you were repaired—but with plain, ugly glass instead; but, the glory that once was yours still looked eastward toward the morning sun. I was baptized in your little "boiler room" and confirmed on the front bench of your chapel, one lovely morning in May. I remember sitting there awaiting my turn, looking upward at the peaceful figure of Jesus in the big oil painting which SO DEAR TO MY HEART used to hang near the organ. That old organ of yours looked like the one up in the Tabernacle. Of course, the pipes were dummies— but how splendid they seemed way up high behind the pulpit. Your main assembly room was magnificent, with its so-high ceiling and the fancy carving way way up there in the center. The floor of your chapel slanted downward. People sat on long wooden benches. The visibility was perfect! 'The bishopric and choir sat on the stand— which was a grand affair, with a rail running in front of it. I have been in every one of your rooms but one. The Tower Room. Somehow or other I've never found an opportunity to go up that narrow, rounding stairway. What mysterious things might be there! I married the boy I sat next to down in your missionary classroom. So that room holds sweet memories for me. Oh, how the memories and ghosts of the past walk through your silent hallways now! Farmers, lawyers, bishops, teachers, stake presidents, musicians, mothers, fathers, leaders of industry, and artists all have loved you. They have learned counsel in your rooms — and have sung the songs of Zion therein. My favorite memory is the year I attended seminary in your far northeastern basement room. 'The walls were plain granite and very cold in winter. 'The floor was uneven cement. Yet, it all became Bible land for me! Adam, Father Abraham, Isaac, Jacob, Joseph, Moses — all the great men of the Old Testament became alive in that humble room. I know the very music we 677 sang surely must have become embedded into the stone. We meant it that much! Last winter, while waiting for the doors to open for Relief Society, I started doing sketches of you. People have discovered how very difficult you are to photograph. You are so big that in order to get all of you in the camera's lens, the photographer must climb a fence and walk out into a cow pasture. 'Then, your doorway and one window (the stained-glass one) become hidden by locust trees. The back is easy to photograph — but who wants to remember that only? So—after many, many sketches — using an artist's right to subtract trees — I finally got you down on_ paper. About that time, my Relief Society president informed us that we were to have a bazaar; and that each woman should donate an article she had made herself. I can't sew— I can't embroider — my cakes taste like leather — so the only thing left was my sketch of you. It sold! Orders came in by the dozens! I am so delighted! The money is going to be used to help buy the drapes for our Relief Society room in our new building. I hope you won't mind, dear old chapel. You will be torn down shortly. This way, all the ones who have adored you will have your picture to remember always — pasted safely in their Books of Remembrance. My heart still aches a bit when, coming out from our brightly lighted new meetinghouse, I see your dark tower and spire. I must remember that outgrowing things we love, is never a pleasant process.
