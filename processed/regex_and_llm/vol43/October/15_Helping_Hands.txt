Helping Hands Flo Whittemore had not invited her to come into [| my home that morning. I had not even been wishfully thinking (as I have so often since) that she would come. Yet, at nine o'clock, there she was, energetically scrubbing away on my dirty woodwork and looking as cheerful, I thought to myself, as though she enjoyed it. "Why?" I questioned almost aloud as I watched her from the davenport where I was lying. Then, as if an effort to discover "why" was too much exertion for a body weakened by illness and a mind weary of trying to solve problems, I said a bit querulously to myself, "Oh, well, if she wants to do it, it's perfectly all right! Only I don't see why she should enjoy doing it!" She was our very capable, very much loved Relief Society president. When I had answered her knock on my door that eventful day, she had smiled pleasantly as she asked: "How are you this morning?" Glad to have anyone come in, I quickly bade her enter. But before I could tell her of my aches and pains and discouragement, she had said: "I think you'd better just lie down again and take it easy. You've been pretty sick, you know. I didn't have anything in particular to do today, so I thought I'd just slip in here and wash your woodwork and clean your windows for you. I know it must be hard to lie there and look at all these things that need doing." All of a sudden, a spark of interest stirred inside me. "It will be nice," [ acknowledged, "to have the windows washed, especially this one in front of me. It's so smoky, everything looks gray outside." "I know. Almost gives you a gloomy aspect of life, doesn't it?" she replied cheerily as she proceeded to undo the bundle of cloths she had brought. Hmmm! I thought, "almost!" Why shouldn't I be altogether gloomy just lying here day after day. And I retired again into my morbid thoughts. I had known Lola as president of Relief Society; I had been a visiting teacher for nearly two years. When I became ill, she had called, bringing me a bouquet of blooms from her rose garden. I loved those! But why should she suddenly take a notion to come into my house and clean it up? My conscience was beginning to prick a little—but I don't care, I told myself, how dirty it is. It doesn't matter to me; I'm just too tired. But she doesn't need to act as if she so much enjoyed doing it. I'm sure I shouldn't enjoy cleaning up somebody else's dirty house. Oh, well! I felt it, but refused to give happiness a chance. Instead, I pulled down the corners of my mouth again and turned my face to the wall. If Lola was disappointed, she gave no sign; but resolutely filling her pans with clean, warm water, she began scrubbing the woodwork in the bedroom, all the while whistling or humming a cheery accompaniment to the rhythmic movements of her hands. Listening, my thoughts went back many years to a sunny morning at my grandmother's home in a little town in Utah when [ had first met Estella Wight, who became a cherished and lifelong friend. She was gaily singing as she scrubbed out a wash on the board. A girl of ten years, I looked at her with wonder, to be singing on wash day! Now I was far away from Estella, but here was another person much like her, cheerily singing her way into my life, whether I would or not. Then I happened to glance at the clock. It said "11:30." Lola will be hungry, working as she has all morning, I thought. Maybe I could get some lunch ready. I arose and went into the kitchen, and, by the time lunch was on the table, Lola had finished the woodwork and windows in bedroom and bath. I don't remember what I prepared for lunch or what we talked about as we ate; nothing very personal, anyway. Then Lola washed the dishes while I wiped them. HELPING HANDS "Now you'd better lie down again," she said, "you've been sick a long time and you are not strong yet. You must remember to take it easy for awhile. Don't try to do everything all at once when you can get up and around." Her smile, her sympathy, warmed my heart, and I went back to the davenport gladly, with not the least desire to argue. (How I'd changed! ) Two hours or so later, my small kitchen was spick and span: walls, cabinets, floor, doors, and windows. As Lola prepared to leave, I got up to thank her. I expressed appreciation, in a matter-of-fact way, for her efforts to help me, and I was glad to have my house clean, too. "It was nice of you to do it," I said. And then, as she chatted happily about making some bright ruffled curtains for the kitchen windows, the miracle happened. I glanced down at her hands that must have been lovely when she came to my house that morning. I could see that they were small and beautifully shaped with tapering fingers that looked more like they 675 should be skimming over piano keys than scrubbing dirty woodwork. But now they were red, rough, and scratched, the nails cracked and broken. Dismayed and ashamed, I would have cried out, but before I could say a word, Lola was gone. Remembering the diamonds in the bands that circled her finger, I knew she did not have to do the menial tasks that she had done so willingly in my home. For me—to help lift me up out of the discouragement and despair into which I had fallen and give me a new hold on life for love of me, she had scrubbed and scoured, not caring, not thinking for a moment what was happening to her hands. They looked so sore, they must have hurt her, but there was no word of complaint—just song! At that moment, it was as though a spring shower had suddenly descended upon my spirit and was cleansing it of all the accumulation of doubt and despair and weakness that had piled up during months of illness.
