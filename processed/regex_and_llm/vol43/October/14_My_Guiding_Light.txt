My Guiding Light Lucile Tournear Y mother was so quiet and M unassuming that many of her closest neighbors never really learned to know her; yet she was truly a friend to the friendless, and a comfort to the lonely hearted. My early childhood would have been desolate indeed without her love and devotion. How clearly I remember a conversation that went something like this. "Mamma, will you read to me now? Just a few pages, Mamma." I would carry a book into the kitchen where my mother was ironing, or to the dining room where she sat at her sewing machine. Sometimes I followed her to the back porch where she scrubbed clothes on a washboard. Her explanations that she must first finish her work, were always gentle and patient. Never did she raise her voice in anger or impatience. More often than not, she would leave her work only to finish it later in the evening when I was tucked safely into bed. Due to her kindness and tact, I was eight years old before I realized that I was blind. When a teacher was found to teach me braille, Mother learned braille with me. Since we could see the teacher only once a week, Mother felt that my progress would be too slow with so little help, so my education became another of her many tasks. When I did enter a day school for the blind, I was able to go into the fifth grade with the children in my own age group. Had she merely been the traditional busy housewife, this would have been noteworthy enough, but since Page 670 she was the sole provider for four children, it was remarkable. By taking in washing and sewing, working in the homes of neighbors, and remaking second-hand clothing which friends and relatives contributed, we were able to manage. My mother was often asked why she did not send me away to school. In those days, practically all blind children attended special state institutions. Tio such questions Mother would say: "I want Lucile to have the same love and care that my other children have. Now that she has learned to read, we can manage until the class for the blind in Los Angeles is ready." When the class was finally organized, Mother sold our home in the small town of Compton, where we had always lived, and moved to Los Angeles. This was in 1919, shortly after the close of World War I, and houses were scarce and rents were high. Even in those days people were not anxious to rent to families with children, and widows with children presented a real problem. 'Then, too, they were afraid the little blind girl would fall and get hurt, and they did not want to be responsible. However, finally we were able to rent an apartment, with the understanding that we would move as soon as possible. Within a month we found a house. I know now that Mother's great faith and fervent prayers were responsible for this. EOPLE wondered how Mother would earn our living in a strange community where we knew no one. She had told no one what MY GUIDING LIGHT her plans were, and, to this day, I am not sure whether she had made any definite decision as to what she would do. However, the problem seemed to solve itself, and on her long walks during the househunting period, she had discovered that many people were renting their spare rooms, and that such rooms were in great demand. Since the school I was to attend was near a university and many students. Needed living accommodations, Mother opened a rooming house. S I made friends with the blind students in my school, we also took some of them into our home. There-was Marguerite the little girl whose parents were in the restaurant business, and were busy until late in the evening. 'Could someone care for the child after school hours until her parents could come for her? I told my mother what the situation was, and Marguerite practically, became a member of the family. 'This resulted in a beautiful friendship for me which will never die. Marguerite became much closer to my mother than to her own, because she was with my mother during the years of adolescence when a girl needs a mother's companionship most. Since we lived only four blocks from my school, our home became the favorite visiting place for my friends. Mother never minded their noise. She loved the piano playing and singing, no matter how long the music sessions lasted. I know that she was terribly disappointed that I never became a musician, but she never mentioned her disappointment. My friends were sure that when they came to our house, refreshments would be in 671 order.. Mother's homemade cookies and chocolate cake were always a delight. Whenever there was a school picnic, there were always two or three friends ready to share my lunch with me. Mother's deviled eggs and well-filled sandwiches were always popular. I never could have finished high school and college without Mother's help. In the late twenties and early thirties, there were very few textbooks to be had in_ braille. Mother did most of my reading. She also spent long hours dictating French to me, and I transcribed page after page of it into Braille. I wonder now why my teachers did not assign certain paragraphs to me, so that Mother would not have had to spend so many hours dictating the long lessons. Mother never complained, however; she wanted me to read just what the class was reading. She read French beautifully. Though she had only gone to a small country school which took her through the Fifth Reader only, she had learned to speak French at home as her parents spoke no English. Because Mother had read French prayers in her childhood, she never learned to pray in English. Marguerite and I used to tell her she had two voices, one for French and one for English. The French voice had a nostalgic tenderness in it like soft, sad music. Mother never liked to go to bed until all of us were safely tucked in. When I sat up late typing my college notebooks, she would sometimes drop off to sleep out of utter weariness, but before I finished my work, she would always wake up and bring me a steaming cup of cocoa. 672 "You're so tired," she would say anxiously, "you'll kill yourself working so hard." It never occurred to her to say "I'm so tired." Since we had to take in roomers, we never had much privacy as a family. Strangely enough we didn't mind this too much, because Mother had a way of making them seem a part of the household. They all loved her because she was friendly without intruding in their affairs. Mother loved all people. She took Ester Lo, a lonely Chinese girl student into our home, and did what she could to make the strange girl feel at home in a foreign country. She encouraged Ester to bring her Chinese school friends in, and our house often reeked with the odors of strange Chinese foods which she cooked in our kitchen. The unfamiliar guttural sounds of the Cantonese dialect they spoke made us feel that we had indeed been shifted into a strange new world. When Ester presented Mother with a box of candy at Christmas, and a Christmas card came from her boy friend, Mother's holiday season was complete. Though Mother was much too busy to attend clubs or to have hobbies, she did love to go to shows, both the movies and stage plays. We saw some wonderful pictures and plays together. People used to ask my mother how I could enjoy them. "TI tell her the pictures and read her the titles on the screen," she answered simply. "The stage plays are easier because she can learn the voices." When talking movies came in, Mother's task was much easier, RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1956 and the pictures took on new life. Nevertheless, due to Mother's careful descriptions, I was able to follow the stories of the silent pictures and enjoy them perfectly. Often we could discuss these pictures at home, and Mother would laughingly tell friends that she had tried so hard to make the pictures clear to me that I could tell the story better than she could. HEN my sisters and I had all married, Mother gave up our old home and divided her time among us. We tried to persuade her to live with a friend, or rent an apartment so that she could have a permanent home, but she would not do this. Her life had been lived so completely for her children that she seemed totally incapable of creating a life for herself. I felt guilty when she lived with my husband and me because she insisted on doing all of the housework. I was always to her "her little girl.' It wasn't that she wanted me to be dependent upon her; on the contrary, she was very anxious for me to become self-reliant, knowing that there would come a time when she could not be with me. She went to her heavenly home on a thirty-first of December. How fitting that she should start a new life with a new year! Her work on earth was well done; she would not have wanted to stay here any longer. Because of her, I have never known a world of darkness. Because of her, I have been able to live a busy and happy life. With her as my inspiration, I shall continue to do so. As she always has been, she always will be my guiding light.
