Legal Status of Women in 1842 Married women of the igth Century, particularly in the first half, had little recognition, as individuals, in the legal sense. 'The early basic law of all states, except Louisiana, had its origin in the English common law. The common law is said to: LESSON DEPARTMENT. Comprise the body of those principles and rules of action... Which derive their authority solely from usages and customs of immemorial antiquity, or from the judgments and decrees of the courts recognizing, affirming, and enforcing such usages and customs, and in this sense particularly the ancient unwritten law of England (I Kent Commentaries 492). In the United States it is that body of rules and principles as established and determined in England at the time the various states adopted it. Many of its principles have been changed by statutes and constitutional provisions in the various states, but, except as it has been changed, it is the law today. As we study the common law we should recognize that only broad principles are here treated and that minor variations possibly existed in some states. An unmarried woman at common law had capacity to contract and perform other legal acts, equal to that of a man. When she married, her position as a separate, "competent" individual disappeared, and she was reduced to the legal capacity of "slaves, idiots and infants." In theory, when a man and woman married, they became one person and that person was the husband. The entire legal existence of the wife merged into that of her husband. In her own capacity, a married woman could not, with validity, (1) sign a contract, deed, note or check; (2) acquire or dispose of property without the consent of her husband; or (3) sue or be sued. All personal property of the wife, whether acquired before or during marriage, became that of hei hus- 563 band. Personal property is generally property of a personal or movable nature such as money, jewelry, furniture, etc., as opposed to real property, such as land, which is of an immovable character. A_husband was in the position, legally, of being unable to give his wife a present, since while attempting it he simultaneously vested himself with its title. Upon death, the husband's personal property (including that acquired from his wife), generally went to his estate. She was permitted to keep her "paraphernalia" which included articles of wearing apparel, and personal ornaments, suitable to her rank and degree, but even these, except her clothing, could be taken to satisfy her husband's debts. Similar harsh rules were applied to the wife's real estate. 'That acquired prior to her marriage became subject to her husband's wishes. He could sell or mortgage without her consent. He was entitled to the rents and crops from her land and had no duty to account to her for money received, since it was his money. Real property acquired by the wife before her marriage, not sold by the husband, went to her upon his death. If the wife were employed to act as a midwife, as a seamstress, or in any other capacity, her husband was entitled to her pay. A New Hampshire judge in 1830 described another aspect of the common law when he said the wife:... Was considered to be so entirely under the power of the husband, that she could, in no case, make what in propriety of speech is called a will (Marston Vs. Norton, Superior Court of New Hampshire, 5 N. H. 205). 564 A mother's right to her children was inferior to that of her husband and is illustrated by a decision of the New York Supreme Court in 1842: The question then is, which of these parents, the father or the mother, has the best title to the custody of the child? The opinion of the court has been repeatedly expressed, that by the law of the land the claims of the father are superior to those of the mother (Mercein Vs. The People ex rel. Barry, 3 Hill (N. Y.) 399). In that day women were not given the privilege of voting in political elections, a right now cherished by all freedom-loving people. Until recent years, a woman who had citizenship status before her marriage, lost it upon marriage to an alien. Other Problems of the Day There were other problems and conditions facing the women of the Prophet's day: the ever-present disease, infant mortality, the absence of educational opportunities, the short life span, and extremely poor working conditions for women who worked. Few labor saving devices were known to them. Great physical exertion was generally required of them in their homes. Widespread suffering existed in the country as a result of an economic depression. Public charity was then limited to poorly organized efforts which were meeting only a fraction of the need. 'Those in need were often treated as criminals. Latter-day Saint women of this period were considered, in the eyes of the law, as having a status and capacity similar to that of other women. 'The conditions they faced RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—AUGUST 1956 were not unlike those of other women. However, they had _ blessings not enjoyed outside their circle and problems uniquely their own. 'The eatly history of the Church is full of episodes in which superior women suffered abuses of all kinds from enemies of the Church and were constantly facing an uncertain future. Always there were skeptics who ridiculed their belief in the divine origin of the Church and the revealed principles of the gospel. Some were subjected to the grossest of indignities. Many stood by while their husbands were cruelly treated by ruthless mobs. The Changing Trend From our vantage point in _history, in looking back to the plight of early 19th Century women, we wonder why men and women advocating principles of fair play didn't literally rise up and demand that something be done. 'The present rights of women came only after a long series of events directed against the unreasonable _ restrictions of the common law. At first only small voices were heard to object, and those not in concert. Later, as the chorus against the injustices grew, one by one the discriminating provisions disappeared. The organization of the Relief Society was at the very beginning of a trend of legislative, judicial, and other significant changes which gave to married women a status as individuals and by which they were liberated from the harsh and often cruel dogmas of the common law. It recognized women as capable, responsible adults. 'The teachings of Joseph Smith concerning their role in community betterment, self- LESSON DEPARTMENT improvement, and service to others, significantly enlarged women's sphere. Today, most of the injustices of the common law are gone. 'Their removal was a victory for equitable principles. 'The changes were made by men who curtailed their own interests and rights through an appeal to their sense of justice. In the last half of the 19th Century organized effort brought results for those seeking a more enlightened attitude for women. The rights of women became a frequent subject of discussion and the names of Lucy Stone and Susan B. Anthony, great leaders in the cause, became invitations for debate. At the wedding of Lucy Stone and Henry Blackwell, a statement was tread suggesting how strong they felt about the status of women. (Henry Blackwell became distinguished in the movement to abolish slavery.) 'The wedding statement, in part, reads as follows: While we acknowledge our mutual affection by publicly assuming the relationship of husband and wife, yet, in justice to ourselves and a great principle, we deem it a duty to declare that this act on our part implies no sanction of, nor promise of voluntary obedience to, such of the present laws of marriage as refuse to recognize the wife as an independent, rational being, while they confer upon the husband an injurious and unnatural superiority, investing him with legal powers which no honorable man would exercise, and which no man should possess (Lucy Stone, Pioneer of Woman's Rights, page 165). Following the above they then enumerated their specific objections. The National Woman's Suffrage Association was organized in 1868. Starting in Illinois in 1861, when the first "Married Women's Act" 265 was passed, a series of statutes and constitutional provisions were enacted, by which the common law restrictions upon the right of married women to contract, sue and be sued, acquire, hold and _ convey property in their own right, were removed. 'The right to. Vote was given to women in this country only in recent times, the 19th Amendment to the U. S. Constitution having been ratified on August 26, 1920. Latter-day Saint women, who significantly have always enjoyed an ecclesiastical voting privilege, enjoyed political suffrage rights as early as 1847, when Utah was still Mexican Territory. 'The nght to vote conferred by a state or territory was actually first exercised by Utah women in February 1870. Because of their political experiences and their privileges locally, the women of Utah became active supporters and effective, conspicuous figures in the National Woman Suffrage movement, and _ sent delegates regularly to the National Suffrage Conventions to lend support to this great cause (A Centenary of Relief Society, 1942, page 67). President Brigham Young was aware of the influence of our women when he said on one occasion: Now, sisters, I want you to vote, because you are the characters that rule the ballot box (Widtsoe, John A.: Priesthood and Church Government, page 87). Not only have the legal and political rights of women been extended but women today are achieving recognition in most fields of human activity. It is not the purpose of this lesson to suggest that there is or should be absolute equality between men and women. As we study the role of women in the Church, in succeeding lessons, it will be apparent that men and women are each important in the eyes of our Heavenly Father. 'Their assignments are so distinct and different it is obvious that neither occupies an inferior position to the other.
