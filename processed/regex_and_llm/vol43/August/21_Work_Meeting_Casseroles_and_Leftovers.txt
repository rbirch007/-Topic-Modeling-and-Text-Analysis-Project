Work Meeting) Lesson 2—Casseroles and Leftovers Rhea H. Gardner For Tuesday, November 13, 1956 CASSEROLES play an important role in meal planning. A casserole may be prepared two or more weeks before it is to be served, if it sot is stored in a home freezer; or a day or two if it is stored in the refrigerator. A good casserole has qualities that make it distinctive and give it character; for instance, the meat should be cut in pieces large enough to be readily identified, and vegetables should be chosen that will combine well with the protein food that is used. In addition to flavor, it is important to think also of color and texture. Precook fresh vegetables only until they are about half done so they will not be overcooked when the casserole is ready to be served. Many frozen vegetables, such as peas, corn, and green beans, may be added while still icy. Never mince vegetables, except those used primarily for seasoning. Whole rings of carrots, or if they are small and garden fresh, whole carrots, whole or half lengths of string beans, clusters of bright green broccoli or snowy white cauliflower give character and appetite appeal to a casserole dish. Sutble seasoning is a goal every good cook strives for and is especially important in casseroles. Casserole dishes are best prepared in low, flat baking dishes rather than in deep, smaller ones. Casseroles that are too deep are not likely to be cooked thoroughly. Much of the attractiveness of a casserole dish is created in the topping, and there should be some of the topping given with each serving. Casseroles are excellent for outdoor meals. For your picnic parties, choose those that carry well. Because a high percentage of foods used in casseroles is comparatively soft and not particularly colorful, make sure that you include garnishes, relishes, and salad foods RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—AUGUST 1956 that are crisp and colorful for the balance of your menu. To make a casserole for freezing, prepare it according to the regular recipe, but do not add the topping. If you plan to use it within a week or two, cover the casserole with waxed paper, aluminum foil or a bowl cover before placing it in the freezer. A moisture-vapor-proof wrapper or freezer tape to seal the lid to the dish is necessary when the casserole is to be stored for a longer period of time. When the casserole comes out of the freezer, add the topping and it is ready to bake after it has reached room temperature. Casserole dishes are just one way to use leftovers. Croquettes, meat pies, sandwich fillings, stews, stuffed peppers, meat loaves, chowders and soups are other ways in which leftovers may be attractively prepared. The same rules as those recommended for making a good casserole should be followed. If much of the roast or boiled meat you plan to use is in tiny bits, grind it and, with the use of a binder, such as a thick sauce, crumbs or eggs, form it into balls or in a loaf. Always add one or two fresh vegetables when making a stew or meat pie. A change of seasoning can do much to make an "old dish" taste fresh as "morning dew." Leftover vegetables may be creamed and served as a sauce over a cheese or fish souffle, noodle ring, or croquettes. If you do not plan to use leftovers soon, put them in suitable containers in your freezer or the coldest part of your refrigerator until you can use them. Decide while the LESSON DEPARTMENT food is very fresh: (1) Is there enough to be served by itself or must it be combined with other foods? (2) Can it be cut in regular pieces or is it so broken that it must be chopped or ground? (3) Is it 299 moist enough to be palatable or must it be served with a sauce? (4) What ways of serving it will be best? Once you have made these decisions, you will be ready to make the best use of your leftovers. Note 'Casserole Recipes" by Rhea H.
