Sutherland Synopsis: Annie Griffith, whose father, with his family, has been called on a mission to the mountains to cut stone for the temple, is living with the Williams family in Salt Lake City, and is being educated with Marie Williams under the direction of a private tutor. Both Annie and Marie are very much interested in a neighbor, Parker Josephson, who leaves to attend an eastern university to learn to be a teacher of science. A farewell dinner for Parker is given at the Williams home, and Annie and Marie give him, as a going-away remembrance, a shirt which they have made. Both the girls are lonely after Parker has left, but they continue their studies, and finally a letter arrives from Parker, addressed to both of the girls. HE next two years passed quickly. Marie's seventeenth birthday was celebrated with a party in the Williams' home. Both girls had put their hair up. Annie, reluctantly, because Marie insisted. "Annie is content," Marie exclaimed, "to stay looking like a little girl for the next ten years." "It's not that," Annie said. "I just don't think it matters too much." "Matters!" Marie exclaimed. "Look in the glass and see how much it matters!" Annie had to admit there was some change when she looked at herself. The dark, soft, full wings of hair exposed the high cheekbones and the soft firm line of her chin. It made one notice the soft curve of her mouth and how wide and large her dark blue eyes were. Marie looked older, too, but she had always looked more mature for her age than Annie. Marie's father lamented that, in place of his two Page 538 little girls, two young women had come to stay. "Oh, do you really think so?" Marie had exclaimed happily, but Annie had felt a moment of sadness. She hadn't wanted to grow up away from her own family, but now she had, almost. The young people insisted Annie sing at Marie's party. After she had sung favorites that Marie's mother had taught her and Marie, she sang Welsh songs her father had taught her as a little girl. 'Then she protested she would sing no more and went out into the kitchen to help. Aunt Agnes came out of the pantry and patted Annie on the cheek. "You are a very extraordinary young lady, Annie. You should live in no one else's shadow." "Why, I don't," Annie said. Marie flung the door open. "Do hurry, Annie, they're waiting for refreshments. Oh, Annie, you sing so beautifully. If only I could do half the things you can do." "You can," Annie said. Marie sat on the window sill. "I can't. I can't sing as well, I can't play the piano as well, I'm not as smart, I can't sew as well; but I have the prettiest hair in the whole valley." oni Agnes cleared her throat sharply. She took out the three letters she had received from Parker during the three years he had been gone, and re-read them. "Whatever did you do with the letter Parker sent to you, Annie?" Marie looked at her. Annie leaned over her sewing. "It's around somewhere, or perhaps I lost it. You are a goose, Marie." "She is," said Aunt Agnes. "That's too bad," Annie said. She was setting the table for the evening meal. "No, it isn't. I mean he seems more serious or something. Oh, I don't know what I mean." Annie went back into the pantry for more plates. She shook her head angrily at herself. Why should she feel sad tonight, the night before a party where she was going to wear a dress more beautiful than she had ever dreamed she might own? It was heavy taffeta that changed in the light from full pink to dark rose. BVERYONE went to the dance. The few older people, who felt they couldn't dance, sat on the benches and chairs around the hall. At one end of the hall was spread a large table of planks, and this was covered with white cloths.. There refreshments would be served. The music was lively and quick; the happy sound of a violin rose above the voices, and Annie's heart beat faster just hearing it. Everyone arrived on time, because no one wanted to miss any of the fun. Annie nodded her head quickly, while Marie's voice tinkled on. Parker did look older and steadier. But the little quirk about his mouth that told you any moment he might laugh, was still there. And he was as kind as ever. Annie moved on quickly, because people pressed behind her, and, under his eyes, she became all of a sudden very disconcerted. Marie's voice was in her ear. "Oh, Annie, I'm so glad Papa made us go with him and Mama. If I had gone with the Johnson family, Parker would have been terribly jealous over Billy Johnson, and it would have ruined everything. Just think, I have the first dance with Parker. Won't people be nodding their heads over that!" She tapped an impatient toe, while her skirts rippled like soft waves. The hall was soon filled with people, and Marie and Annie found themselves dancing all the sets. Annie thought how much her family would enjoy this party. Her parents loved dancing and were never - too tired to join in whenever music could be found. Annie felt as if she were floating instead of dancing to the music. It was so much fun.
