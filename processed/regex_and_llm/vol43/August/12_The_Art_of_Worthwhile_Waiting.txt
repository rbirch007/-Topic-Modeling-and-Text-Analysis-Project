She Art of Worthwhile Waiting Pauline Henderson OW wonderful it would be if, at the end of our life, we could add up all the hours, days, and years that we have spent just waiting for something, and be allowed to add them to our total span of life! To say "I hate waiting," however, is to say in effect, "I hate living," for while we wait, life is going on and we are growing older. If we believe that life is good and worthwhile, then it must naturally follow that life is too precious to waste even one minute in the state of bored frustration that to most of us is synonymous with waiting. How many times do we say, "If I only had more time to read!" and then, the next time we are forced to sit and wait, spend the time in mental condemnation of the waiting instead of reading. I know a woman whose wide knowledge astonished me because I knew that she had had only a grade-school education and was the busy mother of a growing family. Then one day I saw her collection of paper-backed books by well-known authors. "I always carry one with me in my purse," she told me, "to read when I have to wait for a bus or at the dentist with the children, or anyplace." Yet it is not necessary to read in order to benefit from these brief periods of waiting. We hear much talk today of the too fast pace we are living. We are told that we hurry too much—relax too little. But even the fastest paced life has its periods of enforced waiting, time which is generally spent in a state of acute impatience that only increases the tension of our lives. How much more profitable it would be to use this time for relaxation —a sort of mental unwinding —a time to let go —to meditate — yes — even to pray, for it is not necessary to be on one's knees in order to pray effectively. Most of us, at least once in our lives, are faced with a period of prolonged waiting. Perhaps it is for the promotion that will bring us greater security — or the return of good health after a time of illness — or the agonizing waiting for the return of a loved one who is away. These are the times that wrench our souls, when frustration builds up within us to a high point, as we wait to reach the culmination of our desires. We spend our time in a state of suspended animation, and each day becomes merely a deary succession of hours to be lived through-as quickly as possible. What a shameful waste of this wonderful gift of life! The time that we must spend in waiting. Is an integral part of our lives — time that, once wasted, will never come again — a time to be lived and enjoyed and made the most of — not wasted in idly marking time. When a situation reaches the point when there is nothing more to be done except wait, then we are automatically released from the necessity of doing anything about it. The struggle is ended, and we are free to give ourselves to something else and let time itself take over. This is the time to take on something new — preferably something as unrelated to the thing we are waiting for as possible. 'That course in music that we have always dreamed of taking someday, or the hobby we have always wanted to pursue —or best of all—seek out someone less fortunate than ourselves and lend a helping hand. If we will spend our waiting time in such a manner, we will be almost surprised to find one. Day that the waiting has ended — our dream has become a reality, and we are ready to enjoy it with a fresh outlook and a new sense of values.
