EDITORIAL <2 VOL. 43 AUGUST 1956 NO. 8 She Priceless Gift of Peace ON the evening before the crucifixion, the Savior stood in the midst of his eleven faithful disciples who had unswervingly followed him for about three years, albeit with little understanding of his role as the Savior of the world. Heavy with impending events waiting for fulfillment, Jesus looked upon those whom the Father had given him out of the world and in love bestowed a parting gift on them—a gift which awaits each one today, if he will but accept it. While still one with them, Christ promised: Peace I leave with you, my peace I give unto you: not as the world giveth, give I unto you. Let not your heart be troubled, neither let it be afraid (John 14:27). As with all gifts from the eternal worlds, this gift must be earned to be possessed, but it is offered freely to all. Every Latter-day Saint woman may possess this peace deep in her soul, which will constantly guide her through the shoals of everyday living to a rock-fast haven in which she guards her knowledge of the love of her Eternal Father for her, and of the mission of his Son, her Savior. Through righteous living, she may comfort herself with the words and live the injunction, " Let not your heart be troubled, neither let it be afraid." Although the atomic age has Page 526 brought a new fear into hearts, and peace in the human soul is badly needed, women of all times and ages in the world's history have been tried and tested, and the blessing of peace has always been of greatest value. A wife and mother is at times sorely tried. When disagreements and misunderstandings arise, her emotions and feelings may become almost desperate. It is then she should recall the words of the Savior promising her peace, and pray and work with herself. When she humbles herself and takes the guidance of the spirit—then this gift floods her being and her heart becomes calm and unafraid. If a Latter-day Saint woman takes the time, when her feelings are sore, to sit down and read the scriptures, that also helps to bring calmness to her. Peace again may be banished from her heart when sickness strikes her family; however, by recalling the words of the Savior in his great hour of anguish, "... Not my will, but thine, be done" (Luke 22:42), and by placing implicit faith in the Lord, leaving the outcome to him, a woman may regain peace within her soul. What mother is there that does not need the "peace... Which passeth all understanding" as she tries to lead her children in the path of righteousness? It is a fact that when a mother's heart is worried EDITORIAL and troubled, when household cares press heavily upon her, and other responsibilities almost overwhelm her, then her actions and words will reflect the state of her inward feelings for "... Out of the abundance of the heart the mouth speaketh" (Matt. 12:34). Think of the difference in the words of advice from a mother whose heart is full of uncertainty, fear, worry, and dissatisfaction, with the guiding words which come from a mother whose 527 heart is untroubled, unafraid, and at peace. Such a wise mother looks beyond her anxieties of the moment, household pressures, and multiplicity of duties, to keep ever foremost in her heart the great goal of an eternal marriage and the eternity of family relationships dependent upon righteous living.
