Vacation Mecca on the Rio Grande Nell Murbarger NE of the newest and least () known of our nation's playgrounds, Big Bend National Park, lifts above the dry plains of Western Texas like a miracle island in a sea of grass. Slashed by deep, spectacular gorges, softened by flower-filled canyons, and shaded by the bold summits of the Chisos Mountains, here is a vacationer's land of delight! More than four centuries have passed since Cabeza de Vaca became the first white man of modern times to look upon this strange mountain empire along the Rio Grande, but only in recent years has the Big Bend country become readily accessible to motorists. Chief factor contributing to that end was the paving of State Route 227, leading south to the park from U. S. Highway go, at Marathon. After dipping and winding for eighty miles through a rough, volcanic region, Route 227 reaches its southern terminus in a scenic, pleasantly wooded cove, at the foot of high Casa Grande Peak. In addition to park administrative offices, here are provided a few tourist cottages, a postoffice, store, cafe, gasoline station, and campground — a road's end cluster of buildings which represents the largest settlement in a land area larger than Massachusetts! Even more remarkable is the fact that only rods from the village lies the borderland of a wild empire virtually unchanged from the time of the exploring Spaniard Cabeza de Vaca — an area still picturesque and unspoiled by civilization. Situated in a great elbow of the Page 520 Rio Grande, 300 miles southeast of E:] Paso, Big Bend National Park ranges in altitude from 2000 feet on the river flats to more than a mile and a half above sea level in the Chisos, and even midsummer finds temperatures singularly pleasant in the headquarters area and higher elevations — the mercury seldom rising above eighty-seven degrees. In winter, the Big Bend country enjoys the advantages of a latitudinal position nearly 400 miles farther south than Los Angeles; and although flurries of snow may fall on the highest summits, temperatures in the middle-to-lower elevations remain pleasantly warm, with healthful dry air and bright sunshine. Next to climatic factors, one of the chief matters to consider in choosing a suitable vacation spot is the question of keeping occupied —the business of "what to do?" In that connection, it seems only fair to issue a word of warning: the Big Bend is still undeveloped along lines of both plushy elegance and hurdy-gurdy cheapness. Persons whose vacationing pleasure depends upon luxurious accommodations and proximity to golf courses, dance halls, moving picture theatres, and midway-type amusements, will find other resort areas more to their liking. URE-footed saddle horses are available for rent at reasonable rates, and from the vicinity of headquarters, a network of safe-hiking and horseback trails fans out through beautifully wooded canyons to many places of interest and spectacular viewpoints. One such trail (restricted to hikers) leads to Totem Rock, near the high, exposed summit of Lost Mine Peak; another foot and horse trail leads to "The Window," a perpendicular rock slit commanding a view of thousands of acres of wild land on both sides of the Rio Grande. Persons ambitious enough to undertake a full-day's horseback ride will find a worthy destination in the South Rim; while packmules and burros are available for overnight, or even more extensive trips. Nature lovers in almost every category will find any of these trips productive of myriad delights. Quail 522 and wild doves add their plaintive notes to the wild concert led by mockingbirds and warblers, and the patient bird watcher even may be favored by the sight of a rare hooded oriole or blue-throated hummingbird. More than 200 species of birds have been catalogued here. March to June finds a dozen species of cactuses flaunting their vivid blooms on rocky knolls; April is the month when Spanish daggers (yuccas) lift their spikes of creamy white bells; and with June and July, the tall heads of the century plants burst into golden glory — their flower stems several times taller than a man! Even late autumn finds the canyons a massed delight of wild flowers and ferns. Forest trees found in the park include Arizona cypress, Douglas fir, Ponderosa and Mexican pinion pines, alligator-barked, and weeping junipers, 'l'exas madrones, maples, and several species of oaks, and, in these environs, more than sixty species of animals make their homes. Inviting one-day side trips easily accessible in the family car, include a thirty-five-mile jaunt to Santa Elena Canyon, where the Rio Grande winds tortuously through a gorge, eighteen miles in length, with rock walls rising sheerly on either side to heights as much as a quarter of a mile above the river! One: wall of this fantastic gorge stands in Mexico — the other in the United States. A short way down river is Boquillas Canyon, of comparable depth; and Mariscal, deepest and most spectacular of three river-hewn slits. For more than fifty years one of the nation's major sources of mercury, Terlingua once boasted the largest population of any mining town in Texas. Only a few old buildings and mine dumps remain. Another interesting side trip may be made into the Grapevine Hills, a weird region of balanced rocks, picturesque stone formations, and strangely eroded spires. Other points of interest in the park include a fossil deposit which has yielded a wide variety of prehistoric marine life, including oysters thirty inches in length, clams four feet across, and a_ forty-five-foot stone crocodile. Long before the coming of American explorers and_ settlers, superstitious Spanish folk had applied to these mountains the name "Chisos," meaning "phantoms" or "ghosts." Although doubtless inspired by the long, gauzy, cloud streamers, which so often wrap about the rock-crowned summit of Casa Grande Peak, it isn't at all difficult to invest that word with a meaning less materialistic. After spending a few days and nights in this still, brooding land of great heights and dark depths, it becomes strangely easy to imagine a figure of the long-ago shuffling along the Lost Mine trail, or sitting under the friendly pines looking out across the jumbled wilderness of the Sierra del Carmen to the distant mountains of Mexico, ranged tier upon blue tier. Neither is it difficult to imagine the former inhabitant watching the serrated peaks as sunset clothes them in crimson and gold, soon blended to royal purple and darkened, at last, to a soft robe of midnight velvet.
