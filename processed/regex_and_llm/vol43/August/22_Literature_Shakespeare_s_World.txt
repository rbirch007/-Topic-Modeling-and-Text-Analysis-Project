HE late Elizabethan period is probably the richest in all English literature, primarily because Shakespeare and his fellow-dramatists stated in such glowing words the conflicts, hopes, and fears of their most exciting age. During preceding decades and centuries the theater itself had been molded into the magnificent art-form which was ready for Shakespeare's guiding hand. Under his touch it flowered into one of man's most powerful devices for communicating his inner self. King Lear, V, 2. 2 If Elizabethan man had had no burning inner struggle or dream, the theater, polished and poetic though it may have been, would have lacked material of sufficient stature, since the essence of great drama must be great conflict. The time was ripe for the emergence of some great spokesman for the English soul. 'The theater was ripe. But in order to understand why the Elizabethan air was so heavily charged with conflict, we must first examine the traditional belief of the "ordered universe," and the threats emerging to challenge this belief. The Ordered Universe Throughout the Middle Ages one of the unquestioned commonplaces had been Aristotle's viewpoint that "God and nature create nothing that has not its use." 'The general belief was that God's over-all purpose in creating all nature and the universe was to glorify himself, and that the best way to glorify him was to know him. It was believed this could best be accomplished by studying the two great books which God had given man for this very purpose, "the Bible, and the book of the universal order of things, or of nature," as Ramon Sabunde wrote in his Natural Theology, about 1425. Central to both the natural and the divine orders, indeed the bridge between the two, was man who, by his own free will, could make of himself either angel or beast. And as man was created to serve God, so all nature was created to serve man. According to this belief, man, then, created in the image of God was key to the entire universe; indeed, man was but a tiny model of the universe. Man must be studied and known, as must every phase of nature. Since God is perfect, so must man and nature be perfect, both in themselves and in their inter-relationship. The "Primary Law of Nature" instilled in man various lesser laws which he must obey: to worship God; to obey parents and civil government; to rear and educate children; to give equal liberty to all; and to hurt no man. 'The same natural law considered kings the rightful rulers of England, since a king was servant of the greater law which reigned above. Within the English commonwealth were recognized four orders of men, each self-contained and content to abide by his own law; namely, gentleman, citizen, yeoman, and laborer. Three levels of order—man, the civil body or state, and the universe —were all believed to be inter-related, and that the law could not be broken in one realm without causing chaos in the other two. Because they thought the movements of the planets governed the lives of men, astrology was not considered black magic, but as an established revelation of the will of God. Ulysses stated the concept in Shakespeare's Troilus and Cressida: The heavens themselves, the planets and this center, Observe degree, priority, and place. But when the planets In evil mixture to disorder wander, What plagues and what portents, what mutiny, What raging of the sea, shaking of earth, Commotion in the winds, frights, changes, horrors, Divert and crack, rend and deracinate, The unity and married calm of states Quite from their fixture! Oh, when degree is shaked, Which is the ladder to all high designs, The enterprise is sick!
