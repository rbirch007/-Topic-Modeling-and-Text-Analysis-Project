The Ordered Universe Throughout the Middle Ages one of the unquestioned commonplaces had been Aristotle's viewpoint that "God and nature create nothing that has not its use." 'The general belief was that God's over-all purpose in creating all nature and the universe was to glorify himself, and that the best way to glorify him was to know him. It was believed this could best be accomplished by studying the two great books which God had given man for this very purpose, "the Bible, and the book of the universal order of things, or of nature," as Ramon Sabunde wrote in his Natural Theology, about 1425. Central to both the natural and the divine orders, indeed the bridge between the two, was man who, by his own free will, could make of himself either angel or beast. And as man was created to serve God, so all nature was created to serve man. According to this belief, man, then, created in the image of God was key to the entire universe; indeed, man was but a tiny model of the universe. Man must be studied and known, as must every phase of nature. Since God is perfect, so must man and nature be perfect, both in themselves and in their inter-relationship. The "Primary Law of Nature" instilled in man various lesser laws which he must obey: to worship God; to obey parents and civil government; to rear and educate children; to give equal liberty to all; and to hurt no man. 'The same natural law considered kings the rightful rulers of England, since a king was servant of the greater law which reigned above. Within the English commonwealth were recognized four orders of men, each self-contained and content to abide by his own law; namely, gentleman, citizen, yeoman, and laborer. Three levels of order—man, the civil body or state, and the universe —were all believed to be inter-related, and that the law could not be broken in one realm without causing chaos in the other two. Because they thought the movements of the planets governed the lives of men, astrology was not considered black magic, but as an established revelation of the will of God. Ulysses stated the concept in Shakespeare's Troilus and Cressida: The heavens themselves, the planets and this center, Observe degree, priority, and place. Conflicting Doctrines to "Ordered Universe" A contrasting doctrine had appeared with John Calvin's Institutes of Christian Religion, published in 1536, perhaps the most influential book of the Protestant movement. Calvin stated that, because of Adam's fall, man was "'a shapeless ruin, partly ruined, partly corrupted..." whose will did not perish but "was so enslaved by depraved lusts as to be incapable of one righteous desire.' 'Thus in Adam's fall the order of nature was reversed, and so the body ruled the soul. Since man was considered fallen creature, 957 Copyright 1901 SHAKESPEARE'S HOME, STRATFORD-ON-AVON, ENGLAND nature was no longer to serve him; instead, the elements, beasts, birds, and plants, were now ready to offend him. Here we see the basic conflict in the nature of man, one which all Elizabethans felt and knew. On the one hand, man was thought of as perfection and the center of the universe; on the other hand he was a corrupted animal, ruled by his own appetites, living on a vile planet. But, even though fallen, man was so important that the Son of God came to earth to save him, and man could maintain order by fulfilling God's principles of grace and redemption. But while the Elizabethans were aware of their "ordered existence," they were forced with increasing urgency to acknowledge four threats to it. (1) Copernicus (1473- 1543) had dared suggest that the sun, not the earth, was the center 558 of the universe, a view later substantiated by Galileo (1564-1642) who, with the first telescope, found rough craters on the moon, thus proving that, at least, some celestial spheres were not perfect orbs. (2) The Italian Machiavelli's most bitterly hated book, The Prince (1513) had defied the concept of a just, God-governed order for people by naming, instead, self-interest, craftiness, power, fear, and unscrupulousness as the actual tools which every sovereign used to insure his_personal dominance. (3) The French satirist Montaigne's Essays (1580) had presented man as lower than the animals, since, unlike man, animals never waged organized warfare against their own kind. (4) Even more devastating had been the well-known writings of Francis Bacon (1561-1626) who had never attacked the concept of an "ordered universe": he had ignored it. He had held it to be man's duty to separate scientific from religious or moral knowledge. Instead of conjecturing about intangible mysteries, he believed it was man's duty to learn the secrets of the natural world about him, that he might put them to use for his own benefit. Whenever men have shared their mutual hopes, fears, joys, and hungers, then they have most nearly become brothers. How fully the Elizabethans predicted modern man in their own great hunger to hear their own thoughts expressed in words! Shakespeare, then, through the Elizabethan stage, fulfilled this hunger, thus linking together men of that time, and of all time henceforth. These great conflicts in the religious, philosophical, and scientific beliefs of the Elizabethans were bound to have their influences on the writings of Shakespeare. Shakespeare's Life Shakespeare was born in 1564 at Stratford-on-Avon, a rural metropolis some eighty miles west and north of London. His parents were solid, middle-class people, his father a glove maker who was, at one time, mayor. His mother was the daughter of a local farming family tich in lands. Shakespeare must have attended the local grammar school, which had Oxford graduates as headmasters, and which gave him a solid acquaintance with important books, notably the Bible and the Greek classics. Actual proofs of how he spent his early years are sparse, but we do have record of his marrying Anne Hathaway when he was in his eighteenth year, she in her twenty-sixth. Some four years later he went to London, leaving his wife and three children in Stratford. By 1592, when he was twenty-eight, he had established in London a reputation as an actor and outstanding playwright. It is not known why his LESSON DEPARTMENT wife remained in Stratford during the next twenty years, but it is known that, not too many years after Shakespeare left Stratford, the local Puritans became sufficiently strong to pass an ordinance forbidding the performance of any plays. In London Shakespeare _ prospered. He became part owner of a playhouse, The Globe, and wrote all the plays for a company of actors who stayed together for two decades, and, under James I, came to be known as "The King's Men." During his life there he wrote thirtysix or thirty-seven plays, a sequence of sonnets, and two long poems. He invested his money in the second largest house in Stratford, bought the tithes of the local parish as an investment, and was one of the wealthiest landowners in the district. In 1611, after writing his final play, the highly successful The Tempest, he retired to Stratford, where he lived the life of a respected country gentleman until his death in 1616. His detailed will directed most of his property and lands to be given to his daughters and sister. He left money to several friends in Stratford and to actorfriends in London to buy memorial rings, and to his wife he bequeathed his second-best bed, a sign neither of bitterness nor of affection since she was entitled to one-third of all his property and use of the house as long as she lived. To us, the most interesting part of his will, as it reveals Shakespeare's belief, reads as follows: I commend my soul into the hands of God my creator, hoping, and assuredly believing, through the only merits of Jesus Chnist, my Saviour, to be made partaker of life everlasting. (Burgess: The Bible in Shakespeare, page 113). Soo Shakespeare's London "The spacious times of the great Elizabeth" were in Elizabethans' hearts and on their backs, not in their physical London. Roads were little more than mudbogs, and the recently invented carriages were so awkward and rough that most people still travelled horseback — that is, if they travelled at all. Less than a mile square and still surrounded by its medieval wall, London bulged with about 200,000 people living in crowded, squalid quarters, with great noise from carts, wagons, coopers, potmakers, and shopkeepers rushing forth constantly yelling, "What do ye lack?" On the streets everywhere hawkers chanted, "Small coals!' or "Hot peas!" or "Hot, fine oatcakes!" Porters sweated under their burdens in the narrow, crooked streets choked with filth, while open sewers, which were constantly a hazard to travel, carried off the garbage and refuse left in the streets until it rained (though human waste was carried off to sea in barges). No one bathed, but the rich used perfume to sweeten their persons, even as scents were used in homes to counteract the smell of mildewed, rotting rushes which were used as floor coverings. Rats and vermin bred freely in the large slums, and the plague was an annual summer visitor. If, during the hot, damp plague season more than thirty persons died in a week, the playhouses were closed temporarily, and, for at least two summers in Shakespeare's time, the plague forced the closing of the playhouses during the entire season. 'True daughter of the Renaissance that she was, she loved music and dancing, and constantly defended play production on the grounds that where else could actors keep in practice for her royal command in her palaces at regular intervals, particularly during the Christmas season? In 1485 thirty years of civil war had ended, and the Tudor line of kings, climaxed by Henry VIII, had built a strong, unified England; but the fear of anarchy and mob rule was ever in English hearts. 'The English defeated their great enemy, Spain, by sinking her Armada in 1588, but a more successful Spanish invasion was forever imminent and greatly feared. Drake and Hawkins pillaged the Spanish Main, while other daring Englishmen sank a Spanish fleet in the Bay of Cadiz and then sacked and burned the city. During Shakespeare's lifetime England was ever at war, first with Spain, then France, then Ireland. Thus Shakespeare in his plays proved his intimate knowledge of his fellow countrymen. His great success was not by chance, since he knew so well what they loved and hated. 'The Elizabethan Englishman was intensely patriotic, dressed most lavishly, loved display of words, action, dress, music, and dance. He loved rough sports: gambling, bulland-bear baiting, and, above all, the drama. He was coarse and brutal, and loved to witness hangings, of which it is said more than three uundred took place each year in London alone. Young men brawled in the streets day and night, hardly 561 considering themselves "men" until they bore the scars of swordplay. At night London was a terror to the honest and older people. With no police force, citizens were appointed as constables, but their hearts were not in it, and gangs of rogues, rowdies, and thieves roamed the night city at will. The Maturing Shakespeare While the young Shakespeare made it his business to give in his plays what London wanted, we can still trace his maturing by his growing contempt and hatred for brutality, indecency, infidelity, coarseness, and any taint of uncleanliness. Nor were these the only qualities of his mind: constancy, bravery, courage, loyalty, love in the home, he esteemed; while cowardice, cruelty, usury, slavery, hypocrisy, he roundly condemned. And so we see this gallant definer and defender of our heritage strenuously at crossfire — with himself, his world, his past, and his future. He was the typical Elizabethan mind and heart. Thus, for the Elizabethan age, man was potentially an angel, yet rotting in his grave; sensitive to the most ornamental poetry and loving vocal music so fully that everyone could "bear his part," yet hungry for gory spectacle in theater, war, and tortures both animal and human; patriotic to the core, yet extremely touchy of individual rights and wary of exploitation or slavery; hungry for facts, for practical knowledge, yet still believing "there might be something in" ghosts, witches, and superstition; eager for personal wealth, yet indifferent to, if not disdainful of the suffering poor forever surrounding 562 him — the list might be extended, even swollen, almost to infinity. These traits are those of adolescence. The young in heart have ever felt the appeal of spectacle, speech, sparkle, and spirit. Particularly in the first half of his career Shakespeare wrote to young audiences, of young audiences. Actually, he wrote of a young nation to a young nation — one which has never been bounded by time, for the young in heart are always with us. The Elizabethan age was youth, incorporating within itself all its inner fears, insecurities, grandiose dreams, weaknesses and hopes.
