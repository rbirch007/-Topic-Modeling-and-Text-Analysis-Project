The Merchant of Venice The play was probably written in 1594, and was acted many times previous to its presentation before King James I, in 1605. He enjoyed it so much that it was again presented at court two days later. We have no record of its being performed again for almost one hundred years after King James' command performance. From 1701 to 1741 Shylock was presented as a farcical, comic rogue; after 1741 he achieved status and depth at the hands of more perceptive interpret- LESSON DEPARTMENT ers. During the nineteenth and twentieth centuries his role was coveted by great Shakespearean actors. The Merchant of Venice has been most popular, both with audiences and actors. Traditionally, its appeal has not been questioned; why it appeals is still a matter of controversy. Some critics feel Shylock to be entirely alien to the play, a hard, bitter miser of another species who intrudes gratingly upon the sweet music and romantic adventures of a delightful Shakespearean comedy. For others, this light tone, beauty, and "happy ending" merely provide continuity and a chance for members of Shakespeare's acting company to perform this moral essay dealing with justice, mercy, tolerance, ways of getting money, and unselfish friendship. Since Shakespeare's works have been all things to all men, this issue remains unresolved. Plot The setting of the play is in Venice, Italy, and provides a return to the land of the classics which held such glamor and excitement for Elizabethan Englishmen interested in new learning and world-wide culture. Antonio, an honest bachelor merchant, is approached by a handsome, spendthrift Bassanio, his best friend, who needs three thousand ducats to woo Portia, the beautiful, witty, wealthy lady of Belmont. Because Antonio's money is tied up in shipping, he finds it necessary to ask the Jewish moneylender Shylock for the loan, offering his ships and cargoes as security. Because he hates usury so intensely (and hence Shylock) Antonio always loaned his own money with- 771 out charging interest, and, likewise, did not believe in paying interest; but Bassanio's urgent need forced him to make an exception. As a gesture of friendship toward Christian Antonio, who had but recently spat upon him, Shylock agrees to make the loan without charging interest, but "in merry sport" suggests Antonio sign a bond for security which will permit Shylock to cut a pound of flesh wherever he chooses from Antonio's body, if the loan is not repaid within three months. Feeling that Shylock has even been kind, and confident that at least one of his vessels will return soon, Antonio signs the bond and gives Bassanio the money, who leaves at once for Belmont with his friend Gratiano to renew his acquaintance with Portia and try to win her hand. Before the death of Portia's wise and wealthy father he had devised a test for selecting her husband. All her many suitors are to be shown three small caskets. On the gold one is written, "Who chooseth me shall gain what many men desire." The silver casket carries the promise, "Who chooseth me shall get as much as he deserves," while the lead casket warns, "Who chooseth me must give and hazard all he hath." If the suitor unlocks the casket which contains Portia's portrait, she becomes his bride. The gold casket contains a skull, the silver casket the portrait of a blinking idiot, while the leaden casket, which Bassanio chooses because of hints contained in a song which is sung as he deliberates, contains Portia's portrait. As symbol of her love, Portia gives him a ring, which 772 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1956 he promises to wear forever; likewise her maid Nerissa gives a ring to Gratiano, who has just wooed and won her. Lorenzo, another of Bassanio's friends, arrives at Belmont with lovely, fiery Jessica, daughter of Shylock, who has just deserted her lone father, taking jewelry and several bags of his ducats as she elopes with her Christian husband. The marriage vows are scarcely pronounced when word comes that none of Antonio's ships has arrived, and Shylock has appealed to the justice of the Venice court for his pound of flesh. Bassanio and Gratiano leave their wives in the care of Lorenzo, and return to Venice to help Antonio. But Portia refuses to be left out, so, disguising herself as a young lawyer with Nerissa disguised as her clerk, she artives at the trial just as things are going badly for Antonio. Assuming the name of Balthasar, a young doctor of Rome, Portia acts as judge, first attempting to soften Shylock in her famous speech on mercy. Unsuccessful, she awards Shylock the pound of flesh, instructs Antonio to bare his bosom to Shylock's knife—when gallant Bassanio offers to take Antonio's place and give his life for his friend. Then Portia, calling attention to the requirements of the bond, forbids Shylock to take one drop of blood on pain of death. Shylock relents. 'Then Portia states the Venetian law which permits the state to seize half the property of anyone conspiring to murder, but Antonio refuses to take his legal half on condition that Shylock become a Christian and leave his property to Lorenzo and Jessica at his death. Portia charges no fee for saving Antonio, but, instead, accepts the rings of Bassanio, her husband, and Gratiano, as tokens of gratitude. All return to Belmont where, as they return, Lorenzo and_ Jessica speak lyric lines in praise of love and night. But in presenting him to us, he raises many of the questions which vexed his contemporaries and teased them into attempting answers just as they vex and tease us today. For example, what questions can be more pertinent for our own day than the following: (a) Is money to be gained in life at the expense of higher values? (b) For a Christian or for a Jew is it "honorable" to gain money—by usury, by leaving a father but taking his wealth, by a court decision which is more just than merciful? (c) Can it be that some of us are daily being seduced by the golden casket to choose it, rather than the unglamorous lead casket? Shakespeare does not pretend to answer these and other soul-search- 773 ing questions in the play; he does embody such questions in characters and relationships, that we may see artistic reality and judge for ourselves. He leads us to a larger understanding of the people concerned, that we may judge more wisely. Most of these questions are focused most sharply in_ three characters: Antonio, Portia, Shylock. HE success of the Relief Society was assured by its significant inception under the direction of the Priesthood of God. Inspiration from the same source during each of the 115 years of its existence has built an organization, strong but sensitive to need, and willing to serve. We shall now consider specific teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith regarding the relationship of women to the Priesthood, and the application of these teachings in present-day living. Instructions Through the Priesthood At an early meeting of the Relief Society the Prophet Joseph Smith clearly stated that the activities of the organization would always be directed by the Priesthood. You will receive instructions through the order of the Priesthood which God has established, through the medium of To show the role and responsibilities of Latter-day Saint women in those appointed to lead, guide and direct the affairs of the Church in this last dispensation (D. H. C. IV, page 607). The sisters sensed the importance of his directions and were eager to follow the Prophet's wise counsel during the formative years of the Relief Society's development. During his lifetime the Prophet, with other General Authorities, met with them often, giving refinements of the original instructions and timely suggestions to meet new situations which arose. A close-working relationship existed between them, and the women soon learned that their greatest strength stemmed from their willingness to serve as aids to the Priesthood. The members of the first Relief Society presidency were called to their respective offices by the Priesthood who set them apart individually, blessed them, and _ gave instructions regarding their new duties. 'This was carefully completed 778 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1956 before "president Emma Smith and her counselors took the chair." 'This procedure remains unchanged today, and is a great spiritual blessing to women of the Church, regardless of the capacity in which they are called to serve. Women Share Priesthood Blessings In April 1842, the Prophet gave a significant lecture on the Priesthood to the Relief Society. A synopsis of it, reported by Eliza R. Snow, concludes with this comment: "The Spirit of the Lord was poured out in a very powerful manner, never to be forgotten by those present on this interesting occasion" (lIbid., page 607). The Prophet explained how women of the Church would come into possession of the blessings of the Priesthood. He spoke of delivering the keys of the Priesthood to the Church, and said that the faithful members of the Relief Society should receive them in connection with their husbands He exhorted the sisters always to concentrate their faith and prayers for, and place confidence in their husbands, whom God has appointed for them to honor, and in those faithful men whom God has placed at the head of the Church to lead His people; that we should arm and sustain them with our prayers. You must put down iniquity, and by your own good example, stimulate the Elders to good works (Ibid., IV, pp. 604-605). He emphasized that women might attain the blessings of the Priesthood "by a virtuous life... And diligence in keeping all the commandments." He also cautioned: All must act in concert, or nothing can be done, and should move according to the ancient Priesthood; hence the Saints should be a select people, separate from the evils of the world—choice, virtuous, and holy (Ibid., IV, page 570). The late Elder John A. Widtsoe has written of the sharing by Latter-day Saint women in the blessings of the Priesthood: The Priesthood is for the benefit of all members of the Church. Men have no greater claim than women upon the Dlessings that issue from the Priesthood and accompany its possession.... The man holds the Priesthood, performs the priestly duties of the Church, but his wife enjoys with him every other privilege derived from the possession of the Priesthood. This is made clear, as an example, in the Temple service of the Church. The ordinances of the Temple are distinctly of Priesthood character, yet women have access to all of them, and the highest blessings of the Temple are conferred only upon a man and his wife jointly (Priesthood and Church Government, page 83). The fact that women are not ordained to the Priesthood should not be regarded as a denial of a blessing. The different and distinct roles of man and woman entitle them to different blessings, for indeed, "... Male and female created he them" (Gen. 1:27). Latter-day Saint women who do not have members of their families who hold the Priesthood, can still enjoy blessings of the Priesthood by calling upon the elders as need may arise to administer to their spiritual needs, and through Church service they may be directed by the Priesthood in their various callings. Perhaps the rarest gift within the power of God to bestow, that of motherhood, is a blessing only women can enjoy. Upholding Church Leaders Relief Society members are not only expected to sustain their husbands and sons in their Priesthood callings, but to uphold those who preside over the Church, whether they be General Authorities, stake officers, or ward leaders. Latter-day Saint women are given the opportunity in Church assemblies to cast a sustaining vote for the leaders of the Church. A vote to sustain and honor the leadership of the Church is a pledge to uphold those who preside. In the home, children should never hear criticism of Church leaders from the lips of their parents. Such an attitude might plant a seed which will not only destroy the child's confidence in the Authorities of the Church, but lessen his respect for parents who display such disloyalty. Abundant happiness and_blessings are found in the Latter-day Saint home where the husband and wife, with mutual affection, honor and sustain each other in their respective callings.
