Developing Safeguards for Youth Delbert L. Stapley Of the Council of the Twelve Ore of the serious problems confronting parents today is a growing tendency of moral laxity among youth. 'Too many parents are not fully aware that such a condition exists. 'Therefore, blind to the evils to which their children are exposed, unsuspectingly, therefore thoughtlessly, they contribute to an increase of immoral and sinful practices among them. Satan is not asleep during this age of freedom from restraints and fast living. But knowing his time is short and how best to attack the weaknesses of youth, he has cunningly schemed to take advantage of them. In a period when their developing emotions and physical attractions for the opposite sex begin to unfold. It is a new sensation and experience for which, with rare exception, young people are not prepared by proper and wise teaching to understand, therefore they have not developed adequate safeguards to control. Yes, Mothers, factual cases reveal an alarming breakdown of the true moral concepts of virtue so sacred and important to Church youth. The concepts of true modesty are disappearing in our present era of freedom in boy-girl relationships with abbreviated styles of dress which expose parts of the bodies that should be clothed as a protection against persons of evil mind and intent, who unscrupulously would take advantage without thought of cost to purity and cleanness of life, also of conscience. Page 718 May I suggest to you mothers the dressing of your daughters modestly. Give your daughters the protection modest dress invites. Immodesty is suggestive and in poor taste for the daughters of Zion. Encircle them always in the armor of righteousness to keep them free from undesired advances. The unrestrained freedom in early association of boys and girls without good teaching, counseling, and controls encourages familiarity and an open display of affection which leads too often to the intimacies of necking, petting, dare games, self-abuse, and immorality. Boys and girls are not only dating at very tender ages, but are pairing off as steadies and doing things together constantly, as monopolizing each other's dances, which build up familiarity, intimacies, and liberties that should signal caution to every parent. A police officer talking to a group said, "If you keep two young people close enough long enough, you are going to have difficulty." Desiring thrills is rather predominant in the lives of youth today. Automobiles used for parking on highways, in lonely places, and even in front of homes, are natural conveniences for necking and petting which induce youth to transgression and sin. Cars can be a curse as well as a blessing and are one of the greatest contributing factors to the downfall of youth in morals and the sacrifice of virtue. Cars and drive-in theaters can and do become DEVELOPING SAFEGUARDS FOR YOUTH brothels of sin to young couples alone in such darkened surroundings, with doors closed and windows up. So many of the motion pictures shown are of poor standard, emphasizing shapeliness of figure and are full of sordid and sexy scenes, which, in such an _ environment, arouse the physical emotions, and with the example before them on the screen cheapening morality, weaken the powers to resist the urge to sin. Young people are subjected to an abundance of trashy bookstand literature and novels that utterly disregard the commandments of the Lord relative to the relationship and association of the sexes. Modern philosophies of thinking tend toward materialism. As a result, youth become confused and frustrated. Many mothers, worried about the modern ways of courting in cars, are not wisely meeting the problem and question their right to limit and infringe upon the activities and social life of their children. Parents and Church leaders shy away from teaching young people the place of sex in dating and in growing up. Therefore, when emotional conflicts arise in the boy-girl relationships, and being without proper knowledge or understanding of moral principles and good standards of behavior nor fully realizing the consequences for violation of God's law of chastity, they, in a moment of weakness, commit sin. Later, they awake with an alarming realization of its seriousness, causing their souls to be filled with sorrow and remorse. Often they are unable to account for the act, for- 719 getting the emotional situation they had allowed themselves to get into. HIS soulful statement by a young girl is so often true: "We are not taught what chastity and morality really mean until it is too late." Then added, "When we girls were little and would ask questions about such things our mothers would say, 'shh, be quiet. You are not supposed to talk about such things.' So we read what we could from whatever source, then exchanged notes and ideas with each other. By the time we had developed physically, and had the urge to experiment and go out with fellows, we didn't go to our folks for help and information — we were afraid of being slapped down — so we just experimented some more until we had gone too far, or at least had guilt feelings, with no place to turn for help." Another girl explained, "Just a simple, honest answer to a few of my questions and problems which were so vital to me then may have made the big difference." Youth who sin often blame parents for not teaching them properly — the home failing to assist them in growing up emotionally, physically, and sexually. The subject is difficult to approach and somewhat distasteful, therefore it is neglected. Parents, no doubt, feel that they are inadequate to discuss such matters with their children because they lack an adequate vocabulary of moral terms that they can employ to advantage in such teachings. The primary teaching place for standards of morality and chastity is the home. Therefore, parents cannot escape 720 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1956 the responsibility of qualifying themselves to inform their children wisely, intelligently, and with understanding on these important, delicate matters, rather than permit them to learn from those whose concepts of right and wrong might be warped. OUTH should be taught early how to handle their emotions, parents revealing to them such facts as good judgment dictates they should know. The gospel with all of its truth should be put to work in a big way by parents when teaching children about these matters. Sex has a place in the gospel, and it is the job of parents properly to interpret it for the understanding of their children to encourage them to keep their bodies clean and pure and used only for the purpose for which God created them. Good teaching and proper motivation are essential in this important area of service to youth. Parents must teach morality as a gospel concept to their children. Virtue is in the great scheme of life as God has furnished the pattern. 'Therefore, teach. The value of the body, that it is an eternal habitation of the spirit. Young people should understand that truth, and thus gain the desire to keep the body clean, morally pure, and always clothed in righteousness, honorably fulfilling the purpose of its creation and thus be assured the joys of purity of life and clean conscience. Teach youth the reasons for the why of living a clean life. We are living in a modern world which cannot be changed. Young people must learn how to live in the presence of these modern conditions and still express a wholesome, integrated response to them. We should help build in each young person a set of standards and life objectives that would cause him to cut out the things that destroy and cultivate those that build security and happiness. Build up meaningfully the value of a clean life and make it desirable, attractive, positive, and inviting. Morality should not be taught always in a negative manner with emphasis upon its evils and_ remorses, but from a positive approach extolling the beauties and _satisfactions of a virtuous, clean, moral life, for virtue is its own reward. Parents must recognize that their children are exposed to multiple kinds of activities in this modern world of amusements and commercialism that invite the temptation to sin. So many activities are shining allurements that appear attractive, but whose pathway, if followed, will undermine the _ basic qualities of good character and virtue. Somehow parents must succeed in teaching their sons and daughters chastity as a way of life despite the existence of these and other temptations to which they are constantly subjected. Young girls like to be popular and sought after. Often they falsely assume that in order to "arrive" they must yield to advances and intimacies of boys. Do you pal with them? Do they share their problems and secrets with you, knowing that they shall receive sympathetic understanding and helpful counsel and guidance? Or do they prefer to go to young people of their own age and social group or clique to unburden their hearts concerning personal problems and seek counsel and advice that should come from the home? 'Too many young people are afraid to discuss intimate matters with parents because they are shrugged off for lack of interest, or time, or it is something not to be talked about, when to youth it is important and maybe a pressing matter, a problem with which they are presently faced. A satisfying answer and wise direction may be needed to protect and safeguard them against pitfalls that might be imminent in their lives. Youth require understanding and help in the process of growing up, for it is the most critical and important time in their lives. ARENTS should constantly analyze the home to be sure it is an ideal home according to the highest Church standards, with a proper environment conducive to joyful and fruitful living. Does the home promote love and affection? Is there respect of children toward parents and parents toward children? Are the teachings of the gospel fully lived in the home? What about daily family prayer? Is the recommended weekly family home night religiously followed? Is the family home program of activities and functions occasionally revised 721 to meet the changing needs of children in growing up to enliven interest and promote development and progress in family and social relationships? Is the gospel taught and the principles of morality discussed in language that will interest youth and give them understanding to help them obtain the basic, wholesome satisfactions in life which include deeper fundamental loyalties such as love of God, love of the Savior, love of the gospel, love of the Church, love of fellow men, self-respect, the attainment of a happy marriage and an ideal family life, which provide a bulwark against material and worldly things? Are parents counseling, guiding, and assisting their children to find and cultivate wholesome Church companionships that will as far as possible assure dating with Latter-day Saint boys and girls that eventually will lead to their marrying within the Church, and properly in the temples of our God? Have parents worthily set the example of temple marriage before their children and honorably fulfilled all covenants and obligations received and accepted in the house of the Lord? Can these important responsibilities and opportunities for blessings mean any more to children than to parents? Parents should reflect back upon their own youth and _ activities. Problems are not so much different between then and now except in today's more modern world of fewer restraints and less inhibitions they are found in diverse and various dress which parents are not excused from knowing if they desire to give wise counsel and guidance to their sons and daughters. 122 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1956 Can immorality be a contributing cause to fewer temple marriages? Do young people, and particularly those who have sinned, understand the principle of repentance and how it applies to obtain forgiveness for sins? If not, they may ignorantly assume all is lost and continue in their sins and thus lose opportunity for a fruitful and happy life, Parents should practically apply religion into family relationships and life, developing the art of living with children in love and helpfulness which represents a functional side of our religion. Religion should be on an _ understanding, practical plane where children can live and enjoy it. Parents should be careful not to domineer and unnecessarily restrict the wholesome and beneficial activities of Church, school, and the social life of youth. Admittedly, however, some restraints and rules of conduct for good behavior are required. There also should be a controlled pattern of parent activities to provide a rich home life and full association with children. If parents are out of the home too frequently or do not find opportunities for joint recreation and other satisfying activities with children, the teen-agers of the family are cast adrift and may seek satisfactions elsewhere which may not always be wholesome. Mothers, unless absolutely necessary, Should not obtain employment, but rather fulfill honorably their chief mission of wife, mother, and homemaker. What is more important or could pay greater dividends? The answer is obvious. Children should possess a constant feeling of security in the home. Strained relationships within the family or a feeling of insecurity within the home often leads a boy or girl to seek associations where they feel they are loved and wanted, which could be among questionable associates. Permit children to feel free to bring their friends home to parties and socials after dates, and then the parents welcome and cordially help entertain them when wisdom dictates. It requires kindness, love, and comradeship on the part of the parents to solve the problems of youth. There should always be a close parent-child relationship, one where confidence rules supreme and where the rights and views of children are respected and listened to courteously in the spirit of helpfulness.
