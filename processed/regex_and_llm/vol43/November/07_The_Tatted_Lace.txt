She Satted Lace Norma A. Wrathall The lamplight holds in circled radiance The rocking chair, the floor, the fireplace, And finds its sheen upon her head bent low, As in and out her shuttle weaves the lace. Her work-worn fingers move with quiet skill, She smooths the lace across her knee, and then Pauses, plucks faulty stitches, winds the thread And makes the pattern true and fine again. So, through the years, the handmade lace adorned A petticoat a happy bride might wear— A loving gift of doilies—pillow slips— A baby's dress—a handkerchief's white square. And who might guess what dreams or plans were in Those wheels of filigree her shuttle traced? Or know what cares, like broken stitches, were Pondered, and with serenity replaced? I like to think that from a ball of thread Her fingers cast the pattern of her days; And that the tatted lace, of quaint design, Held love, forbearance, faith, that marked her ways.
