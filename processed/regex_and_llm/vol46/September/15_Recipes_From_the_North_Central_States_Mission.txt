Bowen HE golden shaft of summer sunshine smiled on the small girl at the console of the great organ on this, her special day. Was she playing the organ? Hardly, for she was yet only ten or twelve years old, her feet only able to reach the foot pedals by hard stretching. "Next," came a voice from the organ, and her fingers nimbly moved to the next key. "Next," and she played the following note, "next," and again she played the next note. What was she doing — this small one at the world-famous organ? This slight half-adult, half-child, with long golden braids above the wide-set, deep, smiling eyes, was an assistant. Young? Yes, very young, but capable, most of the time, if she kept her two-track mind on both the book she was reading and the organ she was helping to tune. 'That voice with the sing in it was the voice of Father, the technician of the great organ. "Next," his voice sang out to her, and her nimble fingers touched the next note. "Next," he called again and she pressed down the following note. "Next, next, next," and so it went until all the voice of the organ was in perfect tune. Father would busy himself in the organ for a few minutes, then come down to the console. Expertly he would run his agile fingers over the keyboard listening and _ selecting the off-key notes. Perhaps it was the terrific heat wave or the sudden cold after the evening thunder shower that put the great, sensitive organ Page 584 out of tonal quality. As Father played, suddenly a mawkish screech or a thunderous belch would scream out, instead of the usual gracious song of the note, for the great organ was like a barometer, sensitive to changes in the weather. In summer it was extremely temperamental. Its behavior could be disastrous to the great master who played it, had it not been for the tuner and his assistant. Father's attuned ear found the flaws, consulting with the young assistant, and a new set of stops would be set. Then Father would go back to the great chamber of the organ, where thousands of wires and _ intricacies awaited him, and with his tuning forks, he would again be busy, adjusting each note to perfect tone, while the young daughter was at the console playing the notes. Tuning of this amazing, intricate, delicate mechanism was a must, daily, in preparation for the noonday visitors and tourists who flocked to hear the great master in the noonday recital and see the fascinating, formidable, unique structure that housed it. When tours of people passed through the building just to eye the building and the organ, and to listen to the unbelievable acoustics, they were also amazed if they saw the young assistant at the keyboard. She was quite unaware of them, however, wrapped up in her work and her book. Father was calling now. His little assistant sat alert. They were going to change the voice again. She managed so well that once they started to tune, he seldom came down again except to try the whole of the organ or several voices together. Then he would run his fingers over the keyboard or lose himself in a few moments of sweet harmony. His melodious voice, that could sing so well and was part of the ward choir, was calling to her.
