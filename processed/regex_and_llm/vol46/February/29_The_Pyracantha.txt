Hart and Clarence Gohdes, Dryden Press, New York, pp. 84-95) For Tuesday, May 19, 1959 Objective: To see in Jonathan Edwards a harmonizing of various phases of New England Puritanism. HILE performing the office of tutor at Yale College for his second year, Jonathan was seized by an illness which kept him in bed for almost three months. Upon his recovery in 1726, at age twenty-three, he was offered the great opportunity of serving as a colleague of his distinguished grandfather, the Reverend Solomon Stoddard, who had been minister in the far western Massachusetts town of Northampton for fifty-four years, and soon to retire. Solomon Stoddard was loved and respected throughout the entire Connecticut Valley. So directly had he defied the wishes of the Boston clergy that he was known more familiarly as "Pope" Stoddard. Particularly during recent decades, as the influence of the Mothers declined, he had become the most powerful single force in New England Puritanism. And Jonathan Edwards was to be groomed as his replacement. The Half-Way Covenant In 1662, ten years before Solomon Stoddard had come to Northampton as a young man, violent dissension had arisen in New England churches over the Half-Way Covenant, a Page 132 compromise measure which no one liked, yet one which many felt necessary, if the churches were to perpetuate themselves. Within each community were three groups: those who came to New England for adventure, profit, and freedom, but were members of no church. Second, those who attended church but who had never been granted membership. 'Third, actual members of the church body, or "visible saints," who could partake of church sacraments and who could vote in church council which was also town council. To be eligible for church membership a person had to have received a personal spiritual manifestation. Few second-generation children had had such an experience, and even fewer grandchildren of the originai founders. While the churches were full, the number of actual members had become alarmingly small. 'The Half-Way Covenant of 1662 granted membership to secondand third-generation Puritans who attended regularly and who believed the basic creed, but who had not received spiritual illumination. Many fervent believers felt this liberalizing compromise to be the beginning of the end.
