Our Homes—An Individual Responsibility Elder Mark E. Petersen Of the Council of the Twelve (Address Delivered at the Officers Meeting, Relief Society General Conference, October 5, 1958). T surely is inspiring, my dear sisters of the Relief Society, to be with you here today. It is wonderful to catch the inspiration of this meeting. I have been so thankful for the contact I have had with Relief Society. I am sure that President Smith and I both feel that you have remarkable and extraordinary leadership in your General Presidency and in your General Board. We are grateful for these sisters, and for the remarkable cooperation given to them by the stake officers as represented by all of you here. It was really thrilling to see the wonderful response to the roll call, and as the sisters arose, as Sister Parker called for them, and they came from such widely scattered areas and were in such splendid numbers, it was a mark of great loyalty on their part. I am thankful for the music that we have had here. I am always very proud of the stake in which I live. This is the Bonneville Stake, and today is no exception as we listen to the beautiful music of these lovely ladies. I would like, for one, to thank them for their being here. As I visit the various stakes and listen to the Singing Mothers in so many of our stake conferences, my heart always swells with pride because of the remarkably good work being done by these sisters musically. Page 76 I was glad we sang as our opening song, "How Firm a Foundation, Ye Saints of the Lord." It is always good to look back to the foundation of things. It is always wonderful to refresh our minds concerning the basic fundamentals of our organization and the reasons for our existence. Every time I sing that song, I am reminded of many of these basics which are so important to us. May I quote to you briefly from the address of the Prophet Joseph Smith as he organized the Relief Society. We hear a great deal about his remark that the sisters were to provoke the brethren to good works. When the Prophet spoke about this, however, he said that the sisters were to provoke the brethren to good works in looking to the wants of the poor, searching after objects of charity, and in administering to their wants, as Sister Spafford has explained. But then there was an additional clause in that sentence. The Prophet said that the sisters are to assist "by correcting the morals and strengthening the virtues of the community." It is that about which I would like to speak briefly with you today. I mention it to you as individual Latter-day Saint women to consider, not on an organization basis, but for individual consideration in your private family life. I believe there is a greater need OUR HOMES—AN INDIVIDUAL RESPONSIBILITY 77 for strengthening the virtues and correcting the morals of our communities, now, than at any other time in my recollection. I believe we are passing through the worst moral breakdown of our generation. This breakdown is striking into homes in all parts of the Nation. It is beginning to reach into the homes of the Latter-day Saints. In the spirit of the words of the Prophet Joseph Smith, are you willing as a homemaker to interest yourself in this problem,' and then do something to protect your own family? GUPPOSE your child was involved in some great difficulty. Suppose it was the young married couple next door. If we bring these matters close to home, they become more real to us. Many of us live such sheltered lives so far as the evils of the world are concerned, that we do not realize what goes on about us. We read about some instances in the newspapers, and too frequently we feel that such things cannot happen to us. But now, in the midst of these new conditions, we must awaken to the facts, and we must meet them. When we talk about juvenile delinquency, we comfort ourselves with the thought that, after all, only about six to seven per cent of all the juveniles in the United States ever get arrested. But what about those whose sins are of a kind for which arrests are not made? For instance what about the intimacies that develop in the wake of young people dating too steadily at too early an age? What about the evergrowing menace of petting, a terrible perversion which is spreading among the young people of the Nation of the high school age and up? What of the rise of drinking and smoking among teenagers? What about the rise of the gang spirit among both boys and girls? What about the ever-increasing spirit of taking a dare which leads to the breaking of most of our standards, including the loss of virtue? There is the delinquency also among young married people, some of whom engage in drinking parties in their own homes and patronize public taverns, and there violate the standards of the Church. Some of these young married people organize into card clubs of various kinds, and it is not long until there is, at least, minor gambling going on. Betting and other forms of gambling in the bridge clubs, and canasta clubs, and other types of card clubs are prevalent in the United States. One of the great difficulties with this type of partying going on among young married couples is that it often leads to dating with each others husbands or wives, leading to a breakdown of the sanctity of marriage.
