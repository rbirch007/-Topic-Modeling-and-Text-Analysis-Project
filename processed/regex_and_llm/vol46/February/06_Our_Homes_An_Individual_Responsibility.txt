Our Homes—An Individual Responsibility Elder Mark E. Petersen Of the Council of the Twelve (Address Delivered at the Officers Meeting, Relief Society General Conference, October 5, 1958). T surely is inspiring, my dear sisters of the Relief Society, to be with you here today. It is wonderful to catch the inspiration of this meeting. I have been so thankful for the contact I have had with Relief Society. I am sure that President Smith and I both feel that you have remarkable and extraordinary leadership in your General Presidency and in your General Board. We are grateful for these sisters, and for the remarkable cooperation given to them by the stake officers as represented by all of you here. It was really thrilling to see the wonderful response to the roll call, and as the sisters arose, as Sister Parker called for them, and they came from such widely scattered areas and were in such splendid numbers, it was a mark of great loyalty on their part. I am thankful for the music that we have had here. I am always very proud of the stake in which I live. This is the Bonneville Stake, and today is no exception as we listen to the beautiful music of these lovely ladies. I would like, for one, to thank them for their being here. As I visit the various stakes and listen to the Singing Mothers in so many of our stake conferences, my heart always swells with pride because of the remarkably good work being done by these sisters musically. Page 76 I was glad we sang as our opening song, "How Firm a Foundation, Ye Saints of the Lord." It is always good to look back to the foundation of things. It is always wonderful to refresh our minds concerning the basic fundamentals of our organization and the reasons for our existence. Every time I sing that song, I am reminded of many of these basics which are so important to us. May I quote to you briefly from the address of the Prophet Joseph Smith as he organized the Relief Society. We hear a great deal about his remark that the sisters were to provoke the brethren to good works. When the Prophet spoke about this, however, he said that the sisters were to provoke the brethren to good works in looking to the wants of the poor, searching after objects of charity, and in administering to their wants, as Sister Spafford has explained. But then there was an additional clause in that sentence. The Prophet said that the sisters are to assist "by correcting the morals and strengthening the virtues of the community." It is that about which I would like to speak briefly with you today. I mention it to you as individual Latter-day Saint women to consider, not on an organization basis, but for individual consideration in your private family life. I believe there is a greater need OUR HOMES—AN INDIVIDUAL RESPONSIBILITY 77 for strengthening the virtues and correcting the morals of our communities, now, than at any other time in my recollection. I believe we are passing through the worst moral breakdown of our generation. This breakdown is striking into homes in all parts of the Nation. It is beginning to reach into the homes of the Latter-day Saints. In the spirit of the words of the Prophet Joseph Smith, are you willing as a homemaker to interest yourself in this problem,' and then do something to protect your own family? GUPPOSE your child was involved in some great difficulty. Suppose it was the young married couple next door. If we bring these matters close to home, they become more real to us. Many of us live such sheltered lives so far as the evils of the world are concerned, that we do not realize what goes on about us. We read about some instances in the newspapers, and too frequently we feel that such things cannot happen to us. But now, in the midst of these new conditions, we must awaken to the facts, and we must meet them. When we talk about juvenile delinquency, we comfort ourselves with the thought that, after all, only about six to seven per cent of all the juveniles in the United States ever get arrested. But what about those whose sins are of a kind for which arrests are not made? For instance what about the intimacies that develop in the wake of young people dating too steadily at too early an age? What about the evergrowing menace of petting, a terrible perversion which is spreading among the young people of the Nation of the high school age and up? What of the rise of drinking and smoking among teenagers? What about the rise of the gang spirit among both boys and girls? What about the ever-increasing spirit of taking a dare which leads to the breaking of most of our standards, including the loss of virtue? There is the delinquency also among young married people, some of whom engage in drinking parties in their own homes and patronize public taverns, and there violate the standards of the Church. Some of these young married people organize into card clubs of various kinds, and it is not long until there is, at least, minor gambling going on. Betting and other forms of gambling in the bridge clubs, and canasta clubs, and other types of card clubs are prevalent in the United States. One of the great difficulties with this type of partying going on among young married couples is that it often leads to dating with each others husbands or wives, leading to a breakdown of the sanctity of marriage. Then there is the problem of obscene literature. Some of the worst obscenity you can imagine is now being peddled to the young people throughout the Nation. In the United States there are 110 publications which are considered obscene, and yet they are all being widely distributed. About twentyfive of these, and they're some of the worst, are being distributed in the Mountain States. In Utah only three out of the twenty-five, thus far, have been banned from the newsstands. Then there is the problem of television programs and movies which have an evil influence. 'The condition is made worse by an unwillingness on the part of many parents to supervise what their children See. Then, too, there is the problem of the relationship of school activities to Church activities. At a recent stake conference which I attended, I was talking with some of the sisters about their problems in the MIA—the problem of getting participation on the part of the boys and girls of the teenages and the early twenties. 'The sisters said that some young people of that stake just did not have time for Church work any more, they were so busy in their sororities and fraternities. They were perfectly willing to accept the proposition that the sororities and fraternities took first place, and that the Church would have to fit in secondarily. And if it was a situation in which young people were so busy with sorority activities that they couldn't bother with the Church at all, then it was quite all right, they thought, for the young people to discontinue Church activities until they got out of school. And then we have the increasing problem of rural parents sending to the large cities their boys and girls of tender age, either to find jobs or to go to school. Some of these young people are sent to Salt Lake City and Ogden and other centers without any thought of supervision, and without any effort on the part of the parents to establish them in homes that are clean and suitable. Some of the young people, upon coming to the larger centers, obtain employment in places where they learn perversions of the worst kind. E could go on and list other modern problems, but, of course, time will not permit. What are some of the causes of these difficulties? May I mention a few? They all come directly to our homes. Among them are lack of parental restraint and discipline; lack of parental example; lack of interest on the part of parents in character building activities; lack of religious faith in the home with its attendant lack of obedience to the commandments. 'Then there is the absence of interest in the minds of parents with respect to such community evils as the obscene literature I have mentioned; the increasing patronage at taverns and nightclubs even by many of the parents themselves; and the rise of gambling, an acceptance for which is created in the minds of people who begin to learn to gamble and love it at their card clubs. As a result of this, when proposals are brought forth to be put on the ballots (as will be put on ballots in two of our states here in the Mountain West this year), there is less resistance to them. Parents fail to realize that what they do becomes a pattern for what their children will do, and especially is this the case if the examples are evil. Nearly every one of these difficulties is related in one way or another to the family circle. Each one contributes to a breakdown of the home. You, the mothers, are the homemakers. Our homes are pretty much what you make of them. I realize the part the father must take, but I realize too, that, in most cases, fathers want to please their OUR HOMES—AN INDIVIDUAL RESPONSIBILITY 719 wives. I believe that with proper co-operation between husbands and wives, good home conditions may develop. Our communities are reflections of our homes. If all homes in our communities were good ones, we would have only good communitics. It has been said that the hand that rocks the cradle is the hand that rules the world. The hand that rocks the cradle also molds the community life of our towns. 'The hand that rocks the cradle is the hand that develops our home life. 'The hand that rocks the cradle is the hand which builds our defense against the infiltration of evil in our family circle. But what if the hand that rocks the cradle is lax and never disciplines and never trains? HAT are some of the things we can do in our own homes to correct the morals and strengthen the virtues of our communities? How can family life be rebuilt to achieve these ends? First and foremost, we must set our houses in order. There must be love and harmony in the home. There must be a conversion to the principles of the gospel on the part of the parents, to be followed by a conversion on the part of the children. 'There should be daily living of the gospel in the home, the establishment of Christian Latterday Saint habits in the home. We should endeavor to make the home a temple, and we should so live, and so train our children to live, that, when we are out of the home, we also will live the gospel as well as we do in the home, making consistent our life in the home and out of the home. We must develop a great willingness to stand for the right, a determination to uphold our standards regardless of social, business, political, or any other pressures. We must develop good character traits by our living the gospel, stressing cleanliness of life, honesty, loyalty, devotion. And we must build respect— self-respect, respect for each other, respect for the Church, its leaders, and its standards, respect for law and order. We must try to develop a closer family unity, a "togetherness" in family life. We must do all we possibly can to see to it that our outside activities do not divorce us from our children and our homes. We must preserve the daily habit, night and morning, of family prayer in the home. We should have a regular Family Hour, or home evening, as we used to call it. We should observe the Word of Wisdom in the home. Let none of us discount the Word of Wisdom. I want you to know that there are many who begin the moral breakdown of their own children by themselves setting an example in violating the Word of Wisdom. We should develop group attendance of the entire family at the meetings in the ward and at stake conferences. We should develop within the home a uniform understanding of family discipline. 'This is something which could well come out of our Family Hour where the children can participate in establishing the rules and the regulations of the home. When they help to make the rules, they will be more likely to live up to them. Sometimes we are horrified at instances of desertion where a man will desert his family, or occasionally 80 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—FEBRUARY 1959 where a mother, falling in love with some other man, will desert her husband and her children and go to live with the other man in another town. 'These instances do not happen often, and they shock us when we hear of them or read of them in the newspaper. But I would like to ask you, what is it that constitutes desertion of the family? Are there other means of desertion than walking out on them? For instance, is failure to uphold Church standards in the home a form of desertion? Is failure to teach our children properly a form of desertion? Is failure to set the proper example a form of desertion? Is lack of discipline? Is failure to give direction? What kind of person do we want our child to be? We must then be that kind of person ourselves. Your child's habits of living begin with you. Your child's attitude toward the home and the community begins begins with you. "'Tis the set of the sail and not the gale that determines the way he goes." And we set the sail. The home itself is the foundation of morals and virtue. Therefore, if we are to correct the morals and strengthen the virtues of our communities, we must do something about the preservation of wonderful homes in our communities. If we build genuine Latter-day Saint homes, if, in our homes, we convert ourselves to the principles of the gospel and live those principles ourselves, if we convert our children to these same principles, if we teach them to love truly the Lord Jesus Christ and what he stands for, then our children will resist evil. They will grow up to be a righteous generation. 'They will build strength into the Church. They will lift the morals of our community. They will save their souls. And that we with you.
