Lemon extract Cooking With Dry Mlk Dr. Marian Bennion Chairman, Department of Food and Nutrition, Brigham Young University OU, as today's homemaker, find on your grocer's shelves many new products and also many old products in new forms. One such food which can be put to very good use in your kitchen is nonfat milk solids, often called dry milk. Most of the water is removed from fresh, liquid skim milk to obtain nonfat dry milk. 'This, then, is a mixture of protein, milk-sugar, minerals, and some of the water soluble vitamins, especially riboflavin. The use of dry milk in many dishes may be an excellent way of stepping up the nutritive value of your family's meals. Newer methods of processing have made it possible to preserve maximum fresh milk flavor, also, and to give you a product that is readily soluble and easy to use. Economy may be the keynote in your food budget. If so, dry milk will fit nicely into your plan. It is one of the most economical forms of milk you can buy, costing only about one half as much per quart as comparable fluid skim milk. Remember, however, that the fat has been removed from the dry milk solids, so the price cannot be strictly compared to fresh whole milk. These are the days of modern conveniences, and the use of dry milk in cooking blends in well with this theme. It is convenient to store since it does not require refrigeration and takes very little shelf space. It should be kept, however, in a tightly covered container, so that it is not left open to the air. Storage temperatures no higher than 75° F. Are preferable, also. It is unwise to keep dry milk over a period of more Page 102 than six months unless it is in an airtight glass, metal, foil, or polyethylene-lined container, as otherwise it may become offflavored and caked. If the dry milk is kept in air-tight containers at 40° F., it will keep as long as two years. Use from your stored supply and add to it, so that none of it gets too old before it is used. Dry milk is convenient to use. It may be reconstituted with water and used as any liquid milk if it is a grade A product and clean utensils are used in the mixing process. It may also be used in many cases as the dry product. In recipes for breads, cakes, and other baked goods, the dry milk may simply be added to or sifted with the other dry ingredients in the recipe. 'Then, water may be used for the required liquid. Other liquids, as stock, fruit juice, or even additional milk, may also be used, depending upon the recipe. You may adapt most of your favorite baking recipes for the use of dry milk in this way. A good general rule to follow in deciding how much dry milk to substitute is to use three to four tablespoons of dry milk and one cup of water as the liquid to replace each cup of milk called for in a recipe. You may like to add an additional amount of dry milk to many of your old favorite recipes or to new ones you are trying. Since dry milk is high in food value, you may markedly increase the nutritive value of a product in this way without seriously affecting the flavor or texture, in most cases.
