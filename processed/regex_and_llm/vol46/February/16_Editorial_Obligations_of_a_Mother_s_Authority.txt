D EDITORIAL m2 VOL. 46 FEBRUARY 1959 NO. 2 Obligations of a Mothers Authority THE term General Authorities in the Church has a meaning peculiar to itself, and likewise the different degrees of authority exercised by stake, ward, branch, and mission officers. 'There is also the personal authority of a man bearing the Priesthood in his own home. Then there is the authority of a mother in the home. In her sphere of service she must assume its obligations and know the duties required of her before she can fulfill her obligations. Church authorities constantly teach the obligations of parents, and in Section 69 of The Doctrine and Covenants, the Lord commands parents to teach their children, when eight years old, to understand the doctrine of repentance, faith in Christ, of baptism, and the gift of the Holy Ghost, and to pray and to walk uprightly before the Lord. This law applies to mothers as well as fathers. Mothers must assume authority in this training, for the sin of not training will be upon the heads of parents, not their childrens', the Lord warns. The acceptance and exercise of this authority by a mother lays the foundation in her children of a love and respect for authority resulting in an obedience to authority. 'The children who are trained to see the blessed results of obedience to authority will not give mere lip service to it. A mother who wishes to exercise authority properly, may apply to the training in authority of her children many of the attributes set forth by Page 96 the Lord for the exercise of the authority of the Priesthood in Section 121 of The Doctrine and Covenants—to influence by persuasion, gentleness, and meekness, and by unfeigned love, reproving with sharpness betimes, then afterward showing forth an increase of love toward the one whom she has reproved, lest the child should consider his mother to be his enemy. The mother herself must set an example of obedience to authority, for there are two facets—the authority a mother wields and the authority to which she yields. Obeying that authority over her enlarges her soul and assists her in exercising her own authority. Nor will she ever criticize those over her. President Joseph F. Smith declared, "There never should be a day pass but all the people composing the Church should lift up their voices in prayer to the Lord to sustain his servants who are placed to preside over them." Teaching children in the home the absolute necessity of obeying gospel principles and the Authorties of the Church, and of the heaven to be inherited eternally by those obedient to authority, will reward a mother for any sacrifice she may make in order to fulfill daily and hourly the obligations of authority which are inherent in her role of mother. In days to come she will realize the promise "Her children arise up, and call her blessed..." (Proverbs 31:28). —M. C. S.
