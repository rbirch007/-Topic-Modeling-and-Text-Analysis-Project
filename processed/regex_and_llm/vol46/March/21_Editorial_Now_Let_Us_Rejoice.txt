NE hundred and seventeen years have passed into the annals of time since the organization of Relief Society, March 17, 1842. More than a century has gone, and we come to another time of special evaluation of our heritage as members of Relief Society. It is a time for us to contemplate, with historic perspective, our traditions, our beliefs, the practices and _ principles which have guided the lives of our mothers in the generations past, which are now our daily pattern, and which will be the legacy of our daughters. The gospel came to the women of the early Church as a cause for great rejoicing. From a profound and earnest searching, a glorious fulfillment had come. The generations of the past, the wide and wonderful vistas of their own day, and all the frontiers of the future reaching into the eternities, for them were linked together in the great sunlight of the restoration. Even hardships became opportunities, and the women felt a desire to enrich their individual potentialities and to unite with their sisters that their hands might be strengthened, their spirits uplifted, and their services multiplied. This rejoicing in the gospel became the luminous horizon for the divinely directed pathway of women. The sisters of Nauvoo saw the boats ruffling the waves of the wide river, as families arrived day after Page 1/0? Day to unite with the Church, and the women who were first in the - "City of Joseph" considered the needs of the newcomers as "'a loud call for relief"—not for the providing of food and housing alone, but for spiritual enlightenment and for kindness and comfort in an alien place. Long before the formal organization of Relief Society, the women had hoped for a pattern and a design to guide them. When the Prophet Joseph Smith turned the key and gave direction to their efforts, the women rejoiced, for they knew that the blessings of the Lord would be with them in their ministrations. Thus, the restoration of the gospel and the organization of Relief Society by the Prophet became the first great traditions of the Society, and a beacon light for times to come when menibership would be multiplied, when responsibilities would be diverse and far-reaching. In accordance with instructions, so well expressed by the Prophet, and "in harmony' with — their natures," the women looked first to the needs of their households, the honoring of their husbands, the rearing of their children, and the tasks of making their homes orderly and beautiful, employing with energy all they possessed of time and talents. Then afterward they reached out, EDITORIAL with true charity, to serve their sisters, realizing the urgent and special needs of children, the aged, those who were ill, and the ones deprived of close family associations. 'Their quick and willing footsteps along the streets of Nauvoo were a blessing, and doorways were lighted by their faces. These women, as necessity committees, were the first visiting teachers, establishing a treasured design for Relief Society. In the early days the women realized the need, also, as we do today, of a favorable environment outside the home in order that their children might grow strong and beautiful and help to build a world suitable for the coming of the Savior. To meet this responsibility, another tradition has become dear to the hearts of Relief Society women — a feeling of devotion to all the auxiliaries of the Church, active participation, and a spiritual dedication to the ways of worship of their children. Relief Society women realize that the Church cannot prosper and 171 reach the full extent of its glorious destiny unless the governments and the social systems of the earth will permit the telling of the gospel story. So the sisters willingly lend their strength and their influence to the improvement of the larger environment. It is a_ tradition among Relief Society women that they hold as a privilege and a duty their right to vote in elections, and to accept and honor the opportunity to help direct the affairs of the city, the state, and the Nation, as well as in the world, where their children must live and work out the temporal patterns of their eternal destiny. The women of Relief Society cherish tenderly their sisterhood, their concern for each other, their kind and loving charity, their blessed companionship. They think of their places of gathering as havens for their singing and their prayers, for their learning and their giving — and for their rejoicing in the gospel. —V. P. C. Os Old Hlome Helen M. Livingston The tree grown tall holds up long arms of shade, Her leafy fingers spread against the sky. The brook runs close around the old white wall, And asters blossom in the field close by. The wide, deep-seated chairs are on the porch; The daisies still are growing in the grass. The evening mists are on the balcony; The dark-gray shadows linger there and pass. And who will sit upon the porch tonight? And who will walk upon the balcony? The moon, slow-rising, dimly lights each pane; A bird's low call pours softly, silvery. Oh, mourning dove, once nestled with your mate, Within the sheltered tree you call—and wait.
