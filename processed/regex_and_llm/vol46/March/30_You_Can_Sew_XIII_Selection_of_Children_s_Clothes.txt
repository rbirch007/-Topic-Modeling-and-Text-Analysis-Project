You Can Sew— XIII — Selection of Childrens Clothes Jean R. Jennings EWING for children of all ages is, no doubt, one of the most important phases of the home-sewing program. Fortunate, indeed, are children whose mothers sew with skill and a flair for fashion rightness. Too much emphasis cannot be placed on the importance of fashion in children's clothing. Even though there are always stable styles, such as pinafores, smocked dresses, the standard frocks with full skirts and tight-fitting bodices, there are new innovations in styles and materials which are noteworthy. Children can and should be as aware of good taste and good grooming as their elders. Early training along this line can save much unhappiness and personal difficulty in later years. 'The very young respond to what they are wearing, and this does not change as they grow older. Many behavior problems stem from dull, drab, unbecoming, or uncomfortable clothing. The selection of fabrics appropriate for children's clothes and colors that are becoming should always be a vital phase of sewing for children. Clothes can be chosen with a view to suiting the child's personality. His physical characteristics of coloring and type must be taken into consideration, as well as his habits of action. The dainty, demure little girl will no doubt look her best in clothing different from that worn by a sturdy, active type. The influence of a mother's good taste and guidance can be of value to boys and girls all their lives. Page 190 Clothes for children can be made just as attractive, just as becoming, as the ready-made ones. Mothers, in their sewing, must pay attention to important little details to achieve this. 'Too often they are impelled by economy to buy cheap fabrics, C instead of buying the best available, appropriate fabric. If these nicer materials are styled with an eye to becomingness, fit, and fashion, they could no doubt duplicate, at a fraction of the cost, the attractive garments shown in the most exclusive shops. Top designers of children's clothes never skimp on skirt fullness. 'They use two full widths for the perky look. 'They make deep hems — the deeper the better. They make collars appropriately narrow and, if tiny pockets and tinier puffed sleeves are needed for a chic appearance, that is the way the dresses are made. They size clothes to fit when new, not for children to grow into. 'Too many mothers make or buy clothes that never look their best because they are old and shabby before they fit. (Let-out possibilities will be treated in a later lesson. ) Designers say that mothers too often lose the style of their children's clothes in their desire to be practical. 'There should be no compromise with becomingness at any age. A grain of practicality is fine, if all style is not sacrificed for it. A clever seamstress can learn to "fit out" physical defects in her children and compensate for "the awkward age" when they are growing up and nothing seems to fit. Chest and waist measurements are important in cutting clothes to fit children. Rarely are individuals of the same age exactly the same size. Alter the size of pattern pieces to fit the child's own measurements. This is done in accordance with pattern instructions. Make sure that shoulders are not too wide. Scarcely anything is as annoying and conducive to bad temper as a shoulder seam that drops down and catches the arm every time it is raised. It ls equally bad in appearance. Waistlines look better on girls, as well as boys, if they are properly located — not too high and certainly not too low. The length of a little girl's skirt is every bit as important to her smart appearance as is her mother's. 'There are certain fabrics that are always favored for children. There will be new designs and new colors from season to season, but, in the main, smooth surfaces that are easy to iron and avoid picking up lint and dirt are preferable to novelty weaves. Sunfast colors are essential in children's clothes, and fabrics should be firm enough to hold buttons and buttonholes or hammer-on snaps. At a time when the choice of fabrics ls almost limitless, it seems that the wise mother should choose for her children the ones that are easy to care for. Any child will be happier in clothes he doesn't have to worry about spoiling. All budgets will be happier without big cleaning bills. So why not dress the children exclusively in clothes that can be laundered easily? You will find in this class plenty of sturdy types, as well as those that are dainty and very dressy in appearance. Start today to make the careful selection of style, color, and fabric the first important step in a successful family sewing venture. --
