The lure of "new country" has always proved an irresistible challenge to certain venturesome souls, while country "new to me" even to this day must be seen by many descendants of explorers just as soon as can be after they begin hovering over unfolded maps with forefinger extended, and finally utter the fatal phrase, "I've never been there." But once on the way, of what does "sceing the country' consist? If we are alert and growing, each time we make the same trip it ls uniquely new. And to hear the same trip described by another is to make us wonder if we were there at all, particularly if the observer secs and loves people rather than precipices and picture postcards only. William Byrd and Michel GuilJaume Jean de Crevecoeur loved nature, observed her keenly, and were at home out-of-doors, but their true interests lay in the people they saw: how they lived; what they believed; what form of society, commerce, and government they erected to serve their desires best. Therefore, as we peer over their shoulders to read their journals as they write, our reward is at least four-dimensional: (a) we are at home in a new natural setting; (b) we meet a new culture at firsthand; (c) by the subjects of their comments and the interpretations they give, we come to know the observers better than what they observe; (d) because they wrote personally and accurately, by reading them we can "break the time-barrier" and live both then and now. Wilham Byrd (1674-1744) (Text, PP _17, 73°83.) So magnificently does William Byrd symbolize the myth of the Cavalicr Gentleman of the pre-Revolutionary South that it is difficult to see through honor and accomplishment to discover the person beneath. Yet the reward of knowing him more than justifies the effort. Since half his years were spent in England, half in America, either culture might claim him as its own; rather it was he who could with comfort claim them both. Sent to England at age seven to begin acquiring the education and breeding of a gentleman, he was entirely at home in London, Paris, and Holland, until at the death of his father, he was called home at age thirtyone to inherit his estate of 26,000 Page /67 768 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1959 Dementi Studio Richmond, Virginia WILLIAM BYRD acres and become one of Virginia's first gentlemen. In London he had studied law at the Middle Temple, absorbed Pope and Swift, and was an intimate friend of the leading dramatists of the day, Congreve and Wycherly. Fe toyed with the idea of becoming a man of letters, and wrote some satirical verses in the style of the day which still survive. For his excellent description of an albino Negro, he was asked to join the Royal Society, a connection which he prized throughout his life, just as he prized his many scientific and cultivated friends in England with whom he kept up an intimate correspondence until his death in 1744 at age seventy. He returned three times to represent the colony of Virginia at the court of England, sometimes. Staying "at home" for several years. Thus, because his version of a "Bible Commonwealth" continued to remain in Mother England, and because he had no feelings toward England but loyalty, affection, and enjoyment, he was far more the Englishman in America than were either the Puritans, the Quakers, or the mixed founders of the Middle Colonies. In large measure William Byrd loved Virginia because he had succeeded in bringing so much of his English life there. 'The mansion he built at Westover on the James River stands today as one of the loveliest country estates in Virginia. (See Text, page 15.) 'Though his lavish manner of living cost him dearly, and for a time placed him in heavy debt, he furnished his home with elegant carved furniture, drapes, sculpture, and personally commuissioned oil paintings of many of his English friends. His personal library of 3,600 volumes was second in size only to Cotton Mather's; that he used it constantly is proved not only by his many allusions to classical culture in his writings, but by his average day, as recorded in his secret diary, published for the first time after 1940: January 27, 1711 I rose at 5 o'clock and read two chapters in Hebrew and some Greek in Lucian. I said my prayers and ate boiled milk for breakfast. I danced my dance. It rained all night but held up about 8 o'clock this morning. My sick people were all better, thank God Almighty. I settled several accounts; then I read some English which gave me great light into the nature of spirit. I ordered Tom to plant some (1-c-s) seed. I ate goose giblets for dinner.
