What's in a Story? Norma A. Wrathall HY don't you write a story \\) for The Relief Society Magazine Short Story Contest? Maybe you have already started one. Or maybe all you need is to get started. Let us begin with a definition, and then consider some of the ingredients of the short story. A short story has been defined as a prose narrative which depicts characters in processes of struggle and complications. 'The narrative usually centers around a principal character, with a special problem, against a specific background. A dominant purpose or theme should be in evidence, and the effective short story is marked by dramatic interest, involving significant human experience. In every story, six elements are present: setting (place); persons (characters); events (plot); idea (theme); emotion; and style. I have not listed them in order of their importance, for in any given story one element may predominate over the others. But in every story, all are present to some degree. There are three main steps in building the story: (1) finding the story; (2) building the plot; and (3) developing the narrative. Perhaps you have noted an expression on someone's face showing great joy, strong anger, or deep sorrow. 'The emotion seemed so intense that you kept thinking about it. You wondered what could have caused anyone to feel that way. You may remember a similar feeling in yourself, and recall the circumstance. You may wonder what events might have led to it, what had happened to the person to cause him to react in that way. And you have the germ of a story. Or, you may know of a series of events in which everything worked out rather smoothly, eventually turning out as planned. But you might wonder, what if something had happened to change those plans? What if, at a certain point, things had gone wrong? What then? And you would begin to have a story; you would think what the people (characters) might have done in the emergency, how each would have reacted to disappointment; what would have been the outcome. Or, you may have visited a place that fairly teemed with a story, such as a remote house; an abandoned farm; a seemingly peaceful village with an undercurrent of turmoil. Or, you may have a belief that you want to portray in dramatic action. I have read much discussion as to whether one should begin with theme, character, or plot. It is like trying to decide the old question of the chicken or the egg? As far as we are concerned, perhaps we should begin with the element that suggested the story in the first place, and then build in the other parts. This means that if we start with character, we decide on a certain characteristic in the person (such as truthfulness). We then portray this quality by presenting the character in incidents to which he will react to reveal this quality. If we start with plot, we have in mind a series Page 299 300 of significant incidents, then decide what characters would be necessary to bring to pass these events. In the same manner, one might begin with theme or setting. We can't have a story at all without people. N the short story, there is room for only one leading character. This person must be in focus most of the time in the story, if not in actual fact, then by reference. He should be rounded out as much as possible by his thoughts and feelings, his reactions to certain events, his reactions to other people, his appearance, the way he moves, walks, and talks. One or two minor characters, not so fully characterized, may be used to bring out the main character. But they must always be subordinate to him. Don't have any "scene stealers" in your dramatic situations. If you do, the story will be muddled and the reader confused. The character should be made sympathetic to the reader, so that the reader can identify himself with the story-person, and will care what happens to him. And before you can cause the reader to identify with the character, you, the writer, must identify with him yourself. You must be able to feel as he feels, think as he thinks, before you know how he will react to certain situations. Make your character natural and human. Even an evil character must be shown in such a way that the reader will think, "Well, I guess that's what I'd do, if I were that kind of person!" Everything that you write about the character must bring out the characteristic that you are trying to portray. (2) Characterize by telling or showing the thoughts and feelings of the person. (3) Develop character in dialogue, ° which includes the speech of the characters in conversation, and also what other people in the story say about the main character. In writing dialogue, train your car to listen to your characters speak. Speech is one of the most difficult things in writing. For it one is not careful, all the characters will be talking alike, and chances are, in the same way as the author! (4) Appearance is important, especially so if appearance can be made to show, by comparison or contrast, the inner feelings of the person. For example: Aunt Sade's hair had always been held tightly at the sides by two brown combs. It wound into a smooth coil at the back, always in the same place, with the same thickness and smoothness. A lock had never been known to escape either of the combs and curl upon her high white forehead; it would not dare! (5) You can individualize your character by some little trait— such as pulling at his ear while thinking. (6) Movement is a language in itself, and sometimes shows far more than any of the other ways. Examples: Diana's bare toes skimmed over the dew-wet grass, a naughty elf fleeing from the prosaic morming ritual of getting dressed. Old Ernst placed one foot ahead of the other with such calculated economy of motion that one had to watch him a moment to know if he really moved. Remember, you cannot characterize your actor and then have done with it once and for all. You must keep on characterizing him right up to, and including, the end of the story. Don't believe it if someone tells you that she is not interested in plot. As well try to bake a cake without a recipe, or sew a dress without a pattern. The plot is the framework of the story, the skeleton upon which is placed flesh and skin and coloring. It consists of a series of dramatic happenings, arranged in climactic order, and containing struggle or complication. The first part of the plot is the situation. It contains the want of the principal character, and the problem which seems to prevent him from getting it. This part may end with the dramatic climax (the point at which the character despairs of attaining his desire). 'The second part is the solution, in which the character brings about, by his own effort or decision, the solving of the problem. To avoid a "forced result," it is well to have treated in the situation every factor used in the solution. 'The want should be introduced as early as possible in the story. It must be strongly motivated, so that the reader will be interested in knowing how it turns out. By complication is meant not merely a series of obstacles or stumbling blocks which can be rather easily overcome. For instance, we might be writing about a young girl who is going to college. We give her a strong motive for wanting to earn her degree in June. To do so, she must get credit in French. She is not linguistic, and the subject is hard for her, but by studying late at nights, and taking many notes, she thinks she can pass. But she loses her notebook just before the test. However, she finds it again in time 301 to "cram" for the exam. These things would hinder her, but they would not be complication. Suppose that when the girl found her notebook at the last minute, she stayed up nearly all night to study. But she was so tired and unnerved that right in the middle of the test she began to weep and ran out of the room, thus flunking the course. So, according to the rules, she would not be allowed to graduate in June. Now, based upon what you might have shown about her, what will she do? Will she accept meekly the ruling of the committee? Or will she find some way out of the dilemma? 'This would be complication, because it would change the course of action, and it would involve emotional conflict in the leading character. SOMETIMES the character may attain his original want, only to find that it is not what he needed or wanted after all, and a substitute goal is found. 'This usually grows out of, or is a variant of, the original want; it may be in the nature of a decision. Sometimes the struggle exists almost entirely in the mind of the character, and a decision is the outcome. But in any case, the pattern is the same: the well-defined want; the complication which seems to prevent its attainment; the solution to the problem. Write out your plot in outline form, in whatever way you like. I usually write in short sentences, double-spaced typing, then go over it and see if the incidents lead logically into each other. After I have written my outline, I often write the end of the story, rapidly, just as it comes to me, and 302 sometimes the first two or three paragraphs of the beginning of the story. Then, even with all the interruptions which happen to my writing, I can go back and get into the mood of it again. Each writer must decide what is the best way for her. Now we have finished for the time being with steps one and two. We are ready for the final step, the actual writing. Well, nearly ready. Who is to tell the story? If a character tells it, you will write in first person—the "T' story. If the author tells it, you will write in third person. 'Think it out in several ways. Now write your story. Write. Keep going, to the end, if possible, without thinking of wording or construction or technique. When you have finished, put the story away for several days, or even weeks. Forget it. This step is just as important as any part of the writing. When you are ready to begin again, read it entirely through from beginning to end as if you had never seen it before. Now you begin the real work of writing, the revision. Be sure that you have your writer's tools at hand, including your Roget's 'Thesaurus, a good dictionary, and a book of RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—MAY 1959 English grammar and composition for reference. Ask yourself these questions about the story: (1) Would the narrative move along just as well and be just as true and interesting without this incident? If so, leave it out. (2) What is the purpose of each incident? Does each one carry out, or lead toward, the general purpose of the story? (3) Is each incident, and the act of each character, properly motivated? (4) Does the story have suspense and emotional appeal? Cutting and shortening the story are also a part of revision, for you will always have to meet certain word-length requirements. As you see, there are many things to consider. But the best way to learn to write is by writing. Read published stories, and see how our principles of the short story are carried out by the authors. Read and study good reference books and articles in writers' magazines. And be sure to keep a notebook of ideas and characterizations, scribbled in quickly as they come to mind. Now put this article away. Write your story. You will work, and dream, and despair over it. But whatever its fate, if you have given it your best, you are a winner; for ahead of success is achievement. You have achieved something, and the result is uplifting and rewarding.
