You Can Sew—XIV —Childrens Clothes —Ynfants and Soddlers Jean R. Jennings T is a wise woman, indeed, who | approaches the making of a layette with a practical and not a sentimental plan. Too often young mothers let their feelings run away with them and buy entirely too many and too frivolous clothes for the coming baby. The kinds and number of garments are matters of personal decision, but wise mothers plan for very few infants' clothes. Babies grow fast and are soon too large for the first tiny clothes. Often friends send gifts, and soon there is much more for the baby than can ever be needed. When selecting clothing for the infant, keep these three important points in mind: the garments must be comfortable; they must be easy to launder; they must be easy to slip on and off. To insure perfect comfort in infants' and small tots' clothing, first make sure the fabrics are soft. Fine nainsook, lawn, batiste, soft flannel, and soft crepe are satisfactory. Many of the new synthetic fabrics are non-absorbent and do not readily ventilate, so are, therefore, not as good for the wee ones. Do not use any materials that will, in any way, irritate sensitive skin. Rough textures and stiff or starchy types are a very poor choice for children. In most cases, especially for night clothes, those that tie are better than those that button. Give preference to a front closing and to taglan sleeves which will give freePage 254 dom and more comfort than set-in sleeves. Avoid tiny collars that crumple up around the neck, no matter how cute they are. Resist garments that slip over the head. They are hard to adjust and hard to iron. Young mothers with growing families can be kind to themselves by keeping children's clothes simple and uncluttered so they are easy to launder. 'The fewer the frills for everyday clothes the better. When baby begins to crawl and learn to walk, his clothes need special consideration. Plan garments which allow the greatest freedom for getting around on hands and knees and taking the first steps. Both girls and boys fare better in a cover-all type of garment that is buttoned at the crotch for easy changing. At this stage they are difficult to keep clean and frequent changes are necessary, so simplicity is the keynote for comfort. Make such garments in pretty colors which can be washed often. Soft, flexible fabrics, with smooth surfaces which do not pick up dirt, should be used. They should always be pre-shrunk and color-fast. All seams should be narrow, flat, and smooth. All stitching should be very secure as these clothes will receive hard wear. Buttons and buttonholes must stay fast and keep their shape.
