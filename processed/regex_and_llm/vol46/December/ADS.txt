Common Sense In October 1775, five months after Lexington, Benjamin Franklin proposed to Tom Paine that he write a history of the rebellion which the latter agreed to attempt. Later, Paine recalled that when he landed in America he found "the disposition of the people such, that they might have been led by a thread and governed by a reed." But in his own mind there was no confusion or hesitation, for he wrote, "My own line of reasoning is to myself as straight and clear as a ray of light." World Revolutionist As editor, soldier, and the author of sixteen issues of The American Crisis, Paine continued to serve his country throughout the war. The first Crisis, which appeared in De-' cember 1776, has the same impassioned brilliance as Common Sense, and is almost as famous. It begins: These are the times that try men's souls: The summer soldier and the sunshine patriot will in this crisis shrink from the service of his country; but he that stands it NOW, deserves the love and thanks of man and woman. Thanking God that he feared not, he kept alive hope in darkest hours, was sent on a desperate last appeal to France, where his reputation was largely responsible for the success of his appeal for money, munitions, and clothing. From the moment this convoy reached our shores, our fighting force's fortunes continued to improve until Cornwallis surrendered at Yorktown. Granted a farm and an annuity of eight hundred dollars for his services to his country during the Revolution, Paine continued to write, but he also invented the smokeless candle and an iron bridge which he was promoting in France just before their own revolution broke out. He returned to England where he published The Rights of LESSON DEPARTMENT Man, which expressed his view that "Government, like dress, is the badge of lost innocence," and which made his most sustained: appeal for the abolishment of monarchy. It was this book which caused him to be so much hated by his brother Englishmen who still loved their king, despite his human faults. Benjamin Franklin had - said, "Wherever liberty is, there is my country." Paine believed and acted his conviction that "Where liberty is not, there is mine." He proclaimed his strong conviction that the American and the French Revolutions were but the beginning, and that soon men_ everywhere would be freed of their chains which traditional governments imposed upon them. "The world is my country, all mankind are my brethren, to do good is my religion." It was in this spirit that he went to France to try helping the international revolution there. Soon after the fall of the Bastille, which was the symbol of aristocratic cruelty and oppression, Lafayette gave Paine the key to the Bastille to be sent to Washington. Even though he could not speak any French, Paine was elected to the French Assembly, and was one of nine men chosen to draft a constitution for the new France. But in 1793 when the guillotine: became popular and the Assembly voted to kill Louis XVI and his family, Paine fearlessly opposed their decision to kill their king, for which his French friends imprisoned him to show their disapproval of his independence. Again, in contrast to Franklin's philosophy of "getting on in the world," Paine was not practical. Declared a traitor in England, im- 853 prisoned in France for saying what he thought, he next proceeded to alienate all Christians by publishing his The Age of Reason, really in the hope of saving some common belief in God among the French, who were being carried away by their excesses during the revolution. While he never tried to convert anyone to his own views, he believed wholeheartedly in his nght to state his religious belief even as he granted the same right to everyone else. Nevertheless he attacked the Bible as being false theology, and a secondhand account of false revelation which he could not accept. Appearing almost ten years after the end of the American Revolution, almost twenty since he wrote Common Sense, this book was reviled by the new American generation which had forgotten Paine's past services. Though he never opposed Jesus as being the greatest teacher and most moral of men, Paine's attempt to justify his deistic or "Natural Religion" in place of the Bible, caused him to be universally hated. When he returned to America eight years later he found his life a miserable one. If only he could have chosen the moment of his death, he might have died a hero; as it was his great tragedy was to live too long and speak too often what he believed, regardless of the consequences. Yet in his earlier years speaking the truth as he saw it, even when he was alone in his opinion, was the source of his greatness, and so must it ever be for so untrammeled a nature as Paine's. Religious Beliefs Paine's credo of religious belief formed part of the first chapter of 854 The Age of Reason (Text, page 143). Stating his belief in God, in "happiness beyond this life,' and in the equality of man, he disavows belief in any creed. However, he can never be called an infidel, since by his own definition: "Infidelity does not consist in believing or in disbelieving; it consists in professing to believe what he does not believe" (Text, page 143). Paine's Bible is the creation, both of man and of the marvelous world in which he lives, since "Every man is an evidence to himself, that he did not make himself' (Text, page 147). He believed that man can come to know God only through his reason, and that, by using the methods of science, man can possess the "true theology." Because it was rational and reasonable, Paine believed that the "moral duty of man consists in imitating the moral goodness and beneficence of God manifested in the creation toward all his creatures.' 'True to his early Quaker influence, he believed that the world had become too reasonable and enlightened ever again to admit that any human suffering or cruelty was necessary. If man were freed from the evils of traditional social, economic, and political dominations, he would be benevolent to his own kind since "man is naturally the friend of man, and... Human nature is not of itself vicious." If unhampered by institutions, man's reason and humanity would inevitably bring kindness and peace to society. Humanitarian With such a philosophical core, Paine wrote his Agrarian Justice and other pamphlets advocating the RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1959 specific reforms which were the fond dreams of his later years. He opposed slavery, dueling, capital punishment, and, most of all, social and financial conditions which caused unbelievable suffering among the poor. While the far-sighted Paine advocated international copyright laws, disarmament, and an international association of nations, his most radical proposal was to tax the wealthy so that every person should receive fifteen pounds at age twenty-one, and from age fifty on, ten pounds a year. Such a sum would provide a decent minimum income, and stop the mass begging by the aged which for so long had characterized the "civilized" world. Tom Paine deserves our gratitude and our study of his mind and writings, not so much because he was either good or bad, as because he was throughout his life a spark. At the crucial moment he quickened men's imaginations into action, and they, seeing good in his writings, did what their new-found convictions dictated they must do. Tom Paine wrote that, whenever a problem arises, we should clear away all precedent and think as if we were the first men who ever thought. This he did throughout his life, with the amazing results we have just reviewed. But most amazing is it that a militarily unskilled minority of English would dare insist that they were right in their concept of man and government, that the greatest nation in the world was wrong. Such sheer creative seeing and doing required a superabundance of courage and faith. In these qualities Tom Paine led the colonists to action, victory, and identity as a separate Nation. The religious person in his contacts with other people, whether he operates as a therapist to get a discorded life moving again or whether he operates as a parent getting children back on good terms with each other, or whether he operates as a member of the community somehow bridging a gap between two groups that haven't been getting along, or whatever he is, a religiously mature person wants that which has stopped the growth of life to be removed, wants to enact the principle of the second start (Mrs. Bonaro Overstreet, Devotional Address delivered at Brigham Young University, June 1958). Dr. Harry A. Overstreet stated: Either, in religion, we serve a_ belief that encourages man's growth or we serve a belief that keeps him immature. The two beliefs are basically and forever incompatible (Overstreet, Harry A.: The Mature Mind, page 271, W. W. Norton & Company, Inc., used by permission). Vital Importance of Increasing Level of Religious Maturity We could list many reasons justifying the importance of each and every one of us diligently working toward increasing the level of his religious maturity. But two stand out in glaring significance. One, the challenges of living creatively, harmoniously (or perhaps even living at all) with one another in a nuclear age emphasize the need for rapidly producing a generation of emotionally and religiously mature persons. And two, the possibility of man eventually becoming like God, achieving the Latter-day Saint philosophy of eternal progression and accepting the invitation of Christ to "Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect," (Mt. 5:48) must surely be built upon the foundations of emotional and religious maturity. 860 CHORAL MUSIC for RELIEF SOCIETY i a a ( ) ) CHORUSES Come Ye Blessed of My Father—Madsen.20 Go Ye Forth With My Word—Madsen...25 ) Incline Your Ear— Wilkes oe 20 ) Lord, Hear Our Prayer—Verdi. 20 ) Lord's Prayer— Gates _ eeeeeeeeee eee 20 Music—Marsden.20 ) My Redeemer Lives —Gates ee 20 ) O Lovely Land, America—Madsen...20 ) Open Our Eyes— Macfarlane 25 ) Send Forth Thy Spirit—Schuetky 20 Music Sent on Approval Use this advertisement as your order blank (eet Sa ae a COCt DAYNES MUSIC COMPANY 15 E.
