'These were the words of God the Son fortelling his time of birth into mortal life, words uttered to Nephi on the Western Hemisphere, while in far-off Judea, Mary "being great with child" was being found a resting place by Joseph, where the Son of God would be born. It has been approximately 1959 years since then, yet every day every person by his beliefs, convictions, and actions declares his acceptance or rejection of the Messiah. Especially at this season one reads the tender and simple, albeit the glorious words recounting the Lord's birth. 'The shepherds come again to kneel in reverence at the manger after they heard, in fearful wonder, the heavenly strains "Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace, good will toward men." The shepherds may never have known of the visit of the Magi; of the flight into Egypt; nor of events which transpired on the Western Continent — of the day and a night and a day when there was light and a new star arose marking the birth of the Savior. For contemporary with the years of Jesus' life, only a little information may have come to one and then another, until his public ministry was companioned by his disciples. But this is the day of the dispensation of the fulness of times when Page 812 all things are gathered together in one, and a Latter-day Saint has available the fulness of the gospel to recreate a sequence of Christ's ministry. As one studies his life from year to vear, the delineation becomes clearer, the contrasts sharper, the details fuller, and the radiance and glory brighter as humility, understanding, and worship become ingrained. As life's experiences and _ trials etch one's soul, the significance of the events of the Savior's mission take on a deeper richness. A mother senses more keenly the meaning of a sword which should pierce the side of Mary also; one studies the words of the Messiah for doctrinal knowledge; sickness in the home sends one to read of the healings and promises of Jesus; poverty and want seek solace in the comforting words of the Master. One sins and one hunts for encouraging words spoken to the repentant sinner; one endures trials, without justification to mortal perception, and one contemplates the humiliation and _ indignities heaped upon the Savior, and reflects upon the Lord's silent submission. Mental or physical sufferings recall the exquisite pain of the Redeemer in the Garden of Gethsemane where he sweat drops of blood. One heeds his words to repent, one takes his yoke upon him, and, finally, one struggles for that peace which passeth understanding, bequeathed to all by the Master. Jesus Christ is the Shepherd who guards and preserves the flock in the fold, through whose door alone his sheep may enter who know his voice. 'lhe mysteries of godliness are opened to one's searching gaze. Jesus Christ is the way and the truth; all life is in him. To scale the heights of exaltation, his flaming, living light defines the narrow way of ascent. None enters those jaspered walls but those who have taken upon themselves the name of Christ. Nearly two thousand years ago the Babe was born in a lowly stable. To know him and the Father who 813 gave his Only Begotten Son as the Supreme Gift to the world, is the meaning of Christmas. Each individual places the value of this Gift to himself. The blind, the indifterent, the wicked value the Gift as did those of the Savior's day who "went back and walked no more with him"; who "went away sorrowful"; who "sought to take him." They were among those who cried "Crucify him." But to those seeking to follow the Christ with humble hearts and contrite spirits, the Supreme Gift may bring joy and glory unspeakable, and the promise of eternal life. —M. C. S.
