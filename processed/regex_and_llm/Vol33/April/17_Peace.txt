Peace
PROVIDENCE has a way of seeing that we get the right mates, if we're obedient. Anyway, that's how it was with Henry and me.
My father wanted me to marry Eph Wilson, and Brother Matt Wilson, Eph's father, was as eager as mine to see us make a go of it. I suppose they felt that a friendship at the as theirs could yield only good fruit.
When father asked me, "Why don't you like Eph, Elvira?" all I could say was, "He has big feet and he dances like a clodhopper," but Eph had more serious faults than that. My granddaughters would call him a wolf. You see, a girl couldn't tell her father that.
It mattered about the dancing though, too. Glory! How I loved to dance! Just the twang of a fiddle set my blood to leaping like a mountain brook. It still does.
But there was one reason why I enjoyed being squired around by Eph, and that was Melissa Bates. There was a girl for you, and she was wild about Eph. It wasn't just spite in me either. I had an old score to pay off with that young lady. See my hand. That scar. Still puckers my palm. Well, I owe that to her. Pushed me against a stove. She claimed it was an accident, but I saw her eyes.... But that's another story.
The best dance of the year was held on a midwinter's night, and the man you went with was supposed to be the one and only. We began to plan for a high after Christmas, and
the year I met Henry I had decided that I was going to have a new calico dress for that party. Mother had given me my first pair of boughten shoes, I mean, the first pair that had been made back East. They had shiny pointed toes and high heels and high tops. They were elegant and I was proud of them.
A calico dress wasn't bad, even for February, when you remember that we made our dresses with tight-fitting basques and lined them and had very full skirts. I knew I'd be happy in a new calico, much as I'd love a silk or velvet.
Sister Comer, out at the flour mill, needed a girl for two or three weeks, so I went to work for her, helping to cook for the mill hands and caring for her family and doing general housework. When I visited at home, Mother and I planned the new dress. But I couldn't see my way clear to getting hoops for it. I was earning two dollars a week, and that was good pay, too, but hoops cost four or five dollars and so would the calico, so how could I get both?
One night I was sitting by the Franklin stove in Mother's sewing rocker, crocheting lace, when father said, "Elvira, I suppose you'll be going to the big dance with Eph."
I felt panicky. Eph? The big dance? Suddenly I shouted, "No!"
Father went white and got up and strode outside. When he came back in, he said, "I've been figuring, and I think I'll be able to get the hoops for your dress."
