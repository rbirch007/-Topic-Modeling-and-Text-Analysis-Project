Her righteousness knew its boundaries. They were distinct boundaries, with good and evil clearly fenced, and with never a misty line between to baffle us. She lived within those boundaries and taught us to do the same. We sat in church of a Sunday; she saw to it. 'The very roots of memory seem to begin at the meetinghouse in the faint scent of talcum drifting from under the collar of her dress, the warmth of the hall releasing it in a sweet aura around her. If food is one of the joys of life — and surely it is — then my mother gave us joy, daily. Oh, the mysteries she placed before our hunger — taken from the oven or the shiny black top of her coal range. And no one was ever forgotten. For the late or absent, the choicest portions were stored in the dark cavern of the warming-oven, safe and succulent, waiting to heal weariness or discouragement — luscious roast, beans baked brown with homemade chili sauce, parsnips boiled and then browned in butter, Danish dumplings exquisitely shaped, floating among tiny islands of golden fat where bits of green parsley grew like palms—there were the celestial lemon pies and the king of pies, dewberries, and brown betty with cream, rich and golden. At the end of a day working in the fruit, or after a day's skating on the humped canals, or in the twilight following an afternoon's swimming in the creek, there waited the treasure at the end of the rainbowed hours: my mother's heavenly food and she, neat and clean, always there to serve it. Only a few times a year she left us for a half day's shopping. The house was clean and unbelievably empty, and hollow, with a cold feeling even on the hottest days. We watched for her return and ran to meet her and carry the mysterious parcels with the strange goodies for us, which we always knew she had hidden away. The President of Relief Society in our ward told me they were planning to have a ward dinner in a few days, and would I come along with a group of other women to help with the preparations for the dinner? First, I thought that I must have misunderstood her, but no, it was no mistake, she had really asked me to help with the preparations for the ward dinner, just as if I were in no way different from the others. "Oh," I stammered, "do you really, really think that I can be of any use to you?" "Of course," she answered, cheerfully. "Why not? We all know that you can do almost all the things that others can do." 'Those were words I needed most to hear; they warmed my heart; gave me faith and confidence in myself; yes, they meant a lot to me. You see, I am both blind and deaf, and I have grown used to the idea that people around me consider me as not being capable of doing anything very useful. Whenever | wanted to be of some kind of help, J was never accepted. "No, this is too dangerous for you'; "No, this is too hard for you"; or they were afraid I would spoil something. 'The only place on this earth where they thought I belonged was in a good comfortable chair. How could I make them understand how wrong they were and how much I longed to be among them and do my little share? Just a couple of days ago, I was again asked to help with a dinner. There I was, crumbing an unbelievably great amount of bread, helping to mix it together with some other ingredients, so that the result would be a nice dressing for the turkeys. The kitchen was filled with activities, busy women went about doing their tasks, I could not see them, neither hear them, but I knew they were chatting. By the sense of smell and touch, I could tell what some of them were doing. One of them came to talk to me; when people are talking to me, they have to use their hands, and this woman had some very wet and cold hands, so I knew she was cleaning vegetables.
