Literature Comes of Age Lesson 17—Expanding Horizons Elder Briant S. Jacobs (Textbook: America's Literature, by James D. Hart and Clarence Gohdes, Dryden Press, New York, pp. 161-192) For Tuesday, October 18, 1960 Objective: To outline how new and complex relationships within the new Nation expressed themselves through its literature. ROM the times of John Smith through Jefferson, feelings of hope and fear and hatred and faith continued to accumulate with mounting acceleration and density until the crescendo became fury. Armed conflict with oppressive fellow Englishmen appeared inevitable, though never desirable. Likewise, the Nation's unpremeditated preparations for more than a century and a half found it unprepared to declare suddenly that it was sufficiently mature to emerge overnight as a_ self-justifying, self-sustaining fledgling in the world community. Rash though such headstrong action appeared to the sovereign powers as well as to tens of thousands of Loyalists who fled or abstained, actually there was no choice. Access to greatness was suddenly thrust upon the colonists. Either they acted at once to achieve their new identity or they perished. That the American colonies succeeded in winning the war is miraculous, a miracle which ever renews and brightens itself the farther we are separated from it in time. Once peace treaties were signed in 1783, the colonies had a moment to examine their separateness and to ask who they were. Even while rejoicing in their dearly won freedom, they were also somewhat disconcerted by their own audacity, yet Page 482 proud of it. By the end, in 1815, of the War of 1812, called by opponents, "Mr. Madison's War," national pride not only triumphed over other emotions, but burst forth in soaring poetry and tinseled oratory as dazzling as "the rockets' red glare' over Fort McHenry which inspired Francis Scott Key to set his inspiration to music — the music by his contemporary Englishman, John Stafford Smith. Likewise, when Samuel Francis Smith composed his "America" in 1831, the music he chose was "God Save the King." How aptly, then, do these two most "American" patriotic songs demonstrate the strong ties which bound the new Nation to Mother England: habitually, unconsciously, and with no apparent objection or incongruity, English tunes were employed to immortalize the United States' independence from her! Although during the Revolutionary War years General Washington was plagued by such problems as loyal Massachusetts volunteers who threatened to quit and go home before they would be commanded by officers from foreign New Jersey or New York, predominantly the war compelled the colonies to unite as thev met a common foe.
