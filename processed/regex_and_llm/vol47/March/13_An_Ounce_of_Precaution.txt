An Ounce of Precaution Mabel Harmer HE members of the Marshall family were enjoying their usual sound sleep one night when the father was awakened by the smell of smoke. After arousing his wife, he rushed into the next room and jerked the two eldest boys out of bed. He didn't take time to investigate whether or not there was any danger. He simply shouted, 'Fire! Scoot!" The lads grabbed bathrobes and scooted. He went across the hall, picked up Debbie and Dina, the six-yearold twins, and followed the boys outside. He knew that Mrs. Marshall had already escaped with the baby and two-year-old Jean. It had taken less than three minutes for all of the family to get out of the house. Only then did he go back inside to phone to the fire department. How was it that each one knew exactly what to do in case of such an emergency? It was because Mr. Marshall had taken the precaution of holding a fire drill the very day the family had moved into the new home. It was still so new that the blaze had started from paint cloths left too close to a radiator. Fortunately, the fire was confined to one room. Still more fortunate —due to that ounce of precaution— the family escaped safely. Almost every day, especially during the cold winter months, the newspapers carry headlines of death by fire. More often than not, the victims are children. Surely many of the deaths could be prevented. Page 186 What could be more simple than taking the family through a fie drill, showing them how to get out if the stairways, or other usual exits are cut off? Why not teach children such simple procedures as keeping their heads near the floor if a room is heavy with smoke, or, if possible, to put a wet towel over the nose and mouth to assist in breathing. One young woman who lost her life in an apartment house fire, could have been saved if she and her husband had felt the door to find out if it was hot, before opening it to let in the death-dealing smoke. They could both have escaped through the window. As it was, he lost hold of her hand in the darkness and only he reached the window alive. One more simple precaution. Everyone who is old enough should memorize the telephone numbcr of the fire department. Often one is too excited to look it up correctly. Sometimes there are no lights by which one can look it up. The least anyone can do is to have the number on a card above the phone, along with that of the police department and the family doctor. AST year there was a total of over 35,000 deaths on the highway. Next to this avenue as an exit from life, the home takes dubious second honors as a setting for accidental death. If adults choose to risk their lives by improper wiring, driving through the night when half asleep, or climbing on unsteady ladders, there isn't much anyone AN OUNCE OF PRECAUTION can do about it. But children are different. We should do everything in our power not only to protect them from danger, but also to teach them how to help protect themselves. One conscientious young mother in our neighborhood has trained her children to come home and ask permission before eating any candy that may be given to them. She conceived the idea so that she could keep track of how much they ate, and when, but the rule paid off in another way. Five-year-old Ann was playing out with friends when they found some "candy" in a garbage can. Why people will be so criminally careless as to put dangerous pills or poison out where youngsters can get hold of them is beyond comprehension, but it does happen. The other children urged Ann to eat the "candy" but, true to her training, she went home first to ask permission. The pills were labeled For adults only, to be taken sparingly. Had the child eaten them in any quantity, the result might have been disastrous. How many children have been killed or maimed by having firearms pointed at them in play? One such tragedy occurred on Christmas day with a weapon that had been given as a gift. As is usually the case, this gun was "unloaded." It is only common sense — although far too uncommonly exercised — to make sure that all firearms in the house are unloaded and are out of the reach of children. But one should go a step further than this and insist that, even in play, no child aims even a toy weapon at another child's head. The dread of every parent is that a child may be molested by a sex pervert. It is not wise nor necessary to frighten a child unduly, but there are certain precautions that can and should be taken. Fortunately, many school officials are now taking the responsibility of teaching children how to avoid such dangerous encounters. First of all, a child should be warned never to get into an automobile with a stranger. Even going for a visit in the same neighborhood, it is a simple matter to have the child telephone and let mother know that she has arrived at her friend's house. Two small girls who lived in the suburbs had been instructed that they should never get into an automobile with anyone except friends of the family. When a man who lived in the same neighborhood, offered to drive them home from the end of the bus line, they accepted with provision, "First you must give us your telephone number, in case anything happens to us." The neighbor gravely wrote down his phone number, then drove them safely home. 'Their mother impressed the warning a bit more deeply for the future. HERE are certain danger spots besides automobiles that children should be warned about. Foremost among these are movie houses, rest rooms, and public parks. Recently, police dogs have been added to the force that patrols the parks of some cities. 'They are valuable in flushing out anyone who might be lurking in the shrubbery. When a movie is being shown that will attract a large audience of chil- 188 dren, the public safety department has a number of extra officers on hand. At this writing, the body of a Camp Fire girl has been found, ravished and slain, while selling candy. How easy it would have been for some man to have invited her to step inside the house while he made a purchase. A frend of mine said that after she had invited a Girl Scout indoors to buy her cookies, she warned the girl never to go inside another house. Almost every parent knows the anxiety and terror of having a child lost. In the great majority of cases children return home safely, even after an expedition into unknown streets; however, once in awhile they do not. As soon as he is able to do so a child should memorize his name and address. Even earlier, a simple precaution is to sew his name and address on a tape inside his clothing. Always he should be taught that the policeman is his friend. It is hoped that there is no one so benighted in these days as to frighten a child into good behavior by the threat of calling a policeman. While a dog may be man's best friend, a strange dog, on the other hand, may be a dangerous enemy. Every youngster should be cautioned against petting strange dogs. I shall never forget the agony I suffered when our three-year-old boy was attacked in the face by a strange dog. For some time we feared the sight in one eye might be gone. 'True, the dog was tied up and the child should not have gone near; but he didn't know that. We had failed to warn him that all dogs are not playmates. In some places the irrigation ditches and streams claim the lives of an appalling number of tiny victims every year. Increased watchfulness on the part of parents is the most important thing that can be done to lessen the number of these tragedies. But there are some precautions that can be taken with older children, such as_ teaching them to swim. Even in places where it is too far to swim to shore, a child who has learned to handle himself in water may be able to stav afloat and keep from getting panicky until help comes. The rudiments of artificial respiration should be learned, for many a life has been saved by the simple method of breathing into the patient's mouth. No one wants to turn a child into an "accident -chondriac," fearful that any move may send him to a hospital, but it is wise to teach safety rules persistently, one at a time, and parents should see that these rules are practiced. 'The rewards may be the saving of a life, and the life you save may be that of your own child. ><
