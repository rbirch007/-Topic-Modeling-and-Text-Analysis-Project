Sribute to the Singing Mothers President David O. McKay [Comment at the Seventh Session of the 124th Annual Conference, Tuesday, April 6, 1954] HEN we realize that these mothers come in small groups from various, and in some cases distant, parts of the Church, and have had only one or possibly two opportunities to practice as a group, we marvel at the expression and inspiration that Sister Madsen succeeds in bringing out of this wonderful Singing Mothers organization. An Appreciation to Slorence Yepperson ITladsen Vida F. Swenson How priceless—those rehearsals! What a privilege we have had To learn to sing together. Sister Madsen, we are glad That you have come among us here in this great latter-day To bless us with your leadership, in your most special way. That precious instrument, the voice, can lovely be—or queer. We're sure at times disturbing sounds, in discord, reached your ear, The title, Dr. Madsen, is appropriate, indeed. You know just how to cure our ills and help us to succeed. Directions you give clearly; and your method is unique. We can't help knowing what to do whenever these words you speak: "Relax your jaw; don't "punch each word; get quickly to the vowel. Don't spread the e; look pleasant; you're not happy, if you scowl. And sing the message, not just words. You've much improvement shown! Just solo voices take those parts and sound just like one voice. Now get your breath right under it. 'That combination's choice, Just pantomime uncertain notes; the audience can't tell. To spoil it, it just takes one voice, so mark your copies well. Drive carefully; and do keep well; and a good breakfast eat. Leave early so you'll be on time. Let's all be kind and sweet." Your patience was unlimited; your helpfulness, supreme. The type of singing you produce surpasses any dream. Unselfishly you've shared with us your time and talents, too. And we regret the time's so short that we can spend with you. We're grateful for the full support your husband's freely shown. Yes, Dr. Franklin Madsen, your devotion's truly known. To those who've play ed the organ or piano faithfully, We say a hearty "Thank you!" You responded cheerfully. Dear Sister Madsen, we are proud when you step to your place To lead us Singing Mothers; and we read your radiant face. Though uttering not a single word, you speak; and we all stand, Responding to the message of your trained and guided hand. To feel the spirit of the song, we need but watch your face That mirrors each directive act performed with skill and grace. We sing the glorious hymns of praise, inspired through leadership That thrills us to our very souls and holds us in its grip. We catch the meaning of the words that are to music set, And feel a thrill of ecstasy we never shall forget. God bless you, Sister Madsen; may your life be sweet and long, That other added thousands may participate in song Because of your great leadership, so humble and sincere. May joy be yours as joy you've given, through each succeeding year. Down in the pasture a frog concert had started, and from overhead the white eve of a star looked down. Summer twilight—how many times she had watched it from this old porch, how many times in the last fitty years! Fifty years—a smile lighted her wrinkled face. Was it possible that fifty years had come and gone? She looked down at Will resting on the steps, his graying head against the porch post. Will McCarthy—seventy-five. His hair had been raven black and his eyes bright and sparkling fifty years ago when he had brought her here as a bride. Funny, she thought, but wonderful, too, that I love him more now than I did then. What a long time ago that was, and how much a body learns in fifty years. From the steps Will McCarthy spoke without turning his head, "It's been a good day, Mother, a wonderful day. Did you think when | brought you here, Susie girl, that we'd live to see this day?" Susie shook her head. "I was too busy hoping you'd get me into the house without letting me fall to worry about what I'd be or not be doing in fifty years." "Yes, I guess you were," Will laughed, and turned to look at her. "Fifty years," he mused. "For the most part it's been good, too. You say Nina took her boys to the show —and Marian? Out with Howard, I guess." Page 386 Susie said nothing. Rocking gently back and forth, she was busy with her thoughts. Fifty years held so much to remember. She had come here as a bride to this big rock house, and in all that time she had never lived anywhere else. 'The house, the meadows, the orchard, the farm fields—here she had lived and laughed and worked and _suffered, with Will always by her side. And she had come to know his every like and dislike; she could almost always tell what he was going to say before he said it. 'Together, they had saved and struggled to buy the "south forty." She had hepled him plant the raspberries and set out the orchard trees. She had even helped shingle the barn. Will had helped her raise a flock of chickens and plant a garden each year. Not only once, but several times they had papered each room in the house. 'Their house that echoed with so many tender memories. In the big, north bedroom every one of her six children had been born, and there one baby girl had died. How many meals the seven of them had eaten together in the kitchen or, when there was company, in the dining room. How many songs they had sung around the organ in the front room. 'The organ was squeaky and out of tune now, and of course it was out of style, so they had moved it to one of the upstair bedrooms.
