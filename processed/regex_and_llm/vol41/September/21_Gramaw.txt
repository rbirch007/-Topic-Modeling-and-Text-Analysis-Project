Gramaw Kathryn Forbes Clyde skipping home from school and saw Gramaw sitting in the low chair rocking back and forth, back and forth. Sometimes Gramaw started rocking by the window and rocked way over to the machine, and then she would pick up her chair and start all over again. Gramaw always made me feel all warm inside, just lke a little light was turned on. On Saturdays when I went to visit Gramaw she would say, "Eh, an 'eres my gurrl," just like I was special. Pa had a joke with Gramaw about putting ""atches'" where there werent any and leaving them off when she ought to have said them. He would say, "Well, Gramaw dear, will you 'ave an 'erring or a hegg today?" - Once I asked my father what made Gramaw talk so funny, and he said, "Hush, child, she'll hear you." Whenever Gramaw came to visit, we always had something very good for supper. Ma would go outside to the cellar under the granary and take me with her to help carry the things. I liked to go into the cool, dark places, which smelled of whitewash and apples and carrots and potatoes. Ma kept the milk in a little cupboard with screen wire all around it, to keep out the bugs, and on the days when Gramaw came, she would take a pitcher and a big spoon down cellar and skim off the Page 610 | was always glad when I came thick cream for our strawberries. Other days, when we didn't have company, she would pour the cream in a big crock jar for churning. Once when I was only six, I caught the scarlet fever from Jimmie. -'I'he Marshal came and nailed a yellow sign up on our front porch and he told Ma that nobody could come in or go out of our house for three whole weeks because we were quarantined in. When people would come past our place, they would walk out in the street until they passed by. Ma said it was because they were afraid they would catch it. But Gramaw, she wasn't frightened, no sir, she just got herself quarantined in with us, so she could tell me stories and play games. When all the scales had dropped off me, Ma washed my hair and gave me a bath in the tin tub, and then Gramaw and I had to go sit under the apple tree all afternoon while Ma stuffed up all the doors and windows and fumigated the whole house. The only thing I didn't like was that Ma had to burn my best doll. Scarlet fever was fun, though, and I told Ma I wished I could have it all over again, so Gramaw would come and stay with us. Whenever Gramaw had supper at our place, it meant we would have thick, juicy pieces of ham, and buttermilk biscuits so light that Pa said we'd have to close all the doors and windows or they would float nght outside. Then there were good milk gravy and new potatoes and green peas, fresh out of our garden. Ma "GRAMAW"" would scold Gramaw because she just "pecked at her food like a little bird," but Grimaw always said, "T've enough, and you've enough, and very good it's been." After supper I hated to hear the Seth Thomas clock make the little buzzing noise that meant it was getting ready to strike, for when that happened, Gramaw would reach for the little black lace bonnet, with 611 strings that tied under her chin. She would kiss everybody goodbye but me, because Ma always let me walk clear down to Plummer's corner with her. There Gramaw would feel in her big pocket and say, "'Ere child, 'ave a_ peppermint." Then she would kiss me and tell me to run back home, but I couldn't run because my feet felt heavy like flatirons at leaving Gramaw.
