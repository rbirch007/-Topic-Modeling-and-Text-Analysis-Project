Rather it is directed to those who prefer the fruits of their own labors; those who find the spring catalogue of the seedsmen the most fascinating document in print; those who react instinctively to the challenge of a freshly plowed or spaded plot of ground. Home gardening, probably almost as old as man, has experienced the greatest uplift in its history during the last few years with the discovery of the means of preserving foodstuffs by quick freezing. When man perfected this new means of putting Jack Frost to work, more was accomplished than just to introduce an ingenious kitchen appliance. Bartiers to the seasons were broken down, barriers between the menus of the frigid, temperate, and tropical zones—and in our little backyard gardens—the barriers between surplus wastes and economic usage. The home freezer has given "the man with the hoe" a new incentive and value. Thanks to the home freezer, the products of a garden that could be enjoyed only for a brief season, can now be prisoned in an icy package and kept practically garden fresh throughout the year. These are widely discussed in seed catalogues, government bulletins, and magazine articles that you probably have in your home. Others are available at your library or through contact with your county agent, your state agricultural college, or state department of agriculture and home economics. A garden should be carefully planned, not only to insure the planting of varieties that do well in your particular climate and altitude, but of equal importance, to insure that what you raise will find a ready market on your table. A great mistake of tyro-gardeners is the growing of vegetables the family do not care for. Consider the turnip! If _ turnips carry a low popularity rating with your family, plant them sparingly, or better, not at all. Use the space for crops the youngsters like, and, if you must, get your turnips downtown at lunch. Another mistake is over planting, the temptation to use up that package of seed. If your garden must be laid out in long rows, it is often wise to divide these rows in half with stakes, and limit plantings of a variety to a half row. N planning a garden for freezing the products, one must give first place to those that freeze well and are old standbys: corn, peas, beans, beets, carrots. 'To round out your garden patch, some _ space should be allotted to small fruits, strawberries, and raspberries. Here are a few general suggestions: SPINACH and BROCCOLI: keep plantings small and follow with later plantings, ten days to two weeks apart. Corn: If your season is long enough for early and late varieties, plant each in blocks of several short rows rather than one long row. You will get better pollination and better filled ears. Ear worms can be controlled by dusting with an approved insecticide or dabbing on crank case drainings as soon as the silks appear. Peas: The larger varieties, such as Stratagem, Dwarf Telephone, or Giant Stride yield heavier and are easier to pick and shell. CABBAGE and CAULIFLOWER: Buy the plants for these! Dust with a powdered insecticide several times during the growing season to outwit the bugs. After cauliflower heads have formed, tie up leaves around heads to prevent sunburn. STRAWBERRIES: 'The everbearing varieties are best for the home gardener. They provide fruit all summer and well into the fall. The Centennial is recommended. Plant in rows eighteen inches apart, spacing plants a foot apart. After three or four years of bearing, dig up a third of the patch and replant with young runners. Repeat this every year, and you will have young plants always coming on. Everbearing strawberries tend to bear heavily and then go into short rest periods, followed by new bearings. These bearing and rest periods vary with ages of plants, so by staggering the ages of your patch you will always have berries for table use. Keep well watered, and if berries become small, scatter a good RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—APRIL 1954 commercial fertilizer along the rows and wet down with lawn spray. For regular irrigations, row waterings are best. RASPBERRIES: Here again, the everbearing varieties are recommended. Raspberries require plenty of elbow room; three to four feet between rows and at least two feet between plants. Single plants fill up unused corners attractively. If heavy snows tend to break down canes, drive stakes into ground in the fall and tie up plants. Remember that the canes that grow this year bear the berries next year. Weeps: If treated right they are the gardeners best friends. A_well-weeded patch is necessarily a well cultivated one. Get after weeds early before they get the upper hand and stunt the growth of the things you've planted. Time for Freezing One of the delights of growing a garden is that many varieties mature quickly. 'The dividends accrue rapidly both in terms of table use and largess for the freezer. Nearly all garden products can be frozen sat isfactorily; the exceptions being onions, lettuce, and other salad greens, radishes, and tomatoes. With your own garden close at hand, you can pick, pack, and freeze each product at the peak of its goodness and thus retain vitamin and flavor richness. Freezing is one of the simplest and least time-consuming ways to preserve foods at home. A complete guide of the preferred methods of preparing, packing, and cooking all types of foodstuffs suitable for freezing is to be found in Home and Garden Bulletin No. 10, Home Freezing of Fruits and Vegetables, published by the Superintendent of Documents, Washington, D. C. It will be mailed to you for fifteen GARDENING FOR THE HOME FREEZER cents. More detailed directions may be found in Your Home Freezer, by Ann Seranne (Doubleday & Co., Inc., New York, $3.75) and The Complete Book of Home Freezing, by Hazel Meyer (J. P. Lippincott Company, Philadelphia, $4.95). One general rule should be remembered. Nothing but the best is worthy of freezer space. What you put into your locker, if properly handled and protected, can be preserved in the same good, fresh condition in which you freeze it—but it will not be improved. It is extremely important to be sharply critical of the fruits and vegetables you select for freezing. Discard everything with bruised or overripe spots, as well as the underripe that have not reached their full peak of flavor. Preparation for Deep Freeze Everyone who uses a deep freeze for any length of time picks up many ideas and practices that are. Not always found in the books. Here are a few gathered here and there that may contribute to a fuller and more varied use of your freezer: Vegetables, being pre-cooked before freezing, need less time for cooking than fresh ones. Cook just long enough to tenderize and thus preserve vitamins, bright color, and fresh flavor. Vegetables should be cooked at once after thawing, starting while they are still partially frozen. Corn is best cut from the cob after blanching. For winter gourmets a few whole ears may well be frozen. For this, choose choice ears and leave the husks on. Do 255 not cook or otherwise wrap before freezing. 'This insures a firm cob and no cobby taste after cooking. Snap beans are usually cut or broken into short pieces. For vatiety, try freezing a few packages of the whole beans. These are particularly nice for salads and can be substituted for asparagus either as a base or for top garnishment. Cantaloupes and watermelons when frozen provide another tasty salad ingredient. Cut choice ripe meat into balls or cubes and pack in a container. For the thrill of perfect "fresh" peaches or apricots in the wintertime, try putting a few choice ones in the freezer whole and unwrapped. Allow an hour at room temperature for peaches to thaw. A half hour for apricots. Peel and slice just before serving. Dieters who cannot eat sugared fruits will find these a delectable addition to their menus. Fruits, such as peaches, apricots, and pears, tend to turn brown when frozen. This can be prevented by adding one half teaspoon of ascorbic acid to each quart of the sugar syrup used in packing them. All fruits should be served as soon as thawed. A few ice crystals in the fruit improve the texture for eating raw. Allow six to eight hours for thawing a one-pound syrup packed package in the refrigerator; two to four hours at room temperature; one-half to an hour in a pan of cold water. Fruits packed in dry sugar thaw somewhat faster.
