What It Takes Kay Islaub AUREEN got up as soon as M the alarm went off. She moved automatically to close the window and then around to Harvey's bed. "Harvey! Wake up, honey! It's six-thirty." The last was lost in a yawn. In the bathroom she surveyed herself in the mirror. If there was any comfort in being forty, it was to be forty and not look it. She had very few wrinkles, and the new haircut certainly minimized the effect of gray that was just beginning at her temples. Maureen was grateful that she did not look as tired as she felt. She was bone-tired, dogtired, several varieties of tired. It wasn't just that she had been up with Dickie at least half a dozen times during the night. Even after his coughing quieted down, and she got back into bed, she couldn't sleep. Her thoughts kept going to the dark-eyed girl sleeping in the room that used to be John's before he went overseas. John's wife had been with them a month now, and she still seemed as tense and frightened as she had the night she arrived. She had been weary and bewildered that night, and the welcoming committee had been noisy—the entire family: seven-year-old Dickie and the fourteenyear-old twins, plus Grandma Dunn and, of course, Harvey and herself. It was difficult to restrain the children, and even Grandma Dunn had been curious and excited about John's wife. When Maureen had finally taken Beverly up to John's room, she had suspected the girl was on the verge of hysteria, and as they sat down on the edge of the bed, she had taken one of Beverly's hands and said, "We're so happy you came to be with us, Beverly. We all want you to be as happy as possible here." Beverly's eyes had clouded with tears, and her "thank you" had been barely audible.
