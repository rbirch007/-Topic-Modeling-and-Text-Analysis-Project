This unique and attractive city was the setting for the International Council of Women Conference, June 7-18, 1954, which brought together approximately five hundred women representing thirty-four free nations. The International Council of Women is made up of National Councils of Women of many lands, and brings together, triennially, Page 648 women of all races, many nations, creeds, and classes, in the belief that the good of humanity and the wellbeing of the home and the family will be advanced by a greater unity of thought, purpose, and action on the part of organized women. The Council aims to present factual data in its efforts to focus the attention of organized women on matters related to family and community life, and to encourage efforts to correct unfavorable conditions, as well as to chart courses that will promote the well-being of the home and society. Resolutions designed to aid in the accomplishment of the aims of the Council are drawn up and voted upon at the triennial meetings. However, no member National Council can be committed, in any case, to any cause or project by International Council action, except by its own vote. Each participating National Council is allowed a number of voting delegates according to the size of its membership. The National Council of Women of the United States, the oldest member of the International Council, was allowed ten voting delegates and six alternates at the Helsinki conference. 'The following sections were held: arts-lettersmusic; child welfare; cinema; education; finance; health; home economics; housing; laws and suffrage; migration; moral welfare; peace and international relations; press and public relations; radio and _television; trades and professions. Sister Marba Josephson was assigned to the section on radio and television. I was assigned to the section on moral welfare. In these sections we were both given ample opportunity to express our opinions and viewpoints and to have voice in decisions. Some of the more serious moral problems brought to the attention of the group were: drug addiction, prostitution, extensiveness of sexual immorality, illegitimacy with its many attending problems, alcoholism, and practices which are destructive to the dignity of women, such as "'bride price," and the question of "the right of a widow to the custody of her children,' which exist in some countries, and the common practice, which exists in most countries, of holding "beauty contests in which women appear in scanty bathing suits and are judged very much the same as are prize cattle.' These matters were regarded as demanding the attention, the condemnation, and the action of organized women. The latter part of the conference was devoted to executive and general sessions. All matters to go before the general sessions were reviewed in the executive sessions, and resolutions and other matters THE INTERNATIONAL COUNCIL OF WOMEN CONFERENCE 653 requiring vote were presented at the general sessions to the body of delegates. Total of twenty-four resolutions was adopted by the Council having to do with matters of concern to women and in harmony with the purposes and aims of the Council. Examples of the type of resolutions adopted are: one on cinema, urging the importance of providing good family entertainment—films portraying sound moral standards and no undue violence; a resolution urging that measures be taken to do away with illiteracy, particularly in countries where this is prevalent; a resolution on moral — standards, wherein the International Council "reaffirmed its belief in the necessity of applying high moral standards to men and women equally." Drawing forth, perhaps, the greatest interest was a resolution having to do with the use of atomic energy. This resolution was designed to unite organized women to work toward the prevention of the destructive and aggressive use of atomic energy and to promote its pacific uses. Two new councils were admitted to membership—Nigeria and Pakistan. The introduction of the chairmen was a very colorful and impressive patt of the conference procedures. Interesting and enjoyable were the social functions given in private homes. Considerable recognition was accorded the United States delegation at the conference and in the election of officers. Mrs. Rose Parsons, a member of the United States Council, was re-elected as a vice president of the International Council. Mrs. Eunice Carter was elected a convener, and Mrs. Alice Stetten and Miss Grace Spofford were elected as vice-conveners for the next triennial meeting. In my opinion, the United States Council made an effective contribution to the conference. Dr. Jeanne Eder of Switzerland was re-elected president of the International Council for the next triennial period. Undoubtedly good was accomplished by the conference by way of greater understanding and ap- 654 preciation of the problems of the different nations and by the friendships established. Good will grow out of the conference because the attention of organized women was brought into focus on problems which should be of concern to women. I was impressed with the sweep of the conference, the willingness of the women to work together for common goals, and with the power inherent in the conference through its connection with so many important organizations of women in the world. Participation in the conference was a great experience. HE, experience was made more pleasant and profitable through the association with President and Sister Henry A. Matis of the Finnish Mission. I felt greatly blessed in having opportunity to meet with some of the Relief Society sisters of Finland and to observe the capable leadership, the many talents of the sisters, and their devotion to the work of Relief Society. The meaning of Relief Society in the lives of the women is very evident.
