Carlyle's Life Carlyle was the son of a stonemason, a staunch Scotch Puritan who had an uncommon gift of forcible expression. Stern, devout but kindly, his integrity, belief in LESSON DEPARTMENT hard work, harsh criticism of the leisure classes, distrust of the fine arts, and hatred of hypocrisy were deeply impressed upon his son Thomas. The eldest of nine children, Thomas Carlyle so loved books and reading that his parents decided to make the necessary sacrifice to give him an education. So at the age of fourteen he walked the hundred miles from his home town of FEcclefechan to Edinburgh to begin his study for the ministry. He was not a distinguished scholar, and _ left school without taking a degree; le abandoned as well his father's strict religious beliefs, because he felt they curtailed his freedom as an individual. He taught school for a few years, and was tutor to the children of a wealthy family, but he hated teaching, and despised the aristocratic rich, both for their lives of ease and for their feeling of unquestioned, inherited superiority. Throughout his life earned for himself the lasting friendship of intelligent, strong men and women. While at school he met a young teacher, Irving, who gave him access to his vast library and who introduced him to Jane Baille Welsh, a brilliant, beautiful girl, the only daughter of a wealthy doctor. Miss Welsh aspired to a literary career and Carlyle became her confidant and literary advisor. Their lengthy correspondence is one of the most spirited and exciting in the history of love. Poor, without any prospects, tormented by his own spiritual, political, and economic turmoils, Carlyle courted Jane for four years. In the meantime, through his readings, he discovered German literature, parCarlyle © 485 ticularly that of Kant, Goethe, and Schiller. He supported himself by making translations of their works and by articles on German literature in the Edinburgh Review, the most distinguished journal of the day. The philosophy of the Germans regarding the significance of the spiritual attributes of all things, appealed strongly to Carlyle. Goethe was one of his great life-passions, and, perhaps more than any other man, Carlyle is responsible for bringing to the English people an appreciation of Goethe and the contemporary German writers. Yet, save for Coleridge and John Stuart Mill, Carlyle had no use for his fellow English writers. In 1826, at the age of thirty-one, his courtship was successful, for Miss Welsh finally accepted his proposal of marriage. She was intensely loyal to her husband throughout the forty years of their marriage, but she also was a dynamic individualist and the humors and distempers of their married life have become proverbial. Their home was the center of a loyal circle of friends. Both the Carlyles were noted for their brilliant conversation, yet, as Tennyson noted, "Mr. And Mrs. Carlyle on the whole enjoyed life together else they would not have chafed one another so heartily." During the "hungry Forties," with the great potato famine in Ireland, two fears constantly hung over England like a pall: the fear of plague and disease, brought on by starvation and poor working conditions, and the fear that the mob LESSON DEPARTMENT would rise against the wealthy, ruling classes. Increasingly Carlyle fought for the rights of the exploited working classes, but he also became more strongly convinced that the masses could never be trusted to improve their own sordid lot -— indeed Carlyle blamed the trend toward democracy as the cause of England's suffering. 'This conviction that England could be saved only by the emergence of a great leader became increasingly vital to Carlyle. In 1837 he published his great History of the French Revolution, (Excerpts, text, pp. 482-484) presenting in France's disaster the living horror of what might happen to England if the mass population ever got out of hand. Carlyle's Heroes and Hero Worship, a series of lectures in 1840, again stated his belief in the need of a strong leader behind whom the people of England could mass their strength. In 1843 his Past and Present (text, pp. 485-501) reaffirmed Carlyle's belief in the gospel of work and 489 deeds (pp. 486-488), and his admiration for the fundamental English virtue of obeying that "inner feeling of what is true" (page 488). Of the many Victorian voices which gave England balance and bound her down to stern realities in an era of grave doubts and severe growing pains, both industrially and politically, Carlyle's stature and unyielding integrity dominate the period. A contemporary giant, a peasant and, at the same time, a firm believer in artistocracy, patient, penetrating, and fearless, 'Thomas Carlyle still remains one of the living attributes to the tenacity and spiritual strength of the English peoples. Also to consider the purposes uppermost in the minds of the founding fathers in drafting the Constitution of the United States. Four Purposes of a Constitution T is generally agreed by students of government that constitutions have four esscntial purposes. The first of these is the establishment of the offices and divisions of government and the definition of the powers of each division and office. Thus 490 in the case of the Constitution of the United States, Articles I, II, and III provide for the Legislative, the Executive, and Judicial Departments respectively. Over ninety per cent of our Constitution is devoted to the establishment of these three great departments and the definition of their functions and powers. Second, a constitution usually, if not always, states the source from which the government it creates, derives its power. The Constitution of the United States boldly locates this source of power in the people themselves. It establishes Congress as a representative body without attempting to outline the method by which Congress shall be elected. This is left for the individual states to determine. Third, the Constitution of the United States establishes and defines the civil rights of the American citizens and sets up the machinery for their protection and vindication. 'These civil rights are declarations of fundamental individual rights, with which government is prohibited from interferring. Among those named are the rights of religious liberty, freedom of speech and assembly, and others to which reference will be made. The fourth function of a constitution is to make provision for changes by orderly and free methods. In the case of the American Constitution, such change is brought about by the method of amendment as set out in Article V. The framers of the Constitution made provision for a government which is unique in many respects. Evidence of their genius is manifest in the reconciliation and compromises which they were able to RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1954 achieve and resolve by charting new, untried ideas. The American Constitution was indeed a new experiment in government for which there was no pattern to turn to as a guide. 'There was no governmental structure in existence to serve as a model, and many of the framers' ideas had never before been conceived, much less tried. Federal System of Government What is generally conceded to be the most striking innovation under the American Constitution was its creation of a federal system for the new American state. By federal system is meant the creation of a dual government. Every citizen of the United States is subjected to the jurisdiction of two governments, with the single exception of citizens residing in the District of Columbia. Citizens are subject to two sets of laws, dual legislative, executive, and judicial departments. In the national field, within the framework of the powers enumerated and granted to the National Government, a citizen is subject to federal law and jurisdiction. In the state field, he is at one and the same time also subject to state law and jurisdiction within the state in which he lives or transacts business. Allegiance is owed by every American to both governments, with the single exception above noted. He pays his taxes to both and participates in elections conducted by each. He may commit an act which is a crime under federal law, but which may not be at all illegal under state law, or vice versa. The framers had a reason for creating this dual system. 'They purposely provided that the states LESSON DEPARTMENT should be the main centers of administration and legislation. Matters pertaining to local interest, such as property rights, suffrage, personal rights, and civil and criminal offenses, were, in the main, left to the states to administer and control. Matters pertaining to interstate and foreign commerce, relations with foreign nations and states, national defense, coinage, and other matters of national interest and importance, were given to the National Government to administer and control. It would have been impossible, at the time the Constitution was writ-. Ten, to have organized a centralized and unified national government such as developed in England. In the first place, England is small in area and homogeneous in population. America is tremendously greater in area and complexity. By providing for the administration of local affairs in each state, the peculiar needs of each locality within state boundaries may develop a government best suited to its conditions and requirements. Another important feature of the federal system, as we know it under our Constitution, is that by leaving legislation and administration of local matters in local hands, the need for a vast governmental machine in Washington was eliminated. Even so, the personnel of the National Government has continued to grow and expand at an alarming rate. Without the federal system of national and local control existing side by side, the vastness of the federal machinery of Government would be immeasurably greater than it now is. The system of states furthermore lends to the fostering of the im- 491 portant principle of self-government. Each state government offers a laboratory in government for all to see. From each one the best ideas may be taken and incorporated in the others. Separation of Powers Perhaps the second most important principle of government found in our Constitution is that which is known as "the separation of powers." That is to say, the powers and functions of government are not united in any one department or division of the state. On the other hand, they are divided among the three great co-ordinate branches of the Legislative, E: xecutive, and Judicial Departments. Under the English system a struggle went on for centuries to determine whether the Crown (the executive ) or Parliament (the _ legislative) would be supreme. Parliament finally established itself in the position of primacy. The founding fathers were extremely suspicious of the power of government, and in drafting the Constitution took pains to see to it that one division should not dominate and control the others. These divisions or departments are so set up as to act as a check upon one another. Nothing is actually said in the Constitution about the separation of powers, but this principle is implicit in the provisions for separate and distinct divisions or departments. There is much merit to the philosophy of separation. It tends to prevent one department, which might seek to carty out policies which are bad, from being able readily to do so. Thus if Congress attempts to pass legislation which is bad for the country, 492 it may be vetoed by the President, and, thus be prevented from becoming law, unless re-enacted by a twothirds majority which may override a Presidential veto. 'The provision to override is likewise wholesome, because it prevents a President from blocking, at his whim, a legislative program which perhaps a vast majority of the people and public opinion may be in favor of having enacted. Many such examples could be cited to show the usefulness of dividing the powers of government in such a way as to prevent domination by one segment of it. It must be admitted that there are some weaknesses in this system. In times of crisis when prompt and decisive action is called for, the government cannot act as quickly as might be desirable to meet a sudden emergency. On the whole, it can be said that the advantages of the constitutional separation of powers far outweigh the disadvantages, and during the whole period of our national existence it has had sufficient flexibility to meet every test encountered. People Source of Power In the beginning of this lesson, reference was made to the pronouncement of the Constitution that the people of the United States are the source of the powers of Government. It is not stated in the Constitution itself, in explicit language, that the Government detives its powers from the consent of the governed, but the principle of popular sovereignty is implicit in the system which it created. The Tenth Amendment declares that all powers not expressly granted to the National Government are reserved RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1954 to the states or the people. 'The language of the Constitution and its amendments which deal with individual nghts and their protection are conclusive evidence that the people are the source of political power. 'The choice of members of Congress is expressly given to the "people" and not to the states. The Amendments to the Constitution which followed the Bill of Rights (first Ten Amendments) forbid the restriction of suffrage based upon race, color, previous condition of servitude, or sex. Thus the night of the people to control the machinery of government is protected, and the principle of popular government established beyond question. Military Power Subject to Civilian Control In another very important sense the Constitution provides for the protection of popular government by establishing the principle that the military power shall be subject to civilian control. The colonists were firm adherents of this principle. It was deeply imbedded in English political thought by the time the colonies were established. Under the Constitution, the command of the armed forces is firmly placed in the hands of the President, which office is civil and not military. Also, the Constitution provides that Congress may not appropriate funds for military purposes for more than two years in advance. The right of the states to maintain a militia was recognized, and its command belongs to the governors of the respective states except when called into the service of the United States. Civilian Control of Government Paramount LESSON DEPARTMENT In addition, the right of the people to keep and bear arms was preserved as a means of preventing any military group from organizing and seizing power over the people. The right of all individuals to be indicted by the grand jury proceeding and forbidding the denial of the night of habeas corpus (See Your Rugged Constitution, page 95), when coupled with all the other safeguards of individual liberty, insure that civilian control of government shall remain paramount at all times. 'The 'only circumstance under which military control may be exercised is in time of war or national peril when the exigencies of national safety demand it.
