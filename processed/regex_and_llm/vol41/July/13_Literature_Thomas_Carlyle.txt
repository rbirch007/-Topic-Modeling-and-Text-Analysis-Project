In approaching Thomas Carlyle, how fully we need not only word and picture; we need also the ring of the author's voice as he boomed out his gnarled sentences of homespun wisdom, soul-shaking prophecies, and profound insights reaching deep into the human soul — deeper still into the very soul of England. Carlyle's was one of the strongest, sternest Victorian voices. While Tennyson and Macaulay were chanting in popular harmonies the theme "Zion prospers; all is well in Zion," Carlyle, Ruskin, and Arnold were Jeremiahs in the wilderness, warning England of the shallow, easy comfort which might well wither her soul and stunt her destiny. A study of their writings will give us a better understanding of England today. Carlyle's Life Carlyle was the son of a stonemason, a staunch Scotch Puritan who had an uncommon gift of forcible expression. Stern, devout but kindly, his integrity, belief in LESSON DEPARTMENT hard work, harsh criticism of the leisure classes, distrust of the fine arts, and hatred of hypocrisy were deeply impressed upon his son Thomas. The eldest of nine children, Thomas Carlyle so loved books and reading that his parents decided to make the necessary sacrifice to give him an education. So at the age of fourteen he walked the hundred miles from his home town of FEcclefechan to Edinburgh to begin his study for the ministry. He was not a distinguished scholar, and _ left school without taking a degree; le abandoned as well his father's strict religious beliefs, because he felt they curtailed his freedom as an individual. He taught school for a few years, and was tutor to the children of a wealthy family, but he hated teaching, and despised the aristocratic rich, both for their lives of ease and for their feeling of unquestioned, inherited superiority. Throughout his life earned for himself the lasting friendship of intelligent, strong men and women. While at school he met a young teacher, Irving, who gave him access to his vast library and who introduced him to Jane Baille Welsh, a brilliant, beautiful girl, the only daughter of a wealthy doctor. Miss Welsh aspired to a literary career and Carlyle became her confidant and literary advisor. Their lengthy correspondence is one of the most spirited and exciting in the history of love. Poor, without any prospects, tormented by his own spiritual, political, and economic turmoils, Carlyle courted Jane for four years. In the meantime, through his readings, he discovered German literature, parCarlyle © 485 ticularly that of Kant, Goethe, and Schiller. He supported himself by making translations of their works and by articles on German literature in the Edinburgh Review, the most distinguished journal of the day. The philosophy of the Germans regarding the significance of the spiritual attributes of all things, appealed strongly to Carlyle. Goethe was one of his great life-passions, and, perhaps more than any other man, Carlyle is responsible for bringing to the English people an appreciation of Goethe and the contemporary German writers. Yet, save for Coleridge and John Stuart Mill, Carlyle had no use for his fellow English writers. In 1826, at the age of thirty-one, his courtship was successful, for Miss Welsh finally accepted his proposal of marriage. She was intensely loyal to her husband throughout the forty years of their marriage, but she also was a dynamic individualist and the humors and distempers of their married life have become proverbial. Their home was the center of a loyal circle of friends. Both the Carlyles were noted for their brilliant conversation, yet, as Tennyson noted, "Mr. And Mrs. Carlyle on the whole enjoyed life together else they would not have chafed one another so heartily." No children were born to their marriage. Mrs. Carlyle died in 1866, at the height of Carlyle's fame and influence. From that moment he was incurably lonely and disconsolate, and old age was upon him. In approaching Sartor Resartus— in approaching Carlyle and the Victorian Age, we should remind ourselves how basic, how modern (really how timeless) are the problems dealt with. That which plagued Carlyle, today, plagues us still. Carlyle felt the greatest threat to England's future was materialism — the insane lust for things; money and gadget-worship; and greed. ""The RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1954 modern hell," said Carlyle, "is the fear of not succeeding," and today, 120 years later, his definition of hell is not less modern, but only more intense and terrible. Further, success now, as then, is popularly measured in things, not thoughts, in prosperity rather than in inner peace. Carlyle's self-discovery began from his awareness that things as they appear on the surface are vastly different from things are they really are. For Carlyle clothes do not make the man. We should not forsake our divine, inherent sense of right and wrong and slip into the easy, _ comfortable sloth of judging either the physical world or people by surface appearance only. Carlyle asks us to search constantly for the spiritual reality beneath. Thus, in this great satire on clothing, Carlyle but reaffirms age-old truths: that language is the clothing of thought; the body is the clothing of the soul; and the universe is "the living Garment of God woven upon the roaring looms of time." In the first section, "The Everlasting No," (text, pp. 466-470) Carlyle, speaking as Diogenes 'Teufelsdrockh, (Toy'fuls-drock) a German professor, recounts his spiritual growing pains. He has lost his simple faith of childhood and passed into a state of doubt, denial, and unbelief. Listen to Carlyle's full tones and living words as he expresses his feelings in "The Everlasting No":... Now when I look back, it was a strange isolation I then lived in. The men and women around me, even speaking with me, were but Figures; I had, practically, forgotten that they were alive, that they were not merely automatic.
