Be Ye an Example Velma N. Simonsen [Address Delivered at the Annual General Relief Society Conference September 30, 1954| feel very thankful for the privilege of being here today, and it is gratifying to see the overwhelming number who are assembled here. It is an evidence of your devotion to the worthy cause in which we are engaged. The Lord Jesus Christ, teaching his disciples, called attention to the importance of their position and place in the world. He did this, notwithstanding that men considered the disciples of small consequence. He told them that they were the salt of the earth, the light of the world. He encouraged them to effort and achievement by showing them that their exalted positions would avail them little unless they made proper use of their high callings. He pointed out to them that, "... If the salt have lost his savour, wherewith shall it [the earth] be salted?" (Mt. 5:13). They were called, "... The light of the world. A city that is set on an hill cannot be hid" (Mt. 5:14) but were cautioned that " Neither do men light a candle, and put it under a bushel, but on a candlestick; and it giveth light unto all that are in the house" (Mt. 5:15) meaning that, while they were full of light and knowledge it was improper to hide that knowledge. It should be diffused abroad for the benefit of all. | They were then counseled: "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works, and Page 724 glorify your Father which is in heaven" (Mt. 5:16). 'These conditions and instructions apply to us today just as much as they did to those who lived in the days when these precious words were uttered. The commandment, "Let your light so shine before men, that they may see your good works,.." is just as binding upon Latter-day Saints as it was upon the former day saints. We sisters sitting here in this building are among those that are the salt of the earth, and in us is vested the gospel light, but it will avail nothing unless we consider ourselves of some consequence, unless we realize the importance of the positions and responsibilities which we hold, and let our light shine forth collectively and individually for the world to see and to follow. We have been admonished by our Priesthood authorities to be a light unto our sisters, to be leaders of our Father's daughters in this generation. How will we discharge this responsibility? First by example. Example is the greatest quality of leadership—not only in the Church but in every phase of life. Brigham Young said, ""The greatest and loudest sermon that can be preached, or that ever was preached on the face of the earth, is practice. No other is equal to it." It has been said that, "Precept is instruction written in the sand, and the first wave that comes along may obliterate the record, but example is record written BE YE AN EXAMPLE in rock which may endure throughout all eternity." Example is everything, and precept very little unless backed by example. President David O. McKay said, "In example we prove our worth." First we must consider the example of our own lives. Are we conforming to the ideals and_ standards of the Church? We do not need to go into a discussion of what these standards are. We know what they are, or we should know, if we are leaders, and we know within ourselves if we are living in accordance with these standards. HAT we do not fully realize, perhaps, is how important the light of our own life is in teaching others these ideals and standards. If we would truly teach a truth to another we must first live that truth. If we would teach others honesty, we must be honest. If we expect loyalty from those whom we lead, we must loyally support those who lead us. Questioning, before those whom we wish to lead, of the instructions given us by our leaders injures the fine spirit of loyalty and co-operation that makes for successful leadership. If we would be zealous in fulfilling the commandment to spread the gospel of Jesus Christ to our neighbors, we must impress them with the value of the gospel in our own lives as reflected by our good works. Living the good life is really the only way to teach the good life. Even our personal appearance is important in leadership. We have been told over and over again that our dress should be becomingly modest, neat, and clean. Many of today's fashions are not becoming 725 to Latter-day Saint women. Let us not adopt such fashions to ourselves, but do let us be an example of fastidiousness in our neatness and cleanliness. There is nowhere in the Church or in the world where good examples should be more evident than in our homes—that is where effective leadership should be most manifest. Since the art of making a good home is woman's greatest achievement, and since the maintaining of a home is her first responsibility, our homes should be examples of cleanliness, thrift, and spirituality, where the spirit of the Lord dwells, where religion is taught, both in our conversation and by our actions, and where our daughters are taught by example the fundamental arts of housekeeping. Under these conditions when the responsibilities of homemaking come to them, they will be free from the sorrows and perplexities which come from ignorance and inexperience. Today, as we read the frightening Statistics of divorce and delinquency even among our own Latter-day Saint people, and see the evil effects that come from broken homes, let us resolve that our own homes will be examples where children may see and absorb the sacredness of family life and the joys and responsibilities associated therewith, and that they may have a high view of marriage, realizing that it is not an arrangement to be terminated lightly. 'The greatest hope for the raising of the standards of the home, and thus of the community and the nation, is by the examples set by parents who know and love the Lord. Today, I have but briefly men- 726 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1954 tioned two obligations resting upon us as Latter-day Saint women—that of making our personal lives worthy examples, and in maintaining true Latter-day Saint homes. Let us sense seriously all the responsibilities that rest upon us and be worthy examples in all our responsibilities. May the Lord bless us all and be with us in maintaining our homes that they may be truly a city set upon a hill. May we, each as a Relief Society sister, be a_ lighted candlestick unto the thousands of women who look to us for guidance and leadership.. As _ President McKay has said, may we by our examples prove to be of great worth in building our Father's kingdom. >
