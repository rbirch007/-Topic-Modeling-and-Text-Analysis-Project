Old Year Grace Sayre The last day of the year has wound the clock, The last faint ember on the hearth burns low, December, pausing, opens up the door; He turns to go. Indeed we might say that Browning s concept of God ls centered about the word love; in similar manner Brownings own attitude toward man and woman, is based upon the same key word. Like Shakespeare, Browning saw the world as a stage, whose players are brought to life through the craftsmanship of a Master Artist. Like Shakespeare, Browning created all types of humanity, motivated by all the human passions: selfishness, cruelty, fleshly and intellectual lust, hate, jealousy, sloth, loyalty, love, faith, spirituality. He seeks neither to judge humanity nor to analyze and explain it, but only to condense the reality of a character into a few lines of poetry. Immediately the question pre Browning, who never qualifies his ringing belief both man and God, admit that evil is also an inherent part of man's nature? Browning was fascinated by the workings of evil in man, because wherever there is evil there must also be inner conflict and struggle— a complexity of motives which challenge his powers of perception and creation. 'Then, too, Browning believed that, since this life is largely a testing ground, it would not fulfill its purpose were evil non-existent. Finally, the presence of evil in a person did not make him less than human. Despite our weaknesses, Browning believed we should regard each other with understanding and sympathy. Love for mankind, then, enabled Browning to depict all types of characters. Browning's more typical use of love, however, was to elevate the beauty, the worth of married love; here the truth of human love becomes the very keystone of his view of life. Browning's love poems are numerous, and saturated with his burning testimony that love between the sexes is the highest good on earth. In "The Last Ride Together' (not in our text), the poem concludes: What if we still ride on, we two With life forever old yet new, Changed not in kind but in degree, The instant made eternity,— And heaven just prove that I and she Ride, ride together, forever ride? The Dramatic Monologue Browning is one of the most modern of Victorian poets. So much did he expect of readers that, for the most part, they were bewildered and could make nothing of him. Two sources of his difficulty should be mentioned. First, Browning had saturated himself so completely with his historical and imaginary materials that, to an extent, he freed himself from details in his intense concern for creating the whole. This goal of condensing into a few concentrated lines a living, unified character was so strong that Browning sometimes neglected relationship between the parts, and his readers became confused. A second cause, producing similar difficulties, was that Browning chose the spoken monologue as his medium, and with his goal of bringing the reader immediately and intimately into a dramatic, crucial moment in the life of the speaker, the monologue wanders loosely from one idea to another, and supplies little connection between widely scattered subjects. My Last Duchess Note to Class Leaders: In presenting "The Last Duchess' it would be advantageous for copies of the poem to be in the hands of class members. Surely, for many readers, if they have loved one poem in English literature it has been "My Last Duchess" (text, page 659). And justly so, for where else in fifty-six lines is condensed an entire way of life as exemplified by two characters whom we come to know as well as these? The great delight in the poem comes when it ends abruptly and we begin to realize how far we have come in so short a distance.
