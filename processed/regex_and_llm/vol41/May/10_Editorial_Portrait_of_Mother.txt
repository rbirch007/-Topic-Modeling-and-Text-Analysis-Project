At first, when they were small, the message seemed to refer to their grandmother, but later, upon returning to the beloved family home, they knew that the message referred to their own dear mother. We are coming, Mother, coming— From the loom and from the mine— Though it be our last sweet homing On that mother breast of thine. In times of separation from our mothers, whether it be the absence caused by the temporary affairs of earth, or that longer waiting until the time of reunion in the heavenly home, we often try to picture in memory the portrait of our mothers. Sometimes an evanescent happening or even some familiar scene — a flower garden, a thread of music, a lighted window — may bring to us, unexpectedly, a vivid and strangely comforting portrait of mother. The dear face may be remembered well, and the busy, untiring hands, the quick step in the kitchen, the quiet step to a child's crib. One woman may remember that once, upon admitting some misdemeanor of childhood, the mother only looked at her with tears in her eyes, and had to wait to find something to say. Another woman may Page 302 recall how swiftly her mother's fingers flew in sewing white lace upon an infant's dress. 'The portraits are varied and beautiful. Though we may not realize the full influence of these memories— these portraits, we know that in large measure they determine our attitudes, our actions, the very course of our lives. One woman has been influenced throughout her life by seeing repeatedly, in memory, her mother, who had come to visit the daughter at college, standing, after class had been dismissed, with her hand laid tenderly upon a bench in the room, tears in the mother's eyes. Then she _ said, "Such a thing as this learning was never possible for me, but I am so grateful that you can be here." Another woman, sincerely devoted to the work of the Church, recalls, as an indelible etching, the portrait of her mother — how she hurried down the poplar-lined street, taking her little daughter with her; how quickly the mother mounted the wooden steps to the Relief Society Hall, how soon she was taking charge of the meeting — then the music, the prayer, the quietness, and the light upon the mother's face. Strange how much is inscribed upon the pliant mind of a child through no direct word being said. It is by spiritual communion that much of earth's best instruction is given and received. EDITORIAL Many of us may be fortunate enough to recall the portrait of a mother who honored and respected the individualism of each of her children, who realized that each child is different from every other personality, the heritage given by the parents coming from ancestral sources remote and near, but always in a new combination of characteristics, talents, and temperament, with an individual, everlasting spirit. The mother has been the life- 303 giving vessel, but her duty and her influence are best exercised through love, patient direction, and inspirational example. And mothers who are wise, as well as devoted, may, over the years and into old age, share in the shining hopes, ideals, and accomplishments of their children, and these mothers may be comforted by knowing that the portraits of all mothers are forever indestructible. —V. South HE words so often used by the florist industry bring an intriguing thought to me. What has been said to me by way of flowers? What have I tried to say to others? When I was a little girl, no taller than a window sill, my world seemed to be made up mostly of flowers. Dozens of flowering house plants lined our windows. My view of the outside world was always framed by flowers and, as I grew taller, by lovely foliage and flowers. October, there was often wind and sleet and snow, but inside it was always summer. Mother's geraniums were deep red, bright red, old rose, pink, white, and salmon color. 'The Martha Washington geranium, with its gray-green leaves, had single flowers of old rose, with deep rose throats. 'The fuchsia was the wonder plant, with its delicate stems and _ lavender-red blossoms which hung down like bells on a Christmas tree. 'The Marguerite had fern-like foliage and large white daisy blossoms. 'Then there was the California pink which had a blossom of fine pink fringe and no leaves at all; it had queer needles like those of an evergreen! Every night through the long winter months, mother patiently carried her plants from the windows to the kitchen table where they would be safe from frost. Every morning she gave each plant a drink. One year she took new slips and repotted all the plants, using rich loamy soil from under the wild English willows. I have never learned any more of her secrets of Page 304 growing house plants; that is why I cannot grow them as well as she. Perhaps Mother was never conscious of saying things with her flowers; we were not conscious of receiving the messages. But they told us many things just the same. They told us that here was love and patience, not only for the plants, but for the children who lived with them. They told us that home was beautiful and warm and safe. As the plants cost mother not a cent, they told us that there are many beautiful and wonderful things in the world which have nothing at all to do with money. 'The blossoms cheered us through childhood illness and gave us courage when father was away at the hospital for three whole months. They taught us that God can do some very remarkable things. I can close my eyes and see the flowers and people marching through my girlhood. It is the junior prom. There are new formals, flowers, ecstasy! Now I am returning from a college spring formal. The young man in the tuxedo takes the rose from his lapel and presses it into my hand. 'The little flower tells the sweet message for which he cannot find adequate words. My wedding... There are flowers everywhere. Mother is saying, "Norma, the pink double hollyhocks have bloomed early this year, just for you." She is so happy including her flowers in the plans. "We'll decorate the table with glads, the white and pink coronas." I see again my wedding bouquet.
