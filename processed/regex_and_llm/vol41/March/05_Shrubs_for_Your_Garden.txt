You can have a whole garden of shrubs, colorful the year around. You can use them to give your garden complete privacy, to separate one section from the other, and, best of all, the shrub garden is easy to maintain and inexpensive. Shrubs are woody plants of bushy habits, developing several stems instead of a trunk as does a tree. 'They are a permanent part of the garden, yet, surprisingly, little thought or imagination is given to the selection and arrangement of shrubs. 'Too many shrubbery plantings are dull part of the year. One reason for this is the monotonous over-planting of a few well-known kinds. The overplanting of Spirea Van Houtti is an example. Every garden has its own peculiarities and some varieties of shrubs will not thrive in it, but there is such a wealth of material to choose from that there is no excuse for the overplanting of one variety. Before making a selection of shrubs consider the location, and the exposure, whether the shrubs will be in sun or shade; whether you have heavy soil which retains the moisture or soil that is light and dry. Consider, too, the amount of space to be filled, for this will determine whether you will select a dwarf, medium, or large-growing type of shrub. With wise selection of shrubs, you can screen out unsightly views, make mass plantings, have a flowering hedge, or divide your garden area into rooms for greater beauty. At the same time you will shut off, to a degree, the noises from a busy street, creating a beautiful retreat for family and friends. Foundation plantings using deciduous shrubs offer the gardener an opportunity to create a planting that is original and suitable to his own home. With thought and careful placing of shrubs, he will have a planting that will not be seen around every other house along the street. Flowering shrubs planted close together for a hedge should be left unclipped, but should not be allowed to. Grow without a yearly pruning so they become an unsightly tangle. Daphne cneorum is a choice plant for edgings for the shrubbery border or for rock gardens. One day John brought a traveler to the house. 'The traveler was inquiring about the best place to ford old Bear River, and he also wanted some dinner for himself and some hay for his own team. In true Yankee style, John invited him to stay to dinner and then took the horses to the corral and gave them huge forkfuls of hay. Virginia hardly knew what she could feed the man, or the family either, for that matter, but so as not to disappoint John, she had a chicken killed and fried it, baked hot biscuits, mashed the potatoes, brought butter and milk from the cool cellar, and, with a little honey for dessert and John to tell bear stories, the dinner was a great success. When the man was ready to leave with John, to show him where the ford was, he turned to Virginia, and, after thanking her for the nice dinner, he handed her a ten-dollar gold piece! 'l'o John's utter surprise and great disgust, she accepted it. Never had such a thing been heard of before in the valley anywhere. Imagine, taking pay for a meal of victuals. But Virginia was desperate—she could hear the skeleton rattle. Pa couldn't. Then the great day came. John was in the field mowing when the canvasser drove down the lane; but John spied him and, quickly as he could, he drove his horses to the near end of the field, unhitched them, and hurried to the house. But he arrived just as the canvasser had delivered the picture, pocketed the money, and was getting into his buggy. Virginia had accepted the picture hastily, handed over the money, and got rid of him almost before he realized it, for she had seen John coming and remembered the threat. She hated scenes. John said, "How do you do," and the canvasser answered, "How do you do," and drove away. John came on quietly into the house, as Virginia drew a long breath. Simultaneously they turned, and there was the picture of John propped carefully against the kitchen door. "What the...?" began John, but Virginia cried, "Oh, that man—the picture doesn't look a bit like you, John. See the dark hair and the mustache! Oh, John, I'm so sorry." John only stared at the picture.. The handsome face so unlike him in color, and yet there were the merry blue eyes and the dimple, which John noted, silent, but inwardly pleased. Virginia broke in, "It's just horrid, John, and I accepted the money to pay for it to surprise you." "And I love you dearly," John said, putting his arms around her. "It's not so good, darling, but we'll hang it up along with yours." And he did. Thus spoke President Dwight D. Eisenhower at the dedication ceremonies at the new District of Columbia Red Cross Chapter House on October 1, 1953. "We have many examples nowadays of man's selfishness, man's brutality and inhumanity to man, man's readiness to forget the golden rule and to live by some standard that he thinks will immediately advantage him at the expense of his fellows," the President said. "We have this in the international field. We have it far too often and discouragingly among groups or classes at home. 'The Red Cross, the nature of its slogan, of its purposes, the work that has been done through it, and the people that belong to it, bring to us, as we tend to gather discouragement about such things, realization that man is also made of nobler qualities than those of selfishness and greed and personal advantage. He is made up also of sacrifice, of nobleness, and love for fellow humans." The Red Cross has been distributing gamma globulin since 1944, largely as a measles prophylaxis, but recently, the organization was called upon to undertake an immediate and dramatic expansion of its operations to make available all the gamma globulin possible for the inoculation of children in the polio fight. In accordance with standard Red Cross practice, this gamma globulin was provided to the American people without charge for the derivative. As a result of this plan, the Red Cross will turn over to the Office of Defense Mobilization more than 11,118, c00 centimeters of gamma globulin by the end of 1954.
