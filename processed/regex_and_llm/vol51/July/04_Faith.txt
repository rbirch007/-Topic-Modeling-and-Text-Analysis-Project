Lucretia filled a steaming bowl with cornmeal mush for Jim, taking care not to spill on her bright rag carpet. The new cabin had a sweet smell about it, a neatness and warmth, but there was no pleasure in the pretty room for her this morning. She went and stood unseeingly at the tiny window. "Aren't you going to _ eat, Crishy?" Jim's face was clouded but his voice tender. "'You need it, for the baby's sake as well as you own." She could not even look at him. Her eyes were red from sleeplessness and frustrated weeping. "T couldn't swallow a bite." They had talked half the night. Now there was nothing left to say. Jim ate his mush and milk in silence while Lucretia nervously busied herself about the room, smoothing the fresh straw 494 Helen B. Gibbons mattress on their bed, pulling up the clean quilts. With the dipper from the bucket, she watered the geranium on the window-ledge. Half her mind kept silently insisting, I won't go! I can't go! But the other half yearned for the sweet safety of her husband's approval. Without turning to look, she could see his square bronzed face, and _ his light, bright hair. Even in homespun work clothes he looked magnificent. She had often reflected that this handsome outer form was but the mirror image of his powerful inner character.
