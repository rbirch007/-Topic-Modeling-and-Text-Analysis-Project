A wise man said, "Human. So0- cieties are happy in proportion as they have their treasure in that class of goods which are not lessened by being shared." The gospel of Jesus Christ requires from each member a personal investment of time, intelligence, talent, and wealth. This investment is in the development of the kingdom of God. In this kingdom (society) the welfare of others is the major treasure and service is the medium of exchange. Every member has the opportunity for full participation in 72 service to his fellow men. There is no limit to one's sharing in this treasure; therefore, as the wise man suggests, there is no limit to his happiness. Human weaknesses, selfishness, covetousness, and greed are the obstacles in the way of the realization of this ideal. A Lay Leadership The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints depends upon a lay leadership. Every member in good standing is eligible to hold office. Service in this respect begins very early. Young people ten and twelve years of age are frequently called to positions (Sunday School class officers, deacon presidencies, secretaries, etc.). The Church does not have a trained or professional ministry. It does have a program of training for leadership that begins formally with the deacon age and _ continues throughout the life of the individual. Because of this opportunity for training, the Church can and does tightly expect an improved leadership from each new generation. Each person suggested for office in the ward should be approached, in regard to the calling, by the bishopric. Officers of organizations may Suggest names of desired persons, but the first contact should be made by the bishopric only. After the interview with the bishopric, at Which time they satisfy themselves that this person is right with the Lord, properly prepared for leadership, and the assignment being considered is the proper one for this person, then and not until then, the bishopric should refer the person to the officer in charge and the member of the bishopric assigned to that organization. The officer in charge should then interview the new appointee and explain in detail the responsibilities and obligations of the calling. Every help should be given the new officer or teacher to assist him in successfully meeting this new opportunity for service. Many people who serve success. Fully in the Church are not highly trained for their assignments; but any member who is willing to work and put forth a sincere effort to know what the assignment requires, and who is humble enough to recognize that he is being called to the Lord's work, asking in faith for divine help, can fill well any assignment he may be called to in this Church. The Member's Part in Accepting a Calling It should be clear that any calling in this Church is a real opportunity. - First, it is an opportunity for service. It is doing something for others with an unselfish motive, voluntarily putting forth an effort for the betterment, relief, or general welfare of someone else. (2) It is an opportunity for self-improvement. One cannot do anything for others without receiving a greater benefit for himself. This may express itself in mental and spiritual growth as well as developing in him greater human understanding. (3) It is an opportunity for closer association and better communication with God. One cannot sincerely and conscientiously accept a part in the Lord's great program without feeling his influence in every righteous effort one puts forth in advancing his work. In accepting a call, one must realize that, for him, this call js the most important assignment in the Church. To the extent that one excels in carrying out one's assignment, the work of the Lord excels. It is the unified effort of all officers and teachers that makes the Church program succeed. Responsibility of Church Members. The responsibility is not only on the officers and teachers for the advancement of the work of the Lord. To be a good follower is an essential qualification for group membership. There are so many assignments in a ward, involving so many different people, that each person, no matter what his special calling or area of service may be, must also be an active participant as a ward member. In fact, one quality of good leadership is being able to identify oneself effectively with the group in a variety of ways. It is important that all ward members accept the opportunities for training provided by the organizations of the Church. By their participation in the program, growth and development, both spiritually and mentally, are assured. At the same time, the activity of the membership provides the sustaining and stimulating influence that officers and teachers need to help them make their efforts vital. Importance of the Sustaining Vote The members of a ward find it quite simple to raise their hands when the names of people are read to fill the various offices in the Church. Sometimes the presiding officer, so familiar with this procedure, does this assignment in such a routine way that the members, without thinking, raise their hands to sustain. 'This is not a healthy situation to have in any ward. However, more frequently, the lack of thought in the process of sustaining leaders is on the part of the members. 'Their indifference is clearly expressed in their failure to support the sustained leader with their participation in the activity involved in the discharge of his duty. Sustained by Action. When a member of a ward raises his hand to sustain a person in office, he is exercising one of the most important duties and privileges given to the membership of the Church. In the 20th Section of the Doctrine and LESSON DEPARTMENT Covenants we read, "No person is to be ordained to any office in this church, where there is a regularly organized branch of the same, without the vote of that church" (verse 65). The vote of a member should be carefully thought through before it is expressed. 'This is not a democratic process in the common meaning of the term. We believe the leadership is divinely called and operates under the inspiration of the Lord. Therefore, when the appropriate officer proposes and presents people to be sustained, the membership have the privilege of raising their hands for or against the recommendation. Usually the presiding officer will explain that the majority rules, but he would give consideration to the objections of the dissenting individual, after the meeting. By a member's raising his hand to sustain, he approves the selection, the assignment and he fully agrees to support the leader in carrying out the duties of his office. The responsibility of the sustaining member is met by his activity in helping to make the officer or teacher successful. Proper action ls more important than words or signs. "Verily I say, men should be anxiously engaged in a good cause, and do many things of their own free will, and bring to pass much righteousness" (D & C 58:27). Active support in the Church requires doing many things without having to be commanded. This admonition applies to both officers and members. Limitations in Church Callings At the time a person is called to serve in any office in this Church, careful instructions should be given as to his duties and responsibilities. A true quality of leadership is to recognize the extent of the responsibility of the office. 'The individual also should seek diligently to know his duty in the office to which he has been called. (D & C 107:99- 100. ) There is a close and interlocking relationship with every activity and function in the organization of the Church. It all comes under the direction of the Priesthood and insures the orderly operation of the kingdom. When duties are clearly defined and the directions followed, effective operation of the program is assured. 'This still leaves room for the expression of the personality and the initiative of the leader in executing his assignment. The secret of success in all Church work is being in tune with the Spirit of God and keeping in mind always the purpose and objective of the plan of life and salvation — which is to bring to pass the immortality and the eternal life of man. 1. Many Called But Few Are Chosen. In the 121st Section of the Doctrine and Covenants, we are reminded that to be chosen, one must be in tune with the Lord. The things of this world can and do become so important that they interfere with the discharge of our assignments in the Church. The rights of the Priesthood are inseparably connected with the powers of heaven and can only be controlled upon the principles of righteousness. One cannot serve the Lord satisfactorily in any office or calling in this Church unless the principles of righteousness and the purpose of the Church hold first place in one's thinking and acting. 'There is no place for substitutes, or halfway marks in this particular. The inability to achieve this dedication is why "many are called but few are chosen." 'They may not be chosen because of their failure to use the opportunity and knowledge with which they have been blessed to pursue the work of the Lord. (See D & C 95:5-6.) 2.
