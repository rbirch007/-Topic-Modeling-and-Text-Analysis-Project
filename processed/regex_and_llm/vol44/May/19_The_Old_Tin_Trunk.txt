THE OLD TIN TRUNK As the horses strained forward, the wire grew steadily tighter until Father judged it sufficiently well "stretched." Setting the brake, so the wagon could not settle back, he and Mother then started stapling the wire to the proper height on the posts. Ten days later, with two miles of fencing completed, our homestead, for the first time, assumed a definite identity. Before, it had been only 160 acres of prairie sod, lost in the midst of a prairie-sod world, and cryptically designated in records of the Land Office, "NW14 S. 27, Twp. 7, R. 6 E." Now, however, we could see the actual extent of it! All the land encompassed by these three tight, singing wires — all these rolling 321 hills, that rocky butte where the lupines grew tallest, those green draws and fertile slopes — all this was our homestead. Our home, our empire, our world, our universe! The curlews and prairie chickens were free to fly over this fence and to light upon our land. The meadow larks and buntings could nest in our fields if they chose; and the jackrabbits and coyotes might pass through the fence and pursue their age-old ways. With all these we would share gladly our little square of prairie. But never again would we need share with the ravaging hordes of range cattle! The accumulation of twenty years was being sorted—some items to be packed for shipping, some to be sent to the Deseret Industries, and much to the trash heap. The basement held the most formidable array of things that had - been saved to the last. 'Toys, books, and fruit bottles could not all be taken. Only books would be sent, which could not be replaced. One big trunk that held Hawaiian missionary relics would be sent intact. My eye caught the old tin trunk. That was a problem, a sentimental one, too. It had belonged to my grandmother, After her death, it had fallen into mother's hands, and now I was heir to it. For years, when I was a girl, it was brought out of its resting place every spring and fall for airing and cleaning. 'The contents were most fascinating. 'There was the red plush family album, with pictures dating back to the old country, the stern faces of our ancestors, the women with severe hair-dos, and dark men with foreboding mustaches. 'There was an account book kept by my grandmother when she tan a store, soon after arriving in the valley in 1860; baby clothes that had belonged to my mother; and, added to these relics, were 322 treasures of mine and my children. The former things had outlived any sentiment for any but me, and the top of the trunk was rent and bent. The bottom was worn through with rust, and I knew it wouldn't stand the long trip to California. What to do with it was something to ponder. The relics were finally stored in a stout box and labeled. I set the trunk to one side, still thinking of its disposal. 'The time was going by fast, and my husband had warned me about taking anything superfluous. Surely, the trunk was just that. Silly to be so sentimental. Many times the stories had been recounted about my grandmother's trip across the plains. I could see her as a young girl of seventeen pushing a handcart all the way, with her belongings in the tin trunk. | Grandmother had been a small person, with shining brown eyes filled with love and compassion for everyone and everything. She was the idol of her large posterity. Her lovely, well-kept home was a sanctuary for all who entered it. Everything about her was famous to all of us. Her currant biscuits, flowers, and genuine hospitality were known throughout the State. The hardships she had borne rested lightly on her shoulders, as her great faith carried her on to higher achievements. We loved to hear her tell of her trip across the plains with its dangers and privations. Now the trunk was one of the last links with that great migration. However, it was useless now and could do no good to anyone, as I thought. The curb was piled high with refuse. I carried the trunk out and put it beside the other items. I was still disturbed about leaving it. The garbage truck was almost full when it arrived at our place, so the man was only able to take about half of the things. The trunk went with the first load. I felt a little easier after it left and decided to forget it and lock my storehouse of memories for the time being. A half hour after the departure of the garbage truck the phone rang. It was my aunt from out in the country, my grandmother's oldest living daughter. I had forgotten that she might be interested in the trunk. "Whatever you do," she said, "don't dispose of the old tin trunk.... You know that trunk is over one hundred years old, was purchased in London, and carried all of mother's belongings as she pushed it across the plains." My heart sank. I stammered out, almost incoherently, the details of the disposal. I assured her that I would do everything to get it back. "Tt belongs in the museum," she said. Of course it did, why hadn't I thought of that? Fortunately, the garbage man returned for the second load. I rushed out when he came back and told him of my plight. He told me that he had thought it an unusual old piece and had put it to one side and would bring it back to me. I paid him for his trouble, and the trunk was returned to repose in the museum as its rightful place of abode.
