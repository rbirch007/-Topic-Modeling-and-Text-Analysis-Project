Work ITleeting—Living More Abundantly (A Course Recommended for Use by Wards and Branches at Work Meeting) Lesson 1—Spending Money Wisely Elder William F. Edwards For Tuesday, October 8, 1957 Objective: To show that it requires skill and wisdom to spend money wisely. We are all blessed with the same amount of time, and with a sufficient amount of time to achieve great success in life. But some use their time more effectively than others and achieve a richer life. There is a parallel in the financial art of living. With minor exceptions, we are all blessed with sufficient income to live a good life, but some people achieve it more fully because of how they use what they have. In this lesson we want to see clearly the wise way of spending money. [N this busy work-a-day world, do we always remember that we are not happy unless we are confronted with a shortage of time? Can you think of anything worse than a surplus of time—a period of time each day you wish you could avoid because there is absolutely nothing you desire to do? Source of Happiness Happiness comes from making wise choices of things that you desire to do, 'The Master was con- 472 stantly mindful of this principle of life. He taught the importance of choosing to do the things of eternal value in contrast to worldly things. "Lay not up for yourselves treasures upon earth, where moth and rust doth corrupt, and where thieves break through and steal: But lay up for yourselves treasures in heaven, where neither moth nor rust doth corrupt, and where thieves do not break through nor steal " (Mt. 6:19-20). That was the challenging issue confronting the Prophet Joshua who testified, "choose you this day whom ye will serve... But as for me and my house, we will serve the Lord" (Joshua 24:15). First Things First The Chinaman, Ling Po, saw the importance of wise choices when he said, "What a fool does in the end, a wise man does in the beginning." The successful student must learn how to balance his time between study and play, and which to do first. The housewife must choose between preparing in advance for Sunday, with its blessings, or doing extra work on the Sabbath Day. Brigham Young expressed it this way for the fathers: "This is the greatest wealth we possess—to know how to direct our labors nightly, spending every hour advantageously for the benefit of our wives and children and neighbors." Success or failure is largely a matter of doing the right thing at the right time, putting first things first. Use of Precious Time Life will never be more important than it is today. "This life is worth RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—JULY 1957 as much as any life that any being can possess in time or in eternity. There is no life more precious to us in the eyes of eternal wisdom and justice than the life we now possess. Our first duty is to take care of this life' (President Brigham Young). Time will never be more precious. Oh, how important it ls for our happiness that we use _ this precious time doing those things that will bring us the greatest measure of satisfaction. Use of Precious Money All that has been said about the use of time applies equally to money. We must recognize that we will be unable to acquire all of the things we desire. 'This is a blessing. There would be few "temporal" thrills left in life if we could. Would we be equally interested in working hard at our job if, when pay day came, there was nothing we desired to do with our earnings? The challenge is to learn what we need most, what will give us the greatest satisfaction, so that the more beneficial things will be obtained and those not obtained will be the less important. Let us apply the principle to two women, each with twenty dollars, and both entering an attractive store. The first woman is favorably impressed with what is displayed just inside of the door. She forgets all of her other desires and spends her money on these items. After the money is spent, she continues throughout the store—only looking, of course. The second woman is also favorably impressed with the first displays.
