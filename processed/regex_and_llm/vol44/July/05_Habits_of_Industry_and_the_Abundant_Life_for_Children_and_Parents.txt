Are our commandments, as _ parents, such as can be bound about the hearts of our children—will they truly lead them, keep them, talk with them, and be a lamp and a light to them always? It has been said we start to train a child at the birth of his greatgrandparents. I am grateful for the training the paternal grandparents gave our children when in far-off England a great-grandfather prayed for divine direction to find the true Church. In a dream, he saw the Page 420 For the commandment is a lamp; and the law is hall in which two Mormon missionaries were preaching. He accepted their teaching, and after years of frugality brought his ten children to Utah. Struggle and privation followed them, but their loyalty and devotion to the Church never failed. Gratitude increases with thoughts of the great-grandmother who gave birth to her children in a dirt-roofed dugout, and who sold their only cow that her husband might go back to Switzerland to carty the gospel message to his native land. She then sewed salt sacks for one dollar a thousand to support her family. The maternal grandmother left her home in Christiania, Norway, when only eighteen years old to immigrate to Zion alone for the cause she had espoused. She reared a family of twelve children and never faltered in her faith and devotion to the Church. When the maternal grandfather was a boy of fourteen, he drove his widowed mother's ox team across the plains and then supported her by herding cattle on the foothills surrounding the city. Their cabin floor was covered with straw, ready to burn, if their Prophet Brigham Young gave the word when the invading HABITS OF INDUSTRY AND THE ABUNDANT LIFE 421 Johnston's Army came into the city. Memory takes me back to my childhood. 'The farm was_ large, the house had many rooms, and the family was ever increasing, with our own children, workmen, and guests. There was always work to do and meals to prepare. Milk and butter, fruit and garden vegetables, chickens and lambs were our responsibility. Wool was washed, carded, and made into warm quilts for the family and the sheep camps. I never remember of being tired of the work. We all worked together. An older sister always had a younger one to do the upstairs or the kitchen work. Mother prepared the meals with the vegetables and took care of the milk, but it was accepted by all that she needed help when we had finished our appointed tasks. What pleasant memories of the fun and jokes we enjoyed while picking over the peas on the shady lawn or singing the arias of the operas in which the girls had participated. While picking cherries, strawberries, raspberries, and currants in the cool of the morning or evening, these melodies became very dear to our hearts. About four o'clock, nearly every afternoon, all would go down to the Cottonwood Creek for a swim. We followed a path through the tall, sweet-smelling meadow grass to enjoy an hour of complete relaxation and enjoyment. OTHER'S admonitions have carried through to my children when she said, "Let your head save your heels," and, "It is your tesponsibility to pay your tithing, but someone else's responsibility to spend it." It has always seemed such a shame to take thirty minutes to do the dishes when they could be done in ten. 'There is real satisfaction in two or three hustling with plenty of clean towels, cloths, soapy water and rinse water so the dishes can drain shiny bright or be wiped and put straight and clean on the cupboard shelves. Every child can do a good job if, as adults, we realize sustained effort cannot last too long. It is better to give them a boost and let them enjoy the satisfaction of a finished job. Kathy's assignment was to clean the bathtub, which was accomplished with all the scouring and rinsing necessary for a_ six-year-old, although her school dress suffered from cleanser. Michele was to dust. Yes, the lamp shade was taken completely off and rubbed inside and out before it was precariously replaced. Grandfather said to Jamie from across the table, "If you will stay on the farm with us, you can have your pony and Queen, the collie, and we will have good times together." Jamie looked up with his big brown eyes and said seriously, "I would like to, Grandfather, but my mother needs me." Yes, he was needed—he had a job to do —a definite responsibility—his mother "needed" him! When we are needed, how much more can we do —how many difficulties can be overcome, for with need, there is appreciation, which is the greatest reward for work! We have all been interested in ten-year-old Robert Strom and Charles Van Doren, two T''V celeb- 422 tities, who have won great sums of money recently. I have wondered what their home, family, and _parents contributed toward their amazing mental powers. Mr. Strom, Robert's father, said: We feel there are a great number of gifted children all over the country who are lost because their very young curiosity is not satisfied... They become frustrated, get lethargic habits and their interest is atrophied. Mrs. Strom said: I have not worked since the children were born. I am staying home and bringing them up myself. We never have a question unanswered, an idea undiscussed. We always had a policy that if something wasn't finished we couldn't start something else. My favorite motto is "Not failure, but low aim is crime." As Jong as they tried their best, they knew no one was going to reject them. Once something excites their interest they will ask many questions, run the thing down to the bottom. If we can't answer, we always point out how they can find it. "Talk to me" is a favorite phrase around our house. "All right," I say, "what will we talk about?" Then we discuss the project of the day or some scientific idea. Robert said: I think I have been curious since I was born; so often kids don't get a chance to learn enough to get their curiosity satisfied.
