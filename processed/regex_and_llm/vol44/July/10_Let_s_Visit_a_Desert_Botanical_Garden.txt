Lets Visit a Desert Botanical Garden Marijane Morris ountain-born, island born, or from the desert country, you will find yourself completely fascinated when you begin the nature walk in the Desert Botanical Garden of Arizona at 'Tempe. This garden (on Highway 89) is not marked with road advertising. There are only a simple sign and stone entrance posts proclaiming what it is, and marking the way into an enchanted land of thousands of desert plants in a single setting. In this world plants grow erect, climb, creep, or are just small growths in the ground. 'Their very grotesqueness will rouse your admiration and respect. The Desert Botanical Garden is operated by the Arizona Cactus and Native Flora Society, a non-profit, educational organization supported, in part, by an endowment from the late Gertrude Divine Webster, but, principally, by membership and donations. It costs the visitor nothing to see these plants in a setting produced by many hours of planning, vision, love, and dedication. All that could be required of anyone is a camera permit and that the tourist stay on the black-topped walks and away from the cacti. (There are seeds that might be killed and plants too small to be seen.) For a small amount, the visitor may purchase a bulletin giving detailed information about each species. 'The different kinds of cacti and other desert plants are numbered to correspond with the numbers in the pamphlet. You may be surprised to find that cacti are found almost exclusively Page 430 in the New World. There are something like one hundred genera and 1,800 species, wild and cultivated. Here in the Desert Botanical Garden, there are 8,000 to 9,000 species of desert plants, of which about 1,300 are cacti. Once we let a cactus or any desert plant "stick us," we are forever awed by this vegetation that survives in an area of little rainfall, a high percentage of sunshine, and high temperatures. Only the most heroic of plants can prosper in a desert. The plants now on our deserts seem even more remarkable when we learn that they are relics of a heavy plant population that once occupied the area when rainfall was greater. 'These survivors gradually built up resistance to drought conditions as the area became increasingly dry. Many of us know by sight the stately Saguaro (so-wha'ro). This cactus is Arizona's state flower and the best example of the desert's living reservoir. The pronounced ribs of this plant expand and contract in accordion fashion to store and use water. 'The one in the Botanical Garden is approximately 180 years old. The Saguaro produces a waxy, showy flower, followed by red, juicy fruits of delicious flavor, and, if you have ever wondered at holes found in their stems, they are produced by the Gila woodpecker. 'This bird finds the Saguaro a wonderful place to nest. 'The Saguaro is a sensitive but stubborn plant. Whenever it is disturbed either by wind DESERT BOTANICAL GARDEN es: W. We continue through the Desert Botanical Garden, we find at stake number six, a cactus called the "Old Man of the Desert." Here is truly an oddity. 'This plant is young and is expected to attain a height of thirty feet or more. Before you finish your walk, you may have a favorite cactus picked out. Maybe it will be the creeping plant called the caterpillar cactus. This one is found only on the Magdalena Plains of Baja, Callifornia, outside of cultivated gardens. One wonders where its roots are, as its branches crisscross over each other. If we consult our pamphlet, we read that its roots form the portion of the stem against the ground and that it grows forward at the rate of about five inches a year, dying off at the back at the same rate. In its native soil it forms dense colonies. It is correctly named; it looks like a caterpillar on the crawl. Quite possibly, we may look at the next plant and exclaim: "A Saguaro" But it isn't. It resembles its relative, the Saguaro, and meets the Arizona cactus just south of the border, but from there on it belongs to Baja, California, and Sonora, Mexico. It lacks the pronounced ribs of most cacti and has a bumpy surface much like an Indian totem pole. As this garden contains not only cacti, but desert plants of all kinds, you will discover that the century plant and the Joshua tree, and many other plants in this garden are not true cacti. When it rains these stems leaf out with small groups of leaves at the base of each spine. It is the aluminum lath house we reach about half way through our walk. 'There is enough lath in this desert shelter to reach to the heart of Phoenix, westward some eight miles. (The soil consists of silt, sharp sand, and fertilizer. ) In this lath house are some of the garden's most unusual plants.
