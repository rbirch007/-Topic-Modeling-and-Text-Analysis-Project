Creation of Earth and Man The Book of Moses records the visions and the writings of Moses as revealed to the Prophet Joseph Smith. A Successful Trip Many saints living in all parts of North America have thrilled with a trip to Palmyra, New York, to witness The Book of Mormon pageant at the Hill Cumorah. In this we can find a practical application of these fundamentals by imagining the experiences of two families. The first family did things the right way. Nearly a year in advance they decided to make the trip. This gave them time to prepare for the trip. They soon found that going by one route and returning another they could visit along the way almost all of the important spots in Church history. 'This stimulated a review of Church history to identify the exact location of the spots and to become more fully acquainted with the history. Now people in history became more important, and this family became better acquainted with the lives of early Church leaders. 'This also provided time to prepare financially for the trip. N contrast, let us imagine another family doing the same thing, but doing it the wrong way. This family made a sudden decision to take the trip. It was necessary to borrow money from the bank. Both families spent the same amount of time taking the trip and the same amount of money. Savings Should Precede Spending Savings should precede spending, with only justifiable exceptions. The First Presidency would like to urge every member of the Church to follow the example set by the Church and to live within his income. Anyone who lives beyond his income is inviting disaster. Borrowed money is not income. Borrowing on capital account, within your reasonable capacity to pay, may be sound, depending upon circumstances. But borrowing to live on is unsound, whether it be an outright loan or installment buying. We urge the members to be frugal, thrifty, industrious, temperate, saving, and to live righteously (President J. Reuben Clark, Jr.). Some will ask: How could we ever obtain many things like an automobile, a refrigerator, or a television set, if we could not buy on credit, because it seems impossible to save first? 'There is only one correct answer. 'The more pressing your financial position, the greater the need to save before buying. Only the well-to-do can afford to buy on credit, and they are the ones most likely to avoid it. It is better to live simply, plainly, be sparing in every indulgence, even if it is harmless, than to replenish your purse for luxuries by going into debt (Joseph F. Smith), 696 Buying on credit usually means paying the equivalent of a premium price; it is the expensive way and, in the long run, the hard way of acquiring things. There are justifiable occasions for encouraging debts, such as buying a home, and this will be discussed fully in a subsequent lesson. But, generally, it is unwise to buy and pay later. Never be argued out of your soul; never be argued out of your honor; and never be argued into believing that soul and honor do not run a terrible risk if you limp into life with the load of debt on your shoulders (Bulwer Lytton). Most people have a greater opportunity to increase their economic satisfactions by doing those things that increase the value of money than by increasing the supply of money available for spending. AMLET is not only Shakepeare's most popular play, but it is generally conceded to be his most provocative one, as witnessed by its thousands of performances in the theaters of all nations. As early as 1702, George Farquhar called it "Long the Darling of the English Audience, and like to continue with the same applause, in Defiance of all criticism." It has been estimated that more books have been written about the character of Hamlet than any other name in fiction, and more than any "real" person, save Christ, Napoleon, and Lincoln. Jn recent decades the number of books "explaining" him continues to increase. Thus, apparently, we moderns continue to find new vitality in the play each time it is read. And even though no one completely understands the frustrations which make up Hamlet's character, he seems to tell us more about ourselves from over the centuries than do writers of our own day, so constant is his popularity. Probably the reason Hamlet is so provocative is because, without any close rivals, his is the most complex character in Shakespeare. So inexhaustible are the depths of conflicts within him, so vastly does he range from the highest pinnacles of hope to the darkest caverns of self-contempt and despair that each of us, regardless of our identity at this present moment, finds some large phase of himself mirrored in Hamlet. So complex is he that, as A. C. Bradley points out, he is the only character among Shakespeare's myriad creations who could have written Shakespeare's plays. A brilliant example of the various responses which he evokes is told by Frederic Harrison, an Englishman with a lifelong devotion to Hamlet, who recalls his disappointment at the French production of Hamlet he saw in Paris. "Why, that isn't Hamlet at all!" he complained to a Frenchman seated near him, who, in turn, replied, "My friend, you forget that Hamlet is not only an Englishman. There is a French Hamlet, a German, an Italian, a Russian Hamlet, each different in personal and national idiosyncrasy, but all profoundly true to Shakespeare's ideal of the inscrutable spirit of the ill-starred Prince of Denmark." This varying concept of Hamlet among various cultures is not to be questioned, so logical is Page 697 698 it. How much more true must be the variation within each culture; Hamlet, therefore, must appear as variously to each of us as we are different from each other. And an awareness of this truth is not to belittle him nor destroy his power, but only to acclaim the unconscious genius Shakespeare employed to incarnate most nearly his inexhaustible self within Hamlet, the largest single creation of his pen. It shall be the purpose of the second lesson on Hamlet to discuss him as the central, integrating symbolforce of the play, about whom all else hinges; indeed, in no other of Shakespeare's plays does one character so completely dominate. As preparation for such an approach, our present lesson attempts to point out how thoroughly Shakespeare prepared a magnificent setting or frame around the emerging character of Hamlet; how lesser characters within the play, even though they receive his contempt, his envy, or even his rapier, best serve to mirror the various conflicts within Hamlet himself. At midnight on the battlements of the castle, the ghost of Hamlet's father appears to him and his best friend Horatio, and tells Prince RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 Hamlet that Claudius has murdered him after first winning Gertrude's affections. Next the ghost orders him to be revenged on his uncle, although he should spare his mother. In hesitation at performing this command, Hamlet pretends to be mad, and repulses Ophelia whom he formerly loved. He arranges for a play to be presented to the court which represents the crimes of his uncle who, seeing the play for what it is, betrays his evident guilt to Hamlet's satisfaction. King Claudius flees from the play to kneel in private prayer. Here Hamlet discovers him, yet cannot bring himself to kill him while he is in such holy pursuits. In a furious interview with his mother in her bedroom, Hamlet, it seems to her, is about to kill her, but, instead, he accidentally kills Polonius, who had been planted by Claudius behind the draperies to eavesdrop. Hub and Wheel Hamlet is such a tremendous experience because Shakespeare has been so successful in making Hamlet's confusions, contradictions, his hopes, and despairs seem entirely real. As the hub of the play's structure, Prince Hamlet receives along the spokes of the wheel the support or contrast of the minor characters, whose functions might be likened to the rim. As the influence of their actions and philosophies flows from 700 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 each of them inward to Hamlet, at first it might seem that their weaknesses are allowed to surround or frame him in that the power of his character and the rightness of his cause and method may serve as contrast. But, upon closer examination, it becomes evident that the indecision and corruption flowing in to Hamlet often receive his contempt, his satire, or his vehement condemnation, not merely because he recognizes them as evils within themselves, but because, consciously or unconsciously, they are dominant within his own heart, thus making even more chaotic his own inward conflicts and bewilderment. Knowing the place of their marriage in the eternal scheme of things, husband and wife move forward with purpose and tresourcefulness, weeding out nonessentials. But seek ye first the kingdom of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be added unto you (Mt. 6:33). 706 With spiritual goals in mind, parents are inclined to put first things first and to make decisions which help the family and its members move in the direction of perfection. Parents who see the possibilities of the future, reflect sound planning into everyday living. 2. Love at home: If the spirit of temple marriage has really been captured, kindness and consideration will predominate in all family relationships. 'Tolerance and understanding will beget patience and the feelings of others will be protected at all times. If parents respect the rights of each other their children will do likewise. Latter-day Saints consider rearing a family to be their most sacred responsibility. Children are regarded as a blessing and, when so regarded and reared under the influence of the Church, the real spirit of the home is born. This spirit, often called love, is the spirit that shrinks the problems of life to the small size. (See Lesson 13, "Love —A Basic Ingredient," for a further treatment of this subject. ) 3. Relationship to Priesthood: It is in the temple where the wife's relationship to her husband's Priesthood is most vividly taught, and if a Latter-day Saint home is to operate successfully, this should be seen in proper perspective at the outset. (See Lesson 4, "Women and the Priesthood." ) - 4. Family prayer, which is inspired and begun on the wedding day and continued thereafter, will invite the Lord's guidance to bless the marriage and family life of the home. This observance can do more than any other single thing to influence children for good and give them the RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 security of "family backing" and the companionship of their Father in heaven. 5. Family Heritage: The knowledge that the family unit is linked together eternally makes one more family-conscious and helps one strive to live worthy of his place in the family circle. It makes fellow members more interested in those who have preceded and those to follow, and arouses a pride in one another's achievements. A sense of family loyalty is developed which is strengthened through the years. The Individual Is Affected Temple marriage, along with other ordinances and teachings of the Church, molds the desirable personal characteristics of family members. Such attributes as charity, tolerance, mercy, forgiveness, kindness, and the like, attach themselves to those who live the gospel. 'Temple marriage, being an important part of the gospel, encourages these qualities. (See Lesson 7, "Be Ye Therefore Perfect.") Modesty and fidelity, however, are individual characteristics especially fostered by temple marriage. Modesty, as suggested in the temple, includes modesty of dress, speech, and general conduct. Anything of an unseemly nature is shunned and avoided. Modesty of this kind develops gentleness which permeates the entire home environment. Fidelity is developed in a marriage fashioned for eternity. Husband and wife are inclined to adhere carefully to the marriage vows, giving both loyalty and devotion. All family members learn from the parental example of fidelity to be honest with one an- LESSON DEPARTMENT other, to perform obligations faithfully, and to find joy in so doing. The husband and wife who are true to their marriage covenants make a conscious effort to plan their lives so they can remain close together. Not only their religious life, but their recreational and _ intellectual interests are cultivated together, in order that they do not seek the companionship of others in_preference to their mates. They develop trust and confidence which are never questioned, because of their constant devotion to one another. Their courtship does not cease with marriage. Another aspect of temple marriage which has great significance to the individual is the protective shield of the endowment. President Joseph Fielding Smith has said: If we realize what we are doing, then the endowment will be a protection to us all our lives—a protection which a man who does not go to the Temple does not have.... This protection is what these ceremonies are for, in part. They save us now, and exalt us hereafter, if we will honor them (Doctrines of Salvation, Joseph Fielding Smith, Vol. II, 252-253). Outside the Home Temple marriage encourages Church service. It is important that Latter-day Saint couples regard this in a harmonious way, because it involves the devotion of time, talents, energies, and means to the work of the Lord. 'This requires active membership in the Church for parents and children and will lead to individual growth and development as well as widening interests and friendships. Latter-day Saints who fully understand the gospel feel a responsibility to the community in 707 its worthy efforts. Wholesome associations outside the home are essential to a healthy marriage, as long as they do not become overbalanced. "Men are that they might have joy' —and real joy comes from a well-balanced life. Importance of Returning to the Temple Couples who have been married in the temple receive added joy and understanding each time they return to the temple. The full significance of the temple ceremony is not absorbed from one attendance at a temple session. Included in the benefits ensuing from returning to the temple on occasion after the matriage are: 1. Added knowledge concerning, and clarification of, the finer points of the endowment are received. 2. An opportunity is provided to do something essential for someone else— which develops a spirit of unselfishness and service within the home. 3. It is a spiritual uplift to review covenants made in the temple, and thus we are helped to live close to the Lord. 4. As individuals mature, the temple ceremony becomes more significant as it is viewed in the light of added experience and greater understanding. 5. Parents who are conversant with temple procedures are prepared to assist their children and accompany friends who are visiting the temple for the first time. 6. The romance and beauty surrounding the courtship and marriage are not forgotten. The sweetheart relationship must be maintained throughout a marriage to make it successful. It is an emotional necessity. 7. Emotional comfort and _ intellectual refreshment come from quietness and meditation.
