The Place of Section Two in The Doctrine and Covenants ECTION 2 is the first in chronological sequence of the revelations in The Doctrine and Covenants. It is appropriate that one of the messages of this volume of scripture should be brought to the attention of the student early in this study—the message that The Doctrine and Covenants attests to the reality of the future life by the revelations which tell of the return to the earth of resurrected beings. Section 2 was placed in The Doctrine and Covenants for the first time in the 1876 edition. It is also found in the Prophet Joseph Smith's history (D. H. C. 1:12), and in The Pearl of Great Price, Writings of Joseph Smith 2:38-39. In addition to attesting to the fact of the resurrection, there are two more points to be remembered from the historical background or context, of this section. First, that the coming of the ancient American prophet Moroni to Joseph Smith on September 21, 1823, was the beginning of the ministry of angels in this dispensation. Second, that the misconceptions of men relative to angels were made known early to Joseph Smith. The great importance of the reality of the resurrection will be discussed later during this course. Ministry of Angels The second fact—the ministry of angels—opens up a number of ideas which are important also. Although men claimed that revelation from God had ceased, there was no scriptural justification for such belief. (See Lesson One.) When people of faith have lived upon the earth, the Lord has revealed his will to them in various ways, such as by Page 687 688 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 the ministry of angels. When people believe that revelation is no longer received nor necessary, such communication from the heavens is withheld. (See Moroni 7:22-20, 37.) The coming of the angel Moroni was prophesied for this dispensation. (See Revelation 14:6-7.) Although the scriptures teach that angels are fellow-beings and fellowservants of us mortals and should be thought of as those who have lived on earth and have passed on to serve as heavenly messengers, men have conceived them to be vastly different from us, for one thing, having wings as a means of locomotion. In describing the heavenly being Moroni, Joseph Smith made known the following truths in answer to prayer for further enlightenment from God. While I was thus in the act of calling upon God, I discovered a light appearing in my room, which continued to increase until the room was lighter than at noonday, when immediately a personage appeared at my bedside, standing in the air, for his feet did not touch the floor. He had on a loose robe of most exquisite whiteness. It was a whiteness beyond anything earthly I had ever seen nor do I believe that any earthly thing could be made to appear so exceedingly white and brilliant. His hands were naked and his arms also, a little above the wrist, so, also were his feet naked, as were his legs, a little above the ankles. His head and neck were also bare.... Not only was his robe exceedingly white, but his whole person was glorious beyond description, and his countenance truly like lightning. HE family can be established for eternity only in a temple of God. All true Latter-day Saints have as a goal the blessing of exaltation. Temple marriage is a condition precedent to exaltation in its fulness. Exaltation is not the only blessing of temple marriage, however. Certain blessings are available now to those who embrace temple marriage and live for its benefits. 'They include a proper preparation for marriage, aid in selecting the marriage companion, and adornment of the personality with basic virtues such as moral cleanliness, modesty, fidelity, and spirituality. To this must be added personal protection. When a family is united by temple marriage, happiness is not an accident. It flows from a set of attitudes and practices put into motion by the marriage. They include: family heritage, mutual consideration, family prayer, Church service, spirituality in the home, family organization, proper relationship to the Priesthood, and growth, both spiritual and intellectual. The blessing of exaltation, as well as the many blessings along the way, are available only to those who live for them. The extent to which the individual complies with God's will determines worthiness for blessings. There is a law, irrevocably decreed in heaven before the foundations of this world, upon which all belssings are predicated— And when we obtain any blessing from God, it is by obedience to that law upon which it is predicated (D. & C. 130: 20-21). 704 The Temple—W hat It Is The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints is unique in many ways, but, probably, the characteristic which distinguishes it most from other Christian denominations is its temples and the significant ordinances therein performed. Only in Latter-day Saint temples can ordinances be performed having eternal consequences. Most people consider any elaborate church edifice for public worship to be a temple, but to Latter-day Saints a temple is more than a house of worship. Temples... Are sanctuaries specially dedicated for sacred rites and ceremonies pertaining to exaltation in the celestial kingdom of God (Doctrines of Salvation, Joseph Fielding Smith, Vol. II, page 231). The Marriage Covenant Let us now consider one of the important ordinances of the temple which has profound effect upon the establishment of the family and its role in eternity—the marriage covenant. God instituted marriage in the beginning. This institution of marriage is not a man-made institution. It is of God.... God not only commends but he commands marriage (Gospel Doctrine, Joseph F. Smith, pages 272, 274). The Lord performed the first marriage when he joined Adam and Eve. This was obviously intended to be for eternity, since at that time death had not come into the world. The President of the Church, and only he, holds the keys of the binding and sealing power for eternal marriage. (See D. & C. 132:7.) Marriage for eternity must, therefore, be performed by the President or one properly acting under his RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 authority. In addition to this, the ceremony must be performed in a temple. A civil marriage stands wanting and empty compared to the promises made to those who pledge for time and eternity. (See D. & C. 132:14-17.) <A careful analysis of the promises and blessings expected from these two contrasting marriage ceremonies should be sufficient to persuade all to be married in the temple for time and for eternity. Blessings which attend temple marriage are of an eternal nature. Some of them are available now and here, and they have tremendous influence upon the success and happiness of each member of the Latter-day Saint home. These blessings affect the personality of the individual and assist in the preparation for marriage. 'The temple marriage has a refining influence upon the atmosphere of the home, and it prepares family members for Church and community service. Benefits Before the Marriage Marriage Preparation: 'The preparation for marriage, as for life generally, starts very early in life. By the time the marriage ceremony ls performed, the way of life of the marriage participants has largely been determined. 'Those who have caught the spirit and vision of temple marriage are influenced to live worthy of receiving a recommend for such a marriage. High standards are maintained, uncompromised, and virtue is guarded as a priceless treasure. Those planning a temple marriage realize: There is more to marriage than music and moonlight; there is trust... And LESSON DEPARTMENT sharing... And being loved and deserving to be. There is companionship through years of growing up and growing old together (Excerpts from "Virtue Is Its Own Reward," The Relief Society Magazine, June 1956, page 377). If we are honest with ourselves in marriage preparation, the temple ceremony spells no drastic change in our mode of living, but rather a delightful continuation of the good life already begun. Conformity to the temple vows follows as a natural, comfortable sequel to what has gone before. Choosing a Companion: One of the major causes of strife between married persons is the difference in religious affiliation and background. Those who live for a temple marriage and are determined to settle for nothing less, will go far to avoid this cause of dissension. Chances of marrying within the Church are good for the person who accepts temple marriage as an ideal. He chooses his friends intelligently and discriminately, knowing that from them he will eventually select a mate whom he can take into the temple. Dating is planned with great care. Somehow he doesn't feel quite comfortable with those having standards unlike his own. He realizes that a partner throughout eternity must have ideals, likes, dislikes, and spiritual values which go hand in hand with his own. The importance of similar religious affiliations cannot be overemphasized in the building of a happy home, but it is also important that the marrying parties view in the same light the Church's pattern of living and have the same desire to conform to it. Having marriage permanence in mind at the beginning 705 makes for wise judgment throughout the courting and engagement periods. The Atmosphere of the Home The greatest blessings in this life from temple marriage undoubtedly are those which affect the atmosphere of the home. Basically, the effect of temple marriage upon the family, in this life, is on the feelings and fellowship of family members. Here are some of the ways temple marriage affects the home. 1. Spirituality the foundation of the home: Couples joined in the temple ceremony attach a deep meaning to their life together, because they understand the real purpose of life and have definite, spiritual goals towards which they strive. President Joseph F. Smith once said: "An ideal home is one in which all worldly considerations are secondary' (Gospel Doctrine, page 302). Certainly the temple is the best place to establish this concept in the minds of future parents. The temple ceremony starts the marriage on a high spiritual plane and suggests the formula for keeping it there. It sets into operation the greatest partnership which can be formed. It introduces a spiritual refinement which, if nurtured and cultivated, can help make a home a heaven on earth. It will develop and sharpen the appetite for the best life has to offer. Knowing the place of their marriage in the eternal scheme of things, husband and wife move forward with purpose and tresourcefulness, weeding out nonessentials. But seek ye first the kingdom of God, and his righteousness; and all these things shall be added unto you (Mt. 6:33). 706 With spiritual goals in mind, parents are inclined to put first things first and to make decisions which help the family and its members move in the direction of perfection. Parents who see the possibilities of the future, reflect sound planning into everyday living. 2. Love at home: If the spirit of temple marriage has really been captured, kindness and consideration will predominate in all family relationships. 'Tolerance and understanding will beget patience and the feelings of others will be protected at all times. If parents respect the rights of each other their children will do likewise. Latter-day Saints consider rearing a family to be their most sacred responsibility. Children are regarded as a blessing and, when so regarded and reared under the influence of the Church, the real spirit of the home is born. This spirit, often called love, is the spirit that shrinks the problems of life to the small size. (See Lesson 13, "Love —A Basic Ingredient," for a further treatment of this subject. ) 3. Relationship to Priesthood: It is in the temple where the wife's relationship to her husband's Priesthood is most vividly taught, and if a Latter-day Saint home is to operate successfully, this should be seen in proper perspective at the outset. (See Lesson 4, "Women and the Priesthood." ) - 4. Family prayer, which is inspired and begun on the wedding day and continued thereafter, will invite the Lord's guidance to bless the marriage and family life of the home. This observance can do more than any other single thing to influence children for good and give them the RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1957 security of "family backing" and the companionship of their Father in heaven. 5. Family Heritage: The knowledge that the family unit is linked together eternally makes one more family-conscious and helps one strive to live worthy of his place in the family circle. It makes fellow members more interested in those who have preceded and those to follow, and arouses a pride in one another's achievements. A sense of family loyalty is developed which is strengthened through the years. The Individual Is Affected Temple marriage, along with other ordinances and teachings of the Church, molds the desirable personal characteristics of family members. Such attributes as charity, tolerance, mercy, forgiveness, kindness, and the like, attach themselves to those who live the gospel. 'Temple marriage, being an important part of the gospel, encourages these qualities. (See Lesson 7, "Be Ye Therefore Perfect.") Modesty and fidelity, however, are individual characteristics especially fostered by temple marriage. Modesty, as suggested in the temple, includes modesty of dress, speech, and general conduct. Anything of an unseemly nature is shunned and avoided. Modesty of this kind develops gentleness which permeates the entire home environment. Fidelity is developed in a marriage fashioned for eternity. Husband and wife are inclined to adhere carefully to the marriage vows, giving both loyalty and devotion. All family members learn from the parental example of fidelity to be honest with one an- LESSON DEPARTMENT other, to perform obligations faithfully, and to find joy in so doing. The husband and wife who are true to their marriage covenants make a conscious effort to plan their lives so they can remain close together. Not only their religious life, but their recreational and _ intellectual interests are cultivated together, in order that they do not seek the companionship of others in_preference to their mates. They develop trust and confidence which are never questioned, because of their constant devotion to one another. Their courtship does not cease with marriage. Another aspect of temple marriage which has great significance to the individual is the protective shield of the endowment. President Joseph Fielding Smith has said: If we realize what we are doing, then the endowment will be a protection to us all our lives—a protection which a man who does not go to the Temple does not have.... This protection is what these ceremonies are for, in part. They save us now, and exalt us hereafter, if we will honor them (Doctrines of Salvation, Joseph Fielding Smith, Vol. II, 252-253). Outside the Home Temple marriage encourages Church service. It is important that Latter-day Saint couples regard this in a harmonious way, because it involves the devotion of time, talents, energies, and means to the work of the Lord. 'This requires active membership in the Church for parents and children and will lead to individual growth and development as well as widening interests and friendships. Latter-day Saints who fully understand the gospel feel a responsibility to the community in 707 its worthy efforts. Wholesome associations outside the home are essential to a healthy marriage, as long as they do not become overbalanced. "Men are that they might have joy' —and real joy comes from a well-balanced life. Importance of Returning to the Temple Couples who have been married in the temple receive added joy and understanding each time they return to the temple. The full significance of the temple ceremony is not absorbed from one attendance at a temple session. Included in the benefits ensuing from returning to the temple on occasion after the matriage are: 1. Added knowledge concerning, and clarification of, the finer points of the endowment are received. 2. An opportunity is provided to do something essential for someone else— which develops a spirit of unselfishness and service within the home. 3. It is a spiritual uplift to review covenants made in the temple, and thus we are helped to live close to the Lord. 4. As individuals mature, the temple ceremony becomes more significant as it is viewed in the light of added experience and greater understanding. 5. Parents who are conversant with temple procedures are prepared to assist their children and accompany friends who are visiting the temple for the first time. 6. The romance and beauty surrounding the courtship and marriage are not forgotten. The sweetheart relationship must be maintained throughout a marriage to make it successful. It is an emotional necessity. 7. Emotional comfort and _ intellectual refreshment come from quietness and meditation.
