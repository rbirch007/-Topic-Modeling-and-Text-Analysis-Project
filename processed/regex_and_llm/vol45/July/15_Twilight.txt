This road can be reached from Portland, Oregon, by way of the scenic Columbia River route to Astoria and the sea, where there are delightful beaches, thence by ferry across the outlet of the Columbia River, and by highway again to Hoquiam and Aberdeen, then up the coast and inland again. The fifty miles of ocean strip is one of the most primitive remaining in the country, majestic and serene, and filled with rare wild life. Watch for the sign on the east side of the road which reads "Rain Forest." 'Take the nineteenmile, good graveled road, along the Hoh River, to the ranger station, where the Rain Forest one-mile Nature T'rail begins and ends. Plan to reach this trail before noon, or as near as possible to the middle of the day, in order to view the forest depths in the most revealing light, and have time enough to complete the trip without haste. When you return from the Rain Forest Trail to Highway 101, you can go back as you came, or go on around the peninsula of the Olympic National Park, a loop of about two hundred miles or more, with Port Angeles, on the Strait of Juan de Fuca, at the top. This port has a ferry to famous Victoria in Canada. Another scenic approach road follows United States Highway 410 from Lewiston, Idaho, to Walla Walla, Washington, through Pasco, Richland, and Yakima to Mount Rainier and on to Puyallup and Olympia and the excellent highway that follows the east border of the Olympic National Park along the Hood Canal. The Olympic National Forest is open the year around, but August and September are perhaps the most desirable months to visit this preserve where the virgin rain forests of the Northwest Coast are protected and preserved for generations of those who have and will love the wilderness of woods. On the way to the forest we enjoyed miles of evergreens, including many lacy red cedars. Sometimes we followed the sea, where the setting sun haloed the waves' fine spume with gold. We passed through little towns and ate their fresh wild huckleberry pie, so rare and so delicious. FoROM the ranger station in the Hoh River Rain Forest, on a clear bright morning in September, we walked a mile-long loop-trail through the most luxuriant forest in any temperate climate. Rain forests are usually found only in the rainy portions of the tropics. But the 130 to 150 inches of rainfall here, is more than in most tropical forests. The rain and the mild climate have formed for us this heartstirring wonder.
