From the thirteenth century on, man had been slowly liberated from superstition, ignorance, and _ provincialism by many inventions which helped him rediscover his world: the compass, modern mapping technique, gunpowder, the printing-press. Copernicus and Galileo in astronomy, Bacon in science, Newton in physics and mathematics, and others enlightened man's view of himself and of his world as never before. In literature and the arts, the Dutch and Italian humanists employed the senses to give man's human qualities new emphasis. Politically, the "Divine Right of Kings' was challenged, Feudalism was being replaced by the mise of cities created by industry and modern capitalism. But dynamic as changes were, without a successful challenging of medieval Catholicism's domination over the souls of men, the Renaissance would not have amounted to much. However, with the help of the Reformation, it created modern man, a new individual who ideally defined LESSON DEPARTMENT a new political and economic relationship between himself and his fellows, who delighted in being driven by his newly acquired scientific curiosity, and who achieved a new concept of what the rights of the individual should be in his private worship. The Reformation While as early as the fifth century groups were organized within the Catholic Church to purify it of false practices, the Englishman John Wycliffe (1320 - 1384) took the first dissenting actions which predicted the later Reformation. After first endangering himself by translating the Bible, he influenced many traveling preachers to join him in preaching against the Pope and the corruptions of the clergy, and in proclaiming the right of each soul to judge religious truths for himself. Wycliffe and the followers of his Lollard Movement also believed that all who believed in Christ had a right to the priesthood. After his martyrs death, the Lollard Movement continued to grow in England, as did a similar movement in Bohemia under John Huss _ (1369- 1415). By the time of Elizabeth, these reform movements within the Christian church were large enough to be reckoned with. The main Reformation began with Martin Luther (1483-1546), the son of aGerman miner. During his early lifetime he had witnessed three peasant revolts in Germany, all repressed with great cruelty, while the sale of indulgences within the Catholic church seemed to him most corrupt. In 1517 he defied Rome by nailing his famous ninetyfive theses on the door of Wittenberg Church. While his reformed 553 church was a "high" church, which maintained many of the ritualistic resemblances to Catholicism, he preached that every Christian who had truly repented of his sins could be forgiven without a bought pardon or the need of any intercessor between himself and God. Luther preached that "neither Pope nor Bishop nor any other man has a right to impose a single syllable of Law upon a Christian man without his consent." As Calvin did later, he believed that good works were not to be confined to giving alms, fasting and prayer, but that a good day's work done by an honest tradesman was an important means for coming nearer to God. He granted to the Bible an almost completely new authority, coinciding roughly with the invention of printing by his fellow German, Gutenberg. John Calvin (1509-1564 ) College-trained in the humanistic traditions, young Swiss Calvin's genius lay in organizing Protestant churches under the leadership of lay Elders, or Presbyters. Aside from his new emphasis on the authority of the Bible, his theology was essentially medieval. For example, his concept of predestination was central to the teachings of St. Augustine (354-430), and was part of the belief of the Anglican Church long before the Puritans adopted it. Calvinism is entirely a Godcentered religion; unaided man cannot understand either his own depraved nature or that of God. His best guide, however, toward finding God's way is the Bible, as interpreted by learned theologians. Hence from its beginning, Calvinism stressed the need for leaders and members to be educated, since 554 reason and the intellect were tools indispensable for learning God's superiority and will. Calvin's beliefs are to be found in his famous Five Points. (See text, page 6.) In the Calvinist world God is allpowerful, everywhere present, and the direct cause of everything that happens. Said the Calvinists, let us then search the Bible and our own souls to learn his will, and stand in awe of his justice and mercy. More than any other, these Calvinistic concepts of God and man guided the destinies of the Puritan fathers in their New Jerusalem established in the New England wilderness. The English Reformation In 1534, seventeen years after Luther nailed up his theses, Henry VIII threw off all allegiance to Roman Catholicism, seized the lmmense wealth of all English monasteries and church-owned lands, and established himself as the head of the English Catholic Church, more familiarly known as the Anglican Church, the Church of England, or the Episcopal Church, since it was tuled by the episcopacy or system of bishops. Since Henry VIII then became head of both political and ecclesiastical government, from 1534 to the Bloodless Revolution of 1688, anyone who opposed the King either on political or religious grounds was guilty of treason and in danger of losing his head. In Scotland, where the feudal pattern was strong and where the church owned more than half of all property, John Knox (1505- 1572) was successfully establishing Calvinistic Presbyterianism as the dominant faith, an influence which also spread to England. But when RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—AUGUST 1958 Henry's daughter, "Bloody Mary," became Queen in 1553, she married a Catholic, Philip II of Spain, and succeeded in forcing Parliament to restore Roman Catholicism as the "official" religion. Many Protestant leaders she burned; others escaped to the Continent. But legions of loyal Englishmen who stayed at - home felt their religious consciences to have been violated, and, during the reign of Elizabeth, Mary's successor, this movement to purify the "official" English religion of Catholic impurities, grew to such proportions that the hated, scorned word "Puritan" first appeared. English Puritanism According to Ralph Barton Perry, distinguished scholar of Puritanism, the historical movement known as Puritanism can be divided thus: 1. Reformers in the Anglican church, 1559-1662. 2. Settlers of New England, 1620-1630. 3. Makers and victors of the Puritan Revolution in England, 1642-1660. 4. New England theocracy, 1650-1690. 5. Jonathan Edwards and the Great Awakening, 1730-1750 For the moment we are concerned with the first and third divisions. Under Elizabeth, all church and municipal officials, and all candidates for a university degree, were forced to take an oath to support her as supreme head of state and church. But an alarming number of "Puritans" or non-conformists regarded the Church of England as the dismal result of a half-finished reformation. 'They would purify it of all man-made evils and usurpations which it had accumulated since the days of pure, primitive Christianity. Distrusting man's appetite in whatever form it manifested itself, whether it be appetite for the flesh, wealth, power, social sophistication, religious pageantry and display, they formed their own congregations within the Anglican Church, thus purifying it; the main body of English Puritans did not separate themselves either from state or church. Puritans remaining in Old England dreamed that the Revolution would bring about the new Zion, that throughout New England their purified form of worship alone would be practiced. But scarcely had Charles I and the Archbishop of Canterbury been beheaded in 1649, when it became increasingly apparent that the middle-class Roundhead Puritans were united only in their opposition to a common foe; within their own ranks their idealisms contradicted each other, their enthusiasms were tragically different, and with the dismal failure of Cromwell's Commonwealth in 1659 failed the dream of establishing a Zion in mother England. Not so, however, with their New England brethren, who attempted to establish in the New World a New Zion, not to escape the England they loved, but to fulfill England's true religious destiny. When James I succeeded Queen Elizabeth in 1603, his stern, cruel Archbishop Laud helped James repent of Elizabeth's comparative laxness, and thousands of dissenters, or non-conformists, or Puritans were "harried out of the land," including more than two thousand ministers, many of whom chose to give themselves to the New England dream of establishing a Bible Commonwealth. 556 Probably never before or since has there been such a concentration of religious leaders and college-trained men as pledged their destinies to each other and to their God in desolate Massachusetts Bay. Considering themselves God's chosen people and modern-day Israel, they accepted as their sole authority the Bible. They became the people of The Book. Traditional Englishmen that they were, they forswore celebration of their best-loved holiday — Christmas — because within the Bible they found no precedent for it. In thus purifying the English motherchurch, they personified the logical, inevitable culmination of the Reformation movement. The educated, wealthy, middleclass Puritans landed (less than ten years after the arrival of the Mayflower), at Boston in 1630. They had engaged in prolonged planning, had adequate financial backing, with their own charter in their pockets. (See text, page 4.) This gave them the right to govern themselves. Their fleet of eleven well-stocked ships disgorged everything they needed: tools, ample breeding and producing livestock, staple foods, including more than 20,000 gallons of beer and wine. The Pilgrims By contrast, the tiny group of 101 persons, who landed at Plymouth in 1620, were described by the Archbishop as "cobblers, tailors, feltmakers, and such-like trash." First organized in 1607 by William Brewster, local postmaster at Scrooby, they were plain country-folk who were harried not only out of their native land but out of their heritage as well. By accident of wind and RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—AUGUST 1958 weather they touched at Cape Cod rather than Virginia, and, after exploring in vain for a more promising site, the ship's captain "dumped" them at Plymouth, since he could no longer endanger his ship and his crew while such poor baggage haggled about not being landed in Virginia, as he had contracted with them to do. 'The first winter more than half the Pilgrims died. In the spring they sowed grain upon their leveled graves to conceal their losses from the Indians. Supply ships failed to arrive; they were so poor that for the first three years of their stay they practiced a form of communal living in which everything was owned by all, and for their first nine years they could not afford a minister, but, led by William Brewster, they taught each other. (Each November our thoughts return in grateful memory to the first Thanksgiving observance in Plymouth Colony, held in gratitude for the ending of a difficult year and for a bounteous harvest.) In coming to New England, the Pilgrims forsook both their citizenship and their established English Church for they were Separatists. (See text, pp. 7-8.) They fulfilled the spirit of the Reformation in their belief that each congregation should be entirely self-governed just as each individual should be allowed to work out, in freedom, his own religious destiny. And though Putitans and Pilgrims had much in common, to the Puritans the Pilgrims were anathema, since they saw exemplified in them the horrible extremes to which the ignorant commoners can be carried by undisciplined religious enthusiasm. Furthermore, such Pilgrim separa- LESSON DEPARTMENT tion was most dangerous, for once schisms began breaking off from the mother-church, where would they end? Thus knowing the religious convictions of the Pilgrims and their distrust of all worldly forms of power, we are not surprised that their Mayflower Compact, signed in harmony by all men of the company before they went ashore at Plymouth, was one of our first great democratic documents. In this compact they did "solemnly and mutually in ye presence of God, and one another, couenant, & combine our selues togeather into a civill body politick... Unto which we promise all due submission and obedience." 'Thus, even before the Pilgrims disembarked, the isolation and opportunity which they had sought began to prove beneficial. The American mind was already being "created by the silent pressure of environment." And the leading spokesman for this momentous venture was its governor for more than thirty years, William Bradford. William Bradford, Historian Largely because of our great debt to the Puritan tradition, Americans have always possessed a strong sense of history, to be seen constantly in our monuments, historical spectacles, and, most of all, in our literature. Keeping a daily journal, until our own century a practice. Indispensable to every religious, seriousminded man of parts, was but an extension of the voluminous Puritan journals kept faithfully that one might record his inward thoughts and constantly review them, that he might more neatly come to know himself and, particularly, the will of God concerning him. It was in this 557 spirit that Governor Bradford kept his History of Plymouth Plantation, one of the noblest documents of Puritan literature. Entirely aware that he was fulfilling his intent of recording the history of his colony as he himself had witnessed the history being made, he records the daily happenings objectively and without bias, sparing neither himself nor his fellows. While his account is accurate and modest, his supreme accomplishment is to record unavoidably his own personality as written in his plain style, yet one filled with the simplified dignity which is ever the hallmark of great prose. Many of his images are homely, which but add to their power to convey his thought to the page. And that he knows The Book as well as his own heart is revealed not so much by his references to the Bible (though they abound) as by the rhythm, the diction, the pure idiom of his words. That he deserves the title of the father of American history is almost too obvious to merit comment, and disputed by none. More, he deserves the right to speak for himself, that all may judge. When the Pilgrims realized that their twelve-year stay in Holland must soon end, because many of their children were straying, Bradford had confidence in the promise of New England, despite its terrors: for it is not with us as with other men, whom small things can discourage or small discontentments cause to wish themselves at home again.... We were well weaned from the delicate milk of our mouther-countrie, and inured to the difficulties of a strange and hard land, which yet in a great part we have by patience overcome. Both his own dedication and the 558 group's high sense of purpose and destiny are revealed by his words describing their departure from Leyden: So they left that goodly and pleasant city which had been their resting place near twelve years; but they knew they were pilgrims, and looked not much on those things, but lifted up their eyes to the heavens, their dearest country, and quieted their spirits. Such lines were never penned by a troubled, ignoble spirit. Bradford was very near The Book and the God he loved, as these lines testify. As we might suspect, Bradford was above pettiness and _ trivia. When some of the less dedicated members of the company complained to him that the mosquitoes of New England bothered them, Bradford wrote: They are too delicate, and unfit to begin new plantations and colonies, that cannot endure the biting of a mosquito. We. Would wish such to keep at home till at least they be mosquito-proof. While space will not permit long excerpts from his words as contained in our text, pp. 38-44, which should be read aloud at length, we should be reminded that the great purpose of his History was to show God's presence in all things, honor and power to his name.
