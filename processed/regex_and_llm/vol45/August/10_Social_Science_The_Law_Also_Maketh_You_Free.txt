The Temple an lew toealand Wealtha S. Mendenhall Member, General Board of Relief Society Shew me thy faith without thy works, and I will shew thee my faith by my works... Faith without works is dead (James 2:18, 20). HIS scripture is uppermost in the minds of all the faithful members of this great Church in those beautiful islands that lie anchored in the faraway South Pacific. It was through their combined efforts, sacrifice, and faith that they were able to feel the companionship of the spirit of our Heavenly Father and to receive the needed blessings from heaven to complete the temple of God in New Zealand, at the scheduled time. Brother Wendell B. Mendenhall and I arrived in New Zealand about one week after the banks of the beautiful Waikato River had overflowed, due to continued heavy rains. The work on the temple and college projects had been greatly retarded. A meeting was called on the third of March, and the work yet to be completed on the temple was itemized, along with the approximate number of hours it would take to do the work. It was found that there were not enough days left to complete the buildings at the scheduled time and that it would be necessary to work around the clock. A meeting of all those working on the project was called that evening and future plans were then discussed. 'There were those with little faith who said, "It can't be done," but those faithful labor missionaries and members of the mission said, "T will shew thee my faith by my works, this is the work and house of the Lord, it will be completed!" Page 492 At that meeting, which was also a family night, all the saints living on the grounds, about 500, gathered together, all knelt in prayer and thanked our Father in heaven for his blessings unto them and prayed for assistance, renewed energy, and health. 'These people were certain their prayers would be answered, and the next morning at daylight they were at work with happy hearts, singing as they went about their duties. One morning about 2 A. M. We noticed lights near the temple on the highway and immediately went to investigate. 'There we found the cement crew mixing cement, and our labor missionaries, as well as some proselyting elders, actually running with wheelbarrows of cement to make a curb in order that the road crew could move in the next day. They were working by the light of a truck. Lights were burning in the temple. There we found walls were being washed, the baptistry was being cleaned, those beautiful oxen were actually bronze again and looked as though they were proud of the fact. Wallpaper was still being hung, lights installed, and tile was being laid. It was really a beehive of activity in the wee hours of the morning, and on and on it went until just a very few minutes before the opening of the temple to the public at 10 A. M.
