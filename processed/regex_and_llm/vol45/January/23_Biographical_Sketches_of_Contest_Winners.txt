PEAY MERCY Waters Peay, Provo, Utah, has written many lovely poems, representative of her life and her ideals. She recently celebrated her ninety-third birthday, having passed through many troubles and adversities, but she has retained a cheerful outlook on life, a youthful mind, and much wit and humor. Her motto is: "What can't be cured must be endured." In the late hours of December 1951, she lay in bed listening for sounds of the ushering in of the New Year. These lines came to her mind: Not a bell was rung, Not a song was sung, And thoughts were very few, When the old year just passed away And in came fifty-two. If we pause a moment just to think And listen to the tide, One more year has come and gone And crossed the Great Divide.... Her literary compositions, in addition to poetry, include a charming love story and many fine pieces of prose. She served in Relief Society for many years and was a beloved visiting teacher. She has always subscribed to The Relief Society Magazine. She is the mother of ten children, five of whom are still living. Her grandchildren number nine, and her greatgrandchildren eighteen. Rich O the youthful truth seeker, a wide horizon sometimes opens and startling facts spring from the most common things we see around us every day. The widely used and well-known potato, a native of the Western Hemisphere, took on a new and important meaning to Martha that bright June day when she came in for a word of help for her talk on pioneer industries. She was dressed in a circular cotton skirt, rustling in its crispness and snowy whiteness. Underneath, and just barely showing, were her ruffled crinoline petticoats. She looked very much like a full-blown double petunia in all its summer beauty. "Martha, you look very pretty in your summer finery, and you seem as happy as you look." "Thanks Gramy! Now I need your help. You always know so much about pioneer life, I wish you would tell me about some pioneer project that you really helped with and know all about." ~ "Your new outfit, with its standout skirts, gives me an idea. These permanently stiff fabrics are rather modern. Many of them have been produced since you were born, but the idea of stiff flaring ruffles dates back many years." "How did you make your ruffles stand out?" "We used starch, the thicker we made the starch the stiffer our ruffles ironed out. But each time when our clothes were laundered, the starching process had to be done over again." 'Page 42 "What is starch made of, anyway?" "There are many kinds of starch today," explained Gramy, "but when I was a child we made our starch from potatoes." "From potatoes! That we eat at the table, mashed, baked, or in French fries?" "Yes." "Well, tell me about it." "I must go back to the planting to give you the whole story. In the spring, after the frost was out of the ground, the time was ready for planting. We cut each seed potato into sections, with each piece having an eye or seed germ in it. These we planted in hills about twentyfour inches apart, and they grew into sturdy plants which blossomed with a blue-white flower somewhat resembling the snowdrop. From each piece of planted potato many roots grew out and reached down into the rich black soil and each root grew one or more potatoes. All summer the plants needed irrigation water and occasional hoeing to keep the soil loose. In October, before freezing weather came, we harvested them. It was fun to follow behind the plow, to watch the big steel blade turn over the soil and shell out the ripened potatoes, some large and some small. 'The potatoes seemed glad to get out into the sun and air, and we thought they looked at us with their many dull eyes as we gathered them into buckets and baskets. The large ones we stored in pits for table use, and the small ones we washed at the clear irrigation ditch in the back POTATO STARCH MAKING yard ready for starch making the next morning. 'Harvesting potatoes was a full day's work, and we celebrated with a roast in the evening. We built a good fire with wood chips and buried the potatoes in the hot coals and ashes. While they cooked we played 'Run Sheep, Run,' 'Steal Sticks, and "Pomp, Pomp, Pull Away,' in the light of the bonfire. When we took the potatoes from the fire, they were sometimes a little burned and always very hot tc handle, but we enjoyed eating the flavorful fluffy centers with a sprinkle of salt on them. "After a night's sleep we were up and at our next stage of our harvest —starch making." "How could you make starch from potatoes?" "The first thing we must have a grater. 'This was made by piercing nail holes in a large piece of tin, bent and tacked in a bow on a board. We carefully shredded the 43 small potatoes on this and let the bits fall into a tub of clear water. "A potato is seventy-eight per cent starch, and only about two and twotenths protein. The starch readily separated and made the water milky white. After stirring the mixture well, we covered the tubs and let the mixture stand overnight. Next morning, the pulp, washed clean of starch, floated on top the water and on the bottom of the tub we found a thick layer of damp starch. When this was dried, it crumbled into flakes or granules ready for use for the family laundry. On wash days we dissolved a few spoonfuls of the dried starch in a little cold water and poured boiling water over it and it became a creamy mass. 'This we put into the last rinse water for our cotton clothes to make them iron out smooth and stiff." "Thanks, Gramy, for a very interesting bit of pioneer lore. My teacher and the class will be glad when I tell them about it."
