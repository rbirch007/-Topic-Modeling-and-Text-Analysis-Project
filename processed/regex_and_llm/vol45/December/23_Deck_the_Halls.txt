Deck the Halls Helen Spencer Williams ToPay the beloved old carol "Deck the Halls With Boughs of Holly," takes on a new theme and a new look. Christmas decorations by Florence Williams combine all the wonderful modern ideas, materials, and colors, with the precious old traditions of the Christmas season to make this year the most unique one we have ever had. The only essentials that versatile and creative Florence insists on, are that Christmas trimmings must be gay, heartwarming, and, above all, beautiful, and that we should all know the joy of making them with our own hands. She believes that each year something new should be added, that we should not stand still and always look back, but that we should establish a few new traditions of our own each year, using the many new things and materials that are being made available to us in this wonderful modern day, seeking for anything and everything lovely and praiseworthy and of good report. Her tables are piled high with materials to make a new type of Christmas balls for tree trimming. There are Christmas tree balls of every color combination imaginable, balls that you and I can make with scraps of velvet and nbbon and with bits of gold and silver trimming, braid, old jewelry, and fancy pins and odd earrings. So now is the time to get out the old button box, scrap bag, and jewelry case. Perhaps the only outlay of money for the creating of these tree ornaments will be in the purchase of styrofoam balls of all sizes at the five Page 820 and dime, florist, or display stores, some tinsel ribbon, narrow baby ribbon, some pearl and gold beads, straight pins, and good sharp scissors. 'The florist tinsel and narrow baby ribbon can be purchased in fifty yard bolts which is the least expensive way to buy them. It might be well to begin with a large size ball, at first, until you get the knack of making one. 'The small balls are a bit more difficult to handle. First, cut your velvet in bias oneinch strips. Florence warns that these should be marked first in pencil so that they will be cut true and even, as the covering of these balls of styrofoam with the velvet is the basis of making these ornaments beautiful and professional looking. Stretch the bias strips of velvet as tightly as possible in pie-shaped sections, criss-cross, until the ball is entirely covered. Secure each section firmly with a pin at the top and bottom of the ball, but be sure to hold the material taut as you pin. After the ball is completely covered with the velvet, cover the raw edges with the narrow baby ribbon. Then the fun of decorating the balls begins. 'The colors and combinations of trimmings are yours for the choosing. On one ball Florence used bright green velvet cut from an old discarded party dress. Florence has made over a hundred of these balls, and what a glorious array of color combinations she has used in the making! One is of white velvet with gold braid and tinsel nbbon, with pearls and gold beads stuck here and there onto the ball. The top and bottom are adorned with an ornament made of an earring of pearls. Another one is made of shades of red with silver 822 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—DECEMBER 1958 trimming; a blue and pink one, with gold braid trimming, gold rosettes of tinsel at the top and bottom; still another one in shades of pale green and pink; a silver and purple one with an amethyst trim. Each one is a work of art and originality — each different and so lovely that it would be difficult to choose which is most beautiful. This year Florence will trim her tree with these new kinds of decorations and give many of them as gifts. She may use small ones as table decorations or place cards. Yes, this year Florence will not only deck her halls with boughs of holly, but she will add a new tradition that is different and beautiful, something that she and you and I will find joy in the making, in the giving, and the using, something different which will make this Christmas season especially gay and warm, beautiful, different, and festive.
