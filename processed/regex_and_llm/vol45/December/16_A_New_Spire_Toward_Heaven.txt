A New Spire Toward Heaven Donna Toland Smart HEN the book that records the religious history of this generation is opened, bright on its pages will be the story of the dedication of the London Temple. And at that future time many of the 76,324 pre-dedication visitors may join with Church members in testimony of the power felt within those beautiful, sacred rooms. Approximately 8,000 worthy members enjoyed the Spirit of the Lord through one or more of the six dedicatory services. Untold numbers will yet do so in regular temple ordinance sessions. OK OK KOK Situated some thirty miles from the heart of London, standing white against the brilliant green backdrop of an English countryside, the temple is indeed a house of beauty. Modestly hidden from view except for the sky-piercing spire, this House of God rises majestic behind an ancient, ivy-covered brick wall. Great stretches of lawn sweep templeward, venerable oaks stand in its shadow, bright-headed flowers beckon along its walks. The sight bespeaks of God. Soul-absorbed in beauty, the viewer might well contemplate that here rises a new tradition in_ historical, tradition-conscious England. Nearby is the old—a sixteenth-century manor house, gracious and _ lovely restored residence for the temple president. 'The new London Temple points as sharply as its spire to the future—eternal family progress for millions long gone, and as many of the living of Britain and Europe as open their hearts to the newly proPage 802 claimed, though old message. The pure whiteness of the temple walls sharply contrasts with time-blackened, nation-revered monuments of glorified British history. Within those walls women, especially, are pleased with the grace and harmony of the decor. This is not a large building, only eightyfour feet wide, 159 feet long, spire rising almost 157 feet from ground to sky. Concrete and structural steel are faced with beautiful Portland limestone. Colors are subdued and restful, beige and soft greens predominating. Beautiful imported woods glisten in the fifty-eight finished rooms — teak and teak veneer from Burma, African avodire, and agba, warm-toned cherry. Italian-mosaic tiling in soft tones of rose and blue set off the dressing rooms delightfully. Furnishings designed by architect Edward O. Anderson especially for particular spots are elegantly simple. Many visitors were heard to re-. Mark that the baptismal font was lovely — and many others would agree. A glowing stainless steel font rests on the backs of twelve bronzeantiqued oxen, separated into groups of three. Walls and floor are muted blue-green mosaic, except for one wall of glass windows through which the font may be seen from the main floor. Dominating the whole scene is a painting by Paul F'. Forster (the only painting in the temple) of the baptism of Jesus, a dramatic and moving presentation of text, Matthew 3:15: "Suffer it to be so now " Much interest in the Church and A NEW SPIRE TOWARD HEAVEN 803 Courtesy Deseret News THE LONDON TEMPLE its teachings has been stimulated by this umique religious edifice. To sit in his presence, to look into his eyes, to listen to his power in counsel, instruction, and prayer, is, at any time, to feel through him communion with the Father. At the dedication many saints tasted of this experience for the first time. In six different sessions he preached six different sermons—each inspiring to its particular audience. 'The dedicatory prayer he offered twice a day for three days, each time with grandeur and fervor. Faces that recall the warmth of uninvited tears coursing down their cheeks, ears that still throb remembering the thunderous tones of "The Spint of God Like a Fire Is Burning" — testify of God's joy and approval of his house and servants.
