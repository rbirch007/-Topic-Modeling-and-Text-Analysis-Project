Family Unity Dantzel W. Nelson S the only male member in A our family of seven, our Daddy is slightly outnumbered. He also has to be a bit of a Solomon to help smooth over the temperamental crises that arise. In our family, Daddy acts as the steadying influence in keeping family unity in our home. It can _ be noticed first thing in the morning when the "good fairies" (including Daddy) scurry around straightening beds while Mother is preparing breakfast. With much praise and exclaiming from Mother, a happy feeling of accomplishment prevails at breakfast, and thence into family prayer which is called to order by the ringing of a little bell, the sole use of which is to call the family together. To ring the family prayer bell is a privilege, enjoyed especially by two-year-old Sylvia. Family prayer constitutes an important support for unity in our home, for it is evident that "The family that prays together, stays together." Morning and night, and at mealtimes we unitedly give our thanks for our many blessings and pray for the righteous desires of our hearts. The opportunity of being leader is given to each in turn. Of course, with our Daddy being a doctor, his work frequently prohibits his being with us, but we still are a family and try to carry on the same without him as when he is here. We do strive to be all together at least once a day, and it usually is for the evening meal. As a family, we celebrate as many occasions as possible with a party. Page 76 We find that we have our most fun and satisfaction preparing and _planning to please other members of the family. Even our Family Hour has become more successful since it has been known as our "Family Party." One night a week, we try to have our Family Party. It begins early in the day, as one of the four oldest girls is to plan the menu and help prepare dinner for that night. Whatever she prepares for dessert, we use as refreshments at the end of our party. It's amazing how everyone tries her best to eat all that has been prepared so our little cook for the evening won't be disappointed. Of course, the cook is so pleased if everyone enjoys her masterpieces. As of the present, Mother has to give a few hints and a bit of help, but it won't be too long before all the girls can plan and prepare a meal with skill. Marsha, our nine-year-old, presents an especially well-prepared dinner already, and with very little assistance. After dinner, our party begins with prayer and then opens with the business portion. Responsibilities are delegated, and problems are discussed at this time. Recently, for example, the discussion evolved around the change in bedrooms and sleeping companions necessitated by the expectation of a little newcomer into our family circle. Following the business portion is the program where the children each participate. They tell a story they have heard in school, or sing a song, or play the piano. Not every family night is conducted in this manner. For variety, we may watch home movies, or visit other members of our families, or on special occasions, such as Christmas, we may decorate the tree, prepare homemade gifts, or sing carols together. IR'THDAYS provide another important time for family celebrations. 'These are days when we try to give special attention to one in particular; something that is otherwise difficult to achieve in a large family. Even if the birthday girl i is only one-year-old, she has her own birthday cake and a party, with small gifts from each of her sisters. We blow up balloons and _ sing "Happy Birthday" after a family dinner. Grandparents, uncles, aunts, and cousins are invited to come in during the evening for cake and ice cream. For the older girls, friends are included as well, if this is their desire. Sisters are always included in the party and bring small gifts as do all of the invited guests. Daddy usually gets home for the end of the party and takes pictures so each one may have a record of her birthday event. In twelve years of married life, we have moved seven times, including sojourns from Salt Lake City to Minneapolis, to Washington, D. C., to Boston, to Minneapolis, and finally, back to Salt Lake City. As a consequence, the move has usually represented the only vacation time that we have had. We have always moved together, and our trips in the 78 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—FEBRUARY 1958 car have been most enjoyable. Recently, we motored to the Zion, Grand Canyon, and Bryce national parks. Such excursions constitute an unexcelled opportunity to learn to get along with each other and the lessons of tolerance and adaptability. On-the-spot education relating to points of interest in the history of our country and Church has been possible at places like Provincetown, Plymouth, Palmyra, Kirtland, Nauvoo, and many other interesting sites. For nearly four years, we have traveled in our station wagon, that is expertly packed by our Daddy with a "bedroom" in the back, "sitting room" in the middle, and with Mother and Daddy in the front. Each child gets a turn at each place. We spice up the monotony of travel by playing games; mostly guessing games. Particular favorites are the singing games where each one gets a turn to hum a song while the others hasten to state the name of it. For diversion, a surprise sack is prepared before departure in which are individually wrapped surprises, such as tiny dolls, crayons and coloring books, puzzles, bright shiny measuring spoons for the baby; anything that will keep them occupied. 'These are given out one at a time when tempers begin to flare and little backs get tired of sitting. Often, this has saved the day and helped restore peace to troubled tots. What a thrill it was to take our sweet little girls to see the Salt Lake, St. George, and Manti temples; to answer their questions and explain that, because of the temple martriage of their Mother and Daddy, we may be privileged to continue our family relationships into the eternities ahead, if we keep ourselves worthy. Never has there been such a feeling of closeness in our family, and how thrilled we were when our oldest girl said, "When I grow up, I'm going to marry someone like Daddy in the temple; then we can always be together, too." How we hope and pray each day that she and her sisters will all prepare to achieve this goal! T is a wonderful thing for children to have a Father who really enjoys his children; a Father who is vitally concerned about their spiritual, as well as physical, well-being. The girls look forward to his coming home. He washes their hair, helps teach them cleanliness, plays with them, and even takes them on special trips with him. Our oldest gitls have each had a trip with their Daddy when he has traveled out of the State on speaking engagements. They have journeyed by train, taxi, and plane, and these trips are something they never will forget. Not only is it educational for them, but they get the opportunity to become better acquainted with their Daddy. They come back with a new love for each other and an_ understanding that comes from being treated "like a princess." It is such a good way to get close to each other in a way they can't do at home when Daddy has to be divided five ways. At home, we try to get special time alone with each child, but it is difficult to get away from doctor's duties, home duties, and Church duties, etc. Long enough to spend much individual attention on each child. FAMILY UNITY Many things we do in our family would be rather difficult to carry out in a home where children are older, but certainly the feeling of love and unity can be cultivated in any home. We find it better to welcome our children's friends here and provide space and equipment for them, than to worry about where each one is playing away from home. A good friend once said, "Be grateful and enjoy your children while they are young. You can tuck them all in at night and know they are safe. In a few years, other worries will start. Each child will go her own way and then you will have worries." While this is undoubtedly true, we hope to build a founda- 79 tion of trust and love for each other that will carry us through the future. Only time itself will tell us if we have succeeded. Certainly, we have problems that must be faced and many that have not been solved. There is one source that is a constant guide to us — the gospel of Jesus Christ. 'The messages of our prophets today that we all hear each Sunday as we attend Sunday School and sacrament meeting together are a constant inspiration and source of ideas. The Auxiliaries help us; our faith helps us; and our prayers help us.
