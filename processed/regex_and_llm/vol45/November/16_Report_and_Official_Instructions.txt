Report and Official Instructions President Belle S. Spafford (Address Delivered at the Officers Meeting of the Annual General Relief Society Conference, October 8, 1958) ELIEF Society is growing in numbers and accomplishments, and officers are to be commended for the wise and able direction they are giving to the work. All combine to bring about, under the direction of the Priesthood, a strong and growing organization blessed by our Heavenly Father. The General Board now has a total membership of twenty-seven. Organizations and Reorganizations Relief Society organizations are growing in number. At the close of 1957 there were 245 stake Relief Society organizations, an increase of thirteen over the previous year. There was a total of 4,133 ward and branch Relief Societies, an increase of 195. 'There were forty-five missions, the same number as in 1956. During 1957 there were forty-cight reorganizations in the stakes, and eight in the missions. We welcome the new officers and wish them happiness in their callings. Membership There was a gratifying increase in the Church-wide Relief Society membership during 1957 of 10,714, making a total of 183,436 enrolled members. The membership gain for 1957 was 1,505 greater than the 1956 gain over 1955. Delighted as we are that so many sisters have been enlisted as members, we still realize that our figures show there were 368,303 Latter-day Saint families, in each of which it is assumed there would be at least one sister eligible for Relief Society membership. Meetings to Be Formally Conducted It is the feeling of the General Board that meetings should be conducted in the spirit of sisterly friendliness, but they should be dignified, formal meetings, conducted in accordance with the rules set for them. Executive officers should sit together in a place arranged for them as presiding officers. It is the feeling of the General Board that we should be in harmony with the teachings and general practices of the Church in using the terms "Sister' and "Brother" when addressing persons in our meetings. It is recognized that in some informal activities and associations there may be occasion when it is natural and desirable to address sisters by their given names, but in the regular Relief Society meetings, it seems proper to address a member as "Sister" and to use her surname. Attendance We note a slight increase in average attendance at meetings, it being 36.7 per cent for 1957, as against 36.1 per cent for the previous year. We are delighted to see so many young mothers in attendance. It has been found stimulating to the work and highly desirable to have the young mothers, with their richness of youth, their enthusiasms, interests, and skills, numbered with the mature women, with their rich life experiences, their wisdom, mature judgment, and firm testimonies. 727 Missions Through the missionary program of the Church, the work of Relief Society has been extended to the far corners of the world. It mav be of interest to you to know that on April 3, 1957, the first Relief Society was organized in Hong Kong in the Southern Far East Mission for the Chinese sisters. (There are now four Relief Societies there.) The Relief Society President wrote as follows: Nineteen Chinese ladies were present at the first meeting, and sixteen paid their membership dues the first day. They all are very eager to learn all about sewing and cooking. They felt a little slighted because the men have the Priesthood, and now they think it is wonderful to have something special for the women, too. To have a special organization for them really pleases them. We are very grateful for the Relief Society and for the joy it is bringing to the women here. Many splendid Relief Societies are being conducted by the wives of servicemen, some of them in very faraway places; for example, Libya and Okinawa. Many of these sisters are young mothers. We pray for their well-being. We know they are blessed through their Relief Society activities. The work of Relief Society in the missions is going forward along recommended lines, and great good is being accomplished. The members are serving one another. They are developing their talents and increasing their abilities. 'heir testimonies are growing strong under the influence of Relief Society. Welfare and Visiting Teaching It is not uncommon for us to say, "Welfare is the basic work of Relief Society, and our original assign- 728 RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—NOVEMBER 1958 ment from the Prophet." 'The record of our service during 1957 ls creditable. 'here was a total of 344,183 hours contributed by the sisters to the Church Welfare Program upon assignment of Relief Society. There were 275,469 visits made to the sick and homebound, almost 20,000 more than the previous year. 'There were approximately 30,000 eight-hour days bedside nursing care given the sick on a volunteer basis upon assignment of Relief Society presidents. Yet, when measured against the need for these services, I am wondering if we are doing all we could do, or all we should do. Never before in history have there been so many homebound, particularly homebound aged, among us. Are we exercising sisterly watchcare? Are we calling sufficiently often to cheer them? Are we willingly rendering them little, necessary personal services such as doing a little shopping for them, writing a letter for them, or similar kindnesses? Are we willingly giving a day now and then to put their homes in order as desired? Are we meeting our full responsibilities to the sick, particularly the convalescent, the young mother home from the hospital with a new baby, or the chronically ill? Are we sufficiently aware of and active in meeting the needs of the sorrowing and broken in spirit? 'These are traditional services of Relief Society, my sisters. We are all united in believing this to be our great work; yet, are we, in reality, giving to our great work the same degree of devoted, conscientious effort we are giving to other divisions of our work? There is no division of our work to which we may set our hands that will bring to us such great happiness in its fulfillment as the rendering of our so-called compassionate services. Let us not lose sight of the value of the visiting teaching program in meeting our responsibilities along these lines. Visiting teaching is far more than friendly calling at the homes. It is more than the discussion of the spiritual message in the homes, important as this is regarded by all of us. Visiting teaching is monthly coverage of Latter-day Saint homes designed primarily to keep ward Relief Society presidents, and through them ward _ bishops, apprised of the needs of families within the respective wards, so that none shall go unattended. Visiting teaching figures for last year are heartening insofar as the number of women engaged in the program is concerned, and the number of visits made. 'There were 75,928 visiting teachers, constituting 41.39 per cent of the membership, who made 3,125,813 visits, or an average of 8.49 per cent to each Latter-day Saint family. With regard to our activities in co-operating with the Church Welfare Program, may I remind you that stake Relief Society presidents and counselors designated to conduct the Relief Society departments at the bimonthly regional welfare meetings are referred to by the General Church Welfare Committee as "sroup leaders." 'The function of these group leaders is to help plan the agendas for the departments, submit them for approval to the executive committee of the regional council, and lead the discussions in the departments. The group leaders in the employment and work director departments have no jurisdiction over the other employment REPORT AND OFFICIAL INSTRUCTIONS or work director counselors. 'The group leader in the presidents' department has no jurisdiction over the other stake Relief Society presidents, except with regard to their assigned responsibilities in the clothing program as outlined in the clothing bulletin. She should not of her own volition initiate programs involving the other presidents, nor should she commit them to any action or program. We further remind you that the Relief Society departments of the bimonthly regional welfare meetings are for the purpose of considering Church Welfare matters only. Other aspects of Relief Society work have no place there. In addition we appeal to you sisters, when requests are made by persons or groups other than our own for cash or commodity contributions or service from Relief Society, to refer the request to the General Board.
