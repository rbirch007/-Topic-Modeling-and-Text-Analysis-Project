She wished little Marty would get out from underfoot. If she would only play outside until the mangling was done anyway. In mother's day, Marvilla thought, when the wash was finally done, we could call it a day, but now, well, if I don't mangle the flat pieces why I can't possibly get the ironing finished on Tuesday. When the ironing isn't finished on 'Tuesday, then the mending isn't done on Wednesday, and the sewing and repairing aren't done on 'Thursday, and the baking isn't done on Fnday, and the cleaning and shopping aren't done on Saturday, and things aren't ready for Sunday, and the whole week is behind. Marvilla's thinking had completely exhausted her, but she started trying to push clothes through the mangle just a little faster, and started worrying about what to fix for supper. She should pick the peas in the garden for supper, but there wasn't time, and the beets just had to be pulled and pickled. Marvilla's face knitted into a frown. She looked down at Marty, still lying underfoot. What is she doing, blowing hot air through a straw on my legs? "Mommy doesn't it tickle your legs?" Marty asked. "It surely does, and I wish... Marvilla's voice had an_ irritated tone. "Then, if it tickles, why don't "? You laugh?" Marty's little fouryear-old face seemed a mixture of disappointment and puzzlement. Marvilla frowned, and raised her voice a little higher and replied, "Because I'm much too busy working, Marty, to take time to laugh." Suddenly Marty bounced her little self from the floor at her mother's feet, and with arms akimbo said seriously, "You should never be too busy to laugh!" 'Then, turning, she fled from the room. Marvilla, alone now in the utility room, pondered over the incident. What was she doing wrong? Surely she worked hard enough, she was busy every minute. Why she was a drudge, and she might as well admit it. Marvilla looked about her. She had things convenient, lots more so than her own mother had had. Her own mother had washed with a gas engine, and even before that with a hand agitator and hand wringer. Then on ironing day they had used the stove-heated irons. 'They had laughingly referred to them as sadirons, but nobody had been sad when they used them. Each one of the girls had been proud to take her turn and finish a piece of clothing with never a sign of scorch. Marvilla had to admit things were complicated, too, before the electrical appliance age, but her mother had had something! "What was it mother had that I haven't?" Marvilla asked herself. Mother certainly hadn't been a queen bee sitting on a throne while all the children, like worker bees, slaved about her. But she had been Page 259 260 sort of queenly. She had certainly been the center of the workinghome operation. "What did mother have?" Marvilla repeated. She pulled out the electric plug on the mangle and let it cool. Remembering, Marvilla knew other days when the children gathered around mother in the little armless rocker, and they shelled peas or cleaned gooseberries or learned how to crochet. Peterson BACH child of mine was a little bundle of accomplishments from other worlds, holding within its tiny soul the possibilities of worlds to come. I remember the first one that came to me. I placed its little fist against my face while tears ran down my cheeks in gratitude to God, knowing that I was partner with him in so fine a thing. A great adventure truly had been mine. And every time I walked the path again, I walked with pride down through the valley with my head held high. I marveled at the gift that would be mine and wondered how the child would take the things of earth; what virtues would she make a part of her, or would he be master of the things he met? Would he have the strength and courage of a truly gentle man? Then when I reached the door where none could go save me and that Great Attendant who is consoler at all earth's travails, my heart beat fast with great anticipation and joy came to me as to one who seesthe finish of her task in sight, and I was glad to pay whatever price to gain my prize. Now that they are grown, these babies of mine, my joy hangs on their every word and deed. I ask no greater blessing than that they will take my hand and lead me to a higher plane and christen me with that most gracious name, "Mother."
