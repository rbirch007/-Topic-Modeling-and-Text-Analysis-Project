To Have the Peace Betty Ventura S the second-class bus joggled and jerked down the narrow, dusty road, Lola peered out anxiously, her black eyes seeking their first glimpse of the familiar long row of pepper trees. Only seven weeks away from her home in Santiago, but how good it would be to see Mama and Papa and the little ones again! She had never been away from them for so long. When Tia Rosita's time had drawn near, it had been decided that Lola should be sent to care for her, back in the little hill town of Milpa, and to care for Tia Rosita's six children and Uncle Carlos. It would mean hard work, for Milpa was a tiny place, hardly a town at all, where women and girls still washed their clothes in the stream, and where the corn for each family's tortillas was ground out by hand on' a stone metate. For Lola it would mean long hours of grinding corn and cooking, and washing and ironing, and looking after six little cousins. But Lola was sixteen, with smaller brothers and sisters of her own, and maturity was expected of her. Had she not learned to pat out firm round tortillas in her own home, to cook and help Mama with the little ones, and with Carlos who was ten? So sixteen-year-old Lola had been sent to Milpa, to perform the tasks of a woman. But now 'Tia Rosita was better; the new black-headed baby boy grew fatter and fatter. And Lola could go home! And, oh, what stories she would tell the little ones! Her dark eyes sparkled at the thought. All about the little white-faced burros which went bouncing up and down the hills of Milpa. About the day the goats climbed up the low thatched roof of Tia Rosita's little adobe house, and how all the cousins helped chase them down again. About funny little Milpa itself: rows of brown adobe houses, not painted white like those in Santiago, and the narrow donkey trails which were the streets, and no stores or schools or electric lights, not even a big church. And then Lola would tell the little ones, and Mama and Papa, too, about Mario, the boy who knew so many games for the cousins, who could tell time by the stars, who knew ever so many things about animals and flowers, the boy who had helped her so much when her back grew tired from grinding corn and there were still so many tasks undone. Mario had even washed clothes in the stream with her, something no boy in all Santiago would do. 'There were so many things, good things, to tell about Mario. Perhaps that is why she was so anxious to get home. Suddenly, there lining the road ahead were the pepper trees, and then reaching out to meet them the cobblestone street of Santiago; then rows of white-washed adobe houses, and the plaza where the bus stopped. And there in the bright sun of the plaza were Papa and Mama and the brothers and sisters. "Papa, Mama! Maria has grown Page 263 264 taller. Carlos, I can ride a burro now, even when he runs. Oh, and I bring you all sweet tuna fruit and many greetings from Tia Rosita." All was laughter and Lola knew how good, how wonderful it was to be with her family again. Sometimes the second-class bus was early, sometimes very late, for being old and rickety, breakdowns were frequent. Yet Mario never complained. And he seemed to enjoy the Mutuals. During the programs and games he laughed and frolicked with the better-dressed young people of Santiago. Lola, seated at his side, knew he listened carefully to all that was said, but when Elder Lewis asked a question he would only shake his head and drop his eyes.
