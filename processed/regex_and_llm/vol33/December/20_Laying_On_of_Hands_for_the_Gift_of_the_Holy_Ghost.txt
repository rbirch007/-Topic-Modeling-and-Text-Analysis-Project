Laying on of Hands for the Gift of the Holy Ghost Elder T. Edgar Lyon For Tuesday, March 18, 1947 LATTER day Saint theology differs greatly from all other Christian interpretations of the Godhead, because of its teaching concerning the personalities that constitute this trinity. In regard to the Holy Ghost, our teaching is different and more positive than others. Most confessing Christians believe that the Holy Ghost is nothing more than a spirit that is part of the Eternal God, radiating from him in much the same way that light and heat are radiated from a central source of supply. Latter-day revelation has given us the true conception. Verses twenty-two and twenty-three of section 130 in the Doctrine and Covenants instruct us as follows: The Father has a body of flesh and bones as tangible as man's; the Son also; but the Holy Ghost has not a body of flesh and bones, but is a personage of Spirit. Were it not so, the Holy Ghost could not dwell in us. A man may receive the Holy Ghost, and it may descend upon him and not tarry with him. This scripture teaches specifically that the Holy Ghost is a personage separate from either the Father or the Son, being without a tangible body, yet possessed of individuality and personality, and having the ability to dwell within a mortal soul. However, this indwelling may be either temporary or permanent, according to the worthiness of the person, the needs of the person, or the manner in which it was _bestowed upon him. If bestowed according to the prescribed rules of the Church, the person receives the gift of the Holy Ghost, which should be a lasting, vital part of his life and his faith, The Prophet Joseph Smith explained this in a statement that has become a classic description of the difference between the gift of the Holy Ghost that one receives at confirmation, and the temporary descent that the Holy Ghost may make on special occasions. There is a difference between the Holy Ghost and the gift of the Holy Ghost. Cornelius received the Holy Ghost before he was baptized, which was the convincing power of God unto him of the truth of the Gospel, but he could not receive the gift of the Holy Ghost until after he was baptized. Had he not taken this sign or ordinance upon him, the Holy Ghost which convinced him of the truth of God, would have left him. Until he obeyed these ordinances and received the gift of the Holy Ghost, by the laying on of hands, according to the order of God, he could not have healed the sick or commanded an evil spirit to come out of a man, and it obey him; for the spirits might say unto him, as they did to the sons of Sceva: "Paul we know and Jesus we know, but 856 who are ye?" (Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith, page 199). It is necessary to distinguish between the Holy Ghost and the divine spirit that is to be found everywhere on earth among mortals of all races, creeds, and colors. President Joseph F. Smith very pointedly explained this difference: It is not the Holy Ghost who in person lighteth every man who is born into the world, but it is the light of Christ, the Spirit of Truth, which proceeds from the source of intelligence, which permeates all nature, which lighteth every man and fills the immensity of space. You may call it the Spirit of God, you may call it the influence of God's intelligence, you may call it the substance of his power, no matter what it is called, it is the spirit of intelligence that permeates the universe and gives to the spirits of men understanding (Gospel Doctrine, pp. 73-74). What then, is the function of the Holy Ghost that is conferred upon us as a gift at the time of confirmation? It gives to those receiving the gift, if they are faithful, the right to the companionship of the Holy Ghost. It is also the completion of the symbolic representation of a rebirth that is part of the acceptance of the gospel of Jesus Christ. When John the Baptist appeared in the towns and villages of Judea, he preached a powerful message of repentance, calling upon the people to straighten out their crooked ways of living and bring forth the fruits of righteous living. Then, when this had been done, he urged them to accept baptism by immersion that symbolized the washing away of sins and the burial of the old, sinful self. John did not end his work with the accomplishment of the ordinance of baptism. ?DECEMBER 1946He stressed the fact that there must still be a rebirth by the Spirit, to complete the act of regeneration to a new life. He said:. I baptize with water: but there standeth one among you whom ye know not; He it is, who coming after me is preferred before me, whose shoe's latchet I am not worthy to unloose.... The next day John seeth Jesus coming unto him, and saith, Behold the Lamb of God, which taketh away the sin of the world. This is he of whom I said, After me cometh a man which is preferred before me: for he was before me. And I knew him not: but that he should be made manifest to Israel, therefore am I come baptizing with water.... I saw the Spirit descending from heaven like a dove, and it abode upon him. And I knew him not: but he that sent me to baptize with water, the same said unto me, Upon whom thou shalt see the Spirit descending, and remaining on him, the same is he which baptizeth with the Holy Ghost (John 1:26-33). The elderly gentleman referred to in lesson five who did not count the first forty-five years of his life, had fully caught the significance of this idea that John was preaching. Jesus told Nicodemus that unless a person were born again "of water and of the Spirit, he cannot enter into the kingdom of God" (John 3:5). This idea of the rebirth to a newness of life through water baptism and the reception of the Holy Ghost became the fundamental message of the Christian Church in its public preaching. After having completed a "Bath of Regeneration," as some have called baptism, our elderly friend had had hands laid upon him. The conferring of the Holy Ghost upon him had, in his estimation, completed his rebirth as much as the entrance of his spirit into his body at physical birth had given him mortal existence.
