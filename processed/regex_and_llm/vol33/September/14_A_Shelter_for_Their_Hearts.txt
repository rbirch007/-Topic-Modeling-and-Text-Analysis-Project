And there seemed nothing she could do about it. Vaguely she blamed herself... And, yet, she knew that if anyone was to blame, it was Coralee's mother. For, after all, Coralee was a younger edition of Carol Santley, gaily thoughtless and selfish and irresponsible. Carol always had played the pretty martyr to her babyish golden haired daughter, bemoaning the insufficiency of the annuity her late husband had provided her which, with any capable management at all, would have been ample for the comfort of both. Carol was accustomed to deposit her child, now and again, with relatives or friends, for times varying from an afternoon to a couple of weeks or more, so that she could go away fora "rest." Still, Coralee adored her mother, and was evidently bent on following in her footsteps. Coralee's and Link's had been one of those swift, unconsidered, wartime romances. Link had been barely nineteen, Coralee not yet seventeen, when they had decided to be married before Link went into the service. And, if Madge and Ken Walters had had misgivings at their son's choice of a wife, they had not had the heart, at such a time, to voice them. Carol, suddenly relieved of responsibility for Coralee, following her marriage to Link, promptly found a romance for herself and went off gaily honeymooning to Mexico. There, in a small seaside town, she and her new husband found Carol's annuity sufficient for two, and there they remained. And Coralee liked living as a guest at the home of Link's parents. And so it had begun. At first, Madge hadn't minded at all. Coralee was lovable and cuddlesome, and her presence relieved the deadly quiet of the house after Link had gone away. 'Then, after awhile, there was the baby, Tad. He was round and pink and adorable, and Coralee worshipped him and turned over to Madge the less attractive duties pertaining to his care.
