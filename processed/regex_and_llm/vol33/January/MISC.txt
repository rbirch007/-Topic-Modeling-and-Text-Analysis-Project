Or Sailing To A Charted Course? Lella Marler Hoggan Illustrations by Dora Marler Williams RE you drifting on the sea of life without compass, oar, or rudder? Do you think that by some chance of fate your frail craft will reach a safe harbor? The experience of all the years can hold out no such hope for you. Whatever course you travel, you have no assurance of a satisfactory journey and a safe arrival at your desired destination unless you have some sort of guidance. Those going before us have charted the seas and set signal lights on the rocky coasts. They have made beaten paths through the dark forests and placed guideposts along the highway of life. For hundreds of years civilization has been patrolling the roads that men's feet must travel, in order that humanity might have a less perilous and a more comfortable journey. The high privileges and great advantages that we now enjoy were not easily won. Many of them have come to us over the altar of sacrifice. Men have literally gone through fire and blood and death to remove barriers to progress and_ to establish the light of truth. Among the unforgettable ideals that have helped to shape the course of man's destiny, are the rules that Moses brought to the Children of Israel. Graven on tablets of stone by a divine hand, they come down Page 22 the years to us, and now stand as the foundation of civil law. Another eternal document that has come to us from the past for our guidance and safety, is the Master's Sermon on the Mount. Had mankind but followed the light of this divine beacon, probably we would not have experienced the most cruel and devastating war of all time. These inspired rules of life, these fundamental truths, outline safe paths that lead to a desirable destination. Is it not prudent that we should follow them, that we should walk in the light of the torch of experience to our own betterment? The past has endowed us with priceless gifts of achievement. All of the wisdom and the beauty and the power that the centuries have accumulated has been poured into the vast reservoir of common knowledge. And the child of today has free access to this golden store. Although these blessings are free to all men, we can claim them only through our personal effort. We have to dig and delve to secure the gold that is hidden in the dark recesses of the earth. Other treasures are acquired in a similar manner. We must pay the price if we would receive the reward. We cannot drift with the tide and yet hope to reach the harbor of our dreams. If we desire to enjoy the rich heritage that life holds in store for us, we may be sure we shall have to follow a charted course to a definite goal. Obedience to accepted rules of conduct saves us from becoming human driftwood. A long time ago Confucius declared that "Personal character can only be established on fixed principles." And Aristotle asserted, "Freedom is obedience to self-formulated rules." I is true that "we can write our own price-tags."" But we cannot give ourselves double promotions unless we are able to pass the tests. The germs of truth and beauty that lie dormant within us will come to a full flowering only through study, prayer, work, and suffering. Honesty and integrity, courage and reliability, do not come by chance. Like other worth-while characteristics, they are developed through cultivation and training. We have to live the law to obtain the blessing. Our loved poet Ella Wheeler Wilcox wrote: Who sows in weakness cannot reap in strength, That which we plant we gather in at length. You can be your own worst enemy or your own best friend. Don't be wheedled into wrecking your own life. It is lack of ambition that lets today's convenience outweigh tomorrow's development. Why permit yourself to drift with the tide and be tossed about by the fitful winds of circumstances like a scrap of bleached deadwood? If you find that your impulses are carrying you downstream, let judgment grasp the oars and reverse the course of your sailing. "Make yourself responsible for yourself," urges a modern divine. We can spend our days loitering listlessly along in the lowlands of life or we can lift ourselves to the stars. In charting our course of action, let us accept the guidance that the wisdom of the years provides. Whether our journey be short or ? longer, we can make life richer, more radiant, and altogether more satisfying, if we work with a purpose toward a definite achievement. Notes to the field Only One Relief Society General Conference To Be Held Each Year THE General Board announces, as of January 1946, the discontinuance of the custom of holding two general Relief Society conferences each year. Beginning with the year 1946, only one general Relief Society conference will be held a year.
This action has been taken because it is deemed inadvisable to require the sisters to leave their homes and to spend the necessary money to make two yearly trips to Salt Lake City in the interest of Relief Society, as it is felt that the program for the conference can be so arranged that all needed instructions for departmental work and recommendations in all phases of Relief
Society work can be given at one general conference a year.
Since the educational program of Relief Society commences each October and ends the last of May and Relief Society activities are modified in the summer months, it has been decided to hold the general Relief Society conference each year, beginning with 1946, in October?just prior to the semi-annual general conference of the Church.
In view of the fact that hereafter only one general Relief Society conference will be held a year, it is expected that Relief Society officers will put forth every effort to be present at the October annual Relief Society general conference.

Visiting Seachers' Messages Sources of Strength?Charity Lesson 7?Charity Hopeth All Things, Charity Endureth All Things Dr. Lowell L. Bennion For Tuesday, April 2, 1946 Objective: To show that charity meets offense with patience, understanding, and hope. Ye have heard that it hath been said, Thou shalt love thy neighbour, and hate thine enemy. But I say unto you,... Do good to them that hate you, and pray for them which despitefully use you, and persecute you; That ye may be the children of your Father which is in heaven (Matt. 5:43-45). THIS is strong doctrine. Man seems to be more inclined by nature to give blow for blow than love for hate or good for evil. Critics and skeptics call this Christian ideal good for the kingdom of heaven, but not for this world. Still, we may ask, what does experience teach us? Not infrequently when a person is dealt with unfairly his first impulse is to fight for his rights, to rally the support of his friends, or even to return evil for evil in order to teach the offender the error of his ways. Experience reveals the folly of such a course of action, for argument only stirs more argument as the wind stirs the open fire; and evil not only increases evil, but justifies it in the mind of the first offender. A person of charity knows a better way to deal with an offense against him. Since "charity seeketh not her own," his first reaction to injury or criticism is not to engage in self-defense, but to look within himself to find the cause for the real or imagined offense. If, after making careful study and receiving wise counsel, he finds no cause within himself for the offense, then he is happy that the accusation against him is not justified. And since "'charity rejoiceth in the truth" he is willing to wait, to hope, and even to endure until truth can be his vindicator which it can be and will be far more effectively than he himself could ever be. Hardship and suffering make up a normal part of a person's life. The rains descend, the floods come, and the winds blow, and beat upon every life (See Matt. 7:24-27). Sometimes these things come upon us as
natural consequences of our own ignorance and sins; often they are 
the result of the ignorance and sins of others or of the natural conditions
of earth-life. Whatever their cause, they do not leave us untouched. One
who comes face-to-face with real struggle and suffering will never be
the same person again. Either he will increase in cynicism, bitterness,
melancholy or self-pity, or he will grow in humility, compassion, understanding,
and love. It isn't what happens to us, and more especially what is said about us, that matters, but our attitude towards these things and toward those who offend us. Charity is an unfailing source of strength which enables us to meet
life with courage and faith, with patience, understanding, and love. It
relieves its possessor of the burden of a wounded self and of the need
to waste energy in self-excuse and self-defense, in blame, or retaliation.
It frees the mind and heart for their larger task of knowing the truth and
loving the right. Charity does not mean submission to wrong and to
error; it means more strength for the right and for the truth. LESSON DEPARTMENT

LESSON DEPARTMENT LOOKING back over previous lessons, and remembering particularly the brotherhood principle, we can see that morality is offended whenever justice is violated. Advancements and satisfactions based upon self-interest alone are not enough when it comes to ethics; self-righteousness is no righteousness at all. Real morality is based upon love, equality, fraternity, and justice.
Need and Greed
In 1933, the middle of the great depression, America had between twelve and seventeen million workers unemployed. Several million are unemployed regularly, even in normal years. Furthermore, income varies greatly among those who are able to find work, as shown by the fact that in the 1935-36 period nearly half of America's families received yearly incomes of less than $1,000 each and the 195,000 highest income families received as much as the 13,000,000 families with lowest income. It was President Franklin D. Roosevelt who said that at least one-third of our families are "ill-housed, ill-clothed, ill-fed." During depression years the percentage of poverty goes even lower than that. In America, "the land of opportunity," the land favored with an abundance of natural resources as in no other spot on the earth, poverty and pauperism exist side by side with surplus and waste, people starve in the midst of plenty.
Greed is the explanation for much of the need in the world today. Our great maldistribution of wealth, which permits all of this suffering, is not to be explained on the basis of power and privilege more than ability and effort. It is true, of course, that individuals differ in capacities and abilities as well as in application and effort, so that it is utterly impossible to imagine any individual or level economic equality over a long stretch of time; wealth will quite naturally gravitate to those with ability and industry, and to some extent it should. Even so, any real justice tempered with Christian mercy would require that no one be permitted to go without the necessities of life; the "haves" should feel a responsibility for the "have nots." But more serious even than the widespread tendency on the part of the fortunate to refuse charities and ignore the handicapped, is the fact that economic fortune is so often related to luck, privilege, and a willingness to cheat. While we cannot expect equality so far as ability and industry are concerned, we do not even have an equality of opportunity. Wealth depends too much upon special advantage and deceit, not enough upon effort and honesty.
The profit motive in business, industry, labor groups, and some professions is apparently amoral in its nature, for it takes little heed of ethics or public welfare; expediency is the thing; profit is placed above principle. Advertising all too frequently overplays the qualities of the product in order to sell for profit. Merchandising has likewise in too many instances become a game of deceiving and tricking the public; note the technique of high-pressure salesmanship, for example; and the practice of making goods in order to mark them down again for a "sale"; and the tendency to reduce the quality of goods, jump price ceilings, and in other ways profiteer in wartime. Many big-time industrialists are still ready and willing to exploit the laborer and cheat the consumer in order to build up power and accumulate huge profits. And organized labor, on the other hand, has frequently resorted to work slowdowns, wage holdups, and other questionable devices; in its legitimate struggle for equality, labor has frequently gone rabid and extreme; its cry for justice has too often become a demand for power and special privilege. Professional ethics is probably more highly developed than business, industrial, or labor ethics, but even here is the frequent spirit of money madness. Unless the profit motive can somehow or other be tempered or oriented in the direction of social welfare in all fields of economic activity, justice will never be reached and morality will remain a thing talked about but seldom practiced.
Latter-day Saints should be appreciative of the Church Welfare Program, for its aim is that of security through both self-effort and brotherly love. Emphasis is upon helping people to help themselves rather than encouraging them to accept something for nothing. This is a high moral principle. Personal effort and individual responsibility are of primary concern, as they must always be in any true moral system. But also in the plan is the idea that mercy must go beyond mere justice; greed give way to brotherhood, and the actual needs of people be met by the individual himself if he is able to work, next by his family, and finally by the Church. By sustaining the dignity of labor this program avoids pauperization. By insisting upon moral responsibility toward both self and neighbors it builds character. Fundamentally it is a program of security through industry and mutual aid in the spirit of Christ.
Racketeering
Defined broadly, racketeering simply means to obtain money by fraud, swindle, or other immoral procedures. Sometimes it is outside the law, but often, too, it is technically within the law though beyond all bounds of ethics. The essential idea in racketeering is the circumvention of law for the sake of big money; it is the idea of extortion, or taking money with trickery or force without earning it. The law is evaded sometimes by direct defiance, as in the case of the so-called criminal; sometimes by hiring clever lawyers to find legal loopholes and technical escape valves; and sometimes by bribing public officials for the sake of protection. But always in racketeering there is the spirit of getting something for nothing; the idea that "only the foolish work."
Following are some of the leading types of rackets: (1) Organized robbery, burglary, and larceny. This is usually carried on by criminal gangs and is, of course, in defiance of the law. (2) Dishonest business dealings such as misrepresenting funds, "watering" stock, profiteering on war contracts, promoting fake enterprises, managing banks, stock exchanges, or corporations fraudulent in kind and other varieties. RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE?JANUARY 1946 LESSON DEPARTMENT (3) Organized racketeering of industries. Sometimes this is spoken of as "gangster" control, actually, although this kind of criminal more often escapes the hand of the law, white-collar crime cheats the public out of far more money each year than do the more obvious types of stealing. (3) Kidnapping. This extremely depraved form of racket flourished here some ten or fifteen years ago but the Lindbergh law, which prescribes the death penalty for kidnappers, and the activities of the Federal Bureau of Investigation have practically put an end to it. (4) Bootlegging and the black market. During the prohibition era bootlegging became so prevalent as to constitute a national scandal, and during World War II the black market has expanded to similar proportions. In both cases millions of people have shown themselves willing to take advantage of an artificial legal scarcity and to run against the law for the sake of personal profit. (5) Professional gambling. By playing upon people's willingness to take a chance in order to "get rich quick," and resorting to underhanded trickery, professional gamblers are able to take millions of dollars away from the American public each year. In some areas this is legal, but in most places where gambling establishments operate they do so outside the law. (6) Tribute for "protection." Organized gangs, in the larger cities particularly where they often have an "understanding" with the political machine in power, sometimes extort large sums of money from the various business establishments on the farce of "protection." Unless each establishment hands over to these gangsters the requested sum, it will find its buildings wrecked with a bomb, its goods destroyed with acid, or some other mysterious disaster happening to it?and knowing this, the 69 establishment usually ? comes through. This is one of the most vicious types of racketeering. (7) Betrayal of public office. Where public officials accept bribes, let contracts at inflated figures with the understanding that they will get a "cut" from the contractor, sell their own goods to the government at padded values, or in any other way use their office dishonestly for personal profit, they are betraying the public and deserting all claim to morality. Political Corruption But this is exactly what is happening in so many cases-Organized crime and racketeering, discussed above; would have a difficult time surviving if it were not for the "big fix" where unscrupulous politicians agree to protect criminals, of both the overall and white-collar varieties, in return for votes and money. Political machines are closely organized party organizations that are primarily interested in votes and power regardless of how these are obtained. Party "bosses" with numerous henchmen under them administer their machines in an arbitrary, dictatorial manner. Votes are bought with money and_ personal favors; public jobs are handed out to supporters of the machine regardless of qualifications, and sometimes even jobs, which carry large compensations but little or no work, are created on the books for "faithful" party members; occasionally there is illegal control at the polls and a miscounting of votes. Machine politics is corrupt at its very core for it caters to vested interests, to pressure groups, and not to the will of the people. Corrupt politics is not confined to ~~ any one political party. Blind bigoted party loyalty, with little concern for the issues or the personalities involved, is as un-democratic as it is un-American; and yet we have and have had much of it. Narrow expediency in party politics, at the sacrifice of principle, is as un-Christian as it is immoral; and yet it abounds on every hand., As said in an earlier lesson, what we need is 'more statesmen and fewer politicians?more who are courageous enough to put justice and public welfare before private profit, and fewer who are willing to fleece the public for their own good. Since much of the inefficiency and corruption that is permitted to exist in government circles is there because of an uninformed and indifferent voting public, it follows that the only solution is an enlightened and aroused citizenry. War and Peace The periodic continuation of war throughout the ages, and the tendency for modern wars to be even more total in their brutality and their destructive tendencies than earlier ones is a pretty sad reflection upon the moral progress of mankind. War is basically immoral for it leads to suffering and sorrow rather than peace and happiness. And yet war persists and grows in intensity. But though war is an evil of considerable magnitude, it is not the greatest evil in the world today. Peace is highly desirable, but peace at any price is indefensible and inconceivable. The greatest evil of mankind is injustice, and until this can be eliminated wars will be inevitable and, to some extent, justifiable. There is no point in appeasing the perpetuators of wrong and Oppression; any compromise with error and evil is unthinkable. As wrong as war is, if it is a war for justice, truth, and righteousness it has some justification. (For a justification of killing for righteous purposes see 1 Nephi, chap. 4.) Permanent peace, the goal of mankind and the capstone of moral progress, can hardly be realized without the establishment first of all of equality and justice. Unless democracy and Christianity can be felt internally and lived nationally, there 'is little hope for any world-wide or universal application of these principles. Until we are wise enough and big enough to clean up our own house there is little chance that we will ever be able to help much in the building of a better world. Real happiness, then, can never come without peace, nor peace without justice, justice without brotherhood.
