Social Science The Family in the Gospel Plan Lesson 3 The Importance of Child Training Social Science Committee, Leone O. Jacobs, Chairman For Tuesday. January 28, 1947 Lesson Link: In the previous lesson, ""The Family Organization," we considered essential attributes of husband and wife and the rights and responsibilities of various members of the family. In this lesson we will discuss fundamental principles of child training, stressing the importance of the good example to be set by the parents. Objective: To give specific guidance in child training and to stress the need of developing sound practices in the important calling of parenthood. HILD training is an art of the _first order. Intelligent application of this art is perhaps needed at the present time more than any other thing. Child delinquency has assumed such alarming proportions as to indicate that parents may not be fulfilling their responsibilities toward their children: It is a lamentable fact that often no preparation is made for this most important profession. Education and training are demanded in all phases of business, science, and other fields of endeavor, but in the most noble profession of all, that of parenthood, no training is expected. There are courses in child training being given in our universities, and more students, particularly _ girls, who will be the mothers of the future, should study these courses. Child training demands a great amount of self-control and common sense on the part of parents. Children react so differently to the same experiences that it is a puzzle to always determine the proper treatment. We know that each child is individual in his make-up and that the same rules cannot be applied to everyone alike. There are some principles of child training, however, which are fundamentally sound and which, if consistently followed, will build sound characters. Essentials in Child Training First of all, child training should begin very early. Solomon of old said, "Train up a child in the way he should go; and when he is old, he will not depart from it" (Proverbs 22:6), Parents too often underrate the intelligence of a child by thinking he knows very little until he can talk plainly. The day a child is born is the time to begin his training. Behavior patterns begin from that day. Habits in order, in courtesy, in control of the emotions, and many others, can be taught very tiny children. Most of the routine habits and standards of conduct can be well defined in preschool years. Child training must be persistent to be effective. No habit is formed except by repetition.
