In the days of the Prophet Joseph Smith they rallied to protect his life and the lives of his family; they sacrificed to build the early temples of the Church, and when the time came to choose between the comforts of their Midwestern homes and the rigors of pioneer life as the price of religious freedom, they left those homes, crossed the plains, and with their husbands settled the valleys of the Rockies. They faced the dangers of childbearing without hospitals and often with no medical aid. They endured hunger and cold, attacks from Indians, and the affronts of the world. But, filled with faith and determination, and an understanding of their great objective, they applied themselves to the task. What we now have in the West came in large measure through their efforts. Without them, we never could have achieved so well. Through all their trials, they were saints and mothers combined—like angels of God ministering among men. They helped the poor, nursed the sick, prepared the dead for burial, taught culture, built character, and established faith. Great is their glory. In doing these things they carried out the spirit and the letter of the charge given them by the prophets of this dispensation through the medium of the great Relief Society organization which was established by divine guidance. The women of the Church have a great mission. It can be fulfilled most effectively if it is accomplished in co-operation with the organized Relief Society of the Church. The Relief Society is not a charity organization alone; it is a means through which homes are strengthened, communities improved, and souls saved. One day, while addressing the women of the Society, the Prophet Joseph Smith said: "The best measure or principle to bring the poor to repentance is to administer to their wants. The Society is not only to relieve the poor, but to save souls." He said that the sisters are to search after objects of charity and administer to their wants; assist in correcting the morals and strengthening the virtues of the community; avoid all evil, even its very appearance; they were to pray for one another, Page 147 148 learn to treat their husbands with mildness and affection, and have mercy on one another. He also told them that they were to practice the principles of holiness. IN the same spirit President Joseph F. Smith said that the Relief Society "has not only to deal with the necessities of the poor, the sick and the needy, but a part of its duty—and the larger part, too—is to look after the spiritual welfare and salvation of the mothers and daughters of Zion; to see that none is neglected, but that all are guarded against misfortune, calamity, the powers of darkness, and the evils that threaten them in the world. It is the duty of the Relief Societies to look after the spiritual welfare of themselves and of all the female members of the Church." On another occasion this great leader taught: "The objects of this organization are manifold. It is not designed to look only after the poor and the needy as to their bodily necessities, but it is also intended to look after the spiritual, mental and moral welfare of the mothers and daughters in Zion, and all who are engaged or interested in female work. I commend the Relief Societies to the Bishops, and say, be friendly to these organizations, because they are auxiliary organizations and a great help to the Bishops." These inspired objectives shine like a beacon of safety now in our day. The homes and families of our communities are confronted with serious problems which require the individual and collective attention of the women of the Church. United effort can solve these problems, and the Relief Society is the one organization through which can be channeled best the co-operative endeavors of those women. RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—MARCH 1948 One of the great tasks before us is the preservation of the integrity of the home. Broken homes are becoming like a plague in America, Recent figures reveal that there is one divorce to every three marriages in the United States and in some areas divorces equal marriages in number. This calamity is moving into our own ranks. We need to be fortified against it. The Relief Society provides a great bulwark. Its program helps make homes more secure by training women in every phase of homemaking. Better homemaking makes better homes, and in better homes will be improved character development. This activity enters into the field of child care, also, giving assistance in this regard from the time of birth on. It is a great avenue of service. OTHERS are prepared in Relief Society more effectively to meet the growth of worldliness with stronger faith and a wider knowledge of the gospel. Testimony bearing and lessons in theology are important phases of its weekly program. Other cultural work further helps mothers to make better homes. The arts are taught. 'Touches of beauty are added to the surroundings. Homes become more pleasant. Leisure time is filled with activities which promote happiness and satisfaction on the part of the woman herself, and joy and commendation in the minds of husband and children. Education in literature is an important part of Relief Society work. New horizons appear, bringing fresh hopes and higher aspirations. And then there is the opportunity to help others. Calls upon the Church Welfare Program are tremendous. These postwar years have revealed the great need for the relief work carried on by the Church. Many of our own members suffered much from the late war. Some are still in distress.

But need arises at home, also, and in the future, with national and international affairs so disturbed, who can tell when it will be even greater? Budgets worked out by the General Authorities to meet present emergencies require a great deal of organized assistance from the women of the Church. Sewing, canning, nursing, directly assisting the poor with shelter, food, and raiment, make heavy demands upon the women of the Church. Organized activity is essential. The Relief Society provides it.

ORK meetings are arranged periodically, and the women in groups in their Relief Society rooms, canning centers, and gardens, or individually at home, accept the work assignments. Work meetings are not new to Relief Society women. They have been characteristic of the program from the beginning. Sewing and quilting gatherings were held both before and after crossing the plains. When Johnston's army threatened Utah, the women again went to work to do their part in defense of home and loved ones. While frontier conditions still existed in the West, these women studied hygiene and nursing, and were among the principal boosters of home industry. In 1882 they established the first L. D. S. Hospital in the West at First East and South Temple Streets in Salt Lake City. They saved grain, helped the Red Cross, provided maternity care, conducted exhibits in fairs from coast to coast, erected Relief Society halls for the prosecution of their work, and made connections with other women's organizations on a worldwide scale.

These women have never feared work. They have faced every situation as it has arisen. They will face those that will come in the future with the same willingness and determination. Their program is ever widening, in stakes and in missions. Where Latter-day Saints are, there is the Relief Society. Not only does it encourage women to participate in this great program, it urges their husbands on, also, in Welfare activity, in Priesthood work, and in all other righteous efforts.

The Prophet Joseph Smith is often quoted as having said that the sisters are to "provoke the brethren to good works." Usually the quotation is not given completely as the Prophet originally gave it. This quotation actually reads: "provoke the brethren to good works in looking to the wants of the poor." Relief Society sisters should be ever diligent in searching out those in need. Bishops should be on the alert to have the Welfare program in their wards efficient enough to meet the needs revealed by the work of the sisters. It is all in the plan.

Inasmuch as organized effort on a Church-wide scale is needed to meet the requirements of the day, it is most fitting that the Relief Society now plans for the erection of a women's building to properly house headquarters for these activities, to RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE Marcy 1948 perpetuate the ideals of the Society, and to serve as a great memorial to those of the past who have built and served so well. This beautiful structure will become a source of pride and usefulness to the women of the Church and will be an emblem to all mankind of the purity, faith, and industry of Latter-day Saint womanhood.

The structure will be reared in part by the contributions of the women themselves, who are gathering funds from far and near for this great undertaking. Any woman—from bride to grand-mother—may well be proud of membership in the great Relief Society organization. Such membership is a privilege and an opportunity.

The organization is rich in inspiration, abundant in the service it renders to its members themselves, and devoted to the welfare of others. Its program is productive of those things which homes and communities of today so sorely need. It is God's organization for his faithful hand-maidens of the last great dispensation.

In addition to collecting individual member donations, some Relief Societies are taking steps to raise money as a group in order to help sisters who may find it difficult to make a $5 contribution. One ward is turning over to the fund, for a limited time, all money made by quilting; another has a "sale table" at each Relief Society meeting where handmade articles are on sale following the meeting. These are just a few of the many ingenious methods being devised by Relief Society officers in raising the assigned ward quotas.

Joseph Smith relates that after having had this vision he informed his neighbors, associates, and the ministers of various churches in his vicinity of his experience, and that he immediately encountered opposition and persecution.
The very first reaction of the Smith family to the press that has come to my attention is a paid advertisement which Joseph Smith Sr., father of the Prophet, inserted in the local "home-town" newspaper in Palmyra, New York. The advertisement ran for three consecutive issues, beginning September 25, 1824.
This was not the usual run-of-the-mill type of advertisement of land or livestock for sale. The advertisement was inserted by the senior Smith in an attempt to allay some of the opposition and persecution which had been engendered by his son's bold claims, and which had taken the form of malicious rumor to the effect that the body of Alvin Smith, brother of the Prophet, had been disturbed from its resting place and dissected. Father Smith says of his purpose in printing a denial of the rumors in the Wayne Sentinel:
This method is taken for the purpose of satisfying the minds of those who have heard the report, and of informing those who have put it in circulation, that it is earnestly requested they would desist therefrom; and that it is believed by some that they have been stimulated more by a desire to injure the reputation of certain persons than a philanthropy for the peace and welfare of myself and friends. (Signed) Joseph Smith.
Unless some new sources later come to light to prove otherwise, we may consider it highly probable that Joseph Smith Sr. was the first of the future Latter-day Saints to grasp what the newspaper could mean in the redress of their wrongs. It is perhaps also significant that this initial use of the newspaper came not in the form of a letter or article to a friendly editor, but in the form of a paid advertisement. The above attempt to allay prejudice and persecution may also be considered to be the inauguration of a policy that the Latter-day Saints followed throughout the period from 1824 to 1850, namely, that of the use of the public press, whenever accessible, as an agency for public hearing and redress.
Throughout his history of the Church which the Prophet Joseph Smith dictated to his clerks, he repeatedly refers to the influence of the press in relations between the Latter-day Saints and the American public. In the spring of 1831, only one year after the organization of the Church, he says:
At this age of the Church many false reports, lies, and foolish stories, were published in the newspapers and circulated in every direction, to prevent people from investigating the work, or embracing the faith. A great earthquake in China, which destroyed from one to two thousand inhabitants was burlesqued in some papers as "Mormonism in China."
LESS than two years after the Church was organized, a Mormon periodical was established at Independence, Missouri, with W. W. Phelps as editor. The first issue of The Evening and the Morning Star reached Joseph Smith in Kirtland, Ohio, in July 1832. In commenting on this first issue of the first Latter-day Saint periodical, Joseph Smith said:
Delightful indeed, was it to contemplate that the little band of brethren had become so large, and grown so strong, in so short a time as to be able to issue a paper of their own, which contained not only some of the revelations, but other information also, which would gratify and enlighten the humble inquirer after truth.

So embittered was the public mind against the truth that the press universally had been arrayed against us; and although many newspapers published the prospectus of our paper, yet appeared to have done so more to calumniate the editor, than give publicity to the forthcoming periodical. Editor thought to do us harm, while the Saints rejoiced that they could do nothing against the truth but for it.

In January 1833, Joseph Smith wrote to W. W. Phelps expressing dissatisfaction with the manner in which The Evening and the Morning Star was being conducted. He said:

"We wish you to render the Star as interesting as possible, by setting forth the progress, and faith of the Church, as well as the doctrine; for if you do not render it more interesting than at present, it will fall, and the Church suffer a great loss thereby."

As controversies arose in Missouri between the Saints and their neighbors, reports of the difficulties began to appear in the newspapers, and both groups used the Missouri papers to present their case to the public.

In a letter written from Kirtland, Ohio, to Edward Partridge in Liberty, Clay County, Missouri, Joseph Smith referred to a communication of Orson Hyde on the Missouri difficulties as published in the Missouri Republican. He said it was at variance with the reports he had received through private letters. (D.H.C. 1:448). The interesting thing about this communication to Edward Partridge is the fact that Joseph Smith seems to have given at least some credence to the reports in the Missouri Republican, even though he was generally skeptical of most newspaper reports. He instructed Edward Partridge to gather the correct information and send it immediately to Kirtland, Ohio, so that he could in turn "give the public correct information on the subject."

That as early as 1833 Joseph Smith did not overlook the possibility of using a political as well as a religious newspaper to present the Church point of view is evidenced by another statement from this same letter to Edward Partridge. He said: "We expect shortly to publish a political paper, weekly, in favor of the present administration; the influential men of that party have offered a liberal patronage to us, and we hope to succeed, for thereby we can show the public the purity of our intention in supporting the government under which we live." The party referred to was the Democratic Party under the leadership of Andrew Jackson.

Enough has been given from the writings of Joseph Smith to indicate his attitude toward newspapers as an instrument in presenting the Church viewpoint and to indicate that he was conscious of the fact that the press exerted influence in shaping public opinion. The Latter-day Saint Church issue was only one of many controversial issues occupying the press during the thirties and forties of the past century, and the Saints were not the only group being criticized. But editors seemed to exercise unusual editorial prerogative in their handling of the news of the Church, especially in not printing articles which Joseph Smith and other leaders sought to have published.

Although it is quite certain that the Latter-day Saints were not the only group thus unjustly dealt with by the newspapers, it was undoubtedly this adverse treatment in the public press which called forth from Parley P. Pratt an answer, in book form, which has been one of the most widely printed and circulated pieces of Church literature.

In his preface to the first edition of A Voice of Warning and Instruction to All People, published in New York in 1837, Parley P. Pratt said:

"During the last seven years, the public mind has been constantly agitated, more or less, through all parts of the country, with the cry of Mormonism, Mormonism, Delusion, Imposture, Fanaticism, etc.; chiefly through the instrumentality of the press. Many of the newspapers of the day have been constantly teeming with misrepresentations and lying slanders, of the foulest kind, in order to destroy the influence and character of an innocent Society, in its very infancy; a Society of whose real principles many of them know nothing at all.

"Every species of wickedness has been resorted to, and all manner of evil has been spoken against them, without the possibility of being heard for a moment, saying we do not wish to hear you, we know enough of your principles already. The newspapers, or our good preachers, have told us about you.

"Under these circumstances, what could be done? How were we to correct the public mind? We were few in number, and our means of giving information very limited; the columns of most of the papers were closed against us, their prosperity being at stake the moment our principles were admitted. It is true we published a monthly paper, in which our principles were clearly set forth; but its circulation was limited to a few thousands.

"Having said so much to impress upon the human mind the necessity of hearing and then judging, I would only add that the object of this publication is to give the public correct information concerning a religious system which has penetrated every state from Maine to Missouri as well as the Canadas, in the short space of seven years; organizing churches and conferences in every region, and gathering in its progress from fifty to an hundred thousand disciples. Having at the same time to sustain the shock of an overwhelming religious influence opposed to it by the combined powers of every sect in America.

"What but the arm of Omnipotence could have moved it forward amid the rage of mobs? Having to contend with the prejudice of the ignorant and the pen of the learned; at war with every creed and craft in Christendom, while the combined powers of earth and hell were hurling a storm of persecution, unparalleled in the history of our country."

His quotation states the general attitude of the majority of Church leaders toward the press of the period. The following reaction of a Latter-day Saint, as recorded in his diary, is typical of a number of comments from diaries of Church members who were not General Authorities.

Jesse Wentworth Crosby, born November 25, 1820, in Yarmouth, Nova Scotia of British parentage, emigrated to Chautauqua County, New York with his parents in 1822. In 1838 he was baptized into the Church in Chautauqua County. In an entry in his diary for April, 1839, he said:

"The time passed... It was now April, and all things being ready we set about for Missouri— one thousand miles distant (1000) traveling by land with horse teams and lodging in our wagons; but before leaving our neighbors called often and _remonstrated with us for taking, as they thought, such a random journey. One said, 'Have you read the news: Why the Missourians and the Mormons are at war, they are killing and destroying, and will you persist in going, and running into danger and death?' We replied, 'We have warned you by words, now we warm you by light.'"

If danger or death gets in our way, we intend by the help of God to face the same like men of God, and show all men by example that we have embraced no fiction but an eternal reality, and when the secrets of all hearts are revealed, then, if not till then, you shall know that we are not deceived." This excerpt shows that Crosby, learning through the press that the RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE�MARCH 1948 Church he had joined one year previously was having serious difficulties in Missouri, still manifests a determination to "gather to Zion" and, if "necessary, give up his life in the cause. The statement also indicates that Crosby's neighbors accepted the newspaper accounts and sought to dissuade him from going into an area where he was certain to meet opposition and persecution. It is also evident from Crosby's reply that he or his family had attempted to proselyte their neighbors and had failed, but that they were still speaking to each other despite the rumors afloat in the press. Certainly, a neighbor who comes on the eve of departure to attempt to dissuade one from a course of action which he feels is "a random journey," and one fraught with "danger and death," is acting the part of a friend.

Why did Crosby and his neighbors seemingly remain on friendly terms despite the unfavorable newspaper comments? Why did they fail to follow the usual reaction to bitter criticism and rumor?

An explanation of the continued friendliness of Crosby's neighbors, despite the adverse criticism of the Church in the press, may lie in Crosby's seventeen years residence in the community. I have found numerous other examples which seem _ to indicate that the saints withstood criticism in the press better in those areas where they had been resident for a considerable length of time, and when the uprightness of their characters had been manifest over the years.

Ye shall know them by their fruits. Do men gather grapes of thorns, or figs of thistles? (Matt. 7:6).

Then I was explaining to Bob L., as he wiped the dishes for me, about my inability to throw away damaged dishes. It takes high resolve and strong nerve to do such a deed, but you have a grand and glorious sense of achievement once the wreckage is cleared away.

Need I tell you that Bob has a standing invitation to dinner!

Alberta O. Doxey, President, Eastern States Mission Relief Society, submits the following enthusiastic report of the activities of the Chambersburg Branch: "Over one half of the membership is made up of young women, the youngest, eleven years of age. This little girl sews well and contributes her efforts at work meetings. The majority of the members are in the late teens and early twenties. Under the excellent leadership of Sister Hulda Fields, this society has grown and the true spirit of the Relief Society is evident in all of the meetings.

On October 3, 1947, this Society successfully launched itself into another year. This was in the form of an installation dinner and dance held in the home of Sister Hulda Fields. The program displayed the talent of the women and included a reading, a vocal duet, and a playlet which encouraged activity by depicting the women with the most responsibility at home as the most active in Relief Society, and so learning to live the most rich and full life.

The women all came dressed in formals, but the party was not too formal to enjoy some lively games following the program. Then came a fine dinner and dance. The general theme throughout was the organization of the first Relief Society according to the plan given by the Prophet Joseph Smith. BANNOCK, IDAHO, AND PORTNEUF STAKES VISITING TEACHERS PICNIC, Lava Hot Springs Idaho, September 16, 1947
Bessie S. Hubbard, Secretary, Bannock Stake Relief Society, reports a tri-stake visiting teachers outing and picnic which was held at Lava Hot Springs, Idaho, September 16, 1947.
The purpose of the meeting was to report the splendid accomplishments of the visiting teachers of these stakes and to give them encouragement for the continuation of their important missions. President Milton F. Hartvigsen of Bannock Stake and Sister Emily S. Romish of West Pocatello Stake addressed the sisters on the responsibility and the blessings of their calling.
Musical numbers and practical demonstrations of visiting teaching methods followed. Lunch was served on the lawn to 250 people. A vote was taken and it was unanimously decided to make this meeting an annual affair.
Eliza B. Christensen is president of Bannock Stake Relief Society; Elizabeth W. Hatch, president of Idaho Stake Relief Society; and Emeline W. Marley, president of Portneuf Stake Relief Society.
