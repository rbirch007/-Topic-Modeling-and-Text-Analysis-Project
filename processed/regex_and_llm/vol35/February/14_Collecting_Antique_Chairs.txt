Collecting Antique Chairs
Elizabeth Williamson

If you are a collector of antiques, you know by past experience what you want and how to go about finding it. If you are a beginner, you are probably a little in doubt as how to go about collecting. The simplest way to begin is to absorb the best information around you. Visit museums and historical societies, study the fine pieces, read the magazines which contain interesting and authentic articles dealing with the households of our ancestors. Libraries, and your friends who collect, will help you. There may be a historical house in or near your city which is famous, and furnished with well-preserved pieces, typical of a particular period. You can carefully observe the pieces of antique furniture exhibited in stores, and you can go to auctions. A good antique will be at home in almost any setting. Whether it is an amusing mustache cup, or a beautiful Hepplewhite console, it can lend charm and distinction to your home. Period chairs are always attractive additions to your home. They can be used in the hallway as extras, and occasional chairs may be added to the living room.

Other possibilities are placing these chairs at desks, in bedrooms, at the piano, in the den, and in the sunroom. Rocking chairs are quaint and most welcome if your kitchen is large. By careful observation you will be able to recognize the good lines and the authentic design of an antique chair, and you will become enthusiastic over the romance and the beauty of historic furniture. Ignore the old upholstery; it can be removed and beautiful new fabrics can be used, which will blend with your color scheme and surroundings. However, try to use the fabric which belongs to the period. Each period of history is marked by certain characteristics of design and construction in furniture, and the patterns reflect the spirit of the times, as well as the ideals and activities of the people.

Colonial America, not yet an independent nation, was greatly influenced in its furniture design, as well as in its other customs, by the European mother countries. Nevertheless, the construction of the furniture revealed the simplicity and sturdiness of pioneers and the economic conditions under which they lived. Local woods, such as pine, oak, birch, and maple, were the principal materials, and the upholstery fabrics were, for the most part, homespun materials, although some of the wealthy colonists imported more luxurious fabrics from Europe.

The Pennsylvania Dutch designs of the eighteenth century followed conservative patterns, using simple and useful construction of native woods and homespun fabrics, but some decorative features of painted birds and flowers were added. During the eighteenth century, also, particularly in that period of national development during and following the Revolutionary War and the establishment of the Constitution as the supreme law of the land, English designers still dominated furniture patterns for well-to-do Americans. Thomas Chippendale, the most famous cabinetmaker of England, who died in 1779, while using some of the substantial designs and expert workmanship of earlier furniture makers, added many variations. Using dark mahogany wood, without and working under both "Chippendale marked by sumptuously carved and interlaced lattice work in the backs, and many of them with boldly curved legs, intricately carved. This style, exceedingly popular in Europe, was quickly adopted in America.

A contemporary of Chippendale, George Hepplewhite, whose aim was to blend "elegance and utility" in his designs, developed a style characterized by light, graceful lines, with painted or inlaid decorations of festoons, wreaths, flowers, and musical instruments, in satinwood and mahogany. Hepplewhite's distinctive chair designs exhibited slight, tapering legs, the spade foot, and the distinguishing feature of the shield back. He is credited with having originated the winged chair. Hepplewhite designs are greatly prized by collectors as they are both rare and very beautiful.

Duncan Phyfe is known as an American cabinetmaker, although he was born in Scotland, and did not come to our country until 1783. His designs have had a profound influence on furniture-making in America, and many details, not actually originated by him, can authentically be attributed to his patterns of workmanship.

His first designs were characterized by excellent proportions, graceful curves, often accented by parallel rows of reeding, simple ornaments, well placed and carved with the greatest precision, and decorative motives, such as the lyre, the acanthus or oak leaf, and other graceful arrangements. Later, under the influence of the French Empire styles, he made furniture that was heavy, over-ornamented, and largely characterless.

Victorian styles in furniture came to America during the time of westward expansion, the Civil War, and the development of the United States as a powerful nation of great extent and unlimited resources. Victorian furniture was marked by many and varied influences and much of it was solid, heavy, and ponderous. Elaborate "over-stuffing" was introduced during this period.
