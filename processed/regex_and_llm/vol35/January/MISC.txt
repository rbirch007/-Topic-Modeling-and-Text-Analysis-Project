**KATHERINE FERNELIUS LARSEN - First Prize Poem: "

---

Katherine Fernelius Larsen, of Salt Lake City, is the wife of Rex F. Larsen and the mother of five young children. She is a member of the Art Barn Poets and Poets of the Pacific. Her poems have been published in The Improvement Era, The Relief Society Magazine, Wings, American Bard, The Salt Lake Tribune, and other publications. Last year she won second prize in the Annual Art Barn Poets Contest. A gifted student of literature, Mrs. Larsen plans to continue poetry composition and also to write fiction.

---

And tapped the source of light and energy,
And wrought my work unbound by time and place,
Untrapped by circumstance? Can I erase
Nostalgic pangs, when alien to me
Are clocks and cords and wheels?
Have I been free
To know unmeasured joy and perfect grace?

Why have the centuries behind my blood
Been restless in the labored ways of toil?
Why have they sought to rise from rocks and mud
To flight, or pioneered, or burned the precious oil
Of time for learning? Someday I shall go
Beyond the sea of death—and I shall know.

THE Relief Society General Board is pleased to announce the names of the award winners in the short story contest which was announced in the June 1947 issue of the Magazine, and which closed September 15, 1947.

The first prize story of forty dollars is awarded to Alice Morey Bailey, 256 Iowa Street, Salt Lake City, Utah, for her story "Nugget of Truth."

The second prize of thirty dollars is awarded to Janath Russell Cameron, 237 "D" Street, Salt Lake City, Utah, for her story "The Answer."

The third prize of twenty dollars is awarded to Myrtle M. Dean, 435 East 2d South, Provo, Utah, for her story "Our Children's Children."

This story contest, first conducted by the Relief Society General Board in 1941 as a feature of the Relief Society centennial observance, was made an annual contest in 1943. The contest is open only to Latter-day Saint women who have not at least one literary composition published or accepted for publication by a periodical of recognized standing.

The three prize-winning stories are to be published consecutively in the first three issues of the Magazine for 1948.

Thirty-three manuscripts were submitted in the contest for 1947. Only one of the prize winners for this year had previously placed in the Annual Relief Society Short Story Contest.

This contest was initiated to encourage Latter-day Saint women to express themselves in the field of fiction. The General Board feels that the response to this opportunity will continue to increase the literary quality of The Relief Society Magazine, and will aid the women of the Church in the development of their gifts in creative writing.

Many of the stories submitted in the 1947 contest emphasized the moral theme to the detriment of the quality of the stories. It is suggested that the authors try to improve the literary craftsmanship of their submissions, giving the story plan and particular attention to character development, and giving careful attention to the fine details of word and sentence structure.

The Relief Society Magazine now has a circulation of more than 85,000. There are subscribers in every state of the Union, and in many foreign countries, thus providing a varied and interested group of readers. Writers, recognizing this large and appreciative audience, realize the importance of centering the contest on their very best work.

The General Board congratulates the prize-winning contestants, and expresses appreciation to all those who submitted stories. Sincere gratitude is extended to the three who, in their discernment and skill in selecting the prize-winning stories.

The General Board also acknowledges the valuable contribution of the short story committee in supervising the contest.

Her heels and looked at her small daughter, Penny, in dismay. It wasn't enough to have all this packing to do before the train would leave at six in the morning. On top of it, she must have this nagging worry about Penny.

"Just what did you tell the man at the door, Penny?" "I told him a great big elephant jumped out of the closet and ate you all up," said Penny, with a beatific smile. "Now can we pack my dolly dresses?"

"Penny, you didn't!" "Yes, I did, and I told him, besides, you haven't a minute to spare, and you look a sight!"

"Oh," moaned Anna Lea. "Whatever is Mommy going to do with you? And what is your Daddy going to think when we get to San Francisco, and he finds out you still tell fibs? You're a big girl now, more than four."

Bill wouldn't understand it, any more than she did, but there it was. Modern parents, Bill said, didn't let things like this go on. Wasn't there child psychology, or some way to get at the bottom of it and find out why Penny lied? Bill called it that. Lies.

And it was no use for Anna Lea to talk of an over-active imagination, because Bill had been brought up in a strict household. He thought he was being liberal not to take his razor stop to Penny, or to lock her in the closet. He was going to, he said, if Penny wasn't cured by the time they got to San Francisco.

"It's up to you, Dear. You're the one who is with her all the time. I can't do a thing when I'm traveling like this," he had said. Bill was very young looking, but his jaw was firm, and if the truth had it, that was the real reason why

Anna Lea didn't know very much about children, but even she knew there should be a better way. Bill didn't know how big and heavy his hand was, and when Bill got angry he shouted.

"Who was the man at the door, Darling? Did he say?"

"Oh yes! That was Mr. Jefferson, and he is one of Daddy's friends." The name didn't ring any bell in Anna Lea's memory. Bill was sweet. He had so many friends. He was big and ambitious and forthright.

It wasn't so much that she was afraid of Bill as it was that she was afraid he would stop loving her. "A man's choice of a wife can't be overestimated," he said once. He was reading about a friend's divorce in the paper. "You take Stubby, here. It says incompatibility in the paper, but it goes a lot deeper than that. Stubby was with me over there, and he thought he had the world wrapped up in a little blond cocoon, but what does he find when he gets home? The cocoon turns out to be a butterfly." "Bill, that's clever!" "These war marriages! A guy gets all steamed up and heroic, and everything looks like hot stuff to him. He grabs off a last-minute marriage before he goes out to fight, and when he gets home he finds he's tied for life to some little ball of fluff, if not to a grindstone." "Millstone, Bill," corrected Anna Lea, and added in a very small voice, "ours was a war marriage." "Right," said Bill, "and did I know you? You had me so dazzled I didn't know what went on in that little head of yours." He had stood up then, to come and kiss her. "And you still do," he said, his eyes growing warm, his lips circling her face with kisses. "But I mean to find out." And when he did, what would he know? Would he still love her? Would he think she had character? He had thought so the day he had given her her watch—the day Penny was born. "Honey," he had said, sitting beside the hospital bed after it was all over and she had rested, "I brought you something." He had been down to send the good news telegrams. "It's a good watch, the best there is. Just like your little heart, ticking away all the time you were bringing my baby. I'm so glad it didn't stop," he said, and his face crumpled, his voice too. There were tears on her hands when he snapped the watch on her wrist.

Visiting Teachers' Messages�Our Pioneer Heritage Lesson 7�Building Activities President Amy Brown Lyman For Tuesday, April 6, 1948 Objective: To recognize the high standards in the building activities of the pioneers, which act as a stimulus for us today.

In looking back over Utah's first century, and in evaluating the accomplishments of the Latter-day Saints, their building activities loom large. Not only the number and variety of Latter-day Saint buildings is surprising, but also the style of architecture used, and the stability maintained. It has been the practice of Latter-day Saints to build well. In Nauvoo substantial buildings had been erected by them including the beautiful temple which was destroyed by fire and tornado and even before that time, a temple had been built by them in Kirtland, which is still standing. Naturally, the most famous Latter-day Saint buildings are located in Salt Lake City—the Temple, Tabernacle, and the Assembly Hall, most of the construction of which was done during the first half of the first century of the Church.

Two other attractive old structures are the Lion House and the Beehive House, and near them is the Eagle Gate, all greatly admired by traveling artists and architects. The old Salt Lake Theater, the Social Hall, and the Gardo House, all now gone, were also noted buildings. Scattered throughout the State are other old and noteworthy buildings, including three temples, located in Logan, Manti, and St. George, and a number of attractive tabernacles such as those found in Logan, Brigham City, Provo, St. George, and Coalville. Four additional temples have been erected, in Hawaii, Canada, Arizona, and Idaho Falls, making eight temples which are in use today. These, with the Kirtland and Nauvoo temples, make ten in all which have been built by the Church. Among the many other new and notable Church buildings are the Church Office Building and the Bishop's Building in Salt Lake City, the Joseph Smith Memorial Building, which stands on the Brigham Young University campus in Provo, with other substantial school and seminary buildings, two up-to-date gymnasiums located in Salt Lake and Ogden, and three large, modern hospitals located in Salt Lake, Ogden, and Idaho Falls. And now, in order to extend hospital facilities to remote regions of the Church, a number of smaller hospitals are being established in some of the smaller communities. We point with pride also to our many fine, commodious ward chapels, in connection with which attractive recreation halls have been erected. In addition to these public and Church buildings, hundreds of beautiful and comfortable homes have been built by the individual members of the Church. It can hardly be realized that in one single century a Church with such a small membership, and established faraway in a desert, could accomplish so much in the way of erecting so many permanent, dignified, massive, and architecturally admirable buildings. Latter-day Saints will continue to build magnificently, if they appreciate and build upon the foundation laid by their pioneer ancestors.
