"Give children a chance to be creative," plead experts in the field of child development. And sandboxes, they say, are wonderful—particularly if they are equipped with digging and molding utensils such as shovels and pails, and if the sand is kept moist so that it can be formed easily. A little foresight in providing end sections of egg or orange crates to sit on will keep the children dry. Small sawhorses, according to one instructor in child development, are a favorite among children. Be assured that no matter what the age of the youngster using them, he will be practical in finding many uses for this particular investment.

One Utah nursery school provides a good muscle-builder in the form of an old dead tree, debarked and with the thick sturdy limbs cut back to within two or three feet from the trunk. Resting on several of these branches in a semi-upright position gives safe climbing for any number of pretend games the children may concoct. Other variations of this idea include logs cut varying lengths and placed on end in the ground for climbing from one round to another.

Another favorite among preschoolers—wooden blocks—cost nothing. Scrap pieces in odd shapes and sizes are easily obtainable at planing mills or can be found at building sites. An ample number of these, sanded and stored in a plastic clothes basket, provide many hours of enjoyment edged with education. Another item to be had for the asking is an old steering wheel. Mount it on a pipe or pole anchored in the ground and watch how often Johnny goes for a ride in his new sports car, speed boat, or airplane. The possibilities are limitless, even though the budget may not be.

Add stakes for horseshoe pitching or a pole for tether ball, construct your own brand of jungle gym, or pour cement rounds for jumping stones. But there is one thing you must not do: You must not plan the play yard for parent appeal as is so often done. Give the children the things they want— toys that will develop them and their thinking—and give them a section of the yard to call their own. Hedge it off if you wish, but let them make play real child's play.
