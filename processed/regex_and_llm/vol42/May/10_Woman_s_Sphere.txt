These children had received the same home training, but the natural, individual dispositions of children in a family require different emphasis in the training. It seems natural and easy for some people to express their appreciation while others remain silent, whether because of shyness, diffidence, or ungratefulness is not evident. Some may have the impulse to express thanks but before the expression becomes a reality, other immediate duties push it aside stillborn. Yet an expression of appreciation is welcomed by everyone. No matter what one's worldly wealth, opportunities, and advantages, he will always welcome a sincere word of appreciation. Everyone needs to be built up and encouraged, for each person has moments of self-doubt and despondency, whose frequency depends upon changing factors. One may deliver a speech, or give a demonstration, after many hours of preparation. How disheartening if none commends the effort. Some in the audience may feel that the speaker has been in the public eye so long and been recognized for so many years, that he would not wish to be congratulated. Nothing could be Page 304 farther from the truth, for great people are simple people and subject to the same feelings as the rest of humanity. If it seems out of place to go forward and express appreciation, or if there is no opportunity then, there is always the tomorrow when one may phone or write. There is recalled a friendship between two men which began many years ago. It started by one writing to commend the other on a stand which he had taken on a public issue, unpopular at the time. For some years their only contacts were by further letters, as the one followed with admiration the activities of the second, from afar. The appreciative comments were encouraging to the public servant, and the contacts ripened into a_ personal friendship over the years. A mother in the home needs encouragement. Her heavy duties, albeit a joy to her, become monotonous. It is told how one mother, yearning for a word of appreciation in return for three well-cooked meals a day, finally served her family salad bowls filled with fresh grass clippings. In response to their consternation, she replied that she had decided they didn't know what they were eating, as they never said a word, but just ate and left. Thereafter that mother was showed appreciation in words. One husband who was told of this incident, since then comments "good grass" at the end of a particularly good meal. EDITORIAL While some men seem embarrassed to express appreciation in words, a wife is grateful for appreciation however it may be expressed. Recently a father was uplifted by being told that his daughter had said he was wonderful and very special. That remark was precious to him, for so often children do not express appreciation, although they may feel it deeply. A word of love and appreciation from a child to a mother will cause her heart to sing. Likewise, a mother should express love and appreciation to her children and not confine herself to words of correction, as is too often the case. In times of discouragement, children recall words of praise and are given a desire to do better. Latter-day Saint women receive a great training in unselfishness and independence just being the wives of husbands who obey the call to service in the Church. Likewise, a husband manifests unselfish devo- 305 tion to his family when he encourages his wife to accept a call to Church service which may take her away from him and their children at times. The appreciation of one for the other is a requisite. One should never fail to express appreciation to the great General Authorities, the physicians of souls. They have poured out to them daily the sorrows and unrighteous conduct of others to heal and to bind up, for Church members look upon their leaders as belonging to them —as their own. The appreciation of all Church members to them should be repeatedly expressed, and actions should support their words of appreciation. If Christ himself had to ask, "Where are the nine?" certainly each person would do well to remind himself constantly of the need to express appreciation to his fellow men and to his God. —M. C. S.
