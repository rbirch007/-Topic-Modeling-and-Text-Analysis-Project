Mother Elna P. Haymond Member, General Board of Relief Society What must this earthly home of divine destiny be, to become the celestial family of infinity (President J. Reuben Clark, Jr.)? One to wonder. It causes one to reflect on the home of his childhood and to ask the questions: Did my mother play her part well? Did her mother honor the Godgiven role of motherhood? Am I carrying on the great work they began? In contemplating the joys and responsibilities of motherhood, [ reflect upon the lives of my parents and grandparents and draw from many of their marvelous and wondrous teachings — teachings that cause me to evaluate the role of mother in the home and the farreaching effect her teachings, her actions, yes, and even her innermost thoughts have on the generations to come. In going through valuable family papers, I found a "Last Will and Testament" left to the family by my grandparents. It does not bequeath lands, stocks, bonds, and riches, but it does bequeath a burning testimony of the divinity of Jesus Christ and of the divine origin of his Church, with its saving principles which will lead to salvation and exaltation, if put into practice in the daily lives of their numerous posterity. President McKay, in a talk entitled "Pioneer Women," relates the story of a young mother having given birth to a baby girl during the long, hazardous handcart trek across 'Ta above statement causes the plains in 1856. Years after this baby was born, great criticism was being expressed by some of those present at a gathering, against the Authorities for allowing the handcart company to proceed to Salt Lake City. Over in the corner sat an old man, his face white with emotion, listening to criticism of his and his wife's own story as they crossed the plains. In dignity, and with great earnestness and sincerity, he said, "I ask you to stop this criticism. You are discussing a matter which you know nothing about. Was it a mistake? Yes, but I was in that company, and my wife was in it, too. We suffered beyond anything you can imagine, and many died of exposure and starvation, but did you ever hear a survivor utter a word of criticism? Not one of that company ever apostatized or left the Church, because every one of us came through with absolute knowledge that God lives, for we became acquainted with him in our extremlties. Was I, or the mother of my child, sorry we chose to come by handcart? No, neither then nor at any moment in our lives since. The price we paid to become acquainted with God was a privilege to pay." We are all aware of the many dangers, the periods of near starvation, severe, biting cold, sickness, death, and poverty experienced by the pioneers. During this time the women became mothers, and with Page 285 286 that God-given mother love and devotion, they gave themselves in every way for their children, and for the righteous preservation of the home. This soul-stirring story recalls to my mind a similar one of my Grandmother Doney who came in the Ellsworth Handcart Company in 1856. She, too, gave birth to a daughter during her arduous trip. Grandmother walked twenty miles the day her babe was born. After the birth, she was allowed to ride in one of the two covered wagons in the party. After the tenth day she carried the infant in her apron the rest of the way, fording streams, climbing hills, trudging the long sagebrush and barren waste ahead. She did not complain. She, too, found and knew God through her trials and hardships. Rightly can we say of the pioneer mothers: 'hey loved righteousness because it was right. They were peacemakers because they loved peace. 'They loved the poor, for they administered unto them. They remembered the widow, the orphan, and the aged, for they comforted them. They were pioneers in word and thought and deed. They fought the battles of life with the weapons of love, determination, and faith. They taught spirituality, love, harmony, obedience, and_ tolerance. They honored the Priesthood, taught and lived the celestial law of marriage. 'They prepared themselves for the "earthly home... To become the celestial family of infinity." These precious truths and _ precepts have become the family heritage of many Latter-day Saint families. HEN we reflect on the home of our childhood and the part our mother played, these things come to mind: Mother was gentle, calm, and serene. She taught us to put first things first. She placed purely social activities in their proper position, never sacrificing children, Church, or home to them. 'The stranger was never turned from the door. The hungry were fed, and the naked were clothed (Mt. 25:35-30). We all remember the Christmas and 'Thanksgiving baskets laden with food, which we, as children, took to the families who were less fortunate than we — the turkeys, chickens, potatoes, apples, and flour prepared and sent by mother to gladden the hearts and homes of many families. We mothers of today may well pay homage to the great concourse of mothers who have played their roles well. 'They have brought forth boys and girls, men and women, statesmen, generals, and Church leaders who can look back on their mothers' training and say: "She was the signal light, the beacon. She stood at the crossroads and showed me the way to go." Our beloved President David O. McKay has said: I emphasize the increasing power and influence of the Relief Society and of womankind in general, having one purpose in mind: That increased attention be given and more intensified effort put forth to maintain and preserve the dignity of motherhood (The Relief Society Magazine, December 1950, pp. 798-799). MOTHER His advice to Latter-day Saint women decries the practice of wives postponing, for worldly pleasure, lack of finances, or similar reasons, motherhood and the rearing of families. ""Wifehood is glorious, but motherhood is sublime." President McKay admonishes mothers to "have more religion in your homes, teach the gospel and honor the Priesthood." The late President George Albert Smith said of his mother: But my training was different. I was trained at the knee of a Latter-day Saint mother. One of the first things I can remember was when she took me by the hand and led me upstairs... I can remember it as if it were yesterday. She sat down by my little trundle bed and had me kneel in front of her. She folded my hands and took them in hers and taught me my first prayer. I will never forget it.... It is one of the loveliest memories I have in life, an angelic mother sitting down by my bedside and teaching me to pray That prayer opened for me the windows of heaven. From that day until now, while I have covered approximately a million miles in the world, every day and every night wherever I have been when I have gone to my bed or arisen from it I have felt I was close to my Heavenly Father (Conference Address of President Smith, October 1946, quoted from The Deseret News, Church Section, October 12, 1946, pp. 12, 20). The Prophet Joseph Smith's mother was quick to recognize that her son had been chosen of God as an instrument through whom his gospel was to be restored. She, his mother, expressed faith in him against all odds and persecution of the mobs. She was his constant and devoted champion. Her faith in him inspired his faith in himself at a time when the world was against him. Without his mother's un- 287 swerving faith, at a time when he so needed it, he would have felt much more keenly the opposition against him. President Joseph F. Smith paid tribute to his mother's love for him: It was life to me; it was strength; it was encouragement; it was love that begat love or liking in myself.... When I was fifteen years of age, and called to go to a foreign country to preach the gospel —or to learn how, and to learn it for myself — the strongest anchor that was fixed in my life, and that helped to hold my ambition and my desire steady, to bring me upon a level and keep me straight, was the love which I knew she had for me who bore me into this world. Only a little boy, not matured at all in - judgment, without the advantage of education, thrown in the midst of the greatest allurements and temptations that it was possible for any boy or man to be subjected to — and yet, whenever these temptations became most alluring and most tempting to me, the first thought that arose in my soul was this: Remember the love of your mother. Remember how she strove for your welfare. Remember how willing she was to sacrifice her life for your good. Remember what she taught you in your childhood. This feeling toward my mother became a defense, a barrier between me and temptation... (Gospel Doctrine, chapter XVI, page 394). HEN the Prophet Joseph Smith "turned the key" in behalf of the women of the Church, he placed upon them great responsibilities as mothers in Zion. As the Relief Society is to the women of the Church, so is the mother to the home. It symbolizes woman's place in God's plan. When Joseph Smith thus spoke under divine guidance, he gave to us the plan of Jesus Christ for women, for mothers. God placed on women in all ages the great and ennobling task of 288 motherhood. God's plan to give mortal bodies to his spirit children that they might progress along the paths of righteousness to exaltation, became dependent on mothers as co-workers with him. Jesus, while on the cross in his hour of greatest trial, gave as one of his last considerations his concern for his mother. Now there stood by the cross of Jesus his mother, and his mother's sister, Mary the wife of Cleophas, and Mary Magdalene. When Jesus therefore saw his mother, and the disciple standing by, whom he loved, he saith unto his mother, Woman, behold thy son! Then saith he to the disciple, Behold thy mother! And RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—MAY 1955 from that hour that disciple took her unto his own home (John 19:25-27). In closing, may I again quote President McKay: Motherhood is the one thing in all the world which most truly exemplifies the God-given virtues of creating and sacrificing the mother who, in compliance with eternal law, brings into the world an immortal spirit occupies first rank in the realm of creation (Gospel Ideals, page 456). God gave mothers this great role. It now becomes a challenge to us to make of "this earthly home of destiny' one that might become a "celestial family of infinity."
