On investigation, I found one minute wire in the light bulb was broken. Until the wire had broken, the bulb had given forth bright light—lighting a whole room, and even people outside could see the light and, perhaps, be guided by it. Are not our lives like that? If we are obedient to all the principles of the gospel, our light shines in our homes, in our Church communities, and even the people outside see our light and may be led to inquire into the source of it. But just one of the commandments, one small wire broken, and the light that should be within us, fails to shine, and those whom we could guide are lost. Let us then handle our "light bulbs" with care, that they may be kept intact, and our light so shine that those who watch us (and others do watch whether we are aware or not) may have the desire to walk with us after the pattern of the Savior. "i= a Meant Adventure Nell Murbarger XCEPT for half a dozen gooseberry bushes and a short row of pieplant, our homestead in the High Plains country was completely barren of fruit, either wild or domestic. Not that we hadn't planted fruit trees with dogged persistence and the hope that springs eternal; but either the jackrabbits girdled them, or the cattle and horses ate them or tramped them, or they were winter-killed. Asa result of these recurrent disasters, the only locally grown fruit I knew as a child was that provided by the thickets of wild plums, chokecherries, and buffalo berries, which grew in the sheltered "breaks" on the Indian reservation, half a day's horse-and-wagon drive to the east. Due to the pressure of haying and other work that fell in midsummer, Father never felt we could spare time for more than one fruitharvesting jaunt each season, and, with our entire year's supply of jam and jelly contingent upon the success of that single trip, it was necessary that the time element be calculated with a neat degree of precision. Should we go too early, the chokecherries might be ripe, but the later-maturing plums would almost certainly be hard and green. If we delayed too long, on the other hand, the plums would be ripe, but the best of the chokecherries would have fallen prey to other pickers and to the great flocks of birds that came to feed on them. But, finally, a date for the trip would be selected, and daybreak of Page 522 the chosen morning would find us hurrying excitedly with last minute preparations. Breakfast would be eaten by lamplight, and, while Father harnessed Mike and Lady to the farm wagon, Mother and I would be packing a hamper with boiled eggs, potato salad, fried chicken, and all the other savory morsels necessary to picnic lunches. A "stone" casserole of beans, baked the previous afternoon, would be reheated and wrapped in many thicknesses of newspapers and cloth—a sheathing which aided admirably in keeping the container and its contents warm through all the hours until noon. By the time the sun peeked over the horizon, we would be well on our way, the horses trotting briskly in the cool air, their hoofs ringing musically and striking little spurts of dust from the hard dirt trail that wound across the prairie. Long before arriving at the chokecherry breaks, we would cross the boundary of the Indian reservation and soon would be _ traveling through a strange region quite different from our homestead where the grassy, level plains spread away almost as far as the eye could reach. Here the soil was limey-white, rather than the deep black of the prairie loam, and there was but little grass. There were short, choppy gullies and box canyons, and, mising abruptly from the sun-swept alkaline flats, were many queerly eroded buttes and knolls.
