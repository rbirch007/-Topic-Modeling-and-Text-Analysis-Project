When contemplating the making, however, one is often discour- | aged, because it seems such a, tremendous undertaking. 'Therefore, it is well to remember that, like living one day at a time, we work on only one block at a time; as we learn from day to day, so we progress from block to block. The first block or patch must be perfect—in size, color arrangement, selection of long-wearing. Material, and beauty of design. As the finished blocks are put together, the beauty of the whole quilt grows. Pre-Washing Materials It is wise to wash all materials, including the back, before starting a quilt, as all may not be pre-shrunk. Also, if a piece is not colorfast, it ls good to know it and discard it for one that is. Making a Pattern It is very important when cutting the original patterns for pieced quilts to use materials that cannot stretch, such as cardboard, architect's linen, blotters, or fine sandpaper. Various sizes of embroidery hoops are excellent for drawing - circles and curves. A compass, also, may be used. With present patterns, instructions are usually given for seam allowance. However, if you plan to copy an old one, you must do a little figuring and make your own patterns. With a ruler, measure each patch in a particular block and draw an exact copy on a_ paper. With dotted lines on all sides, you allow one-quarter inch for seams. Cutting Patches and Tearing Blocks Patches for pieced quilts must always be cut with the pattern laid on the weave of the goods—never on the bias. When a diamond patch is cut, as for the star quilt, the pattern is laid with the two straight sides on the up and down weave, and the two bias sides are cut on the bias to meet the points of the two straight sides. With applique quilts, the muslin squares on which the designs will be appliqued should be torn and not cut. Since the average blocks are eighteen inches square, it is easy to take one-yard wide material and tear it in half the entire length of the planned quilt. Then the one-half yard strips should be nicked every eighteen inches on the selvage side, and the square torn apart. 'This method insures a perfectly even quilt when the finished squares are sewed together. Color Arrangement Concentrate ori a pleasing color arrangement for just one section, shading the dark into the light or the light into deeper tones. When the eight sections are sewed together, each shade will match exactly those of the next section, and when completed becomes a glorious sunburst of color. Copying an Applique Quilt To copy an applique quilt is very simple. Use heavy tracing paper or architect's linen, which is transparent and will not tear easily. Cover the entire block of applique, including the muslin background, and pin securely. 'Trace around each flower, leaf, bird, geometric design, or whatever it may be. Number your background, and write on your patterns which block they belong to. Remove and cut out the individual patterns, and with the original be- 107 fore you, copy them on a paper of the same size as the block. (I use eighteen-inch white shelf paper for the background, and cut the individual patterns out of colored construction paper. ) Following the original coloring, or changing it as you desire, cut leaves out of green, tulips of yellow or red, etc., and make a complete block on paper. Remember, when cutting the applique designs out of material to allow for seams, which are usually one-eighth of an inch for applique. When you are through, keep your paper patches for future ideas.
