Openshaw RENE Clifford felt more keenly | the loneliness of her oversized house, where she seemed to rattle around like a forsaken pea in a lonely and enormous pod, as she realized that not one of her children had this year remembered her birthday. For weeks in advance, on other such occasions, they would be inquiring, "What shall we get you, Mother? What do you need or want?" or, "Now, don't make any date for the twenty-third, the family will all be there to celebrate!" And all was anticipation, suspense, and excitement. But this year no one had even remembered or mentioned it. They're so busy living their own lives they don't have time to drop in or think of me, she reflected sadly. 'Then, exasperated at having given way to self-pity, she laid the shears she had used for clipping the long rose stems, hard on the garden bench. I hope I'll never grow into one of those depressing creatures who give up when their brood flies, instead of making a new life for themselves, she mused, her lips pressed firmly together. She picked up the roses she had just clipped, arranging them carefully in her basket-shaped vase, soothed by their beauty and the way the pink and cream petals blended into the yellow-green tones of the vase. She buried her face in the blooms, drinking in their intoxicating §perfume, grateful that she lived in Anzona where myriads of their princely varieties bloomed even in coldest winter, and where their fragrance seemed to rush out eagerly to meet her. She picked up the vase of roses and carried it inside to glorify the kitchen. But there she shook her head despairingly, for she found that the bouquet only tended to emphasize the shabbiness of the room. Everything in it had outlived its usefulness. She recalled the embarrassment she had experienced the Sunday before when some of her guests had followed her into the kitchen as she prepared refreshments. She hadn't anticipated this when she invited them for pie after church. She should at least have new linoleum, she reflected, but when could she ever find time or strength to uproot the present dilapidated one, scraping it bit by bit from the floor? And what assurance had she that the furnishings would not look even worse by contrast? The walls were crying for paint, and that shabby old stove! 'That rusty waterheater! Oh, everything! And no way to replenish anything at all. She smiled ruefully at the incongruousness of it, and glancing in the mirror as she did so, noticed again how like a skein of silver yarn her once dark hair was becoming, and the wrinkles were trying for a Page 101 102 foothold on her brow and about her wide, generous mouth. There was no denying it; her twenty-three years of teaching were telling their tale, and she did not like its ending. She had been defi nitely skidding downhill for weeks. She wondered dully whether she would be able to hold out the halfdozen and one seasons until her teacher's pension was, due. The house should be repaired before she had to quit her work. Where had her means gone, melting away? Schooling, sickness, operations, Althea's accident—all ways for money to vanish with six children. How grateful she was that she had had her teacher's certificate to fall back on. Her eyes brightened and her heart warmed as she recalled how well the children were doing, especially Jesse, who was a man of property. He could help... Me, the thought came timidly, if Laree was disposed. She remembered the joy she had experienced in providing for her own mother, and in sharing her means with her mother-in-law the short time she had lived, and was puzzled that her children could feel otherwise.
