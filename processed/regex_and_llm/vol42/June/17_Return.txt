Gardner Extension Service Home Management and Furnishings Specialist Utah State Agricultural College OOD ironing can give that finished, new look to clothes we all love to see. How our clothes are ironed has its effect on our attitude toward and feelings about ourselves. A spotlessly clean and smoothly ironed article of clothing causes self-respect to rise. A dress with a scorched collar, puckered seams and hemline, wrinkled sleeves, or crooked pleats is devastating to one's morale. Ease in ironing really starts when you buy your clothes and other articles. Give serious consideration to the amount of care needed to keep them looking new before buying. The complete removal of soil during the washing process and a thorough rinsing are essential to beautifully ironed articles. You will save ironing time if you are careful in handling your clothes during the washing and wringing process. Shake out the wrinkles as much as possible and smooth the collars, cuffs and plackets when you hang dresses and shirts on the line. Hang clothes and household articles straight and pin them on the clothes line at the strongest part to prevent their being stretched out of shape while drying. If similar items are hung together, time can be saved when sorting, folding, and ironing. When removing clothes from the line, fold them lightly before placing them in the basket to prevent extra wrinkling. Warm water for dampening lesPage 388 sens the time required for the clothes to stand before ironing. Use a bottle with a sprinkler top or a brush and pan of water for sprinkling. From two to three hours are generally needed for the moisture to become uniformly distributed and clothes ready to be ironed. The best amount of dampness for ironing must be determined by the person who is to do the ironing. Too much dampness lengthens the ironing period and makes seams, collars and plackets more difficult to iron. With too little dampness, it is difficult to iron out the wrinkles. Sprinkle clothes uniformly, then fold, don't roll them. Ironing is easier and takes less time if all the equipment needed is assembled and conveniently arranged before you start to iron. A square of asbestos placed at the wide end of the board to set the hot iron on when it is not in use is convenient and prevents much lifting of the iron. Place dampened clothes at convenient height, either by putting the clothes basket on a chair or by putting clothes in a basket on wheels. Have a clothes rack or table within arm's reach for the ironed clothes. A sponge or cloth in a pan of water and placed nearby will save steps when extra moisture is needed while ironing. An ironing board that is at a comfortable working height helps to prevent the development of fatigue. For comfort, select an ironing board and chair that will permit you to sit with knees beneath the board, feet flat on the floor, and back straight and adequately supported. Good posture whether sitting or standing is never more important than when ironing. It minimizes strain on your neck, arms, and back. Women who iron on a board that is too low have a tendency to apply more pressure than is necessary. It is the reaction of heat and steam that removes wrinkles, not pressure on the iron. This will make it possible for you to start ironing seconds after the iron begins to heat. Take time to smooth each article before you begin to iron. It will save you time, make wrinkle-free ironing easier, and make it possible for you to use long, smooth strokes. Always iron with the straight of the grain of the material and you will not pull the article being ironed out of shape. When ironing skirts and dresses, begin with the hem and iron upward. Move the article away from you as you iron each part to avoid mussing it. To prevent puckers, iron each part until it is dry. This is especially true where there is a double thickness of material as in collars, cuffs, hems, and seams. Iron parts that need to be ironed on both sides, first on the wrong side, then on the right, and you will not need to retouch the right side again. Gardner |: palmate soft, fluffy wool blankets are the pride and joy of all who own them. They are their loveliest when they are perfectly clean. Homemakers who protect them from heavy soil act wisely. Don't postpone washing your blankets for fear you might ruin them. Safe home laundering of blankets is possible, in almost any kind of a washer that permits you to observe the following precautions when washing them: 1. Remove deep stains if there are any. 2. Fill your washer with lukewarm water (100°F.). 3. If water is hard and soap is used, add a mild water softener until water feels slightly slippery to the touch. Run washer until softener is dissolved. 4. Add enough soap or syndet to make a standing suds two inches high. 5. Put the blanket into the suds, pushing all parts under the surface of the water. Let soak 15 to 20 minutes, turning it once or twice by hand. Do not turn your washer on. Studies have proved that agitation while woolen articles are in the wash water is the main cause of wool felting and shrinking. 6 Remove soapy water from blanket by spinning or putting the blanket through a loose wringer. 7. Fill washer with lukewarm (100°F) water. Add about half as much water softener to the rinse water as you did the wash water. Soak blanket five minutes, turning the blanket once or twice. Drain off water. Repeat the rinsing process. 8. Spin or hand squeeze the water from the blanket. Never twist blanket as a means of forcing the water out. G. If you have been working alone to this point, you will now need a helper to stretch the blanket. With each of you working on an opposite end or side, pull vigorously by moving the hands in unison across each end and down the sides. 10. Place blanket over two clotheslines that are a foot or more apart. Let about 12 inches hang over the outside edges of the two lines, with the bulk of the blanket hanging between them. A little breeze speeds the drying and fluffs the blanket. Rearrange blanket on clothesline two or three times as it dries so as to avoid deep markings of the lines. 11. Remove blanket from line when dried and brush with a metal toothed or a stiff nylon brush. 12. Press bindings with a warm iron. Woolens may now be moth-proofed during the washing process. The liquid known as "EQ-53" may be added to either the wash water or the rinse. Follow directions on the container for amount to use and when to add it to the wash water. Blankets, when dried, are odorless and remain mothproof until rewashed. Page 39]
