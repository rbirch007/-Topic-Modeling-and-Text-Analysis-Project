Birthday Congratulations President David O. McKay September 8, 1969 The women of the Church throughout the world, in all dominions where the stakes and missions have been organized, remember with love and gratitude the ninety-sixth birthday of President David - O. McKay—prophet, seer, and revelator. Over the years the example of his life and his teachings has been a beacon and an inspiration to the saints in every land. His steadfast devotion to the gospel as a way of life has illuminated the pathway for individuals, for the families, and for the Church. In a time of world-wide questioning, in an age of multiple directions, in an era of divided allegiance, President McKay has demonstrated spiritual strength, wise counsel, discerning judgment, an uplifted, though humble spirit, and a living faith that transcends life upon the earth, reaches back into the plans and purposes of pre-existence, and looks forward to the glorious eternal life. President McKay has said: "Faith is the eternal light that will dispel threatening clouds. Faith in man is the power that leads to brotherhood; faith in God, the ladder which men climb toward perfection." In his teachings President McKay has emphasized that reverence for God and sacred observances is characteristic of a great soul. Truly, our Prophet, in himself, is a testimony of the gospel as a way of eternal life. "My faith gives me the assurance that God is indeed my father and therefore I must have inherited his immortality." Spirituality, he has said, is the highest acquisition of the soul. As Relief Society women—homemakers, wives, and mothers—at this time of special remembrance—this day of heartfelt gratitude—we recall the inspired counsel that President McKay has given for us in our time and place. In his home and with his family he has exemplified sincerity and devotion. "The dearest possession a man has is his family. In the divine assurance that family ties may transcend the boundaries of death, and may continue through endless ages of eternity, I find supreme consolation and inspiration. When the union of loved ones bears the seal of the Holy Priesthood, it is as eternal as love, as everlasting as spirit." As Relief Society women, we express thankfulness to our Heavenly Father for giving us a Prophet in these latter days—who speaks and lives as directed and inspired by the Spirit of God.

@ "All roads lead to Rome!" Rome—the Eternal City, Rome—built on seven hills. Rome—the ancient city—the ultramodern city. Rome—the pagan city, the Christian city! Rome—the sculpture center of the world, the adopted city of Michelangelo.

We visited Venice, the fabulous city of the merchants which is slowly sinking into the sea, and bustling Naples, before Rome. We were stranded in Naples because of a general railroad strike. An unforgettable memory is the pandemonium of the bus station with peoples of many countries worrying, gesticulating and shouting. We were finally put aboard a chartered bus full of girl students late at night. We all slept fitfully after our strenuous day and, as a street clock struck midnight, we gratefully arrived in Rome and registered to take four comprehensive tours of the city in the next two days.

We surely fell into the category of the typical Americans who race through Europe spending only a few days when they should be spending at least a week in each place, or better still, a month. The first thing we recognized was the oldest thing in Rome, there long before Rome was built—the "golden" Tiber River, still serenely winding its way through the city as it has done for centuries. The legend-laden Tiber flows like molten gold toward the setting sun. We knew if it could speak it would tell us the history of Rome.

The French once offered to divert its channel so that its depths could be explored for treasures but the authorities of Rome, fearing an increase of malaria from such a procedure, would not give their consent. The Aqua Marcia built in 140 B.C. And restored in 1869, brings water from the Sabine Mountains, a distance of about fifty-six miles. Although the water is strongly impregnated with lime, it is pure and excellent.

The panoramic view of Rome from Pincian Hill helped to orient us, and the Borghese Gardens which are now turned over to the city for a public promenade, fascinated us as we saw avenues of white marble busts of the great men of Rome as they stood out against a background of dark pines and cypresses. The Vatican Palace, with over a thousand rooms, was of great interest as we toured its museums, the apartment of Pope Julius the Second, which was designed by Raphael, and a few main galleries filled with art treasures. The Vatican Library, with its 350,000 volumes and 50,000 manuscripts, was impressive.

The highlight of the tour was the visit to the Sistine Chapel. I will never forget what they said was Michelangelo's self-portrait as a grotesque bag hanging in the fresco of "The Last Judgment." It depicted his feeling at having to lie on his back on high scaffolds with paint dripping into his eyes for four years in the cold dampness of this chapel, as he painted this masterpiece of a ceiling depicting God, the Twelve Apostles, and other Biblical characters, while he longed to be working as a sculptor with his beloved marble.

We learned that the Vatican City has its own separate government and its coins and stamps, so we purchased stamps for cards. The second tour of the day included the summer residence of Cardinal Borghese which is now a museum featuring masterpieces by Bernini. Then we went to Janiculum Hill, the highest of the seven hills of Rome, and saw the great monument, one section of which shows Romulus and Remus being suckled by a she-wolf and we recalled the legend of the origin of Rome, founded 750 years before the birth of Christ.

We wandered around in the ruins of the Roman Forum and the old Roman Senate building and remembered that here was the cradle of government and that many laws we have today had their origin amid this group of ancient ruins. It is like a sunken square—holding columns, arches, pedestals, and crumbling walls. We stood in awe as memories of the past swept over us and we realized that we were seeing the spot which for centuries had been the heart of the Roman Empire, the stage on which the great dramas of Roman history were performed—the stabbing to the death of Julius Caesar and the resulting speech by Mark Antony being to us the best known. The great white marble monument to Victor Emmanuel, the Bismarck of Italy, is two hundred feet high. The columns, raised figures, and Victor Emmanuel, unifier of Italy in the center sitting astride a horse, fascinated us as did the Venetian Palace which had served as headquarters for Mussolini. We had glimpses of the palaces of Rome or "palazzos" and saw galleries and gardens which have been decorated with the most precious works of antiquity. In spite of great sheets of rain we got out of the bus to see the gorgeous fountain of Trevi (671 September 1969, of "Three Coins in a Fountain" fame) and threw our pennies into the water and made our wishes. Then, while the rain beat down, we entered the Church of St. Peter in Chains where we saw Michelangelo's great statue of "Moses" and marveled again at the wonder of the figures which Michelangelo could see in a block of marble and release with his skill. The next day we saw the Piazza Navona (Navona Square), an ancient square with an ornate fountain and an obelisk, one of a group brought from Egypt. Then we went into the Pantheon whose porch was built in 27 B.C. The Pantheon, with a dome in the center which lets the rain and sunlight in, dates back to the time of Hadrian in 125 A.D. It contains the first unsupported dome ever built and the model for all domes that have capped public buildings ever since. One of the most beautiful sights was the Marble Stadium, a stadium surrounded by white marble figures depicting some thirty sports. In the period of Rome's glory it was said that the number of Rome's statues equalled that of her inhabitants. We learned about the ancient well-built sewer system which is still used and the Golden Milestone erected by Augustus, from which distances were reckoned to every province of the Roman Empire, and roads were built which joined them in swift, sure communication. We saw the Arch of Titus, erected to commemorate the conquest of Jerusalem and saw, in imagination, long triumphal processions as they passed under this arch. Now the moment had come when we were to visit St. Peter's Basilica, the crowning work of Michelangelo, Antonio Sangallo the younger, Peruzzi Bramante, Raphael, Guido Reni, Domenichino, and many other geniuses. We were instructed not to stray or lose our guide or we could easily become lost in this huge structure. We kept together as we walked toward the reputed tomb of St. Peter which is directly under the great dome which Michelangelo planned. We were told three ordinary churches, spires and all, could be placed side by side beneath the dome alone, with two hundred feet to spare between the tops of the spires and the keystone of the arch. We drove along the Appian Way and saw many ruined sepulchres. It was the fashionable burial place of ancient Rome and the statues and other treasures have long since been stolen. We recalled reading "Quo Vadis" and the visits of Peter and Paul to Rome. It is said that from the Appian Way Paul first gained a view of Rome. We visited one of the forty-five groups of catacombs along the Appian Way. As we entered into their eerie darkness, there was a priest to light the way with a lamp into some of the maze of labyrinths in which the early Christians had found refuge from their persecutions and had also used as their burial grounds. I was impressed as he pointed out to us the Christian sign of a "fish" carved upon a wall.

When families are united in their work and play, work becomes more meaningful and leisure more enjoyable. A casual observer might say that this painting "La Grande Jatte" by Georges Seurat illustrates. A popular leisure-time activity - strolling in the park. However, as one looks and reflects, he may find that there is much more to it than that.

The reality of the subject has been subjected to stringent refining - not necessarily to improve it - but in order to yield its essence. The unessentials have been dropped away leaving only images of incisive clarity. A heat haze blurs the detail and gives a static quality. One feels as if he had captured a moment suspended in time, in which order reigns.

Georges Seurat lived at the time Impressionism was at its height. However, he believed that this system of painting was too haphazard, so he developed a systematic method of painting, which was highly intellectual, methodical, and almost scientific in approach. He carefully organized the space on his canvas by using horizontal, vertical, and diagonal lines in perfect balance.

Then with careful, almost artificial, precision, he placed the confetti-like, round spots of color next to each other, until his canvas was covered in such a way that the colors, being mixed in the eye of the observer, give a sensation of shimmering vibrancy. He called this method Divisionism. Others have called it Pointillism. This painting was first shown in 1886, and it marked the painter's arrival at a maturity in the use of the method he invented. His departure from the established way of painting, and from the Impressionism of his time, opened the way for later, so-called "Modern" art. Seurat must have worked hard to accomplish what he did at such an early age. (He died at the age of 32.) And, as is the case with many highly creative people, he found his recreation in his work. He said, "Art is harmony—harmony comes from placing, side-by-side, contrasting elements and similar elements in tone, in color, and in line."

(Joseph Smith, History of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, Vol. 1, p. 390 [called Documentary History of the Church; hereafter referred to as DHC].) Lesson Department On August 17, 1835, a general assembly of the Church met to agree on publishing a second compilation to be known as the Doctrine and Covenants. The priesthood members present testified to the truth of the revelations. Oliver Cowdery read an article entitled "Governments and Laws in General." The article was Approved and ordered printed in the volume.

In order to present the truth concerning the Church's belief about law, because of accusations that it did not uphold the law, it was felt necessary to prepare this article.

"OUR OPINION"

It was printed in the 1835 edition of the Doctrine and Covenants and appears as Section 134 in the current edition. The following preamble to the article is not a part of Section 134:

That our belief with regard to earthly governments and laws in general may not be misinterpreted nor misunderstood, we have thought proper to present at the close of this volume our Opinion concerning the same.

(D&C 134 introduction.)

This article is an "opinion" and not a revelation, and therefore does not hold the same place in the doctrines of the Church as do the revelations. (Hyrum M. Smith and Janne M. Sjodahl, Doctrine and Covenants Commentary, 1965, p. 852.)

On the other hand, Section 134 was presented to the Church assembled in 1835 and several times since that year as binding upon the members of the Church.

204-05; D&C 38:21-22.) GOD-ORDAINED GOVERNMENTS The Lord raised up men to formulate the Constitution of the United States that principles of freedom might be exercised in this promised land. Only in this kind of political climate could The Church of Jesus Christ be established in the last days. The land of America was kept hid from the nations that this land might indeed be a place where divine prophecies might be fulfilled. (Ezra Taft Benson, Conference Report, October 1961, p. 69.) The perfect form of government was instituted in the days of Adam, but later the sons of men rebelled against these principles of righteousness. (Moses 5:12-13.) Although Section 134 suggests that governments were instituted for the benefit of men, it is evident that all governments are not in this category. (D&C 134:1.) The Lord does not originate laws contrary to eternal principles such as free agency. As Elder Erastus Snow said, all forms of government are better than none at all in that order is maintained by governments though some of them may not be the best forms. (Journal of Discourses, Vol. 22, p. 151.) Nonetheless, God controls the nations though he is not the originator of those governments. (Brigham Young, Journal of Discourses, Vol. 6, p. 342.) 

STANDARD OF JUDGMENT The governments set up by men serve a purpose in the eternal plan by requiring men to govern their lives according to law. (D&C 76:111; 134:1.) The Prophet Joseph Smith said: He [God] will award judgment or mercy to all nations according to their several deserts, their means of obtaining intelligence, the laws by which they are governed, the facilities afforded them of obtaining correct information, and His inscrutable designs in relation to the human family.... (DHC, Vol. 4, p. 596.) This statement is recognized to be true when one realizes that God is the author of righteousness and that, woven in the various laws of the nations, there are laws which are also found in the gospel of Jesus Christ. (Stephen L Richards, Conference Report, October 1923, p. 47.) Also, there are many laws which are contrary to righteous principles. The framers of laws and governments are held accountable for their acts in relation to them, both in making and in administering them. (Smith and Sjodahl, Doctrine and Covenants Commentary, 1965, pp. 852-53; D&C 134:1, 6.) 

SECTION 134 By 1835, when Section 134 of the Doctrine and Covenants was prepared, the saints had suffered persecution, loss of property and lives, and their freedom of religious belief had been challenged. It is understandable why they would want their own beliefs concerning law and governments made known. This section might be divided into the following parts: (1) basic rights, (2) governments and officials, (3) religious societies and beliefs, and (4) noninterference in the rights of others.

BASIC RIGHTS After the statement that men are accountable to the governments under which they live, man's fundamental rights are stated in these words: We believe that no government can exist in peace, except such laws are framed and held inviolate as will secure to each individual the free exercise of conscience, the right and control of property and the protection of life. (D&C 134:2.) President David O. McKay has called attention to the fact that "there is innate in man a feeling that will rebel against tyranny." (Deseret News, Church Section, January 2, 1952, p. 3.) Men have come up from oppression to overthrow coercive governments. The Declaration of Independence and the Constitution of the United States are examples of the declared rights of men and the protection afforded. Where there is an infringement of these basic rights, the following belief is expressed: We believe that men should appeal to the civil law for redress of all wrongs and grievances, where personal abuse is inflicted or the right of property or character infringed, where such laws exist as will protect the same; but we believe that all men are justified in defending themselves, their friends, and Lesson Department property, and the government, from the unlawful assaults and encroachments of all persons in times of exigency, where immediate appeal cannot be made to the laws, and relief afforded. (D&C 134:11)

Class Discussion Under today's conditions, what can we do to help preserve the three basic rights mentioned in D&C 134:2?

GOVERNMENTS AND OFFICIALS Latter-day Saints during this dispensation, and especially during the time of Joseph Smith, have had many unfortunate experiences with government officials who had not upheld the constitutional rights of the saints. The following verse points out the obligation of civil magistrates upholding the law of the land in their decisions: We believe that all governments necessarily require civil officers and magistrates to enforce the laws of the same; and that such as will administer the law in equity and justice should be sought for and upheld by the voice of the people if a republic, or the will of the sovereign. We believe that all men are bound to sustain and uphold the respective governments in which they reside, while protected in their inherent and inalienable rights by the laws of such governments; and that sedition and rebellion are unbecoming every citizen thus protected, and should be punished accordingly; and that all governments have a right to enact such laws as in their own judgments are best calculated to secure the public interest; at the same time, however, holding sacred the freedom of conscience. (D&C 134:3, 5.) In an earlier revelation the saints were counseled to uphold wise and honest men in public office; otherwise, evil would result. (D&C 928:8-10.) It may not always be possible to determine between the wise and the unwise, but the Lord has given some standards by which to make decisions about principles or political issues. Laws which deny men their agency (D&C 98:4-8) and those which permit them to do evil should be changed. (Moro. 7:14-19.)

Class Discussion What measures should a people take to insure that their liberties are held inviolate? The history of the Church provides the answer. Despite the plunderings, mobbings, acts of rapine, and loss of property and lives, the saints followed the counsel expressed in D&C 134:11. By divine commandment the Prophet Joseph Smith appealed to the President of the United States and the Congress for redress of crimes committed against them in Missouri. (D&C 101:81-95; Joseph Fielding Smith, Essentials in Church History, pp. 287-94.) One is reminded that the Lord counseled his saints who had been driven from Jackson County, Missouri, in 1833 and 1834 that they were not to fight for the redemption of Zion, but that he would, in his time, fight their battles. (D&C 105:14-15.)

BREAKERS OF THE LAW Those who break the law should be punished according to the seriousness of the offense. Governments have the right to regulate their societies for the preservation of peace and order. Latter-day Saints are enjoined to bring offenders against the law to justice. (D&C 134:8.) They should also sustain the law enforcement arms of government that all might have justice.

Class Discussion: Do we as Latter-day Saint women by example encourage our children to uphold the duly appointed officers of the law?

RELIGIOUS SOCIETIES AND BELIEFS

Eleven years after Section 134 was written, the Prophet Joseph Smith wrote the Articles of Faith. The eleventh article is as follows:

"We claim the privilege of worshiping Almighty God according to the dictates of our own conscience, and allow all men the same privilege, let them worship how, where, or what they may."

This same statement of belief lies at the basis of what is contained in verse 4 of Section 134. The privilege of religious worship is the right of every man, provided it does not infringe upon the rights and liberties of others. Equally so, Latter-day Saints believe that government does not have the right to prescribe rules of worship, nor dictate forms for public or private devotion.

(D&C 134:4, 7.)

There should not be favoritism shown for one religion over another.

(D&C 134:9.)

The following principles were given by the First Presidency of the Church:

We declare that from principle and policy, we favor:
- The absolute separation of church and state;
- No domination of the state by the church;
- No church interference with the functions of the state;
- No state interference with the functions of the church, or with the free exercise of religion;
- The absolute freedom of the individual from the domination of ecclesiastical authority in political affairs;
- The equality of all churches before the law.

("An address, The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints to the World," Conference Report, April 1907, p. 14.)

EXCOMMUNICATION AND DISFELLOWSHIP

The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints maintains that religious societies have the right to deal with their members for disorderly conduct, but the punishment should not extend to taking their property or jeopardizing their lives.

The penalties for misconduct in the Church are excommunication and disfellowship.

(D&C 134:10.)

To be disfellowshiped means that the person remains a member of the Church, but he is limited in what he may do. He may attend meetings, but he may not participate in the activities of the Church.

Excommunication removes one from the records of the Church and thus he loses all of the privileges of Church membership.

There are three offenses for which one may be punished:
1. The breaking of the moral law,
2. Deliberate disobedience to the regulations of the Church,
3. Incorrect interpretation of doctrine when the correct teachings have been explained and the person is unwilling to accept them.

(John A. Widtsoe, Priesthood and Church Government, 1954, p. 209.)

Since then, the members of the Church have accepted these beliefs as binding upon themselves. To indicate the consistency and application of that early document to the present, the following statement from President David O. McKay is given:

To sum up this whole question: In these days of uncertainty and unrest, liberty-loving people's greatest responsibility and paramount duty is to preserve and proclaim the freedom of the individual, his relationship to Deity, and...
