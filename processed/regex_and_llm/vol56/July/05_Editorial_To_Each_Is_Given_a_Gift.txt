For all have not every gift given unto them; for there are many gifts, and to every man is given a gift by the Spirit of God. (D&C 46:11.) (Italics added.)

In his loving-kindness God allows each person to come into the world possessing in his soul embryo gifts to be used and developed as one's free agency determines. While man's mind is veiled from a remembrance of his pre-mortal life, some of the seeds of his accomplishments there are born into mortality with him.

The Father expects life to bring joy to his children. He prepares them for such joy by giving them the gifts with which to acquire it. One of the essential requirements of the Giver of Gifts is that the gift be used for the benefit of others "that all may be profited thereby." (D&C 46:12.) The use of these precious gifts selfishly, for personal gain alone is not the purpose for which they are given.

The intention of the Lord is that gifts be magnified for the building of the kingdom. The rewards of such increase are that the gifts will be added upon. No one, we are told, lives to his fullest potential. Always there are greater goals to conquer. Efforts to reach that potential are crowned with the increased desire to do more and progress further.

Gifts are to be sought. The prophets exhort the people to "covet earnestly the best gifts." (1 Cor. 12:31.) The blessings of spiritual growth are contingent upon asking in faith for one or more of the gifts. Proficiency is to be gained by personal effort and constant prayer to God to bless those efforts.

Gifts are many and varied. They encompass gifts of knowledge and understanding of gospel doctrine given by the Holy Ghost, talents in culture areas. Talents are given in music, literature, and art, in the realms of medicine, in aptitudes in educational pursuits, in the fields of engineering, and agriculture. The gifts are as diverse as the ability to love all of God's creations, a cheerful spirit, friendliness.

To some is given the gift of leadership, to others the gift of following, not blindly but understandingly. Some are the lawgivers, the wise and sagacious, others are the workers who uncover the secrets of nature. Some possess gifts that even they do not suspect until they are brought to light by obedience to commandments. Some gifts are never discovered because they are not sought.

There are those who resist the disciplines the development of a talent requires. They will not accept the responsibility bestowed with the gift to work toward its fulfillment. The potential for greatness is there but as a fallow field lies unused and dormant, idle in the sun, its potential for growth and production curtailed, so lie buried talents the owners fail to enlarge.

These are the unprofitable servants of the parable of the talents whose condemnation is greater than the loss of the talent. Their condemnation is greater than they would have received had they not been given the gift.

There are those who reject the gifts so willingly given by a loving Father, those whose self-afflicted blindness, whose spiritual unawareness prevent their accepting a glorious opportunity to use the gift in perfecting themselves and in helping build the kingdom of God.

Relief Society is a marvelous gift given by our Heavenly Father to his daughters, which some of the unseeing women of the Church have not yet accepted. The loss to themselves is great and to their families, husband, and children, but the loss to the building of the kingdom of God on earth is equally great. Failure to accept the God-given gifts results in failure to live most abundantly. Each gift is spiritual if used to elevate, to build, to make the world better. No gift is a small one. Its size is determined by the receiver. All gifts are given that each might contribute to the work of the Lord.

Marian Gardner Nielsen

When the three sisters, Gwen, Mary, and Maggie made the final decision, they knew there were still problems to be solved. Getting Gwen's three lively boys settled at Maggie's would entail planning and cajoling. Telephoning Mary's husband in New York about the change of plans was going to take some diplomacy. The real problem was getting their mother out of the hospital and then getting her comfortably and safely back home.

The plan was simple on the surface. Gwen would go to Cedar Mesa on the plane with their mother. Maggie would keep Gwen's children, and Mary would drive down by car first to get the house ready for the mother's homecoming.
 
Gwen was washing the boys' play clothes, mending overalls, and folding and packing their Sunday clothes. She had a batch of bread in the oven. The baby's bed would be taken to Maggie's later. The trip would probably take four or five days, so she must leave plenty of clothes for the children. Gwen hoped she could get a batch of cookies made to take to Maggie. It would be the hardest on Maggie—what with four youngsters of her own—and she did look a little peaked lately. Gwen had wanted to get a babysitter but Maggie had insisted on having the three little boys come to her place.

Maggie had begun to carry out necessary plans, too. She had made room for Gwen's baby bed in the family room. Hy could set up two army cots in the boys' room. She would move her own baby bed into their bedroom. That way, everyone would be comfortable. She would make stew for the boys and maybe set some jello for dessert. Even thinking about food made her squeamish. You'd think after four babies.

Mary was not looking forward to these eight hours of steady driving to Cedar Mesa. It had been nearly ten years since she had driven down that road—ten years of a successful career, and, she admitted wryly to herself, ten years of unsuccessful marriage.
