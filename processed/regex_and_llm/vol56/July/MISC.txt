Goal, Understanding, and Technique

Jean Larsen, Instructor in Child Development and Family Relationships at Brigham Young University

The answer to the question, "Should there be discipline?" comes through loud and clear, "Yes, there must be discipline!" But the what, the why, and the how of discipline and guidance are not so clearly spelled out. Not knowing what is expected on either end of the guidance situation provides some frustrating experiences for all concerned.

We recall the story: Bobby had been playing by the duck pond for several minutes when Father called for him to come in the house. Bobby approached his father slowly, looking down at muddy feet and wet pant legs. When Bobby's father saw the mud and the wet, he said in a stern voice, "Well, Bobby, how many times must you be told to stay out of the mud and water?"

Go get me a willow, maybe a 'willowing' will help you remember." Bobby was gone for several minutes before he slowly approached his father again—this time with his hands behind him. His father's voice was again stern as he said, "Well, I'm waiting, give me the willow." Bobby 494 drew both hands around and produced not a willow but several rocks in each hand. Then he said in a meek little voice, "T couldn't find a willow, but here are some rocks to throw at me."

GOAL As adults being responsible for guiding the child we are concerned about his behavior and ways of altering, controlling, or instigating certain behaviors. Guidance can become a battle of wills between adults and children. But, as in all battles, someone must be the victor. Should it be the adult at the expense of the child's curiosity, spontaneous expression, and self-esteem, or should the child be the winner because of his ability to display compulsive, negative, and violent behavior? Power struggles are a risk to any relationship and should be avoided. The goal of guidance is to let the child know what is expected of him, and then provide experiences which will help him to develop self-discipline or control from within.

UNDERSTANDING Why do children do what they do and when will they change? There is a reason why children do what they do. Sometimes adults don't take time or don't try to understand what is behind the behavior. How many times have you heard a parent say to a child, "Now stop crying or [ll really give you something to cry for," assuming that there is not a reason for the child to cry in the first place. Some behavior, although unacceptable to adults, is normal for the child of a given age and may very well pass on like the seasons. Temper tantrums are a normal reaction to frustration for the toddler age. Getting into things, finding out what possibility an object or material has for manipulation, is a force pushing the child from within. Most parents have the same concerns. "Why can't my child be quiet in Church?" or "Just when I want him to behave, he really acts up." A new situation or one that does not necessarily accommodate to the child usually produces child behavior that the adult does not approve of. The most difficult time in many households is the dinner hour. Mother may be intent on preparing the meal, younger children are tired and hungry, and Father and the older children need a listening ear after their day away from home. Tension runs high in such a situation.

Keys to Child Guidance Sometimes children do what they do because they do not know what is expected of them.

TECHNIQUE Guidance involves evaluation of and reaction to behaviors. Because each of us is an individual dealing with another unique individual, successful techniques will vary. There are some guidelines which may serve as a basis for dealing with behavior in general. First, punishment does not teach or show the child the type of behavior you want exemplified. It may cut off the behavior or traumatize the child about that which he should not do, but punishment does not establish what the child should do. Toddlers are notorious for using primitive social techniques. Usually, if another child has something they want they will bite, poke, pull or hit in order to gain possession. Many parents see this behavior and become very anxious. Usually, someone will suggest that the way to teach them not to bite is for you to bite them back. Will your biting the child teach him what you want him to do? Second, it is not wise to try to cut off a child's curiosity. This is like trying to stop his growth. Foster his asking questions and trying things out. If the behavior is caused because of a growing curiosity, then tolerate or channel, but be very selective when you stop this kind of behavior. Water play is one of the most soothing types of play a child of most young ages can engage in. However, water play and other related sensory types of play need to be channeled. A mother might respond, "No, I can't have you pouring water from container to container here on the kitchen floor, but outside (or a protected area inside) you may do so."

Third, try looking at things from the child's point of view. Rather than expecting the child to come all the way in an intolerable situation, see what you can do to make it easier for him. Sincere praise for just being himself can modify a child's behavior. Some adults feel compliments are not for children, but if there is a basis for them, they should be used. There was a five-year-old in the nursery school who seemed to be overflowing with good ideas. The teacher in the nursery group would frequently comment to this child, "John, you are clever!" One day a parent said to the teacher, "Why are you always telling that child he's clever? Don't you think he'll become egocentric?" The teacher replied that she felt children's behavior was greatly influenced by the way others reacted to them both by words and deeds. Sometime later John was watching a disgruntled repairman fixing a plugged up sink in the nursery school. As the repairman took off the gooseneck and found it filled with sand, it appeared he was going to explode. John, who had been watching the operation, looked up at the man's angry face and said, "My, you're clever to be able to fix that sink." The repairman's anger turned into a friendly smile as he said, "Well, I guess I am clever at that."

A fourth guide would be to feed into the child love, warmth, gentleness, and self-worth. Children learn what they live. A child who has received has something to give back. A fifth and concluding guide deals with letting the child know what is expected of him. Teach the child what to do rather than what not to do. Help the child see reasons, outcomes, and responsibilities. This is the opposite from threats and retaliations. Provide experiences whereby the child can develop inner control through understanding rather than control through fear.

Mary had been cutting and pasting at the art table. When she was nearly finished with her picture she noticed how easily her scissors snipped off small corners and pieces of the paper. Mary snipped and watched the small pieces flutter to the floor. Soon it looked like snow and soon the floor looked like a real storm had hit. The teacher came to Mary and said, "When Mr. Bob, our custodian, comes in to clean tonight, he'll wonder what has been happening in this room." Mary thought for a moment and then replied, "I'll need to get this mess cleaned up, would you hold the dustpan while I sweep?" Make teaching your discipline and take the initiative to discuss, explain, interpret, and demonstrate desirable behavior.

If your guidance relationship is constant and you are accepting of the fact that each experience will not be a successful one—it takes a long time to learn—eventually you will reach the goal of establishing self-worth and controls from within the child.

w Mama left a legacy of happy memories for her family.
Our friends and relatives young and old loved to come to her gracious, hospitable home.
Some who knew Mama best said it was because she was generous, kind, and witty,
with a penchant for making every guest feel special and welcome. To this I wholeheartedly agree,
but Mama had something else which drew people to her.
Mama was an excellent cook. She liked nothing better than to share her cuisine with those she loved.

Unfortunately for her daughters, Mama never used a recipe. All of her dishes were concocted by feel,
consistency, or taste. A pinch of this or a handful of that. Mama didn't even own measuring cups or spoons.
One of the happiest memories Mama left her children was our annual trip to "the Lakes."
Mama prepared for several days for this special event. I can close my eyes now and it seems as though
I can almost taste some of her special goodies. As the tantalizing aroma from Mama's preparatory baking
permeated the air, it appeared that it touched off the appetite alarms of all the youngsters in our neighborhood,
and those children who came to our kitchen door never went away hungry.

For the long drive to "the Lakes," our outing site, Mama usually brought a lunch of milk, cinnamon buns,
or slices of raisin and nut bread spread with freshly churned butter. She would pack them in the folds
of snowy white dish cloths to keep them fresh, since wax paper or plastic wrap had never been heard of
in our town. There was always just enough food to appease our hunger but not too much to spoil
our appetites for the picnic to follow.

And what a picnic! Our favorite food was Mama's baked beans. Their cooking was an all-day process.
How we loved the smell of the molasses and brown sugar, mustard, and the salt pork nestled cozily among
the brown beans. Combined, they produced a flavor that was delectable. Homemade root beer was a must.
This was an exciting change from milk or the ever-popular lemonade. Mama's root beer had a special tang.

We all loved the bottling process and as we put the corks into the bottle tops and tied them securely
to the bottle with string, we would beg Mama to put our mark on just one bottle reserved especially for each of us.
In our home town, most everyone had at least a small garden. Fresh vegetables were common fare.
Mama said they were good for us and so she used them in every possible way. Happily, Mama had the knack
for giving even the lowest variety a special flavor. Pickled cucumbers were our special favorites,
so Mama always included them as part of our picnic menu.

Wheat was indeed the staff of life in our home.
Mama used it in a variety of ways. From a breakfast food which she simmered for hours on the back
of our coal range, to hot cakes, baking powder biscuits, buns, scones, to wonderful crusty homemade bread.
We loved to spread fat slices of this bread with cream skimmed from the milk pan and flavored with sugar,
or covered and dripping with honey or syrup. Long loaves of what we now call French bread were our favorite
for an outing.

What is so delightful as homemade ice cream?

No picnic or outing was complete in our family without it. Since Mama never counted calories, her ice cream was rich and delicious. The best and sweetest part was that which some lucky individual would lick from the dasher. Mama made many flavors and each kind we would avow to be our favorite. Try a delicious peach variety for your family. A dripper cake, usually Mama's special fudge cake recipe, completed our lunch. Even though we had eaten our fill we could never resist this special treat. Papa always concluded our picnics by arising to his feet and proclaiming that this picnic was the best ever. His only complaint being that he had eaten too much. Why did she make everything taste so good? Then, one by one, each one of Mama's children in his own way would echo Papa's words.

Many of you, like Mama's children, have treasured memories. Many of you, as we do, long for "Mama's" cooking. Why not plan a family outing and try these recipes or those you and your family enjoyed and remember so well? Form the dough into loaves by placing it on a breadboard, pressing out the air and then rolling tightly to form an oblong loaf. Pinch the dough together at the ends to seal the edges. Roll in evaporated milk and then in sesame seeds. Place on greased cookie sheets as far apart as necessary to keep the loaves from touching each other when they rise. Let rise, uncovered, for 12 hours.

Bake at 375° for about 30 to 45 minutes or until bread reaches desired brownness. Remove bread from the oven, cut in big crusty pieces, and serve spread with butter.

Her Hobby Is Writing
@ Bertha Anderson Kleinman, Mesa, Arizona, has brought distinction to herself, to her State, and to the Church through her well-developed and useful hobby of writing.
Her verbal architecture encompasses many fields, from prose and poetry to hymn lyrics and pageants.
Her childhood home was situated on the rim of the north bench adjacent to City Creek Canyon in Salt Lake City. On this commanding location, her father erected a pretentious home for those times.
Here Bertha grew to womanhood among orchards of fruitful and beautiful trees, lilac hedges, shrubbery, and terraced lawns.
She watched the sun rise over the magnificent rugged Wasatch Range and watched it set in splendor over the great inland sea to the west.
The ever-changing beauties of the Rockies lent color and majesty to her poetic nature and influenced her writing of the glowing tributes to the great western empire of which she is so much a part.

About her writing, Sister Kleinman says:
"If anything that I have written can help someone else, that is my favorite."
Primary children love to sing her well-known "I Have Two Little Hands."

Soon after her marriage to Orson Kleinman, she moved to Arizona and became one of the most prominent literary women of that area.
She has been described as an outstanding citizen and Church member.
In 1957, the Governor of Arizona proclaimed November 3rd as Bertha Kleinman day in recognition of her many published works regarding the State of Arizona.
She earned the title "Poet Laureate of Arizona."
She received the David O. McKay Humanities award at commencement exercises at Brigham Young University in 1958, largely in honor of the pageant "The Message of the Ages," a literary memorial to 100 years of Mormonism, perhaps the best known of her works.
She updated the text in 1947 to include the pioneers coming into the valley. This pageant was produced every night for six weeks in the Tabernacle on Temple Square.

Sister Kleinman was honored by the King of Denmark for her writings about the land of her ancestry.
Her writings have appeared many times in The Relief Society Magazine, and in the other Church publications, and she has had a book of poetry published which is now sought after as a collector's item.
At the age of ninety-three, she continues to write lovely and moving poetry.

Her six children and many grandchildren and great-grandchildren revere her and are proud of the rich heritage of her written words. Always a devoted Relief Society worker, she was recently honored by the Relief Society of the East Mesa Stake for her untiring service and dedication.

523 Budget Decorating

From earliest history woman has endeavored to decorate, as elegantly as possible, the walls that surround herself and family. And of all the mural treatments developed through the years, tapestry has generally been rated as the most magnificent. Fantastically intricate and expensive, tapestries usually occupied a place of honor in the homes of royal and wealthy families and were prominently displayed at important public occasions.

While you may not wish to apprentice for twelve to fifteen years (the usual learning time) in order to weave one square yard of tapestry in about a year's time, you may still appreciate the effects of fabric on your wall. Modern technology can help you! If your decor is the now-popular Spanish, select a large terry bath towel—one of those richly colored, highly-sculptured ones—for a hallway or appropriate space under which you can place a heavy reed basket or old trunk, repainted. An attractive and most effective duo.

Kitchen terry towels hold promise, too, in creating a tapestry effect that may blend with early American or "modern kitchen." Fruit and flower designs are plentiful. Printed fabrics, padded and quilted, may be hung as tapestries, and panels of sample drapery—with large classic print design—may even be utilized in the traditional bedroom or living room by the mural-minded. Or you may sew twill tape loops to a foam-backed bath mat, as pictured, and hang it from an inexpensive curtain rod or dowel. Here the motif is repeated underneath in a made-at-home flower tree of matching blue and green plastic roses. These are held in place by a three-inch styrofoam ball on a one-half inch dowel and secured in plaster of Paris. The tub is plastic. This combination can be made in an hour for less than $5. Just right for the bathroom or kitchen.

Use your imagination, and let modern looms work wonders for your walls. You won't have a Flemish masterpiece, true. You won't even have a real tapestry (which can only be made by a unique and intricate weaving method) but your home can benefit nonetheless from the centuries'-old tapestry touch.

The Doctrine and Covenants Lesson 97—Eternal Marriage Elder Roy W. Doxey (Reading Assignment: Doctrine and Covenants, Sections 131:1-4; 132.)

Northern Hemisphere: First Meeting, October 1969 Southern Hemisphere: March 1970

OBJECTIVE: The Latter-day Saint woman should understand the truth that a marriage to endure after this life must be performed in a house of the Lord, and seek to attain. An eternal marriage.

INTRODUCTION In conversation with Elder William Clayton, the Prophet Joseph Smith told him what is now recorded in verses 1 through 4 of Section 131 of the Doctrine and Covenants pertaining to marriage. Section 132 is the revelation on celestial marriage, which includes plural marriage as well as eternal marriage. It is believed that the revelation on plural marriage was received as early as 1831, but Section 132 was committed to writing on July 12, 1843. (Joseph Smith, History of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, Vol. 5, p. xxxi [commonly called Documentary History of the Church; hereafter referred to as DHC].) Inasmuch as the Lord by revelation has withdrawn the practice of plural marriage, it is only necessary to mention the principle in this lesson. (The Discourses of Wilford Woodruff, pp. 213-18.) It is well to remember that this practice is proper only when the Lord commands it. (D&C 49:15-17; Joseph Fielding Smith, Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith, pp. 323-24; Jac. 2:27-30.) The penalty for practicing plural marriage by a member of the Church today is excommunication, the same as for others who commit adultery. (Joseph F. Smith, Gospel Doctrine, p. 280.) Exaltation in the celestial kingdom requires eternal marriage but does not require plural marriage. In the revelation regarding eternal marriage, the expression "if a man marry a wife," or a similar expression, is used. (D&C 132:15, 18, 19, 26, 41.) IN THE BEGINNING In the beginning God ordained that marriage should be a part of earth experience for his sons and daughters. The following reveals that truth and also that this principle was intended as a part of the plan of salvation: And again, verily I say unto you, that whoso forbiddeth to marry is not ordained of God, for marriage is ordained of God unto man. Wherefore, it is lawful that he should have one wife, and they twain shall be one flesh, and all this that the earth might answer the end of its creation: And that it might be filled with the measure of man, according to his creation before the world was made. (D&C 49:15-17.) In discussing the marriage of Adam and Eve, Elder Harold B. Lee, of the Council of the Twelve, referred to its perpetuity, as follows: Here [Genesis 2:18, 22-24] was a marriage performed by the Lord between two immortal beings, for until sin entered the world their bodies were not subject to death. He made them one, not merely for time, nor for any definite period; they were to be one throughout the eternal ages. If you were to say that because Adam and Eve transgressed and became subject to death that this eternal union was broken, then just remember that the purpose of the atonement by Jesus Christ was to restore that which was lost by the fall. Their restoration then to each other after the resurrection would not require a re-marriage, for death to them was not a divorce; it was only a temporary separation. Resurrection to immortality meant for them a reunion and an eternal bond never again to be severed. "For as in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive." (I Corinthians 15:22.) (Harold B. Lee, Youth and the Church, p. 128.) Lesson Department PURPOSE OF MARRIAGE There are some churches which maintain that celibacy is sanctioned by God. One of these churches in the days of the Prophet Joseph Smith was the Shaking Quakers. It was to a group of Latter-day Saint missionaries that the Lord revealed through the Prophet Joseph Smith, that marriage was ordained of him and that those who taught otherwise did not represent him. (D&C 49:15.) Marriage makes it possible for the earth to answer the end of its creation. Because man, as a premortal being, was designed to come to earth to work out his salvation, marriage was ordained to bring about this purpose. Consequently, the fundamental purpose of marriage is to beget children that the Lord's purposes may be fulfilled. (D&C 49:16-17.) MARRIAGE OUTSIDE OF THE CHURCH Should a Latter-day Saint young man or woman marry someone who is not a member of the Church? Leaders of the Church have counseled members to marry within the Church. (Discourses of Brigham Young, pp. 196-97; Joseph F. Smith, Gospel Doctrine, p. 279; David O. McKay, Gospel Ideals, p. 465.) Where the parties to a marriage are both strong in different faiths, the chances for happiness are greatly diminished. (John A. Widtsoe, Evidences and Reconciliations, pp. 237-38.) Sometimes young people feel that when they marry a nonmember, he or she will be able to convert him or her to the Church. July 1969 The chances for conversion and the consequent happiness in marriage are far greater if the conversion is made before marriage. (Harold B. Lee, Youth and the Church, p. 132.) The Lord has said that parents should teach their children correct principles to shape their lives for happiness. (D&C 68:25-28.) The mother has a wonderful opportunity in the home to impress upon her children the importance of marrying within the Church. Class Discussion How and when should a mother prepare her children for temple marriage? PURPOSE OF LIFE To work out one's salvation in this life and in the life to come is the paramount purpose of earth life. This means that the purpose of God in creating man is to grant to him, through obedience, the greatest measure of salvation—eternal life. (Moses 1:39.) As man is the child of God, so also man may, by obedience to law, become as the Father. (D&C 132:20-22.) The blessing of eternal life comes to those who fulfill all the laws and commandments. OBEDIENCE TO THE GOSPEL The gospel of Jesus Christ provides the way whereby the sons and daughters of God may return—to his presence in the celestial kingdom. Baptism is the gate to that kingdom. (D&C 76:50-53.) There are degrees in the celestial kingdom, therefore, members of his Church should seek for the highest degree. In the celestial glory there are three heavens or degrees; And in order to obtain the highest, a man must enter into this order or priesthood [meaning the new and everlasting covenant of marriage]; And if he does not, he cannot obtain it. He may enter into the other, but that is the end of his kingdom; he cannot have an increase. (D&C 131:1-4.) The new and everlasting covenant is the gospel of Jesus Christ. There are many covenants within the gospel and marriage for eternity is one of them. To be fully obedient to the gospel involves full discipleship. ETERNAL MARRIAGE After a couple have received the higher ordinances of the priesthood endowment in the temple, they are qualified to receive eternal marriage. Only by the keys of the priesthood held by the President of the Church, who delegates these powers to others, may this union be solemnized.

(D&C 132:7.) The blessings available to those who marry for eternity are these: they shall pass by the angels, and the gods, which are set there, to their exaltation and glory in all things, as hath been sealed upon their heads, which glory shall be a fulness and a continuation of the seeds forever and ever. (D&C 132:19.) ETERNAL INCREASE In Section 131 and in Section 132, verse 19, the Lord has told us that if faithful to the eternal marriage vows, the blessing of "increase," and "a continuation of the seeds forever and ever" will be received. The Prophet Joseph Smith gave the following information on this subject: Except a man and his wife enter into an everlasting covenant and be married for eternity, while in this probation, by the power and authority of the Holy Priesthood, they will cease to increase when they die; that is, they will not have any children after the resurrection. (DHC, Vol. 5, p. 391.) The children born to an exalted man and woman after the resurrection are spirit beings. The parents are tangible, resurrected persons referred to in the scriptures as having spiritual bodies; that is, with spirit element in their veins rather than blood as in mortal bodies. (D&C 88:27; Alma 11:45; Brigham Young, Discourses of Brigham Young, p. 374.) THE DEATHS When resurrected beings are exalted they receive "eternal lives," the power of eternal increase, which is the opposite of the "deaths," or the lack of such power. (D&C 132:24-25.) Only exalted beings will be married in the future life because they proved themselves while on the earth in being faithful to all things required of them. (D&C 132:12.) In the following verses the conditions in the world to come are explained, having reference to those who receive marriage for eternity and to others who did not subscribe to the full law: Therefore, if a man marry him a wife in the world, and he marry her not by me nor by my word, and he covenant with her so long as he is in the world and she with him, their covenant and marriage are not of force when they are dead, and when they are out of the world; therefore, they are not bound Lesson Department by any law when they are out of the world. Therefore, when they are out of the world they neither marry nor are given in marriage; but are appointed angels in heaven, which angels are ministering servants, to minister for those who are worthy of a far more, and an exceeding, and an eternal weight of glory. For these angels did not abide my law; therefore, they cannot be enlarged, but remain. Separately and singly, without exaltation, in their saved condition, to all eternity; and from henceforth are not gods, but are angels of God forever and ever. (D&C 132:15-17.) If there are some who may believe that their marriage is in effect beyond the grave, although not solemnized by God's priesthood in the temple, the following verse corrects this belief: And again, verily I say unto you, if a man marry a wife, and make a covenant with her for time and for all eternity, if that covenant is not by me or by my word, which is my law, and is not sealed by the Holy Spirit of promise, through him whom I have anointed and appointed unto this power, then it is not valid neither of force when they are out of the world, because they are not joined by me, saith the Lord, neither by my word; when they are out of the world it cannot be received there, because the angels and the gods are appointed there, by whom they cannot pass; they cannot, therefore, inherit my glory; for my house is a house of order, saith the Lord God. (D&C 132:18.) Class Discussion How does our desire to project our family relationship into eternity motivate our desire to attain the celestial kingdom? MARRIAGE IN THE RESURRECTION In reference to a question concerning marriage of a woman to several husbands, the Savior said that "in the resurrection they neither marry, nor are given in marriage, but are as the angels of God in heaven." (Matt. 22:30.) What did the Lord mean when he said that there was no marrying in the resurrection? He was speaking of the Sadducees who had rejected the gospel and its teachings about eternal marriage. As far as they were concerned, there was no marrying or giving in marriage in heaven. But if the persons are worthy to be married for eternity, and the opportunity does not come while on the earth, the opportunity for this blessing will come in the world ahead. The necessary ordinances may, for instance, be performed on the earth in a temple by proxy. What of Latter-day Saint daughters who have died without being sealed to a man in the temple? Elder Melvin J. Ballard gave this counsel: Now, then, what of your daughters who have died and have not been sealed to some man? Unless it is made known to you, let their case rest. They will make known to you the agreements and contracts they have mutually entered into. The sealing power shall be forever and ever with this Church, and provisions will be made for them. We cannot run faster than the Lord has provided the way. Their blessings and privileges will come to them in due time. In the meantime, they are safe. (Bryant S. Hinckley, Sermons and Missionary Services of Melvin J. Ballard, p. 260.) Elder Ballard also gives an answer to the same question concerning little children who, of course, did not receive the sealing ordinance of marriage: I lost a son six years of age, and I saw him a man in the spirit world after his death, and I saw how he had exercised his own freedom of choice and would obtain of his own will and volition a companionship, and in due time to him, and all those who are worthy of it, shall come all of the blessings and sealing privileges of the house of the Lord. Do not worry over it. They are safe; they are all right. (Ibid.) What about young men and women who die without the opportunity for marriage in the temple? Elder Harold B. BLESSINGS OF TEMPLE MARRIAGE
Experience has shown that those who marry in the temple generally find success in their marriage. Elder John A. Widtsoe gave the following nine reasons for a temple marriage:
1. It is the Lord's desire and will.

3. It tends to insure marital happiness.
4. It permits the association of husband-and-wife for time and for all eternity.

8. It places the family under the protection of the power of the priesthood.
9. It provides a God-like destiny for human beings.

(John A. Widtsoe, Evidences and Reconciliations, pp. 231-36.)

HAPPY HOMES
The performance of the marriage ceremony in the temple is not sufficient in itself to make a happy home. President David O. McKay has given six elements which lie at the basis of true home building:
1. Let us substitute the present tendency toward a low view of marriage with the lofty view which God gives.
2. Teach the young of both sexes in the responsibilities and ideals of marriage so that they may realize that marriage involves obligation and is not an arrangement to be terminated at pleasure.
3. Instruct young girls in the fundamental arts of housekeeping, so that when responsibilities of wifehood come, they may be free from the difficulties and perplexities which arise from ignorance and inexperience.
4. Marriages should be solemnized, as far as possible, in the house of God.
5. Keep religion in home life.
6. Teach the young that the foundations of a happy home are laid before even the bride and bridegroom kneel at the marriage altar.

Message 1—In the Service of Your Fellow Men

Alice Colton Smith
Northern Hemisphere: First Meeting, October 1969
Southern Hemisphere: March 1970

OBJECTIVE:
To realize that when we serve our fellow beings, we are in the service of our God.

A few years ago a group was discussing the meaning of service. One said, "I'm a business man. Everything I manufacture and sell is to help others have a better life." A teacher said, "My whole day is devoted to helping others develop." A mother said, "I gave birth and thus life to a number of people and I serve them." A lawyer said, "A public servant is well named. An elected official serves others."

Centuries before this, King Benjamin called all his people together to teach them the will of God. In his memorable address he said:
"And behold, I tell you these things that ye may learn wisdom; that ye may learn that when ye are in the service of your fellow beings ye are only in the service of your God."

(Mosiah 2:17.)

What was he saying about service in this verse and the following verses of his speech? Did he mean what the businessman, the teacher, the lawyer, even the mother did, or was he speaking of something beyond the general goodness that people may extend to one another and for which they often receive remuneration and other earthly rewards?

Jesus said:
"Not every one that saith unto me, Lord, Lord, shall enter into the kingdom of heaven; but he that doeth the will of my Father who is in heaven. Many will say to me in that day: Lord, Lord, have we not prophesied in thy name, and in thy name have cast out devils, and in thy name done many wonderful works? (italics added.)
And then will I profess unto them: I never knew you....(3 Ne. 14:21-23.)

And again Jesus said, "For if ye love them which love you, what reward have ye? Do not even the publicans the same?"

(Matt. 5:46.)

In these teachings is God not calling us to a more profound meaning of loving service? Is it enough just to take care of others we love? Do not sinners do the same?

In his early instructions to the Relief Society, Joseph Smith said that one of the purposes of the society was in "Searching after objects of charity and in administering to their wants."

(History of Relief Society, 1842-1966, p. 18.)

Moreover, Jesus taught it was to be done so "That thine alms may be in secret."

(Matt. 6:4.)

Again Joseph Smith said, "Relief Society is not only to relieve the poor, but to save souls."

(Joseph Smith, History of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, Vol. 5, p. 25 [commonly called Documentary History of the Church; hereafter referred to as DHC}.)

Our service to our fellow men, then, has special meanings.

It is to be a part of serving God with our "whole souls" (Mosiah 2:21), to follow Jesus into total Lesson Department righteousness, and do the will of my [our] Father which is in heaven." (Matt. 7:21.) We should love and serve our enemies. We should search out those in need. We are not only to attend to their physical needs but to help them find the kingdom of God, to keep his commandments "that they might rejoice and be filled with love towards God and all men." (Mosiah 2:4.) We are to help them lift their lives to greater goodness, to help them achieve salvation. As we serve our fellow men, then, with these added dimensions, we are in the service of our God helping to bring about his multiple, great purposes.

INTRODUCTION
Have any of us ever known a period of time when our leaders have not counseled, advised, and even urged us to prepare our homes and families that we might be self-sustaining in a time of need? The answer would certainly have to be in the negative. Why, then, are many of us so reluctant or so indifferent, or even so negligent that we fail to heed this counsel? Is it because we have lulled ourselves into a state of false security by using excuses�excuses which 547 July 1969 we can usually find if we are looking for them?

WHAT ARE OUR EXCUSES?
1. That we are not converted. Can we justify ourselves when we accept some of the teachings of our leaders and reject others?
2. That we hear the counsel but we are not ready to heed it. Can we attain salvation without working for it?
3. That there is no immediate need. Illness, accident, strikes, destructive weather (earthquakes, tornadoes, etc.), and other disasters could occur at any time.
4. That we are protected by the Church storehouses. These supplies are only designed to meet needs that go beyond what the family is able to do for itself.
5. That we have inadequate storage space. If there is a will, there is a way to overcome this problem.

UNREST AND OPPOSITION

The attitude of unrest and opposition to established institutions, referred to by President Clark and so apparent throughout the world today, is giving parents, educators, and civic leaders much concern. They are considering probable causes and searching for remedies. Existing conditions convince us that no easy answer is available and that no generalization is adequate for the complex condition involving so many aspects.

President Clark states: Some may say this rebellion shows merely a wish for greater liberty; others may consider it a destructive demand for complete license. As frequently happens, neither extreme view seems wholly right. The truth probably lies somewhere between.

A list of probable factors related to this attitude of revolt follows. President Clark has suggested the first three, others have been added, and still others may be suggested. Our concern in this discussion is to discover some areas where the home may be abdicating important responsibilities.

Question for Discussion

In your opinion, which of the following probable factors seem most likely to be responsible for this personal revolt? Discuss as time permits.

1. A general desire for more liberty.
2. A destructive demand for complete license—freedom from restraint.
3. A personal weakness which may cause an individual to want others to become as he is or to share his point of view.
4. A lack of constructive objectives that give purpose to life.
5. A desire to be different.
6. A wish to escape responsibility.
7. A basic lack of understanding of the value of law and order and of the need for personal restraint even in situations where changes and dissent may be justified.
8. A sincere mistrust by many idealistic young people of what they consider to be the hypocrisy and artificiality of accepted standards.

Revolt for this reason, if recognized for the idealism it is and properly channeled, can be transformed into a force for good.

RESTRAINTS—WHY?

Certain familiar virtues, when developed and exercised, make us good citizens and dependable, upright individuals. These virtues are basic to constructive private life and have been found to be essential to an orderly society as a whole. We may enumerate some of them in such affirmative terms as honesty, respect for life and property of others, or in the negatively expressed commandments of the Decalogue, such as, thou shalt not kill, nor steal, nor commit adultery.

President Clark states: These virtues are named because, looking at mankind as a whole and at the Christian nations particularly, these characteristics seem to be basic to their civilization. They seem to be equally basic to any form of orderly society of which we can conceive. For if every person murdered and robbed and lied and loafed and caroused, if there were an indiscriminate association of the sexes, no ordered society could exist; and without such society we have scant ground for hoping and no ground for believing that humanity could escape becoming merely animal.

How much restraint and what type of conventions needed for an orderly social unit to persist may depend upon (1) the persons or group of people affected, (2) the state of their civilization and righteousness. INHERENT IN ALL CREATION

Speaking of universal laws, President Clark states: The great laws governing the universe—physical, intellectual, and spiritual—are coexistent with that universe as known to us; they are self-executory, that is, they enforce themselves; no one needs to enforce them; they operate independently of man's will, consent, or desire; they operate impartially and universally; every creature, human or animal, every other created thing, is subject to the applicable law. It follows that whether we wish or whether we do not wish restraint, yet restraint is inherent in all creation.

Efforts to ignore restraint, or to evade it, or to destroy it, will meet with failure.

The physical world is fully controlled by law; if it were not, it could not and would not exist. Heat, light, electricity, what we call matter—air, water, earth—all are subject to and controlled by law. Our bodies being matter are equally subject to great physical laws from none of which is there any escape.
