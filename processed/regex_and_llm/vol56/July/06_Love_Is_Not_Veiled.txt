James had not objected then. As she became successful in her work and spent more time at it, James became resentful. He objected when she spent the summer in Europe for the firm. Why couldn't he accept her success? The house was fine and the cook served better meals than Mary could prepare. She had tried to get close to James again, to get their marriage on an even keel. But now it was a cool neutrality.

She was not sure that she wanted to go back to New York—especially since she had stayed in Gwen's comfortably furnished home with its confusion and love. And in the shabby, bulging home where Maggie was a lovely and beloved wife and mother. It was not until Mary came to the Colorado River that she threw off her unhappy mood and began to notice the spectacular scenery.

She still missed the narrow, colorful, trestle bridge which had spanned the river when she was a girl, however the sheer red cliffs were still blazing in the sun, their dark shadows darker still because of the intense sun-glare of their lacquered surfaces. She slowed down to catch glimpses of her favorite places. Here was the famous fault that her geology professor had told the class about. There was the little meadow where the five quaking aspens grew—and there they were! But they were taller, with their golden leaves twinkling in the sunlight.

The long table in the dining room was still scarred where the table tennis net had been screwed on too tightly.
The big, sunny kitchen still echoed with the sounds of pulling honey candy, and sitting down to Mother's cooked dinners.

She had stepped quietly into the corner bedroom and had felt the presence of her father and mother still there. It was a good house. It was a good feeling Mary had as she went through it. She touched the familiar pictures, ran her fingers along the faded and broken piano keys. Even in the daylight she switched on the lights in the living room to see if she could catch the rainbows in the prisms of the chandelier the whole family loved.

She still wondered why her father had never bought a dishwasher. It would have saved her mother so many hours.

Well, if she was going to get to the Merc before closing time, she'd better go. She decided to walk the three blocks to the store. She was stiff from driving and the walk would do her good. Besides, she might see some of her old friends or her mother's friends. Too, she would have to pass Aunt Irene's. Perhaps she would be working among her roses where she was usually seen. It had been difficult to explain to James' friends that all her "Aunts" were really not Aunts at all, but were the older women she knew and loved. Everyone called these dear friends "Aunts." Call them "Mrs Galbraith" or "Mrs Taylor"? That was too formal for Cedar Mesa folk. It had sounded a little provincial, later, when she thought about it. But home again at Cedar Mesa it sounded all right to talk about "Aunt Irene" and "Aunt Hattie" and all the rest. They seemed closer to you, that way.

Aunt Irene wasn't in her roses when Mary passed her house. She stopped at the telephone office and asked to have her mother's phone connected. She walked into the Merc, took a basket and selected the groceries she would need. The clerks were all strangers, the few people in the store busy with their own buying. When she paid the bill she felt distinctly let down. Old Home Town, indeed!

On the corner near home she met Ruth Murphy—Ruth Layman she was now—the tall, weary-looking woman informed her. But Ruth was in a hurry to get supper for her large family so she spent little time talking.

"Tam so glad your mother is coming home. We've all missed her—and worried about her."

"She had a rough time, Ruth, but the doctor says she will be all right. She's coming in a day or so. I'll stay until she is up and around and can do for herself."

"Well, we must get the old crowd together while you're here. I'll give you a ring. See you later, Mary."

Ruth hurried down the street to her small trailer house. The feeling of not belonging persisted. Mary was tired. She wanted to get right home, take a shower and get into bed. She hoped she wouldn't see anyone else now; she didn't feel like talking.

She saw Johnny Jones, her father's old friend, hobble out of his lot to intercept her, but she pretended not to see him and hurried along faster. But the minute she stepped into the house, serenity returned and Mary became receptive to the enfolding arms of the dear home. She picked up an open book from the table, Collection of Great Poems, and leafed through its pages. It was a favorite—book of her mother's and the penned comments on the page margins brought the beloved voice into focus reading to the children in the evenings. When she finally closed the volume and placed it almost reverently on the table, she was ready for bed.

She was going to plan her work for the next day, but dropped off to sleep immediately.

It was a bright, hushed morning when she awoke. For a moment she felt as she did years before when she was waking in that same room—a sense of anticipation, a surge of interest, a feeling of expectancy. A knock on the door brought her to her feet.

"I'm coming," she called. "Just a minute."

"Oh, Mary, it's just your old Aunt Gen. [I'm already in.] She peered around the door. "How good to see you. You haven't—changed a bit still the prettiest girl in town!"

She planted a quick kiss on Mary's surprised face.

"So your mother's coming home tomorrow! We've been keeping in touch with Gwen. It will be so wonderful to have her back. You know," Aunt Gen spoke tenderly, "you look more like her than any of the girls."

Quick tears stung Mary's eyes as she held the frail woman close.

"That's the nicest thing anyone has said to me for years. Come and sit down, Aunt Gen, and 510 what do you have under that napkin?"

"Just a few cinnamon rolls for your breakfast. I knew you wouldn't stop to cook anything."

She surveyed Mary with bright black eyes.

"You're too thin. And your eyes tell me you are not happy. What's the matter?"

Mary laughed.

"Oh, Aunt Gen, you always did say my eyes gave me away. You're wrong. I'm—fine. And now that I'm home, I'm beginning to be myself again."

Aunt Gen noticed the slight hesitation, but she changed the subject.

"You see, I have on my working apron. I am going to stay and help you get this house all ready for your mother's return. Oh, I know," she shook her head, "you think you can do it alone. Well, maybe you can, but we're going to help."

"We?"

"Certainly... Irene, Lydie, Addie, and Natalie. Do you think we girls would let your mother down? After sixty years of being as close as we are? Not for one minute! Now eat your rolls, child."

Mary sat down and helplessly watched Aunt Gen take over—as she always did.

The cinnamon rolls were warm and sweet and she ate the three without even once thinking of her diet. Aunt Irene brought a casserole and greeted Mary with love and a few happy tears. Aunt Lydie and Aunt Addie came in talking a blue streak... Not expecting Mary to answer. Aunt Natalie was late, but her warm greeting and her gentle manner made Mary realize why her mother loved Natalie so much.

It was a lovely day. Actually, Mary didn't accomplish much. Every time she started to dust or vacuum or wash windows one of the aunts firmly pushed her aside. Mostly, she listened to the familiar stories, laughed at the same anecdotes, and hummed the same nostalgic songs.

Just as the sun stepped behind the Bear's Ears and flung its painted shafts at the drowsy sky, the women finished their work. Gwen called later that night to say that the plane would land at the airport at Cedar Mesa at four-thirty the next afternoon. Mary called Aunt Irene who said she would tell the others.

"Don't cook your roast, Mary, save it for another day. I am bringing chicken a la Maryland that your mother likes so well."

"But, Aunt Irene."

"Now, Mary! I'm in a hurry. Goodbye."

The next morning Mary rose early. She went out to the strawberry patch and picked a bowl of the plump sweet berries. As she washed them she thought how her mother liked them and how proud she was of her berry—patch.

All morning neighbors came in and greeted her with familiar affection. They were all so happy that her mother was coming home. Shy Mrs. Sanchez, who helped with the ironing, brought a basket of peppers—huge green bell peppers, round orange-red pimentos, long, pale-green mild ones and slim, dark-green hot ones. Becky brought a big apple pie, freshly baked.

The little schoolteacher's wife, whom her mother had befriended, slipped her choice casserole into the Love Is Not Veiled oven. Aunt Gladys brought a loaf of her stone ground brown bread. There were bottles of sour cherry jelly, Doris' pretty pickles, a ground raisin cake, and a tin of rolls almost ready for the oven. Mary could only thank the people, her voice choking on inadequate words. How hospitable they were! How could she have forgotten her mother's lovely friends? Yes, and hers, too. How could she have cast aside these cherished customs?

The first thing her mother saw when she got home from the airport were the fresh flowers. The house was full of them: roses in tall vases; late velvety clematis in a low crystal bowl; zinnias, asters, petunias, and a pottery jar filled with brown-hearted sunflowers. "The Garden Club! How they must have worked to get them all here, fresh and smiling," her mother exclaimed. She wandered slowly through the shiny rooms smelling the fragrant bouquets, noting gleaming windows and crisp curtains. At last she settled down in her own room.

"How good to be home. How wonderful to be in my own room again. God has been good to me. He sent love to make me happy. He sent comfort to me when I was sorrowful. Children and all of my friends to sustain me in my illness. I am so grateful for his sharing these 'things with me."

She looked up at Mary, her face tender, meaningful.

"Sharing is a part of life, Mary. You have to be able to receive by knowing how to give." 

Mary nodded. She took Gwen's hand and the two sisters left, their mother's understanding smile blessing them.

It was late that night that Mary made up her mind. She put in the call to James in New York, her hand unsteady on the receiver. When he answered she could hardly bring herself to speak. Then, humbly, sincerely, with sweet tones of love, she said, "James, do you really want me to come home?"

And the sudden radiance in her face lighted up the whole room.
