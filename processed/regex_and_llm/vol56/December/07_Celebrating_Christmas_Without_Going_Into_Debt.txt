Celebrating Christmas Without Going Into Debt, Mary E. Bergman
In the early years of our marriage the available money for Christmas expenditures was scarce. After listening to friends expound on the pitfalls of charge accounts that would take months to pay off, lavish gifts to children that seldom pleased them for more than a few weeks, and fancy holiday parties, I realized that it would be most advantageous to fall back on my own resources.

Often, the innocence of childhood can be changed into selfishness by parents who are unable to withstand the onslaught of hardsell tactics employed at Christmas time. Our gifts to Cathy that year totaled an expenditure of fifty-nine cents, but required hours of time and preparation. She was delighted, and her enjoyment of our homemade toys led me to believe that I had stumbled on a precious secret.

Throughout the years, my children and I have learned together the satisfaction of counters filled with delicious home-baked goodies. Amid the pleasant aromas in our home, we stitched "God Bless Our Home" samplers for each adult on our list, and we made stuffed toys of all varieties for the children, and a boxful to be sent to the Primary Children's Hospital.

For the entire holiday season, we become absorbed in sewing, gluing, baking, molding salt clay, painting, building, and so on. Mark, being a normal energetic boy, doesn't always adapt to these quiet occupations. One afternoon while Cathy and I were engrossed in a project, I gave Mark a bowl of vegetables to chop for homemade soup that was bubbling on the stove. He was fascinated by the process of putting raw materials into the kettle and afterward being able to savor the taste of the resulting soup.

Thereafter he became the official soup maker, starting from scratch and doing the entire project himself. We poured this soup into decorated quart jars to be delivered to various people.
