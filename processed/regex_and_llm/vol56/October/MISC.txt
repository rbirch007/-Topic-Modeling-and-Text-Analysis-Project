When one gives evidence as a witness in court, he may either make a solemn declaration or affirmation, or give evidence under oath. In the latter case, if he does not tell the truth, he may be charged with perjury. When one bears testimony to the truth of the gospel, he also makes a solemn declaration or affirmation, but the source of his knowledge is not the same as that upon which he depends as a witness in court. It may have little or nothing to do with the five senses. In fact, a real testimony of the gospel, a testimony that God is real and personal and that Jesus of Nazareth is His Son, results from revelation and is a serious and sacred matter.

In a court of law, hearsay, or secondhand evidence is not admissible regardless of the integrity of the witness submitting it. He must confine his evidence to what he personally knows to be true. While all members of the Church should respect, support, and heed the teachings of the Authorities of the Church, no one should accept a statement and base his testimony upon it, no matter who makes it, until he has, under mature examination, found it to be true and worth—

724

While; then his logical deductions may be confirmed by the spirit of revelation to his spirit because real conversion must come from within. By the same token, members of the Church should not become disillusioned and throw the Church overboard because they have not satisfied themselves concerning every principle of the gospel. All blessings from God come as a result of obedience to the laws upon which they are predicated. This rule, or law, applies to the acquisition of a testimony of the gospel or any related subject. Any accountable person can gain a testimony if he will obey the laws upon which the receipt of such knowledge is predicated. But here, as elsewhere, there are certain prerequisites, which may be listed roughly as follows:

1. The seeker must desire to know the truth of the gospel or whatever is involved in his quest.
2. He must study and learn everything possible with relation to the matter involved. In John 5:39 we read: "Search the scriptures; for in them ye think ye have eternal life." And we are directed in the Doctrine and Covenants 1:37 to "Search these commandments."
3. The applicant must practice the principles and truths which he learns and bring his life into harmony with them. The Savior said: "My doctrine is not mine, but His that sent me. If any man will do His will, he shall know of the doctrine, whether it be of God, or whether I speak of myself." (John 7:16, 17.)
4. He must constantly pray to the Father and exercise faith that the truth will be manifested by revelation through the Holy Ghost. Moroni tells us that by the power of the Holy Ghost ye may know the truth of all things. (Moroni 10:3-5.) When the Apostle Paul answered Christ's question as to who he, the Savior, was, he had the spirit of revelation, and Jesus said to him: "Blessed art thou, Simon Barjona: for flesh and blood hath not revealed it unto thee, but my Father which is in heaven." (Matt. 16:17.) This bears out the claim that direct and personal revelation is available to men if they will learn the necessary prerequisites and conform their lives thereto.

In speaking or writing to members of the Relief Society one is addressing the builders of the homes of our communities and of the Church. Every home has a spirit, and that spirit will depend in large measure upon the testimony which the mother has of the divinity of the work in which we are engaged. This emphasizes the importance of her gaining a testimony and under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit guide and direct the affairs of the home and the lives of her children, having in mind always the relationship which exists between the Lord and His children.

In other words, the mother must have great faith—faith in God, faith in herself and in her companion, and faith in the ultimate triumph of righteousness—a faith which ventures beyond knowledge and by which she may be able to meet the tests of practical life and religious experience. She will be guided by intellectual as well as moral imperatives. A mother who has a testimony will, therefore, live a righteous life—one which the children can emulate with safety; the kind of life which is inspired and directed by the Holy Ghost, whose companionship she will constantly seek.

Of course, the knowledge which the mother gains in her search for a testimony will be most helpful in her presiding over her home. But that knowledge must be observed and incorporated into the daily life of those concerned. He who knows the precepts and neglects to obey them is like one who lights a candle in the darkness and then closes his eyes.

In this age when there are so many broken homes, it is about time to stop building mere houses and begin rebuilding broken homes, and laying foundations upon which future enduring homes may be established. When grandfather and grandmother took each other for better or for worse, when things got worse, as they usually did, the two of them worked it out for the better.

Their marriage lasted sixty years or longer because they knew the darkest hour would be only sixty minutes long. Too many today enter marriage with the door of escape wide open and that door leads to the divorce court. The extended freedom which is enjoyed today requires a stabilizing self-control and constancy just as branches demand roots. In this complicated age of restlessness and speed, we are all oppressed at times with tension and strain. Tension needs tenacity, and strain requires steadfastness. A testimony of the gospel supplies spiritual underpinning without which human life leads but to shipwreck. The most effective and lasting impressions of life are made in the home where it is presided over by parents who know and bear testimony that God is real and personal, that Jesus is the Christ, and that the gospel has been restored. Testimony is like an arrow shot from a long bow. The force of it depends upon the strength of the hand that draws it. (Bacon.) All the hopes, the horizons, the motives that have been given to men depend upon faith in God. We need the type of courage, a product of faith, which will enable us to turn triumphantly toward the east at nightfall, with the calm assurance that dawn will surely follow dusk. Said Frank Crane: Conventional charity drops pennies in the beggar's cup, carries bread to the starving, distributes clothing to the naked; real charity, which is another name for love, sets about removing the conditions that make beggary, starvation, and nakedness. Conventional charity plays "Lady Bountiful;" love tries to establish such laws as shall give employment to all so that they need no bounty. Conventional charity piously accepts things as they are, and helps the unfortunate; active love goes to the legislature and changes things. Charity may swat the fly; love impels us to remove the dung-heaps that breed flies. Charity gives quinine in the malarial tropics; love drains the swamps. Charity sends surgeons and ambulances and trained nurses to the war; brotherly love struggles to secure that internationalism that will prevent war. Charity assumes evil institutions and customs to be a part of Divine Providence, and tearfully works away at taking care of the wreckage; love regards injustice everywhere, custom-buttressed and respectable or not, as the work of the devil, and vigorously attacks it. The best part of the human race does not want help, nor favor, nor charity; it wants a fair chance and a square deal.

Relief Society workers should be unwilling to choose between flaming fanaticism and sentimental surrender. Let personal conviction and generous tolerance meet and merge. The spirit of testimony makes one tolerant of the weaknesses and mistakes of others as it emphasizes the brotherhood of man.

A book on tolerance by Phillips Brooks mentions six brands of tolerance:

First, the tolerance of pure indifference. We may be tolerant because we do not care, because the issue at stake does not concern us.
Second, the tolerance of policy. We may be tolerant because we think we would lose more than we gain by fighting the man or the measure.
Third, the tolerance of helplessness. We may be tolerant because we realize that the enemy holds the field and that resistance will be futile.
Fourth, the tolerance of pure respect for man. We may be tolerant because we respect even a man's right to think wrong, because we agree with Voltaire when he wrote to Helvétius, "I disapprove of what you say, but I will defend to the death your right to say it."
Fifth, the tolerance of spiritual sympathy. We may be tolerant because we feel a spiritual comradeship with the man whose purpose is fine even if his proposition is false.
Sixth, the tolerance of an enlarged view of truth. We may be tolerant because we have come to realize that truth is larger than any one man's concept of it, even if we are the one man in question.

The first three kinds of tolerance are mean; the last three are magnificent.

"True tolerance," says Brooks, "consists in the love of truth and the love of man, each brought to its perfection and living in perfect harmony with one another."

But this is no easy achievement. In this age of selfishness and greed, of birth control and barrenness, of easy divorce and broken homes and juvenile delinquency, in this age of cheap amusements, idleness, and lack of discipline, it is well to search for basic values, to call attention to the fact that the home is the nation's most fundamental institution and that mothers are the first professors in that character-building school.

Let us teach the young women of the land that womanhood is glorified by motherhood. A woman who has a testimony of the gospel will meet the demands of home building with poise and dignity. Motherhood is the inscription on the gate through which the spirits pass to enter the realm of mortal men, and it will be woman's password for admittance into the highest realms of glory.

The Son of God hallowed and glorified that inscription when he bowed through that gate and called a mortal woman, "Mother."

How important it is that every mother have a testimony of him and cooperate with him in the continuing work of redemption. President McKay said:

Motherhood is the one thing in all the world which most truly exemplifies the God-given virtues of creating and sacrificing. Though it carries the woman close to the brink of death, motherhood also leads her into the very realm of the fountains of life and makes her co-partner with the Creator in bestowing upon eternal spirits mortal life.

Artists may make new visions real; poets express thoughts never known before or dress old ones in a more becoming garb; engineers may transform deserts into bounteous fields and fill them with prosperous towns and thriving villages; scientists may discover new elements, and by various combinations thereof create means contributive either to progress or destruction— all these are in a measure revealers of unknown things: but the mother who, in compliance with eternal law, brings into the world an immortal spirit occupies first rank in the realm of creation. (Gospel Ideals, page 656.)

@ Mama said I shouldn't really count too strongly on being the fairy princess in our fourth grade spring operetta, but I knew I would get the part. After all, hadn't I prayed for it with all the faith in my nine-year-old heart? Just because I had straight brown hair and freckles wasn't any reason why I couldn't be the princess, was it? Mama didn't say anything about the straight hair and freckles, of course, but she did remind me gently that there were eleven other girls in my class who would like to be the princess. Ever since we had all been in the first grade, she had always been the princess. Lauramae didn't know how to spell Mississippi, but no one cared, least of all. It would take practically a miracle for me to get the part when Lauramae was around, but miracles had happened before, hadn't they? My Sunday School teacher said they had when a person's faith was strong enough.

Playing that part seemed to me to be the most beautiful thing that could happen in a young girl's life, especially one who had read every book of 741 October 1969 fairy tales in the school library. For months I had worked to get the best grades in our class hoping that Miss Ellerbee would take that into consideration when she assigned the parts. And then, of course, there was the praying. Heavenly Father couldn't possibly ignore a small person who had such a big want that for weeks she had washed dishes and done her piano practicing without being prompted, and who didn't hit her brother even when he took her doll buggy and used it to mix mud in for a fort he was building.

On the day Miss Ellerbee assigned the parts I sat with my eyes closed and my hands clasped, knowing she would say, "Jenny Bennett will be our princess," and then I would give the little squeal of surprise I had been practicing. But what Miss Ellerbee said was "The part of the fairy princess will be played by Lauramae Van Skiver." Lauramae didn't even scream with pleasure as any other girl in the class would have done. She had known all along she would be the fairy princess. The squeal I had prepared died in my throat and almost choked me. I wished that it had. What good was it living in a world where people who didn't even care got the best things and where your most fervent prayers went unanswered? I felt betrayed.

Next time I went to Sunday School, I thought, I would tell my teacher a thing or two. No, on second thought I wouldn't, because I wasn't going to go any more. Feeling dismally wicked, I half listened as Miss Ellerbee 742 went on to read the rest of the parts. "Daffodil will be played by Ruby Raymond." I waited, not eagerly any more but still with a little curiosity for my name to be called. After all, the flowers had pretty costumes, too, although not as beautiful as the fairy princess. It would be nice to be the rose.

"Rose," said Miss Ellerbee, "will be played by Merna Bell." Freddy Hopkins will play the part of Spider." She went on down through the list of flowers, bugs, and birds until she came to the last part. "The part of Toad will be played by Jenny Bennett." I hadn't even thought about being the toad. It was beyond consideration. I hardly heard what else Miss Ellerbee said—something about the toad being just about the most important part in the play since it was the toad who hopped around protecting the lovely flowers from the bugs and birds, and it was the toad to whom the fairy princess said at the end, "Despite your homely appearance, oh, Toad, you have a truly beautiful spirit."

I didn't care what Miss Ellerbee said. If you were a toad, who would notice how beautiful your spirit was? On the school bus going home Lauramae Van Skiver sat. By me. "I wish I could be a toad sometime," she said. I knew she was just being nice. Lauramae was always nice. That's why everyone liked her. I didn't like her. I didn't like her at all, with her fat curls and long lashes and nice ways. "You'd make a terrible toad," I said.

"Maybe I would," she said, "but it would be fun to try. I always have to be the princess." It just wasn't fair. I wanted to cry right there on the bus, but I managed to wait until it stopped to let my two brothers and me off. Instead of going into the house, I crawled into a clump of lilac bushes and wept. I wished that a bolt of lightning would hit me, and then when I was all fixed up in a white dress in my coffin people would say, "Poor little thing. See how pretty she looks—not like a toad—at all."

I was so involved with my fantasy, which made me weep even harder, that I scarcely noticed Mama crawling under the lilacs beside me. "Tom told me you went in here," she said. "I thought maybe something was wrong." "I have to be the toad," I blurted, fresh tears flooding my face. Mama patted my back and rubbed my head until I stopped bawling. "I wanted so much to be the princess," I sobbed. "And Lauramae would rather be the toad. She really would, Mama." Mama thought about it. "Maybe I could speak to Miss Ellerbee," she said.

"Oh, Mama," I cried hopefully, "would you? Please would you?" "Let's think about it," Mama said. "What does the princess do?" "Well," I said, rubbing my nose with my forearm, "she sings five songs all by herself."

VISITING TEACHER MESSAGE � Truths to Live By (Correlated With the Family Home Evening Manual, 1968-69) Message 4—Labor for That Which Perisheth Not Alice Colton Smith Northern Hemisphere: First Meeting, January 1970 Southern Hemisphere: June 1970 OBJECTIVE: To remember that throughout one's life one makes eternal choices. When you were little did you read the fairy tales, the legends, and the myths that told of kings and queens, of castles, palaces, and feasts? Did you then dress up in your mother's old dress, make a crown of paper or flowers, and play that you were a queen? Didn't that seem the epitome of all that you could ever want?

Was some of that make-believe stardust still clinging to you when you read Mosiah 28:10 and discovered that "... King Mosiah had no one to confer the kingdom upon, for there was not any of his sons who would accept of the kingdom."? Then as you read further, didn't the castles and palaces fade as you saw the king's sons, mighty in faith, humble in spirit, persuasive in teaching the gospel, bring many souls into the kingdom of God? You remembered, "And now, if your joy will be great with one soul that you have brought unto me into the kingdom of my Father, how great will be your joy if you should bring many souls unto me!" (D&C 18:16.) Have you ever secretly sympathized with the very rich ruler who having kept the commandments of Moses came to Jesus and asked, "... Good Master, what shall I do to inherit eternal life?" (Luke 18:18.) Jesus answered him, "... Yet lackest thou one thing: sell all thou hast, and distribute unto the poor, and thou shalt have treasure in heaven: and come, follow me." (Luke 18:22.) And when Jesus saw that he could not, he said, "... How hardly shall they that have riches enter into the kingdom of God!" (Luke 18:24.) Have you thought how difficult it would be to sell all you have and give to the poor? The great prophet-leader Nephi had a devoted, faithful brother Jacob who, while on earth, lived worthy to see his Redeemer. This younger prophet taught, "Wherefore, do not spend money for that which is of no worth, nor your labor for that which cannot satisfy... Feast upon that which perisheth not, neither can be corrupted." (2 Nephi 9:51.) 783 October 1969 By joining the Church we have made the first great choice in this life of "that which perisheth not." Each day, each week we make new choices that will determine our future joy or sorrow. Let us consider some of these important decisions:

1. Do we expand our capacity for love and compassion by continually searching to find new ways to help others?
2. Do we follow Jacob's teaching and spend our money for that which perisheth not?
3. Do we labor to increase our knowledge, our faith, and our understanding of others?
4. Do we grow in self-discipline so that our anger is schooled, our charity increased, our peaceful efforts extended, our enemies forgiven and loved?
5. Do we "... Visit the fatherless and widows in their affliction, and... Keep... [ourselves] unspotted from the world."? (James 1:27.)
6. Do we plan and live our lives so that we will endure to the end?

The good we choose perisheth not.

Northern Hemisphere: Second Meeting, January 1970
Southern Hemisphere: June 1970

OBJECTIVE: To point out the woman's a in planning and managing a successful family budget.

INTRODUCTION A woman's role as a successful homemaker means more than being a loving companion to her husband and a devoted mother to their children; it also means working with her husband in the management of a well-ordered home. It means helping to establish a pattern of living conducive to the best interests of the family members who dwell in that home. This pattern of living recognizes the goals and objectives that are vital to its well-being. A wise homemaker knows that these goals involve careful planning and management of the family resources. She also knows that this can be best accomplished through the use of a family budget worked out to fit their own special needs, interests, and desires.

Unless we who are the homemakers are aware of our responsibility in the planning and managing of a successful family budget, we will not be able to solve the financial problems which inevitably arise in every household. We need to develop in our family members an understanding of their individual responsibilities by letting them share in the planning and formulating of the budget. We need to show them what it costs the family to live and why it is necessary to set up a plan of procedure whereby we can reach our goals and objectives.

OUR PLAN OF PROCEDURE In the homemaking discussion printed in The Relief Society Magazine, October 1966, we learned that in planning any budget, certain procedures usually apply to most families:

1. Estimating family income. With our husbands we should make an appraisal of the family income, which will include take-home pay from wages, regular salary, commissions, and part-time earnings, as well as any revenue from pensions, rental income, dividends, interest, or any additional income which might be forthcoming.
2. Calculating fixed expenditures. We should make our family members aware that there are certain expenditures which must be paid regularly—monthly, quarterly, semiannually, or annually—and that these are fixed or obligatory expenses which take precedence over others. The list of fixed expenditures should include tithing and other Church contributions, taxes, housing payments, insurance, and debt.
3. Planning daily expenditures, including incidentals. As housewives we should know that day-to-day living costs offer many opportunities to practice wise spending. Such expenditures include those made for food, household services, clothing, automobile and other transportation, medical care, personal allowances, family recreation and cultural development, and such variables as differing localities, climates, and nationalities might add to or take from the list.
4. Planning savings and emergency funds. We must be aware that every family should make some provision for saving in their financial plan if they are to achieve a sense of security. When possible, it should be large enough to cover both emergency funds and long-term goals such as education, missions, vacations, retirement, and other desired attainments. We can help our families to see the wisdom of establishing a regular savings plan, even though it means the renouncing of some immediate desires. Many budget experts warn against trying to be too detailed and penny-counting, a practice which tends to discourage long-term persistence. A too-complicated budget is quickly abandoned.

CONCLUSION Wise planning and conscientious effort on the part of all members of the family are required to formulate and carry out a successful family budget plan, but it is the homemakers—the mothers and wives—who really put the plan into action. We can be sure that our husbands will be enthusiastic in their approval and that the other family members will be willing to cooperate. Once it is accomplished, it will bring the income, expenditures, and savings of the family into balance and will result in blessings of lasting benefit and fulfillment to all its members.
