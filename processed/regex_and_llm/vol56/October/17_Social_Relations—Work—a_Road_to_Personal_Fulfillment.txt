Immortality and Eternal Life Lesson 
Work—a Road to Personal Fulfillment
Alberta H. Christensen

(Reference: Immortality and Eternal Life, Vol. 2, Selections from the Writings and Messages of President J. Reuben Clark, Jr., Melchizedek Priesthood Course of Study, 1969-70, Lesson 23.)

Northern Hemisphere: Third Meeting, January 1970
Southern Hemisphere: June 1970

To emphasize the value of work in meeting the requirements of happiness and of divine admonition.

Having been introduced to President J. Reuben Clark, Jr. (lesson 1, The Relief Society 786 Magazine, July 1967), we know of his general attitude toward work. We know that he believed in the value of work, that he was introduced to responsibility at an early age and that he liked to work. This lesson develops the idea that to work is following divine admonition, that it is a practical necessity, a privilege, and that it may become an enjoyable activity. The general theme applies to all—man, woman, and child.

Discussion in this lesson, however, will generally be focused toward work within the home and will focus upon the mother's specific position in introducing the children to one of the great challenges of life.

Do you remember the story of "The Little Red Hen"? One version narrates that:

One day the Little Red Hen was scratching in the farmyard when she found a grain of wheat. She asked, "Who will help me plant this wheat?" 
"Not I," said the duck.
"Not I," said the cat.
"Not I," said the dog.

"All right, I will do it myself," said the Little Red Hen. Then she asked successively:
"Who will help me cut the wheat?"
"Who will help me knead and bake the bread?"

To each of these questions she received the same negative reply. When the loaf was baked, savory, and brown, she then asked,
"Who will help me eat the bread?" 
"T will," said the duck.
"T will," said the cat.
"T will," said the dog.

The story concludes with this rhyme:
Lazy folks must hungry go
For they would not help me sow,
Neither would they help me reap
They had rather rest and sleep. 

All alone I baked the bread, 
Lazy folks should not be fed. October 1969

Question for Brief Discussion
How would you define work? An international dictionary defines work as "exertion or effort directed to produce or accomplish something." With this definition in mind, the duck, the cat, and the dog were justified in considering the tasks required to produce the loaf of bread as work. And in the story they did not want to work.

This story implies that eating the loaf was less effort or, at least, a more desirable activity. This nursery story obviously tries to teach children the idea that work is important and that each one should assume some responsibility in producing the good things of life.

What mother has not observed, with amazement, the amount of exuberant energy expended by her children playing jump-rope or the various running and hiding games, who, only a few moments earlier, said they were too tired to sweep out the garage or to wash the dishes?

Questions
1. What makes the difference?
2. How important is the mother's attitude in forming the children's reaction to "work" activities?
3. How will knowing that willingness to work and liking to work are characteristics of maturity help a mother teach her children to work?
4. How will this influence her own attitude toward what might otherwise be considered irksome, monotonous housework? 
How may a mother teach children to see that opportunities for service to others and for personal satisfactions are endless for those who are willing to work?

On 

Eden's gate was no sooner closed than man's earthly work began, for Adam was now introduced to the idea that he must work all the days of his earthly life. Among other things spoken by the Lord to Adam are these words:
By the sweat of thy face shalt thou eat bread, until thou shalt return unto the ground....(Moses 4:25.)

Since thorns and thistles were to be natural obstacles, the implication is that providing the necessities of life from the earth would not be an easy task. Nor has it been throughout the centuries.

In calling our attention to this statement of the Lord, President Clark states, "I believe that we are here to work, and I believe there is no escape from it." (Immortality and Eternal Life, Vol. 2, lesson 23.)

Being able to direct personal effort toward the achievement of definite goals other than to obtain food and shelter is a characteristic distinguishing the life of man from that of the lower animals.

The English poet Milton pays tribute to man and to work in the following poem from "Paradise Lost":
When Adam thus to Eve: 
"Fair consort, the hour
Of night, and all things now retire to rest,
Mind us of like repose; since God hath set
Labour and rest, as day and night, to men
Successive; and the timely dew of sleep,
Now falling with soft slumbrous weight, inclines
Our eyelids: long other creatures all day
Rove idle unemployed, and less need rest;
Man hath his daily work of body or of mind
Appointed, which declares his dignity,
And the regard of Heaven on all his ways;
While other animals inactive range,
And of their doings God takes no account.

Of the ways of men the Lord does take account and he has counseled us as to the spending of our precious days and years. A person who will not work, providing he is physically and mentally able to work, may rightly be termed an idler and the Lord has given, in our day, a direct commandment regarding idleness.

In the Doctrine and Covenants we read:
Now, I, the Lord, am not well pleased with the inhabitants of Zion, for there are idlers among them; and their children are also growing up in wickedness; they also seek not earnestly the riches of eternity, but their eyes are full of greediness. These things ought not to be, and must be done away from among them (D&C 68:31-32.)

How important is work to the world in which we live? How rewarding can it be to individuals and to groups?

President Clark speaks of work as indispensable to pioneer life and cites the struggles of the early members of the Church.

From lesson 23 of the 1969-70 Melchizedek Priesthood course of study, we quote from an address given in 1960:
A hundred years ago armed mobs plundered, burned, and murdered us.

I speak without heartburning or malice and with full charity. Three times we were driven by force from our homes, and all our property that we could not carry on our backs or in our wagons was taken from us. When ninety-five years ago our Prophet-leader was slain by an unrestrained local mob and we began our westward march over plain and mountain under that genius in leadership, Brigham Young, we moved under our own power, without subsidy, without loan, wished on our way only by the maledictions of those who drove us out from our homes and then appropriated, without payment for it, the property they forced us to leave behind. Arriving in the valleys of the mountains, we planted all our wheat, with enough flour to last only to harvest; our nearest food supply was one thousand miles away, reached only by ox teams; thousands of others were on the way to join us. A pest of locusts came, devouring everything on their way. Famine raised its gaunt frame. Then came the miracle of the gulls, the crops were saved, starvation was beaten back—a miracle as real as any that aided the ancient Israel in her escape from the bondage of Egypt. But even then we lived on short rations of the simplest foods, eked out by wild roots dug from the hillsides. So we struggled on against want and misery; toil and hardship were with us daily. But the Church survived; the people prospered. Character endured intact. We took care of our own poor. In times of scarcity neighbors helped one another. Time and time again, we passed through the fiery furnace; we came out of it each time, refined, with the dross burned away, re-inspirited, sanctified.

(Immortality and Eternal Life, Vol. 2, lesson 23.)

Experiences, in many ways similar, were encountered by other groups colonizing western America. In fact, pioneering life at all times has taxed the resourcefulness of the people and has required all individuals to work individually and cooperatively in order to survive. Out of their combined efforts, communities have been built, states and nations have evolved.

Class Participation: Consider pioneer conditions, briefly. Discuss the need and the willingness to work in order to survive in contrast to the trend toward idleness in times of plenty.

For the individual, work may be a door opening into fields more vast than the mind of man has yet envisioned. Though faith may be the great motivator, work is the spade of discovery, for it is work that makes the dream materialize. An intelligent person cannot work in any capacity without learning. To work is to discover. It is through individual effort and application that new vistas are glimpsed, horizons widened, and the ability to accomplish enlarged.

Examples of what work has produced and the possibilities of future accomplishment are breathtaking.

Class Participation: Discuss the following personal statements:

1. I stand in awe when I consider how much intelligent effort goes into producing even one of the innumerable commodities we use in the home, the school, and the shop, from simple electrical and manual appliances to such complex mechanisms as is found in the computer or the satellites that man has made to orbit into space.

2. The libraries of the world, taken collectively, house billions of books. They did not create themselves. They are the product of intellectual effort and of manual skill. I am grateful to all the individuals who have been involved in their production. I am a student, and as I view the stacks of books before me, I realize that books contain much of the best that man has thought and said. I know that ideas and facts contained in them can be mine only if I put forth personal effort to make them mine. At best, only a fraction of the knowledge contained will be transferred to me, a thought which spurs me to better use of my time.

3. The innumerable yards of fabric which one sees in a fabric store are evidence of work already accomplished. Without further labor they constitute mere yardage. With additional work of merchant, designer, and seamstress, the world may be clothed.

Glen C. Beeley, director of a well-recognized arts and craft institution, has some important things to say about work and a woman's emotional world. She believes that work, especially creative work, can and does perform miracles in the lives of unhappy, frustrated, and even physically handicapped women.

She states: I wish you could see the transformations I see in people. I believe that individuals cannot be truly happy unless they are constructively busy. Although some individuals are more inherently creative, everyone can be creative who is willing to work. Many people naturally find creative ways of expressing themselves and of usefully using their available time. Others, less naturally creative, need to be led to creativity, and thus, to happiness. They need to feel that they can do things with their hands. This assurance and this accomplishment lead to a degree of creative experimentation and of mental exploration. They think of new or different ways of making the same article.

But [concludes the director] it is not the articles of art or of usefulness that are really important, it is what creative work does for the individual. This is most rewarding. Individuals (including older people) should and do sense a personal growth from giving of themselves to others and to objectives. Thus they develop a fineness within themselves and a sensibility to the fineness in others. They learn to appreciate what others also are doing and can do. They need to feel, and they do, that they can, figuratively, "Sail their own ships."

The foregoing comments regarding arts and crafts, generally, refer to leisure time activity, although professional status and specific occupations may and sometimes are the result. Many women make special effort to enroll in such classes. They find personal satisfaction in creativity as do women who engage in the homemaking activities of Relief Society.

What may we say of the necessary, daily tasks of the homemaker? How useful is this work? How important is the attitude of the wife or mother in relation to this part of the world's work?

Many thought-evoking statements have been made regarding the need for and the important results of work.
