Thoughts on College Beginnings / Marcella H. Morley
College days have arrived for you, my son.
Amid all of the hurried preparations prior to your leaving—
all of the arrangements, the new clothes bought, and everything packed,
there wasn't much time to think of how this new phase of life will affect the family.

But now, as we travel homeward driving farther and farther away from where you are, and being very definitely aware of the probability of your never really living at home any more, my thoughts are turned toward many things, especially of how proud we are to have a son like you. You have been gaining so much understanding and wisdom in the way you have learned to accept disappointments, to admit mistakes, and also to accept the responsibility for the consequences. This is the beginning of maturity. I am thinking of a recent incident in which you had the necessity to make a split-second decision. You made the choice—which, as things worked out, was the wrong one. [ recall you said, "It was a wrong decision, but I did my best." You were not discouraged, because you had done your best, and you will go on from there to do better, using this experience as a guide. I am also remembering the events of today: leaving home before dawn, and the long drive in the rain. Then, when we had reached our destination, driving around the beautiful campus, with its broad stretches of lawn, and the stately old buildings harmonizing beautifully with the lovely new ones. Then, after we had delivered all of your belongings at the dormitory, which would be your new home for the coming year, we drove you back to the Administration Building—where we would part. I feel that I can now understand to a small degree the feelings of our Father in heaven as he sends each of his children to earth. He dearly loves them and would like them to remain with him always, but for their development and progress, they must be parted for awhile. And so each one, as the time is right, is sent to earth to gain experiences here. Perhaps you were feeling and know to some degree, how very difficult it was for us to leave you walking alone up that beautiful tree-lined path toward the door of your future. As you turned to wave goodbye, you suddenly seemed so very young. This in direct contrast to the thoughts I had been thinking for many months—that you had grown tall and were becoming an adult. And now you seemed almost too young to leave on your own. The years have slipped by rapidly—so very rapidly! As you turned and walked up the pathway, I felt an assurance that many doors would open for you following this day, because of the choice you made when you chose to open the door of education for your future.

What is more appealing to the appetite than the tantalizing aroma of a casserole brimful of flavor? Simple foods, such as stews and leftovers, take on a new elegance and importance when served as casseroles. One-dish meals require little watching, and with a side serving of salad, bread, and milk, little else is needed for a hearty meal. Fortunately, casserole recipes are usually quite flexible. Proportions and main ingredients can be varied without diminishing the flavor or quality of the dishes. Happily, in these days of high food prices, less meat may be used, more potatoes, rice, spaghetti, and vegetables may be added for bulk. Either fresh, frozen, or canned foods, depending on their availability or price, may be used.

Casserole recipes are well known as time savers. Provident homemakers find it a good practice to prepare two or more casseroles at one time from like ingredients, one to be cooked for tonight's dinner, the other to be popped into the freezer to be brought out for unexpected company, for dinner on Relief Society day, or to be taken to a sick friend. Preparation for the Sabbath day may be minimized by frequent use of casseroles prepared in advance.

Casserole recipes cover a wide range of foods. They may be served in a simple or an elegant manner, as the occasion demands. Why not try some of the following delicious recipes? Or you may wish to concoct some of your own. Put a casserole in the oven and relax.

1 lb. Ground chuck
V4 tsp. Minced garlic
1 tbsp. Butter or margarine
3/4 c. Or 1 12 oz. Can tomatoes
1/2 c. Diced green pepper
1 c. Uncooked elbow macaroni
1/2 tsp. Instant minced onion
1/2 tsp. Basil
1/4 tsp. Salt
1 can tomato paste
1/4 tsp. Ground black pepper
1/4 c. Diced cheddar cheese

Brown meat in butter or margarine in a 2-quart saucepan. Add next 10 ingredients. Mix well. Bring to boil. Pour into greased casserole. Cook covered for 30 minutes at 350°. Fold in cheese and serve.

Serves 6-8.
