I assume that it would be a little easier if I were to address myself to the subject: "When Did I Begin to Study the Gospel of Jesus Christ?" instead of "How I Became Involved in The Doctrine and Covenants," because the Doctrine and Covenants gives us the gospel. In fact, if we were to analyze its doctrines, I do not believe it could be surpassed by any of the other three books of scripture accepted by the Latter-day Saints. Furthermore, we would learn that it is the repository of many doctrines of salvation which are only mentioned or inferred in those other books. Formative Years in the Priesthood As far as I am concerned, my interest in the study of the gospel began when I became a deacon in the Aaronic Priesthood. I believe that because I was given responsibility at that time my interest in the gospel was heightened. It was the practice of the counselor in the bishopric who had charge of the quorum to give assignments to the quorum members to participate in giving parts of the lesson. As an ordained teacher, I became the teacher of the quorum under the direction of a member of the ward bishopric. This practice continued in the priest's quorum; consequently, my interest in the gospel became very great during those formative years in the priesthood. This foundation proved very helpful in preparation for a mission, so much that when I was called to be a missionary at the age of twenty-two, I felt that I was prepared to teach the gospel when I arrived in the mission field.

My interest in the gospel was theological; consequently, I learned the doctrines of the Church from all of the standard works, especially with an emphasis upon the Bible since I knew I would use it as the basis of my missionary work. It wasn't until some years later, except for the use of many passages in the Doctrine and Covenants which provided clear explanations of particular doctrines, that I really studied the Doctrine and Covenants in depth.

The True Concept of God

Among many other fundamental doctrines, the missionary teaches the investigator the true concept of God. Of all the doctrines of the gospel this one stands out as one of the most important for people to know — since it is life eternal to know God. (John 17:3.) One of the difficulties a nonmember may have in accepting the concept that God is personal, having a body of flesh and bones, is God's attribute of omnipresence. The apostate "Christian" world believes that God is a spirit without form; whereas the Lord has revealed to his prophets, ancient and modern, that he has a body. He may be said, however, to be a spirit the same as "man is spirit;" that is, he, as well as man, has always been a spirit, from eternity to eternity though in earth-life and the resurrected state the personal spirit is associated with a body. (D&C 93:33; 130:22.)

If God has a tangible body, and he is not a spirit only, then how can he be omnipresent, or everywhere? The "Light of Christ" or spirit of the Lord emanates from God to fill all space. (Acts 17:27-28; D&C 88:12.) Because of this omnipresent power, God has communication with all of his creations, and thus he is everywhere, as the scriptures aver.

Salient Principles of Doctrine and Covenants

The Doctrine and Covenant is different from the other three standard works of the Church because it is a little more difficult to maintain interest in sustained reading unless one has a definite objective in his reading, such as theological. There is relatively no narrative in the Doctrine and Covenants. The exception to this is Section 135, the historical account of the martyrdom of the Prophet Joseph Smith and his brother Hyrum at Carthage Jail 125 years ago.

I presume that it would not be amiss to suggest an exaggerated comparison of the Doctrine and Covenants and reading the dictionary—in both, there is statement after statement of truths not always connected with the preceding information. This fact, however, has its advantages. One does not have to look very far to find rich, inspiring thoughts. Also, one must concentrate on his reading in order not to miss salient principles.

Years ago I realized that many members of the Church might find difficulty in keeping up their interest in reading the Doctrine and Covenants, so I conceived the idea that this book could be enjoyed more and learned better, perhaps, through the commentaries of the prophets, seers, and revelators of the dispensation. With this and other purposes in mind, I set out to compile such a work, using the sermons and writings of the members of the First Presidencies, Twelve Apostles, and the Patriarchs to explain the Doctrine and Covenants.

For well over five years I labored on this project which culminated in the four volumes of The Latter-Day Prophets and the Doctrine and Covenants, published from 1963 to 1965. I have known young as well as old people who have shown unusual interest in the Doctrine and Covenants through the use of these volumes.

Writings of Elder B. H. Roberts

In the years when I was learning the teachings of the Church, I was impressed with the writings of Elder B. H. Roberts of the First Council of Seventy. I remember his quoting a passage from George Rawlinson's Historical Evidences of the Truth of the Scripture Records, as follows:

Of this we may be sure, that if any new forms of evidence should hereafter be needed, to meet any new forms of unbelief, and authenticate afresh the word of truth, they will be found deposited somewhere, waiting for the fulness of time; and God will bring them forth in their season, from the dark hieroglyphics, or the desert sands, or the dusty manuscripts, to confound the adversaries of his word, and to magnify it above all his name. (Quoted in New Witness for God, Volume II, Forewords.)

Under the caption of "The World's Hunger For Knowledge of The Christ," President Roberts quoted the following from the Life of the Master by Rev. John Watson:

Were a parchment discovered in an Egyptian mound, six inches square, containing fifty words which were certainly spoken by Jesus, this utterance would count more than all the books which have been published since the first century. (Ibid.)

Although Elder Roberts used these quotations to support the Book of Mormon, I came early in life to know that we do have the words of Christ in the Doctrine and Covenants, in fact, hundreds and hundreds of them spoken to his Prophet, Joseph Smith.

The introduction to the revelations in this book of scripture reveals the author as the Savior. Examples are:

Hearken, O ye people of my church— 1:1.
—to the voice of Jesus Christ, your Lord, your God, and your Redeemer (27:1.)
Behold, thus saith the Lord, even Alpha and Omega... (54:1.)

A Witness for the Savior

The Lord declared, however, that the revelations were given after the manner of the Prophet's language. (D&C 1:24.) The Doctrine and Covenants stands as a witness for Jesus that he is the Savior of those who want to be saved. The doctrine that salvation comes only to those who accept him and thus he saves only them, is taught plainly. (D&C 42:1; 29:17.)

In truth, the Doctrine and Covenants is an answer to the present thought among some theologians that God is dead, a notion that has received wide publicity. The modern scriptures declare plainly that God lives!

Two important accounts of the appearance of the Savior in this generation are given in the Doctrine and Covenants:

(1) The stirring description by the Prophet Joseph Smith and Sidney Rigdon is a testimony to this fact.

And now after the many testimonies which have been given of him, this is the testimony, last of all, which we give of him: That he lives! For we saw him, even on the right hand of God; and we heard the voice bearing record that he is the Only Begotten of the Father—
That by him, and through him, and of him, the worlds are and were created, and the inhabitants thereof are begotten sons and daughters unto God. (D&C 76:22-24.)

Paraphrasing the words of the author, Doris Donnelly, she said that the terrible suffering that is occurring in the world can only be attributed to God if he is made into a monster. ("The Requiem For God," Religious Education, July-August 1967, p. 318.) To the Latter-day Saint the Lord has revealed the why and wherefore of human suffering, and in doing so he has given us the strength and power to endure. The inspired words given to the Prophet Joseph Smith in Liberty Jail, Missouri, provide this blessing: "Know thou, my son, that all these things (sufferings and the tribulations) shall give thee experience, and shall be for thy good. The Son of Man hath descended below them all. Art thou greater than he?" (D&C 122:7-8.) Truly, when man knows the purpose of suffering, he is better able to endure it. Ultimately, sorrow, suffering, and like adversities are for our development, growth and purification. (Lesson 92 "Adversity," , October 1968.) I believe that the modern standard works of the Church have greatly increased the faith of Latter-day Saints in the Bible, a book which has lost ground among "Christian" people. On the other hand, the Lord said to the Prophet Joseph Smith that the revelations he gave him would have the effect of increasing faith among men. (D&C 1:17-23.) 493 Goal, Understanding, and Technique
Jean Larsen, Instructor in Child Development and Family Relationships at Brigham Young University

@ The answer to the question, "Should there be discipline?" comes through loud and clear, "Yes, there must be discipline!" But the what, the why, and the how of discipline and guidance are not so clearly spelled out. Not knowing what is expected on either end of the guidance situation provides some frustrating experiences for all concerned.

We recall the story: Bobby had been playing by the duck pond for several minutes when Father called for him to come in the house. Bobby approached his father slowly, looking down at muddy feet and wet pant legs. When Bobby's father saw the mud and the wet, he said in a stern voice, "Well, Bobby, how many times must you be told to stay out of the mud and water? Go get me a willow, maybe a 'willowing' will help you remember." Bobby was gone for several minutes before he slowly approached his father again— this time with his hands behind him. His father's voice was again stern as he said, "Well, I'm waiting, give me the willow." Bobby drew both hands around and produced not a willow but several rocks in each hand. Then he said in a meek little voice, "T couldn't find a willow, but here are some rocks to throw at me."

As adults being responsible for guiding the child we are concerned about his behavior and ways of altering, controlling, or instigating certain behaviors. Guidance can become a battle of wills between adults and children. But, as in all battles, someone must be the victor. Should it be the adult at the expense of the child's curiosity, spontaneous expression, and self-esteem, or should the child be the winner because of his ability to display compulsive, negative, and violent behavior? Power struggles are a risk to any relationship and should be avoided.

The goal of guidance is to let the child know what is expected of him, and then provide experiences which will help him to develop self-discipline or control from within.

Why do children do what they do and when will they change? There is a reason why children do what they do. Sometimes adults don't take time or don't try to understand what is behind the behavior. How many times have you heard a parent say to a child, "Now stop crying or [ll really give you something to cry for," assuming that there is not a reason for the child to cry in the first place? Some behavior, although unacceptable to adults, is normal for the child of a given age and may very well pass on like the seasons. Temper tantrums are a normal reaction to frustration for the toddler age. Getting into things, finding out what possibility an object or material has for manipulation, is a force pushing the child from within. Most parents have the same concerns. "Why can't my child be quiet in Church?" or "Just when I want him to behave, he really acts up." A new situation or one that does not necessarily accommodate to the child usually produces child behavior that the adult does not approve of. The most difficult time in many households is the dinner hour. Mother may be intent on preparing the meal, younger children are tired and hungry, and Father and the older children need a listening ear after their day away from home. Tension runs high in such a situation.

Keys to Child Guidance
Sometimes children do what they do because they do not know what is expected of them.

Guidance involves evaluation of and reaction to behaviors. Because each of us is an individual dealing with another unique individual, successful techniques will vary. There are some guidelines which may serve as a basis for dealing with behavior in general. First, punishment does not teach or show the child the type of behavior you want exemplified. It may cut off the behavior or traumatize the child about that which he should not do, but punishment does not establish what the child should do. Toddlers are notorious for using primitive social techniques. Usually, if another child has something they want they will bite, poke, pull or hit in order to gain possession. Many parents see this behavior and become very anxious. Usually, someone will suggest that the way to teach them not to bite is for you to bite them back. Will your biting the child teach him what you want him to do? Second, it is not wise to try to cut off a child's curiosity. This is like trying to stop his growth. Foster his asking questions and trying things out. If the behavior is caused because of a growing curiosity, then tolerate or channel, but be very selective when you stop this kind of behavior. Water play is one of the most soothing types of play a child of most young ages can engage in. However, water play and other related sensory types of play need to be channeled. A mother might respond, "No, I can't have you pouring water from container to container here on the kitchen floor, but outside (or a protected area inside) you may do so."

Third, try looking at things from the child's point of view. Rather than expecting the child to come all the way in an intolerable situation, see what you can do to make it easier for him. Sincere praise for just being himself can modify a child's behavior. Some adults feel compliments are not for children, but if there is a basis for them, they should be used. There was a five-year-old in the nursery school who seemed to be overflowing with good ideas. The teacher in the nursery group would frequently comment to this child, "John, you are clever!" One day a parent said to the teacher, "Why are you always telling that child he's clever? Don't you think he'll become egocentric?" The teacher replied that she felt children's behavior was greatly influenced by the way others reacted to them both by words and deeds. Sometime later John was watching a disgruntled repairman fixing a plugged up sink in the nursery school. As the repairman took off the gooseneck and found it filled with sand, it appeared he was going to explode. John, who had been watching the operation, looked up at the man's angry face and said, "My, you're clever to be able to fix that sink." The repairman's anger turned into a friendly smile as he said, "Well, I guess I am clever at that."

A fourth guide would be to feed into the child love, warmth, gentleness, and self-worth. Children learn what they live. A child who has received has something to give back.

A fifth and concluding guide deals with letting the child know what is expected of him. Teach the child what to do rather than what not to do. Help the child see reasons, outcomes, and responsibilities. This is the opposite from threats and retaliations. Provide experiences whereby the child can develop inner control through understanding rather than control through fear.

Mary had been cutting and pasting at the art table. When she was nearly finished with her picture, she noticed how easily her scissors snipped off small corners and pieces of the paper. Mary snipped and watched the small pieces flutter to the floor. Soon it looked like snow, and soon the floor looked like a real storm had hit.

The teacher came to Mary and said, "When Mr. Bob, our custodian, comes in to clean tonight, he'll wonder what has been happening in this room."

Mary thought for a moment and then replied, "I'll need to get this mess cleaned up. Would you hold the dustpan while I sweep?"

Make teaching your discipline and take the initiative to discuss, explain, interpret, and demonstrate desirable behavior. If your guidance relationship is constant and you are accepting of the fact that each experience will not be a successful one—it takes a long time to learn—eventually you will reach the goal of establishing self-worth and controls from within the child.

Even though Church membership was large here on the West Coast, the saints were still a minority in this teeming metropolis, but the missionaries had found this a fruitful field. George had immediately befriended the missionaries and shared his own experiences with them. Knowing what a real home-cooked meal meant to a young missionary far away from home, he wanted to invite them to dinner, and Lenora had agreed wholeheartedly.

She wanted it to be a very special meal. These young men so valiantly serving the Lord deserved the very best. For days she planned the meal, shifted the food budget, in order to buy the choicest steaks, and put an all-out effort into the occasion. Every minute of that first day that the elders came to dinner was spent in cleaning every corner, making a table arrangement that resembled a page from Home Lovely, and preparing food fit for the most critical gourmet.

It was worth it though—the way those hungry boys devoured every morsel, the praise they heaped on her, George's look of satisfaction. She had fed the children early, sent them to their rooms, and planned an after-dinner relaxation period around the fireplace with hot buttered popcorn, crisp apples, happy conversation, and a background of soft nostalgic stereo music.

Her plans were shattered though as the elders had to eat and run in order to make an early evening appointment. It was some time before they were invited again. Such a meal played havoc with her grocery budget, and she expended so much time and energy in preparing it that she needed a couple of months' rest between invitations.

Lenora put as much dedication into the second meal as she had the first, and the new elders praised her highly. She felt especially happy, and after bidding the young men goodbye, she went to check on the children.
