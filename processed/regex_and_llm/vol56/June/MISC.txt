A candy thermometer, for those who prefer a thermometer for testing. Remember that recipes give sea-level temperatures. To adjust these temperatures to your own altitude, put a thermometer in a pan of water and bring to a boil. Record the temperature. Sea-level boiling temperature is 212° F. So, subtract your boiling temperature from the sea-level boiling temperature. The difference might be two, ten, twelve, or more degrees. Then subtract that number of degrees from the recipe temperature.

If, as in Salt Lake City, for example, the difference is twelve degrees; the sea-level temperature for the soft ball stage is 236°; therefore the candy will be done at 224°. At sea level caramels are done at 246°. In Salt Lake City, where the difference is 12°, they are done at 234°.

CREAM CENTERS FOR CHOCOLATES

1 pint fresh whipping cream
4 c. White granulated sugar
1 tsp. Pure vanilla

Add cream to sugar in a kettle and stir well. Then, with a cloth wrung from hot water, wipe off the inside of the kettle down to the candy line. Remove spoon and boil over medium heat or less.

Important: Mixture must not be stirred during the heating and cooking process, but wipe the inside of the kettle with a cloth three times, down to the candy line, holding the kettle still. Let cook to very soft ball stage (20-30 minutes); test by dropping a bit into a cup of ice cold water.

When this can be gathered into a small ball and rolled between thumb and finger under cold water, without adhering to fingers, it is done. Slowly pour a thin stream of candy, outlining the edges of the cold slab, to prevent candy from running off the slab. Then pour the candy slowly from the center to the edge.

If it runs off in your first efforts, do not touch it; let all candy stand untouched until completely cool. Push it into the center of the slab with a long knife. Then use a long wooden spoon, clean and dry, to beat the candy.

Never scrape the kettle at all! Scraping causes granulation. If the candy grains, add 1/4 cup cream, stir well; reboil. Add a teaspoon of pure vanilla; it must be the pure vanilla as this is one secret of the flavor. Beat and beat, under the partly opened window, until the candy is creamy and firm; then knead as you would bread, for a minute or two.

Divide the candy into three parts. (Yield: 60-70 chocolates)

VARIETIES OF CREAM CENTERS

Plain Centers
Mold the first part into round balls, the size of chocolate centers, by rolling between the palms at an open window, for the plain creams. Place these on wax paper on plates under open window or in refrigerator. Let stand two hours to form a slight crust before dipping in chocolate.

After an hour these balls may be shaped into greater symmetry by very quickly rolling between the palms again. Then let stand.

Coconut Centers
To the second part, add two thirds of a 3/4-ounce can of choice coconut flakes. Mix and prepare as in part one.

Nut Centers
To the third part, add as many broken pecans as desired. Prepare centers as previously directed. If covered these centers can stand in the refrigerator a day or so before dipping.

CHOCOLATE DIP

5 squares bitter chocolate
4 squares semi-sweet chocolate

Use only a few squares at a time, as the leftover mixture should not be re-heated another day for dipping, but used for other things. Melt in a bowl over hot, not quite boiling, water. Lift off burner, and allow to cool for about 2 minutes.

Centers must be cold when dipped, and room must be cool. It is said to take two years to make a good chocolate dipper so don't be discouraged if you don't achieve a professional look at first. Use a fork and quickly dip the centers in the melted chocolate, or roll in chocolate in the palm of the hand. Then set it on waxed paper.

When chocolates are hard, trim off the uneven edges at the base of each. Store in fluted chocolate cups in covered tin containers in the refrigerator.

MINT FUDGE CENTERS

1 pint fresh whipping cream
4 c. White granulated sugar
1 square bitter chocolate, shaved and mixed in before putting on the burner (homogenizes during beating)
1 1/2, scant tsp. Mint flavor added after candy has cooled on the slab, also 1 tsp. Vanilla.

Proceed as for cream centers above, only flatten the balls before cooling. Then dip if desired.

All cream centers are attractive plain, with a half pecan pressed into the top.

HOW TO MAKE BREAD CLAY Do not be afraid to mix what may look to you to be a very large batch. To mix and color is very time consuming, but, if properly packaged and cared for, the clay will remain usable for many months. Recipe 1 and 2 lb. Loaves of white sandwich bread 2 scant c. Of any good white glue 1 scant tbsp. Glycerin 1/2 tsp. Fresh lemon juice Tear away all crust from bread, but leave as much of the white as possible, and break into small pieces. Add white glue, glycerin, and lemon juice. Stir as thoroughly as possible with a spoon then divide into two parts. Take one up into your clean dry hands. Kneading and Pulling Begin kneading and pulling as you would taffy candy. Do not become discouraged at texture and stickiness, but keep pulling and kneading. After about 15 minutes the clay will almost magically begin to adhere and change into a smooth, soft clay. Keep at it until it reaches a uniform, easy texture. Lay the first part aside and begin the same process with the second part. When both are of the same smooth clay stage, put them together, pull and knead until thoroughly mixed. Drop hard, several times, on a clean counter or board to knock out air bubbles and to even up the texture. Caution: Sister Alvord has tried to come to an infallible formula for her bread clay but she cannot know how your climate or atmosphere will affect it. You may find that you will need to leave out 1 or 2 tablespoons of glue (no more, ever!). But before you decide it is too "wet," knead it and pull it to expose it to the air. Let it ripen overnight in your refrigerator (in plastic) before you decide the texture is wrong. 454 The Art of Bread Clay Coloring Sister Alvord suggests that you do all the coloring at one uninterrupted session. This way, you will have all the colors you will need, mixed and ready for months to come. She uses only opaque fluorescent water colors. Shocking turquoise, pink, brilliant orange, vivid yellow, chartreuse, greens, blues, etc., of the brightest colors she can find. These usually come in jars. You will also want black, brown, white (these are tube water colors) and flesh (a mixture of yellow, ochre and a small amount of pink). Be sure to leave some clay uncolored. You will want to variate your colors and you can get into the pastels by using uncolored clay with a speck of the deepest colored clay and work up to desired shade. Divide your prepared bread clay into eight, ten, twelve or as many colors and variations as you would like. (Keep the clay you are not working with clean and covered with plastic.) Using a toothpick, dip into color selected, wipe it across the clay and begin the pulling and kneading process again. Keep adding color in this manner until you reach the stage of deepest color you wish. Knead until all streaks are gone. Be very careful not to leave any color from your hands on uncolored clay that you are planning for other colors, this is what kills the clear colors. Wash hands thoroughly between colorings as the clay will absorb any color stain. When you have all your coloring finished and have placed each color separately in a small plastic sandwich bag, pack them in a cake or cookie tin with a very tight lid and store in your refrigerator. As you work with your clay, remember at all times to keep it covered in plastic as it dries out quickly. Making Flowers To begin, Sister Alvord urges that you make up a good supply of flowers in varying sizes, kinds and colors before you plan for a definite project. With them on hand, your creativity can have more vision. For creating the tiny flowers (all varieties and roses) that are not more than 1/4 to 1/2 inch across that Sister Alvord uses in so many of her jewelry pieces, decorative boxes and arrangements under glass—are the following instructions: ROSES Use 3 shades of color. Center should be deepest, and shade out lighter to edge of rose. Sister Alvord makes up all her centers first—often three dozen or so at a time. You will want to start with fewer as the clay dries out so quickly. You will find as you practice you will increase in speed and dexterity.

Sister Alvord prefers her centers to be completely dry before building out her petals. It gives a good firm base for the rose. Centers are made by pinching off pieces of clay about half the size of a garden pea. (Or larger if you are making larger roses.) Roll quickly into a small ball between your fingers and drop onto plastic sheet. When you have sufficient to give you a challenge, roll and rub each little ball at one end to pull it into a tear-shape. (With a finger.) Place another piece of plastic sheet over your centers and press each with your finger or any object with a smooth, flat surface. Press them 'paperthin.' Keeping the others covered as best you can, peel off a center and beginning with the broad end of the tear drop, roll around a toothpick a little way down so that as you roll it around, you barely cover the tip. Roll it as the tight center of a rose and pinch a little at the bottom so it will stay on the pick. Lay it aside to dry and continue to roll all centers the same.

Attention: This tear shape process is the same way Sister Alvord makes her leaves, large and small, except for the last step.

ROSE PETALS

Petals should be about the same size as the centers. Begin by pinching off again small bits of clay and rolling round between your fingers and dropping to plastic sheet. When you feel you have all you can finish before they dry out, cover with plastic sheet and press again to paper-thin. The petals will all come out fairly round.

Pick up a dried rose center, still on the toothpick. With another toothpick, dab a little white glue at the base of your center. Peel a paper-thin petal from plastic sheet and begin wrapping and overlapping your center and petal. You will not need to add more glue as you will be working again with pliable clay. Pinch your petals towards the back as a natural flower. (Go out and pick one for your model.) Remember, roses are not all of the same shape or variety of shading. Ripples and even small tears are as attractive as on live flowers.

Wrap and pinch until you have a small full rose or bud. Before your rose (or any of these clay flowers) completely dries, carefully remove from toothpick and turn it upside down and gently place tip of pick into flower center. Now, with small scissors, snip off bottom of flower to give it a flat base to glue to surface you are to decorate. (For some flower arrangements or flower trees, this step is unnecessary.)

Daisy, poppy, pansy—any 3, 4, or 5-petaled flower (imagination please! ): Make balls of bread clay between your fingers, drop on plastic, and roll and rub ends as you did for leaves and centers, into teardrops, except, do not flatten or press these, but place together all tip ends of teardrops as base of the flower and pinch a little.

Using toothpick or other wooden tools, roll carefully each petal out on your finger as flower petal, leaving indentions or ridges as you desire and as thin as you can or, shaped as you want. Bend and curve them a little here and there to give them a natural look. Centers for these can be very small balls of complimentary colors or a dab of colored clay from one of your glue guns.

BREAD CLAY PLAQUES AND FIGURES

For making specific designs for plaques and flat figures with a pattern, roll out a portion of the clay between plastic sheets with a small even bottle to about the thickness of pie crust. Place the pattern on the clay and cut around with either a ceramic knife or small very sharp knife. Peel off, and place on prepared plaque or picture. (A burlap covered board, painted and trimmed with cording, gold or silver doilie edging is very attractive.)

Choose next color of clay and pattern, roll and cut, placing figure or design together color by color and piece by piece—a little like dressing a paper doll. Tiny fine lines, swirls, and decorations can be made by using the glue gun as you would decorate a cake.

Finishing touches, of course, are done when the clay is completely dry, painting faces with tube water colors and a very small, fine brush. For all your creations, flowers, leaves, plaques, dolls, decorative pieces, etc., when completely dry, use a soft brush and apply a coat of glaze. Place on the aluminum foil to dry.

Taylor Northern Hemisphere: Second Meeting, September 1969 Southern Hemisphere: February 1970 OBJECTIVE: To show how carefully planned gardens and grounds and careful use and storage of tools can contribute to the convenience and safety of outdoor INTRODUCTION Just as we do not want to be slaves inside our homes with constant sweeping, dusting, and doing dishes, just so we do not want to burden ourselves outside with excessive lawn mowing, weeding and watering. To avoid making ourselves slaves to either, wise and careful planning is necessary.
In the grounds as in the house we are concerned with modifying or controlling the environment so that it is convenient to our needs and easy to maintain and so that it provides safe and peaceful shelter to our families. One garden enthusiast has said that the best life and the best gardens are made up of a wise combination of beauty and utility, convenience and safety.
All of these considerations augumenting. Ment each other, and when the garden is planned with them in mind, the results are pleasing and satisfying.

HOW MAY WE ACHIEVE A CONVENIENT GARDEN ENVIRONMENT FOR OUR HOMES?
The following suggestions may help us in planning our surroundings for convenience.
1. The front yard should provide a pleasant setting that relates the building to the grounds and furnishes easy access to both. Facilities and space should be provided for comfortable outdoor living. The habit of eating out of doors in the cool of the summer mornings and evenings is one which is being widely practiced and which should be highly encouraged.
A. When possible, the garden living areas should be placed so as to take advantage of the greatest number of hours for garden living.
To 465 June 1969 D. B. If possible, the garden living area should be furnished with places to sit, read, play, or dine. There should be space provided for outdoor recreation with emphasis on children's activities. This will draw family members closer together, away from the television set, the public movie, or questionable sources of entertainment.

The service area could be designed to fit the habits and needs of the family and organized and located so as to occupy a minimum of space. When space permits, flowers, fruits and vegetables are welcome additions to the garden. Tomato plants, fruit vines and even espaliered fruit-bearing trees can be trained to the property line fences and walls.

HOW CAN WE PROMOTE SAFETY IN OUR GARDEN SURROUNDINGS?

1. We should be alert ourselves to all possible hazards on our property and remove them. We should keep the premises clear of rubbish and trash, dry grass and weeds and any other useless material. We should keep firefighting equipment handy and be sure the garden hose has a suitable connection to both inside and outside faucets. Any buildings within fifty feet of a fireplace area should be built or surfaced with fireproof materials. Fires should never be left unattended but should be completely extinguished when left.

A circle of about sixteen inches should be cut around the base of trees in the lawn area to insure protection from the blades of lawn mowers and for convenience in the mowing operation.

2. For safety in the garden as well as in the house we should have a first aid kit well supplied and available.

3. Garden tools and equipment can be safe or dangerous depending on how they are operated and stored. Children should not be permitted to operate or handle high-powered lawn mowers, hedge clippers, or sharpedged tools.

4. Fences, gates, and equipment should be kept in good repair.

5. When electrical cords are used in the garden they should be kept away from water or dampness. All electrical equipment should be grounded and used with caution.

6. Movable ladders should be strong, in good repair and stored in a dry place.

7. Outside stairways, raised terraces, ditches, and other hazardous places should be equipped with guard rails.

8. Insecticides, weed killers, chemical sprays and any other harmful or poisonous preparation as well as the equipment used should be stored out of reach of children.

CONCLUSION

By careful planning of our gardens and grounds, we can provide an environment so comfortable and livable and safe that it invites the participation of family and friends for relaxation, for pleasant dining, and for recreation.

The Doctrine and Covenants Preview of Lessons for 1969-70 Elder Roy W. Doxey The Relief Society year 1969-70 brings to an end this study of the Doctrine and Covenants begun in 1957. These lessons began with events prior to the organization of the Church in April 1830, and end at the beginning of the pioneer period of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints. The lessons again are written about a central idea from the revelations. It is hoped that during the year each Relief Society member will finish reading all of the Doctrine and Covenants to complete the assigned reading.

The lessons for 1969-70 begin with Doctrine and Covenants Section 131 (May 1843), a part of which was studied last year with Section 136 (January 1847). Only two of the five sections for study were given to the Church by the Prophet Joseph Smith, the others were given by Oliver Cowdery, John Taylor, and Brigham Young. All of the sections in the Doctrine and Covenants from Section 1 through 133 represent the revelations and writings of the Prophet.

The latest revelation is Section 132, carrying the date July 12, 1843; whereas, the last Section, 133, was received on November 3, 1831, but since it is the Appendix, it appears at the end of the Prophet's revelations. Apparently, Section 134, dated August 17, 1835, was placed in its position because it was not written by the Prophet, but by Oliver Cowdery, though later accepted by Joseph Smith.

After the martyrdom of Joseph and Hyrum Smith, Elder John Taylor wrote Section 135, an historical statement regarding the martyrdom. The final section in the Doctrine and Covenants is a revelation received in 1847 by President Brigham Young, President of the Quorum of the Twelve Apostles, about one year before he was sustained as the President of the Church.

This fact indicates the authority and power of the President of the Twelve in the interim between the death of the President and the appointment of the new President. The Cultural Refinement program of lessons using Out of the Best Books as a basic text has now functioned for five years in the Relief Society organization. Volume 5, to be used during 1969-70, will complete the sixth year of what was planned from the beginning as a six-year program. Volume 1, used in 1964-65 and 1965-66, stressed the individual and human values as seen through literature and the related arts. Volumes 2 and 3, used in 1966-67 and 1967-68, widened the horizon, emphasizing ideals and problems in marriage, parenthood, and family relationships. Now the closing two years of the program, Volume 4 for 1968-69 and Volume 5 for 1969-70, widen the scope still further, stressing a woman's activities and responsibilities in community life and the world in which she lives.

As pointed out a year ago, no individual or family can exist in isolation. In many significant ways a woman's first responsibility is to herself and to her family, but she also needs to function meaningfully with other people—as a friend and neighbor, as a Church worker, and as a citizen of the community, the nation, and the world. Moreover, in all of these things she needs to find a happy balance between work, recreation, relaxation, spirituality, and cultural fulfillment. Each lesson in the Cultural Refinement program for this year will focus upon community involvement that is meaningful, wholesome, and well balanced.

As in former years, each section of the text will include more material than can easily be covered in a single lesson. This is done intentionally so that each class leader may choose those selections that she responds to with enthusiasm and that she feels are especially appropriate to her group. It is far better to cover one or two selections thoroughly and in such a way that they really touch the lives of the women than it is to cover all of the selections hastily and superficially.

Any selections not used in the Relief Society lessons are still available for excellent enrichment readings within the home. The year ahead will be the fourth year in which music and painting are used to supplement the literature in each lesson. Once again we encourage class leaders to harmonize the music and art as fully as possible into the lesson goals for each month rather than use them merely as decoration. We know that this is difficult, but the challenge can be met with careful advance planning, i.e., by careful allocation of time, by using them either as introductory or concluding material, or by using them as special assignments.

Once again we also urge class leaders to get as many Relief Society sisters as possible personally involved in each lesson. If the lesson is simply a monthly presentation by the teacher to women who have not read the material and do not participate in experiencing it, the lesson probably will not be very meaningful. All Relief Society sisters should be encouraged to read the selections in advance, participate in presenting them or at least in discussing them, and then take them home to their husbands and children for family enrichment.

EDITORIAL / TO EACH IS GIVEN A GIFT
For all have not every gift given unto them; for there are many gifts, and to every man is given a gift by the Spirit of God. (D&C 46:11.) (Italics added.)

In his loving-kindness God allows each person to come into the world possessing in his soul embryo gifts to be used and developed as one's free agency determines. While man's mind is veiled from a remembrance of his pre-mortal life, some of the seeds of his accomplishments there are born into mortality with him. The Father expects life to bring joy to his children. He prepares them for such joy by giving them the gifts with which to acquire it. One of the essential requirements of the Giver of Gifts is that the gift be used for the benefit of others "that all may be profited thereby." (D&C 46:12.) The use of these precious gifts selfishly, for personal gain alone is not the purpose for which they are given. The intention of the Lord is that gifts be magnified for the building of the kingdom. The rewards of such increase are that the gifts will be added upon. No one, we are told, lives to his fullest potential. Always there are greater goals to conquer. Efforts to reach that potential are crowned with the increased desire to do more and progress further. Gifts are to be sought. The prophets exhort the people to "covet earnestly the best gifts." (1 Cor. 12:31.) The blessings of spiritual growth are contingent upon asking in faith for one or more of the gifts. Proficiency is to be gained by personal effort and constant prayer to God to bless those efforts.

Gifts are many and varied. They encompass gifts of knowledge and understanding of gospel doctrine given by the Holy Ghost to talents in culture areas. Talents are given in music, literature, and art, in homemaking, and in other fields. The Lord's purpose is that all may be enriched through the use of these gifts.

In the realms of medicine, in aptitudes for educational pursuits, in the fields of engineering, and agriculture, the gifts are as diverse as the ability to love all of God's creations, a cheerful spirit, friendliness. To some is given the gift of leadership, to others the gift of following, not blindly but understandingly. Some are the lawgivers, the wise and sagacious; others are the workers who uncover the secrets of nature. Some possess gifts that even they do not suspect until they are brought to light by obedience to commandments. Some gifts are never discovered because they are not sought. There are those who resist the disciplines required for the development of a talent. They will not accept the responsibility bestowed with the gift to work toward its fulfillment. The potential for greatness is there but as a fallow field lies unused and dormant, idle in the sun, its potential for growth and production curtailed, so lie buried talents the owners fail to enlarge.

These are the unprofitable servants of the parable of the talents whose condemnation is greater than the loss of the talent. Their condemnation is greater than they would have received had they not been given the gift. There are those who reject the gifts so willingly given by a loving Father, those whose self-afflicted blindness, whose spiritual unawareness prevent their accepting a glorious opportunity to use the gift in perfecting themselves and in helping build the kingdom of God.

Relief Society is a marvelous gift given by our Heavenly Father to his daughters which some of the unseeing women of the Church have not yet accepted. The loss to themselves is great and to their families, husband, and children, but the loss to the building of the kingdom of God on earth is equally great. Failure to accept the God-given gifts results in failure to live most abundantly.

Each gift is spiritual if used to elevate, to build, to make the world better. No gift is a small one. Its size is determined by the receiver. All gifts are given that each might contribute to the work of the Lord.

505 Is Not Veiled

When the three sisters, Gwen, Mary, and Maggie made the final decision, they knew there were still problems to be solved. Getting Gwen's three lively boys settled at Maggie's would entail planning and cajoling. Telephoning Mary's husband in New York about the change of plans was going to take some diplomacy. The real problem was getting their mother out of the hospital and then getting her comfortably and safely back home.

The plan was simple on the surface. Gwen would go to Cedar Mesa on the plane with their mother. Maggie would keep Gwen's children, and Mary would drive down by car first to get the home ready for the mother's homecoming.

506

Gwen was washing the boys' play clothes, mending overalls, and folding and packing their Sunday clothes. She had a batch of bread in the oven. The baby's bed would be taken to Maggie's later. The trip would probably take four or five days, so she must leave plenty of clothes for the children. Gwen hoped she could get a batch of cookies made to take to Maggie. It would be the hardest on Maggie—what with four youngsters of her own—and she did look a little peaked lately.

Gwen had wanted to get a babysitter but Maggie had insisted on having the three little boys come to her place. Maggie had begun to carry out necessary plans, too. She had made room for Gwen's baby bed in the family room. Hy could set up two army cots in the boys' room. She would move her own baby bed into their bedroom. That way, everyone would be comfortable. She would make stew for the boys and maybe set some jello for dessert. Even thinking about food made her squeamish. You'd think after four babies.

She hadn't told her sisters about her pregnancy. She knew what Gwen would say, "Well, that settles it! I am going to get Jane to come over." Mary was not looking forward to these eight hours of steady driving to Cedar Mesa. It had been nearly ten years since she had driven down that road—ten years of a successful career, and, she admitted wryly to herself, ten years of an unsuccessful marriage.

And the telephone conversation with James last night had not helped. She knew she had been too brisk, too impersonal on the phone, but habit was hard to break.

"James, Mother is well enough to leave the hospital... Oh, she's fine. She said to tell you hello and she would like to see you."

Mamma left a legacy of happy memories for her family. Our friends and relatives young and old loved to come to her gracious, hospitable home. Some who knew Mama best said it was because she was generous, kind, and witty, with a penchant for making every guest feel special and welcome. To this I wholeheartedly agree, but Mama had something else which drew people to her. Mama was an excellent cook. She liked nothing better than to share her cuisine with those she loved.

Unfortunately for her daughters, Mama never used a recipe. All of her dishes were concocted by feel, consistency, or taste. A pinch of this or a handful of that. Mama didn't even own measuring cups or spoons. One of the happiest memories Mama left her children was our annual trip to "the Lakes." Mama prepared for several days for this special event. I can close my eyes now and it seems as though I can almost taste some of her special goodies.

As the tantalizing aroma from Mama's preparatory baking permeated the air, it appeared that it touched off the appetite alarms of all the youngsters in our neighborhood and those children who came to our kitchen door never went away hungry. For the long drive to "the Lakes," our outing site, Mama usually brought a lunch of milk, cinnamon buns, or slices of raisin and nut bread spread with freshly churned butter. She would pack them in the folds of snowy white dish cloths to keep them fresh, since wax paper or plastic wrap had never been heard of in our town.

There was always just enough food to appease our hunger but not too much to spoil our appetites for the picnic to follow.

And what a picnic! Our favorite food was Mama's baked beans. Their cooking was an all-day process. How we loved the smell of the molasses and brown sugar, mustard, and the salt pork nestled cozily among the brown beans. Combined, they produced a flavor that was delectable. Homemade root beer was a must. This was an exciting change from milk or the ever-popular lemonade. Mama's root beer had a special tang. We all loved the bottling process and as we put the corks into the bottle tops and tied them securely to the bottle with string, we would beg Mama to put our mark on just one bottle reserved especially for each of us.

In our home town, most everyone had at least a small garden. Fresh vegetables were common fare. Mama said they were good for us and so she used them in every possible way. Happily, Mama had the knack for giving even the lowest variety a special flavor. Pickled cucumbers were our special favorites so Mama always included them as part of our picnic menu.

Wheat was indeed the staff of life in our home. Mama used it in a variety of ways. From a breakfast food which she simmered for hours on the back of our coal range, to hot cakes, baking powder biscuits, buns, scones, to wonderful crusty homemade bread. We loved to spread fat slices of this bread with cream skimmed from the milk pan and flavored with sugar, or covered and dripping with honey or syrup. Long loaves of what we now call French bread were our favorite for an outing.

What is so delightful as homemade ice cream? No picnic or outing was complete in our family without it. Since Mama never counted calories, her ice cream was rich and delicious. The best and sweetest part was that which some lucky individual would lick from the dasher. Mama made many flavors and each kind we would avow to be our favorite. Try a delicious peach variety for your family.

A dripper cake, usually Mama's special fudge cake recipe, completed our lunch. Even though we had eaten our fill we could never resist this special treat. Papa always concluded our picnics by arising to his feet and proclaiming that this picnic was the best ever. His only complaint being that he had eaten too much.

Why did she make everything taste so good? Then, one by one, each one of Mama's children in his own way would echo Papa's words.

Northern Hemisphere: First Meeting, October 1969 Southern Hemisphere: March 1970 OBJECTIVE: The Latter-day Saint woman should understand the truth that a marriage to endure after this life must be performed in a house of the Lord, and seek to attain an eternal marriage. INTRODUCTION In conversation with Elder William Clayton, the Prophet Joseph Smith told him what is now recorded in verses 1 through 4 of Section 131 of the Doctrine and Covenants pertaining to marriage. Section 132 is the revelation on celestial marriage, which includes plural marriage as well as eternal marriage. It is believed that the revelation on plural marriage was received as early as 1831, but Section 132 was committed to writing on July 12, 1843. (Joseph Smith, History of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints, Vol. 5, p. xxxi [commonly called Documentary History of the Church; hereafter referred to as DHC].) Inasmuch as the Lord by revelation has withdrawn the practice of plural marriage, it is only necessary to mention the principle in this lesson. (The Discourses of Wilford Woodruff, pp. 213-18.) It is well to remember that this practice is proper only when the Lord commands it. (D&C 49:15-17; Joseph Fielding Smith, Teachings of the Prophet Joseph Smith, pp. 323-24; Jac. 2:27-30.) The penalty for practicing plural marriage by a member of the Church today is excommunication, the same as for others who commit adultery. (Joseph F. Smith, Gospel Doctrine, p. 280.) Exaltation in the celestial kingdom requires eternal marriage but does not require plural marriage. In the revelation regarding eternal marriage, the expression "if a man marry a wife," or a similar expression, is used. (D&C 132:15, 18, 19, 26, 41.) IN THE BEGINNING In the beginning God ordained that marriage should be a part of earth experience for his sons and daughters. The following reveals that truth and also that this principle was intended as a part of the plan of salvation: And again, verily I say unto you, that whoso forbiddeth to marry is not ordained of God, for marriage is ordained of God unto man. Wherefore, it is lawful that he should have one wife, and they twain shall be one flesh, and all this that the earth might answer the end of its creation: And that it might be filled with the measure of man, according to his creation before the world was made. (D&C 49:15-17.) In discussing the marriage of Adam and Eve, Elder Harold B. Lee, of the Council of the Twelve, referred to its perpetuity, as follows: Here [Genesis 2:18, 22-24] was a marriage performed by the Lord between two immortal beings, for until sin entered the world their bodies were not subject to death. He made them one, not merely for time, nor for any definite period; they were to be one throughout the eternal ages. If you were to say that because Adam and Eve transgressed and became subject to death that this eternal union was broken, then just remember that the purpose of the atonement by Jesus Christ was to restore that which was lost by the fall. Their restoration then to each other after the resurrection would not require a re-marriage, for death to them was not a divorce; it was only a temporary separation. Resurrection to immortality meant for them a reunion and an eternal bond never again to be severed. "For as in Adam all die, even so in Christ shall all be made alive." (I Corinthians 15:22.) (Harold B. Lee, Youth and the Church, p. 128.) Lesson Department PURPOSE OF MARRIAGE There are some churches which maintain that celibacy is sanctioned by God. One of these churches in the days of the Prophet Joseph Smith was the Shaking Quakers. It was to a group of Latter-day Saint missionaries that the Lord revealed through the Prophet Joseph Smith, that marriage was ordained of him and that those who taught otherwise did not represent him. (D&C 49:15.) Marriage makes it possible for the earth to answer the end of its creation. Because man, as a premortal being, was designed to come to earth to work out his salvation, marriage was ordained to bring about this purpose. Consequently, the fundamental purpose of marriage is to beget children that the Lord's purposes may be fulfilled. (D&C 49:16-17.) MARRIAGE OUTSIDE OF THE CHURCH Should a Latter-day Saint young man or woman marry someone who is not a member of the Church? Leaders of the Church have counseled members to marry within the Church. (Discourses of Brigham Young, pp. 196-97; Joseph F. Smith, Gospel Doctrine, p. 279; David O. McKay, Gospel Ideals, p. 465.) Where the parties to a marriage are both strong in different faiths, the chances for happiness are greatly diminished. (John A. Widtsoe, Evidences and Reconciliations, pp. 237-38.) Sometimes young people feel that when they marry a nonmember, he or she will be able to convert him or her to the Church. July 1969 The chances for conversion and the consequent happiness in marriage are far greater if the conversion is made before marriage. (Harold B. Lee, Youth and the Church, p. 132.) The Lord has said that parents should teach their children correct principles to shape their lives for happiness. (D&C 68:25-28.) The mother has a wonderful opportunity in the home to impress upon her children the importance of marrying within the Church. Class Discussion How and when should a mother prepare her children for temple marriage? PURPOSE OF LIFE To work out one's salvation in this life and in the life to come is the paramount purpose of earth life. This means that the purpose of God in creating man is to grant to him, through obedience, the greatest measure of salvation—eternal life. (Moses 1:39.) As man is the child of God, so also man may, by obedience to law, become as the Father.

(D&C 132:20-22.) The blessing of eternal life comes to those who fulfill all the laws and commandments. OBEDIENCE TO THE GOSPEL

The gospel of Jesus Christ provides the way whereby the sons and daughters of God may return to his presence in the celestial kingdom. Baptism is the gate to that kingdom. (D&C 76:50-53.) There are degrees in the celestial kingdom, therefore, members of his Church should seek for the highest degree.

542 In the celestial glory there are three heavens or degrees; and in order to obtain the highest, a man must enter into this order or priesthood [meaning the new and everlasting covenant of marriage]; and if he does not, he cannot obtain it. He may enter into the other, but that is the end of his kingdom; he cannot have an increase.

(D&C 131:1-4.) The new and everlasting covenant is the gospel of Jesus Christ. There are many covenants within the gospel and marriage for eternity is one of them. To be fully obedient to the gospel involves full discipleship.

ETERNAL MARRIAGE

After a couple have received the higher ordinances of the priesthood endowment in the temple, they are qualified to receive eternal marriage. Only by the keys of the priesthood held by the President of the Church, who delegates these powers to others, may this union be solemnized. (D&C 132:7.)

The blessings available to those who marry for eternity are these: They shall pass by the angels and the gods which are set there to their exaltation and glory in all things as hath been sealed upon their heads which glory shall be a fulness and a continuation of the seeds forever and ever. (D&C 132:19.)

ETERNAL INCREASE

In Section 131 and in Section 132, verse 19, the Lord has told us that if faithful to the eternal marriage vows, the blessing of "increase" and "a continuation of the seeds forever and ever" will be received. The Prophet Joseph Smith gave the following information on this subject: Except a man and his wife enter into an everlasting covenant and be married for eternity, while in this probation, by the power and authority of the Holy Priesthood, they will cease to increase when they die; that is, they will not have any children after the resurrection. (DHC, Vol. 5, p. 391.)

The children born to an exalted man and woman after the resurrection are spirit beings. The parents are tangible, resurrected persons referred to in the scriptures as having spiritual bodies; that is, with spirit element in their veins rather than blood as in mortal bodies. (D&C 88:27; Alma 11:45; Brigham Young, Discourses of Brigham Young, p. 374.)

THE DEATHS

When resurrected beings are exalted they receive "eternal lives," the power of eternal increase, which is the opposite of the "deaths," or the lack of such power. (D&C 132:24-25.) Only exalted beings will be married in the future life because they proved themselves while on the earth in being faithful to all things required of them. (D&C 132:12.)

In the following verses, the conditions in the world to come are explained, having reference to those who receive marriage for eternity and to others who did not subscribe to the full law:

Therefore, if a man marry him a wife in the world, and he marry her not by me nor by my word, and he covenant with her so long as he is in the world and she with him, their covenant and marriage are not of force when they are dead, and when they are out of the world; therefore, they are not bound by any law when they are out of the world. Therefore, when they are out of the world they neither marry nor are given in marriage; but are appointed angels in heaven, which angels are ministering servants to minister for those who are worthy of a far more and an exceeding and an eternal weight of glory. For these angels did not abide my law; therefore, they cannot be enlarged, but remain separately and singly, without exaltation, in their saved condition, to all eternity; and from henceforth are not gods, but are angels of God forever and ever. HOMEMAKING—Development Through Homemaking Education
Woman's Role in Family Preparedness Discussion 1—"She Looketh Well to the Ways of Her Household." (Prov. 31:27)
Celestia J. Taylor
Northern Hemisphere: Second Meeting, October 1969
Southern Hemisphere: March 1970

OBJECTIVE: To show the need for family preparedness and the role of the Latter-day Saint woman in achieving it.

INTRODUCTION
Have any of us ever known a period of time when our leaders have not counseled, advised, and even urged us to prepare our homes and families that we might be self-sustaining in a time of need? The answer would certainly have to be in the negative. Why, then, are many of us so reluctant or so indifferent, or even so negligent that we fail to heed this counsel? Is it because we have lulled ourselves into a state of false security by using excuses—excuses which we can usually find if we are looking for them?

WHAT ARE OUR EXCUSES?
1. That we are not converted. Can we justify ourselves when we accept some of the teachings of our leaders and reject others?
2. That we hear the counsel but we are not ready to heed it. Can we attain salvation without working for it?
3. That there is no immediate need. Illness, accident, strikes, destructive weather (earthquakes, tornadoes, etc.), and other disasters could occur at any time.
4. That we are protected by the Church storehouses. These supplies are only designed to meet needs that go beyond what the family is able to do for itself.
5. That we have inadequate storage space. If there is a will, there is a way to overcome this problem.
6. That spoilage of stored food results in a waste of money and effort. Do we criticize the program rather than the method when unwise planning and improper storing are responsible for our losses?
7. That we cannot afford the extra expense. Careful planning and wise manipulation of income will overcome this difficulty.

WHY SHOULD WE BE PREPARED?
If there were no other reason than that our Church leaders have so counseled us, it would be the best reason in the world for obeying. In a talk given at a Brigham Young University Leadership Week, Elder Harold B. Lee made the following statement: The only safety and security there is in this Church is in listening to the words that come from the prophets of the Lord, as if from the mouth of the Lord himself. And they have spoken; they have told us to prepare, and it is not for us to argue whether we should or whether we should not. We have the prophets today telling us what our responsibility is here and now.... God help us not to turn deaf ears, but go out while the harvest is yet possible and build on a foundation such that when the rains descend, and the floods come, and the winds blow and beat on the house, our house will have stone walls. (BYU Leadership Week, June 16, 1953.)

President Tanner told of a conversation he had with a member of the Church regarding the importance of family preparedness. This person said "Well, you know, if I knew that this was going to happen next year, if that is when the emergency is going to take place, I would get mine in" —meaning the year's supply. President Tanner replied, "If IT were you I would wait for the emergency.... You remind me of the man who was complaining that he had been paying fire insurance for the last twenty years and he had never got anything out of it." (Welfare Conference, October 1, 1966.)

We pay fire insurance, certainly not hoping that some day our houses will burn down. We pay insurance on our lives hoping that we will long outlive the maturing of the policies. We take other precautions which we hope will insure us a happy and profitable life here on earth, and then we endanger it all by not heeding the warnings and counsel of our leaders regarding this most important phase of our living—preparedness.

President J. Reuben Clark gave us some wise and sound advice when he said: First, and above and beyond everything else, let us live righteously, fearing God and keeping his commandments.... [Second,] Let us avoid debt as we would a plague.... Let us straightly and strictly hve within our incomes, and save a little. Let every head of every household see to it that he has on hand enough food and clothing, and, where possible, fuel also, for at least a year ahead. (Conference Report, April 1937, p. 26.)

WHAT IS THE LATTER-DAY SAINT WOMAN'S ROLE?
In some instances women manage most of the family expenditures. Home management has become a business in itself. This intensifies our responsibility as Latter-day Saint wives and mothers in this important preparedness program.

We need, first of all, to be entirely converted to it, and then we should—if we have not already done so—proceed in a systematic and orderly way to make the necessary preparation, as our incomes will permit, without borrowing or going into debt.

CONCLUSION

An increased sense of security and a deep feeling of peace fill our hearts when we know that we are obeying the counsel of the prophets of the Lord and when we know that by doing so we can take care of those we love in any emergency. Few of us have known an extremity of want or suffering, but those who have experienced even minor emergencies recognize the wisdom of being prepared.

UNREST AND OPPOSITION

The attitude of unrest and opposition to established institutions, referred to by President Clark and so apparent throughout the world today, is giving parents, educators, and civic leaders much concern. They are considering probable causes and searching for remedies. Existing conditions convince us that no easy answer is available and that no generalization is adequate for the complex condition involving so many aspects.

President Clark states: Some may say this rebellion shows merely a wish for greater liberty; others may consider it a destructive demand for complete license. As frequently happens, neither extreme view seems wholly right. The truth probably lies somewhere between. (Ibid.)

A list of probable factors related to this attitude of revolt follows. President Clark has suggested the first three, others have been added, and still others may be suggested. Our concern in this discussion is to discover some areas where the home may be abdicating important responsibilities.

Question for Discussion

In your opinion, which of the following probable factors seem most likely to be responsible for this personal revolt? Discuss as time permits.

1. A general desire for more liberty.
2. A destructive demand for complete license—freedom from restraint.
3. A personal weakness which may cause an individual to want others to become as he is or to share his point of view.
4. A lack of constructive objectives that give purpose to life.
5. A desire to be different.
6. A wish to escape responsibility.
7. A basic lack of understanding of the value of law and order and of the need for personal restraint even in situations where changes and dissent may be justified.
8. A sincere mistrust by many idealistic young people of what they consider to be the hypocrisy and artificiality of accepted standards.

Revolt for this reason, if recognized for the idealism it is and properly channeled, can be transformed into a force for good.

Ad Points to Consider

In the search for cause and cure, may we not justly turn the searchlight upon the homes from which these individuals—these restless resisters of law—have come? We may ask such pertinent questions as these:

1. Is it possible that restrictive demands within the home have been excessive, or has there been a complete lack of restraint?

6. Has the home neglected to teach the need for law and the fact that obedience to law and order brings freedom?
7. Has faith in God and general religious training been absent in the home?

RESTRAINTS—WHY?

Certain familiar virtues, when developed and exercised, make us good citizens and dependable, upright individuals. These virtues are basic to constructive private life and have been found to be essential to an orderly society as a whole. We may enumerate some of them in such affirmative terms as honesty, respect for life and property of others, or in the negatively expressed commandments of the Decalogue, such as, thou shalt not kill, nor steal, nor commit adultery.

President Clark states: These virtues are named because, looking at mankind as a whole and at the Christian nations particularly, these characteristics seem to be basic to their civilization. They seem to be equally basic to any form of orderly society of which we can conceive. For if every person murdered and robbed and lied and loafed and caroused, if there were an indiscriminate association of the sexes, no ordered society could exist; and without such society we have scant ground for hoping and no ground for believing that humanity could escape becoming merely animal.

How much restraint and what type of conventions needed for an orderly social unit to persist may depend upon (1) the persons or group of people affected, (2) the state of their civilization and righteousness.

INHERENT IN ALL CREATION

Speaking of universal laws, President Clark states:

The great laws governing the universe—physical, intellectual, and spiritual—are coexistent with that universe as known to us; they are self-executory, that is, they enforce themselves; no one needs to enforce them. They operate independently of man's will, consent, or desire; they operate impartially and universally; every creature, human or animal, every other created thing, is subject to the applicable law.

It follows that whether we wish or do not wish restraint, yet restraint is inherent in all creation. Efforts to ignore restraint, or to evade it, or to destroy it, will meet with failure. (Immortality and Eternal Life, Vol. 2, lesson 7.)

The physical world is fully controlled by law; if it were not, it could not and would not exist. Heat, light, electricity, what we call matter—air, water, earth—all are subject to and controlled by law. Our bodies being matter are equally subject to great physical laws from none of which is there any escape. (Ibid., lesson 6.)

Question: How can a mother make use of her knowledge of these immutable laws in the training of small children?

President Clark suggests some practical illustrations for the operation of these physical laws. He continues:

If we walk off a cliff, we fall. It is immaterial whether we know the cliff is there, or whether we know we will fall if we walk off, or whether we desire to fall, or whether we step off the cliff by one foot or jump off by five feet; the result is the same and inevitable.

If we touch a hot stove, we are burned; ignorance of its heat, or an accidental touching, or our own preferences, or our lack of knowledge that hot stoves burn—all are immaterial. If we lay hold of a copper wire, heavily charged with electricity, we are killed; again neither ignorance nor accident nor preference will avail us.

Acid burns our flesh, water drowns us, poison gas kills us. These results all come from the operation of laws which are immutable; they execute themselves; they do not depend either upon our knowledge of their existence or upon our consent to their operation or upon our desire that they manifest themselves; the penalty inevitably follows the violation.

Discussion Questions: How may the relationship between the mother and child be affected if the mother (a) merely gives a negative command, (b) explains that a certain action will bring a certain result? One mother's reply: There is no exception unless some other law intervenes to soften or eliminate the penalty. For example, if a person wears asbestos gloves, he may be spared the burning effect of the hot stove. If a person steps off the cliff in a parachute, he may land safely upon the ground. The child thus gradually learns through experience and the verbal guidance of parent or other persons to walk freely and safely through many of the hazards of the physical world. He must, however, also learn—that this is a gradual process—that the effects of many physical laws must not be learned through personal experience if he is to survive physically. He must not shoot himself to learn that a bullet shot from a gun can kill.

From his close associates, especially the mother, the small child learns of many laws vicariously. He later may seek other authoritative sources.

Question: How can the mother establish a relationship that will build confidence in her judgment and admonition?

THE IMMEDIATE VERSUS THE DELAYED PENALTY

The penalty for violation of the immutable laws may be immediate, as in the foregoing examples, or they may be delayed for months or even many years. It is not difficult for adults to know and understand why this is so. It is, however, not always easy for them to keep the effects sufficiently in mind that they are willing to heed the warning effects of the law. It is far easier to comply with the restraints of the law when the penalty is in immediate sight.

Would many persons yield to the habit of smoking if the possible cancer result were always an immediate penalty?

For children, it is even more difficult to maintain interest in a far-away penalty. Discussion Statement: The more mature a person becomes, the more willing he is to comply with the restraints of physical laws where the results are not immediate.

There are innumerable laws in the physical world about which we know very little at present. It is to discover these laws and their effects that research science is making such great effort today, in extensive areas of life. Much has been discovered already.

We have centered our discussion thus far upon the immutable laws which affect our physical beings. "It is inconceivable," LIFE'S AFTERNOON Lesson Department states President Clark, "that affairs of the mind and spirit of men, embracing the moral and spiritual world, are not also controlled by law." (Ibid.)

(This aspect of the laws which affect our lives will be treated more fully in the succeeding lesson.)

We have focused our discussion thus far upon the effects of violating specific natural laws. We must not overlook the truth that obedience to the law brings affirmative rewards.

President Clark phrases this idea beautifully in the following paragraph:

On the other hand, the law operates just as certainly in its blessings as in its penalties. If we avoid the cliff, we do not fall; if we do not touch the stove, we are not burned; if we leave the charged electric wire alone, we are not injured.

So in intellectual and spiritual matters: if we do not offend, we suffer no injury. But in intellectual and spiritual matters, obedience to law brings not only the blessings of mere absence of penalty but also increased power and strength, growth and development, bringing us nearer to the ultimate goal pronounced by the Master: "Be ye therefore perfect, even as your Father which is in heaven is perfect." [Matt. 5:48.]

And this truth is the wonder and the glory of our intellectual and spiritual lives.

The Book of Mormon warns us against contention in the eleventh chapter of 3 Nephi, verse 29: "For verily, verily I say unto you, he that hath the spirit of contention is not of me, but is of the devil, who is the father of contention, and he stirreth up the hearts of men to contend with anger, one with another." One of the cues as to what is essentially wrong with contention is given in the phrase "with anger." Too often our opposition is expressed in emotional, exaggerated charges and counter-charges. In such a state we are not receptive to the whisperings of the Spirit. The Holy Ghost is not able to penetrate the wall of strong feeling which we have erected. We haven't ears to hear the truth.

A dispute is not even a detour toward truth; it becomes an end in itself, a dead end. We quickly become less concerned with finding answers than we are in winning arguments. How rarely anyone is listening in an argument. We wait impatiently while our opponent speaks so we can stun him with our side as soon as he finishes.

The opposition Mr. Lippmann finds "indispensable" has little to do with mere disputation. Rather, it is the presentation and consideration of alternatives that test and refine our own beliefs. Truth has nothing to fear from error and since the very word "definition" suggests pointing out that something is not as well as what it is, we can focus and sharpen our perceptions by contrast as well as comparison.

It must be remembered, however, that Mr. Lippmann is talking primarily about secular truth. That to which the Spirit has testified to us is not illuminated by error.

Discussion Question: How can we usually tell when a fruitful discussion is turning into a fruitless disputation?

"What Is Liberty?" Woodrow Wilson, twenty-seventh President of the United States, in his essay "What Is Liberty" presents an attractive picture of a harmonious society in which each part is adjusted to and balanced against every other one.

It is important to remember, in reacting positively to such a concept, that such an adjustment might be temporarily attained where the basis for the social group is almost wholly evil, that is, men are mere cogs in a machine.

"ANIMAL FARM"
George Orwell's "Animal Farm" is a convincing and devastating satire on a communist attempt to make "all animals equal." There are few endings in English fiction as effective as the concluding paragraph of this novel. What is most heartrending is that, to the exploited animals who are gazing in astonishment at the violent argument going on between the pigs and men, whatever the outcome of the arguments, they are the true losers.

Tyranny, finally, always wears the same face and has the same victims.

Discussion Question
What is the basic difference between the harmony and adjustment we seek in an organization such as the Relief Society and that sought after in the grouping described in "Animal Farm"?

OTHER POEMS
Poems by Henry David Thoreau and James Oppenheim stress the importance of peace of mind and personal integrity in the meaning we can individually give to liberty. Of course, we must understand that the Latter-day Saint definition of liberty is in accord with the Lord's definition of liberty.

Thoreau's "Independence" is as follows:
My life more civil is and free
Than any civil polity.
Ye Princes, keep your realms and circumscribed power,
Not wide as are my dreams,
Nor rich as is this hour.
What can ye give which I have not?
What can ye take which I have got?
Can ye defend the dangerless?
Can ye inherit nakedness?
To all true wants Time's ear is deaf,
Penurious States lend no relief
Out of their pelf:
But a free soul—thank God—
Can help itself.

Be sure your fate
Doth keep apart its state,—
Not linked with any band,
Even the noblest in the land,—
In tended fields with cloth of gold
No place doth hold.
But is more chivalrous than they are, and sigheth for a nobler war;
A finer strain its trumpet rings,
A brighter gleam its armour flings.
The life that I aspire to live,
No man proposeth me;

No trade upon the street
Wears its emblazonry.

Along with his friend and mentor Ralph Waldo Emerson, Thoreau believed strongly in a self-reliance which was, ultimately, a reliance upon God. The fervor of Thoreau in this poem seems out of place if we do not remember this. It is a little unfair to think of Thoreau as the great rebel in American literature and it is even more unfair to believe that the group of which Thoreau and Emerson were a part—the Transcendentalists—were characterized by personal eccentricity and harsh independence.

What all the Transcendentalists insisted upon was the necessity for each person to find out for himself what he believed in. They were sure that men of faith and good will would finally come to essential agreement, since God is the author of all truth but they felt strongly that each person must come to that unity in his own way.

In this poem Thoreau tries to reject all the crutches which men have tried to use to avoid facing the truth and establishing a personal liberty based on that truth.

James Oppenheim's "The Slave" is equally committed to the necessity for each man to free himself before he can conceive the meaning of liberty.
Discussion Question
According to the gospel, what is freedom and when is man free?

"THE BRIDE COMES TO YELLOW SKY"
This selection by Stephen Crane has been praised as an implicit satire on an idealized West, as a major example of literary realism; or as simply a memorable story. Crane himself felt it was a "daisy" of a story. It is surely all of these but, in addition, few narratives equal its sensitive probing of the nature of freedom.

In Walter Lippmann's article we see the part played by opposition in clarifying liberty. In "The Bride Comes to Yellow Sky" we are helped to understand the necessary connection between freedom and responsibility.

There is no talk of freedom in this tale. The characters we meet are not given to philosophizing, but their instinct is sure. Even Scratchy Wilson, blurred by whiskey and unfaltered rage, is sobered and calmed by Jack Potter's announcement that he is now married.
