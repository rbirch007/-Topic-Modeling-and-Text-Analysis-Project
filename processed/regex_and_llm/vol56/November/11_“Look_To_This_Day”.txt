@ The Christmas season is just around the corner, bringing with it excitement, happiness, memories, traditions, caring, sharing, loving, and gift giving.
What a lovely custom the giving of gifts can be, how beautiful the event it symbolizes, but are we somehow losing sight of its true significance?
Unfortunately for some people, Christmas with its gift giving has become a time of frustration, of mental, physical, and financial strain, rather than a joyous time of family fun, traditions, peace, and good will.
Gifts of themselves are not important; it is the desire to give that counts. Christmas should be a time to think of others, to draw close to friends and family, to be mindful of those less fortunate than we, to give of ourselves.

Are we as mothers making lasting lovely memories for our children, establishing traditions, by teaching them by precept and example the joy of giving of themselves?
My husband remembers with pleasure and a tear in his eye the thrill he felt as a young boy sharing gifts from his mother's lovely kitchen, carrying in his little red wagon bottled fruit, jams, jellies, fruitcake or homemade breads to the needy in his neighborhood.
Our children contend that the happiest Christmas they remember was the year we joined with my sister's family in preparing Christmas carols; then, following Church service on Christmas Eve, going to the homes of neighbors, family, friends, and some who were homebound, caroling our hearts out. Our children do not remember the gifts they received that year, but they do remember the joy of giving of themselves.

One mother's gifts to her family are beautiful hand-knit Christmas socks, every one with the name of a family member boldly knitted into its heading.
A priceless gift, a treasured tradition.
