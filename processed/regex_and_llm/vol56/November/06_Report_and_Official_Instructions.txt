President Belle S. Spafford [Delivered at the Officers Meeting of the Relief Society Annual General Conference, October 1, 1969]

Once again it becomes my duty to present to you a brief report of the status of Relief Society, together with a few General Board instructions and recommendations.

The latest available figures are those from the 1967-68 annual report, since the 1968-69 reports were not due in the General Board Office until September 20 and, of course, are not audited and compiled as yet. In order to make the information somewhat more current, growth figures for the previous year will be used in estimating present conditions. Precise figures will be available to you when the compiled report for the fiscal year is published in a forthcoming issue of The .

We mourn the loss, by death, of two valiant and eminently capable members: Evon W. Peterson, General Secretary-Treasurer, and Mildred B. Eyring.

With the growth of the Church, the number of stake and mission Relief Society organizations has grown. There were as 816 of September 1968, 462 stake and 74 mission Relief Society organizations, with 328 mission districts and more than 6,500 ward and branch Societies.

For the past several years there has been consistent growth in membership, but there still remain far too many Latter-day Saint women not enrolled who need Relief Society. We, therefore, appeal to officers and members alike to intensify their efforts in enrolling new members. The membership appeals are almost endless. There are, in reality, few women who cannot be reached by one or the other of these.

Estimating the membership growth for 1968-69 to be the same as for the previous fiscal year (or 11,000), there is now a total membership of 322,500. Of this number 3,500 are non-Latter-day Saints.

Each year we receive gratifying reports of non-Latter-day Saint Relief Society members becoming baptized members of the Church.

It is our conviction that there are comparatively few enrolled members who do not attend some meetings during the year. We must recognize that under certain circumstances a sister may be unable to attend regardless of her desire to do so; for example, personal or family illness may interfere, or the infirmities of age or the hazards of winter roads where distances to the meeting place require car travel. Nonetheless, attendance is a continuing concern and ward presidents should be urged to do all in their power to interest women in regular attendance and to help make it possible for them to attend.

Second session Relief Societies are a boon to countless women, as are well-managed nurseries for mothers with young children.

The General Board requests that great emphasis be placed upon the compassionate service program which is fundamental in the accomplishment of the purposes of the Society and which, according to the reports, seems to be somewhat on a plateau insofar as volume is concerned. Today's troubled, costly, and insecure world, with its measure of tragedy in the lives of many faithful sisters and good families, calls upon us deeply to understand our responsibilities in this area, and to respond to needs with readiness, competency, and love.

It is our opinion that days care of the sick, which declined by more than 5,000 eight-hour days in 1967-68, have not taken any significant upturn during the 1968-69 year. We suggest that you report and official instructions exercise watchcare so that Relief Society will not falter where this service is needed in the home.

Figures on visits to the sick also have remained somewhat constant—between 395,000 and 400,000—as have the number of hours of other compassionate services, which were approximately 750,000 hours. Let us be sensitive to all such needs.

We particularly call your attention to the desirability of Relief Society presidencies calling on a family at the time of a death and making available to it, if desired, the services of the Society in connection with the funeral. We estimate that 1,000 bodies were dressed for burial during the 1968-69 year, and assistance including the providing of food was given in connection with more than 12,000 funerals. Such service is usually welcomed and is comforting and helpful.

At times, our sisters need to be reminded of the value of going the second mile in rendering voluntary humanitarian service as individuals—on their own.

I call attention to an important program which is proving of value in our Latter-day Saint hospitals. I refer to the hospital Volunteer Auxiliary program, sometimes referred to as the "Pink Lady" program. We encourage sisters who have time, health, and an aptitude for service in a hospital setting, where there is a variety of work situations, to contact your local Latter-day Saint hospitals. Full information will be readily given you.

We commend the stakes who have participated in the Red Cross Home Nurse Training programs. Approximately 5,426 women have completed this course, made available to them through the cooperative efforts of the American Red Cross and the Relief Society.

Relief Society sisters consistently support the Church Welfare Program with ward presidents having made approximately 100,000 family visits under the direction. Of their respective bishops, and 94,000 other contacts during the past year. Relief Society members contributed during the year 756,000 hours on Church Welfare projects.

It is estimated that there were close to 5 million visits made to homes during the 1968-69 fiscal year by more than 148,000 visiting teachers. We increasingly see their value as an aid to the bishop in ascertaining cases of temporal need among worthy Latter-day Saint families and also as an aid to him through the Home Teachers in reactivating inactive members.

Visiting teachers are a great asset to Relief Society itself in bringing women who are enrolled into membership and activity. The visiting teaching program has value in fellowshiping new converts to the Church, most of whom soon make excellent visiting teachers themselves, thus enlarging our reservoir of teachers.

Let us aim to enlarge our visiting teaching program and continually improve the quality of the visit and attendance at the visiting teacher meeting, as well as the quality of the confidential report to the ward president.

The spiritual living lessons for the forthcoming season conclude the course based on the Doctrine and Covenants. It is expected that by the time this course is concluded in May 1970, members will have thoughtfully read the entire Doctrine and Covenants in order that they may enjoy fully the spirit of this book of scripture, increase their knowledge and understanding of the revealed truths contained in the revelations, and be guided by them in their day-to-day activities.

Visiting teaching messages correlate with the Family Home Evening Manual and are designed to renew our faith and guide us as we meet life from day to day. The social relations lessons for the coming year are based again upon the writings of the late President J. Reuben Clark, Jr., and are correlated with the Melchizedek Priesthood course of study.

The cultural refinement course will stress the individual and human values as seen through literature, art, and music, widening the scope by emphasizing a woman's responsibilities in community life and the world in which she lives.

LESSONS FOR THE USE OF LAMANITE WARDS AND BRANCHES The General Board is pleased to announce that special lessons for Lamanite Relief Societies are now ready for distribution. This new set of Lamanite lessons has been prepared so as to fit into any standard three-ring binder. The set is wrapped in cellophane and includes sheets of visual aids following each lesson. The cost for a set is $3 postpaid. They are to be ordered from the office of the General Board.

Exclusive of the homemaking discussions that are prepared by the General Board, activities for the homemaking meeting are planned by the local Relief Societies with the General Board setting the purposes of the meeting and giving general guidelines. It is the rule of the stake and mission district board to suggest, display, and demonstrate suitable items which might be produced or demonstrations which appropriately might be conducted. This year's estimate of number of items produced is in excess of 114 million.
