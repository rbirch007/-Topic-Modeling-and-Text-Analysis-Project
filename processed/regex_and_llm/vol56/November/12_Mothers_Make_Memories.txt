Most every mother appreciates gifts which have been prepared or made especially for her and her family—a loaf of stollen bread for Christmas morning breakfast, a favorite dessert set in holly-decorated paper cups, a plate of choice cookies, a holiday salad, a popcorn cake, or special handmade gifts from family members or dear friends. There are so many lovely yet simple and inexpensive items which can be made for Christmas giving.

The little family of a young friend are sure that Christmas is near when the handmade Christmas pillow cases are found on their freshly made beds as they turn down their covers. Such fun for the children, yet so simple to make from a few yards of Christmas design yardage. Children love to be remembered. Since Christmas means so much to children why not try some of the following ideas? They cost little but the pleasure they give cannot be measured.

My daughter, living in the Susquehanna Stake, has made her 835 friends and the children in her neighborhood happy by giving "clip cookies" for holiday festivity. These are made with decorated cookies in the shape of Santas, Christmas trees, stars, or other shapes, wrapped in long strips of plastic wrap, tied individually with bows of ribbon or yarn. A pair of inexpensive blunt scissors is attached.

Another young mother of four covers the crooks of candy canes with a reindeer head cut from red and green felt. She keeps them on a table in her front hall ready to pass out to any small guest who may come to her home.

Have you tried making a cookie tree? They are such fun at Christmas time and for other holidays as well. Gingerbread men, decorated and tantalizing, draped from each limb, will appeal to any child. A one-inch dowel 30 inches in height is used as the trunk of the tree (a broom handle can serve very well, too). Most carpenters and many fathers have the equipment to drill the small holes for the dowels serving as branches. You will need five one-fourth inch dowels, ranging from 7 to 23 inches (7, 11, 15, 19, and 23). A five-inch block of wood can serve as its base. Glue parts securely. Sand lightly. Cover with red or green paint and your cookie tree is ready. You will use it again and again.

To Feruzzi I am indebted for my attraction to this special field of art. I am especially fond of his choice of model for his painting. Of all the Madonnas I have seen, this is the one I find most convincing. Her angelic expression as she gazes upward is a prayer in itself and I am reminded of the scripture, "But Mary kept all of these things, and pondered them in her heart." (Luke 2:19.) Also, I love the way Feruzzi has positioned the child, sleeping against the mother's shoulder. This popular version was repeated in 1962 in color, and I doubt I shall ever grow tired of it.

A print in monochrome which has endeared itself to me is Murillo's "Mother and Child," which appeared in 1955. Bartolomé Esteban Murillo was a Spanish painter of the seventeenth century. He holds a kind of record in that three of his religious paintings have been featured on Magazine covers since I began observing.

I am partial to each one. "The Virgin and Child" which appeared in 1960 shows a mother of exquisite skin and fairness. "Adoration of the Shepherds" which was used in 1966 captures on canvas that thrilling emotion which tiny infants inspire. One has the feeling that the baby has just been bathed and is so soft and sweet! Of all the infants depicted, this is the one I wish most to pick up and hold. At the same time, there is an aura of holiness that keeps me at a distance. "The Rest on the Flight into Egypt," by Gerard David, the 1958 December cover was the first reproduction to appear in full color. And what an exciting combination of contrasts! I think the lithographer must have been very pleased with the results of this screening. I don't know how it compares with the original, but the reproduction is a masterpiece in its own right. Mary's dress is regal blue with a red sleeve and skirt just showing. Her head covering is a subtle shade of gray that looks purple in the shadows of the folds. She is holding a bunch of grapes for the Child Jesus. Fra Filippo Lippi, whose "Madonna and Child" was used in December 1959 is of interest to me because of the number of Madonna scenes in one version or another I have encountered by this gifted artist. He was the first in Italian art to picture the Madonna in the form of a young and attractive Florentine woman. The particular "Madonna and Child" used on the 1959 cover is part of the Samuel H. Kress Collection which hangs in the National Gallery of Art in Washington, D.C. Another painting I would love to see in the original is the "Madonna and Child" by Crivelli, December cover for 1965. I have two copies of this print and each is very different from the other both in textural effect and shading. I have no idea which is closer to the original. Crivelli is supposed to have contrived a type of devotional picture which is never mistaken for the work of another. At the same time, his use of tempera medium—powdered colors tempered, or held in solution by the yolk of eggs has never been surpassed. This is a method which calls for a very precise touch and does not lend itself to easy corrections. When properly applied there results a brilliancy of color impossible to obtain in oil. With indescribable patience, Crivelli delicately stroked his surfaces into embossed patterns which resemble the vitreous effects of enamelware. Curiously enough, I have been to the Metropolitan Museum of Art in New York, where the Crivelli is part of the Jules S. Bache Collection. Perhaps I have already seen the Crivelli! Great paintings to be enjoyed must be seen again and again. One need not be a scholar to become sensitive to paintings. He need only be willing to devote time, attention, and feelings. And for this, one needs frequent opportunities to see paintings. Ideally, we would all like to see or even own originals, but this is usually out of the question. "In default of originals," said Thomas Craven, editor of A Treasury of Modern Masterpieces, "there is but one consolation—the print—preferably the color print." He dreamed of a day when paintings would be as popular, and prints would circulate as freely as magazines. How pleased I think he would be to see a display of covers with reproductions which have circulated as part of the Magazine! In addition, these reproductions are potential missionary tools.
