The Relief Society Poem Contest

The Relief Society Poem Contest opens with this announcement and closes November 15, 1969. Prizes will be awarded as follows: 
1st prize. ----------= $40
2nd prize ------------ $30
3rd prize -------------- $20

Prize poems will be published in the April 1970 issue of The Relief Society Magazine. Prize-winning poems become the property of the Relief Society General Board and may not be published by others except upon written permission from the General Board. The General Board reserves the right to publish any of the other poems submitted, paying for them at the time of publication at the regular Magazine rates.

Rules for the contest:
1. This contest is open to all Latter-day Saint women, exclusive of members of the Relief Society General Board and employees of the Relief Society General Board.
2. Only one poem may be submitted by each contestant.
3. The poem must not exceed fifty lines and should be typewritten, if possible. Where this cannot be done, it should be legibly written. Only one side of the paper is to be used. (A duplicate copy of the poem should be retained by contestants to insure against loss.)
4. The sheet on which the poem is written is to be without signature or other identifying marks.
5. No explanatory material or picture is to accompany a poem.
6. Each poem is to be accompanied by a stamped envelope on which is written the contestant's name and address. Nom de plumes are not to be used.
7. A signed statement is to accompany the poem submitted certifying:
a. That the author is a member of The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.
b. That the poem (state title) is the contestant's original work. That it has never been published. That it is not in the hands of an editor or other person with a view to publication.
c. That it will not be published nor submitted elsewhere for publication until the contest is decided.

8. A writer who has received the first prize for two consecutive years must wait two years before she is again eligible to enter the contest.
9. The judges shall consist of one member of the General Board, one person from the English department of an educational institution, and one person who is a recognized writer. In case of complete disagreement among the judges, all poems selected for a place by the various judges will be submitted to a specially selected committee for final decision.

And so it is with children. Some seem to be thus blessed as they enter in on this tightrope life
well slippered, testing each step, seemingly gifted with a balance all their own.
And though they slip to one side or the other and we hold our breath,
they always seem to know how to right themselves.
It is with excited joy and anticipation we see them perform.
And many are the hurrahs.

And then there are those children who enter as though wearing hiking boots, and act as the clown
who performs clumsily—slipping, sliding, arms and legs askew.
We are at times caused to laugh, yet when we see this young one lose his balance our hearts reach out
and achingly we ask ourselves "Oh, what can I do?"
"What can I say?" "How can I help, don't you know how important you are?"

And then he falls into the net of life and walks about with each step sinking in that net to
the height it was before the step was taken—not knowing quite how to get out.
And few are the hurrahs.

Again, we are caused to ask searchingly of ourselves "What could I have done to prevent the fall?"

And then I imagine myself having a rod to hold out for him and others to take hold of until they find firm ground again.

While my older children were yet quite young, I taught many Mutual Improvement Association classes
and became aware of the fact that those young people whose step seemed so sure
seemed always to carry the balance rod with the words emblazoned on it:
I know where I came from!
I know who I am!
I know where I'm going!

And I marveled. Often these young people were either converts or children of recent converts
to The Church of Jesus Christ of Latter-day Saints.
And it often puzzled me and caused me to ponder "How, when my children are born under the covenant—
how can [I instill in them this same strength?"

And a prayer would be uttered in my heart. "Oh, Father in heaven, what can I say now while my children are yet pliable and young

They must learn from mistakes, I know, but what can I do now that will give them that innate sense of balance

We felt tears sting our eyes and wondered if we really appreciated being born into the Church. How could we be so lucky?
Lucky? Was it luck or had someone paid such a price for us once?

We determined as a family to trace back all our genealogical lines and find the one in each line
to whom the gospel had been preached first.
What had it cost each one to accept the truth? Was it difficult?

We divided into four teams—each team determined to trace one of their grandparent's lines. I had most of the histories put away—somewhere.

Cally was surprised at her calm as she assembled baby food and dishes so Miriam would have no difficulty finding something for the baby's meals.

Miriam and the police car arrived simultaneously, so Cally grabbed her raincoat, gave a brave smile to her friend's encouraging embrace, and stumbled into the rain.

The typical hospital odor brought back to Cally's mind that her husband was lying somewhere within, unconscious, maybe dying. Panic once again overwhelmed her. She had hoped to be allowed to be with him, but he was in the emergency room. She was shown to what would be his room.
The second hand on the clock seemed to take at least five minutes to complete each cycle around the clock.

A doctor leaned into the room and glanced around.
"Are you Mrs. MacMillan?"
"Yes."
"Please come with me."

Cally followed him to a small office.
"Mrs. MacMillan, your husband has been seriously injured. It is imperative that he undergo immediate surgery in order to save his life. We need your permission to perform this surgery."
He took a form from a drawer and pushed it toward her.
"If you'll sign this standard form, we'll proceed immediately. This explains that we have your permission to do what we feel is necessary even though this action might result in the death of your husband."

"Death?"
Cally didn't want her husband to die—and Cally's dulled reasoning abilities heard this man asking her permission to do something to Glen which might result in his death.
"Mrs. MacMillan, your husband has very little chance of living if we do not operate. This is just a legal precaution to protect the hospital and doctors. Please sign it."

She took the pen and signed her name.
The printing was hazy through her tears as her emotions finally found vent. There were no violent sobs or cries—only one or two tears sliding down her cheeks.

The doctor was gentle as he touched her shoulder, signaling that they must hurry.
He escorted her back to Glen's empty room, then hurried away.

Cally stood at the window and watched the rain continue to pelt the earth. It was the kind of drizzle that could—and probably would—go on all day. The gray ceiling of clouds seemed oppressively low.

The doctor had told her that it would be at least an hour and a half before she could have any word.
She walked over and closed the door.
She didn't like the sounds of people walking and talking as if nothing was wrong.
The helplessness of her situation weighed heavily upon her.

Cally could only sit and stare at the rain.

When the door opened again, Cally didn't hear it, because she was asleep.

Glen's bed was rolled in, accompanied by nurses and aides who also wheeled in the standard, bearing life-sustaining blood, liquids, and nourishment.
Glen's eyes were closed, too. His head and one hand were bandaged.
You couldn't see his chest where broken ribs were held in place with adhesive bandages.
There were no other visible cuts or bruises—one wouldn't have thought this man had been near death.

Finally, the room was left quiet with only three occupants: one stood and watched;
one slept in exhaustive fear, the other in drugged and mercifully painless sleep.

There is a family of several boys and girls. The father is a leader in the Church in his area. The mother, sometimes active, finds more and more excuses for not attending Church. She is critical of her bishop and other ward leaders. She 'finds fault with her stake president and many of the General Authorities. She is jealous and envious, belittling others in order to try to enhance her own position.  
Now the children are grown. The boys, some of whom have gone on missions, follow the good example of their devoted, faithful father. The girls without exception have married outside (Joseph Joubert in Pensées.) the Church, moved to large cities where they are inactive, two of them refusing to let their children be baptized.  
Such problem people are not new. Alma recognized in his day that:  
The wickedness of the church was a great stumbling block to those who did not belong to the church; and thus the church began to fail in its progress.... And he saw also that the example of the church began to lead those who were unbelievers on from one piece of iniquity to another. (Al. 4:10-11.)  
Once a great sinner himself, Alma perceived that in the Church there was inequality, proud people considering themselves better than others and thus despising them, turning their backs on the needy, the naked, the hungry, the thirsty, the sick and afflicted, wearing costly apparel, envying, persecuting others, being full of malice and in conflict. And Alma said, "Now this was a great cause for lamentations among the people." (AL. 4:13.)  
There were others who were humble, succored the needy, fed the hungry, and suffered afflictions for Christ's sake. The general condition, however, was so bad that Alma gave up the judgment seat to work solely with the Church. Are there any of these problems among us? If so, what should we do?  
Jacob, speaking to the Nephites, said: Wherefore, ye shall remember your children, how that ye have grieved their hearts because of the example that ye have set before them; and also, remember, that ye may, because of your filthiness, bring your children unto destruction, and their sins be heaped upon your heads at the last day. (Jac. 3:10.)  
In later years when the dedicated sons of Mosiah were traveling to preach to the Lamanites, the Lord said to them, "Go forth among the Lamanites, thy brethren, and establish my word; yet ye shall be patient in longsuffering and afflictions, that ye may show forth good examples unto them in me, and I will make an instrument of thee in my hands unto the salvation of many souls." (Al. 17:11.)  
The Apostle Paul advised, "... Be thou an example of the believers, in word, in conversation, in charity, in spirit, in faith, in purity." (1 Tim. 4:12.)  
Modern and ancient lives teach us that our example can help others lead faithful, charitable lives or can lead them to forsake the counsels of God.  
Discussion 2�"She Perceiveth That Her Merchandise Is Good: Her Candle Goeth Not Out by Night." (Proverbs 31:18) Celestia J. Taylor. Northern Hemisphere: Second Meeting, November 1969 Southern Hemisphere: April 1970 OBJECTIVE: To show that buying wisely and saving regularly are basic to good money management.  
INTRODUCTION In the complicated rhythm of today's world, it becomes increasingly evident that these vital, basic values in the home and its management: the patterns which establish our way of life and standard of living, the goals which determine the use of our energies and resources, the choices which govern our spending and saving�all originate in the home. Only to the degree that we develop our ability to meet these challenges efficiently and wisely, will we be successful home managers.  
Since our primary concern this year is a program of family preparedness, we need to know how to utilize our resources to the best advantage by wise buying and regular saving. Not many people are in a position to buy a full supply of food, clothing, and other necessities at once, but by working out a regular program of buying a little at a time, it is surprising how the stockpile will grow. The sense of satisfaction that we feel in seeing food and other needed supplies on our shelves is much the same as that which we feel at having money in the bank.  
DEVELOPING OUR SHOPPING SKILLS Buying wisely is an art which must be learned and practiced if we are to make the best use of our resources. We need to consider the amount of time, energy, and money we have and decide how best to use them in supplying our needs and achieving our goals. The following Lesson Department suggestions may be used as guidelines:  
1. We should anticipate our needs. If we plan ahead of time, we can shop at our convenience, we can take time to find what we want, and we can avoid impulse buying which can upset our budget.  
2. We need to know how much we can afford to spend. This will help us to control our spending and limit it to those things which best serve our purpose. It will also prevent indiscriminate spending.  
3. We need to make a practical shopping list. This will help us to select the items which we want and need. We should let the list govern what we buy.  
A. When shopping for food we should consider the likes and dislikes of our family members. Even food which is to be stored should appeal to the appetites of the family.  
B. When shopping for basic supplies we should buy in quantity and quality suitable to our purpose.  
4. We should compare prices and values in different stores. Merchandise, prices, and values vary.  
5. We should take advantage of sales. Sales offer us an opportunity to purchase provisions at reduced prices and get good values for our money.  
A. We should know what we want and need. Aimless shopping often results in waste.  
B. We should know the original price of sale items to be sure of getting real value.  
C. We should be sure of the quality of sales goods. Some stores stock merchandise for sales which is not of the same quality as their general run of merchandise.  
D. We should know when to shop the sales. Watch advertisements for the most advantageous time to buy needed items. End-of-month and end-of-season sales generally offer the best values. Keep in mind that nothing is a bargain unless needed.

Paying as we go eliminates additional carrying costs. 7. We should buy in quantity when possible and advantageous. 8. We should know how and take time to read labels, seals, contracts, warranties, guarantees, and certifications. 9. We should make intelligent use of reliable consumer buying information. 10. We should establish agreeable business relationships. It helps to become acquainted with the business firms where we shop regularly and to deal fairly and honestly with all who serve us.

CONCLUSION
Learning to shop wisely not only helps us to add to our store of provisions, but it also represents a definite saving of our resources of time, energy, and money. It is our responsibility as well as our opportunity to be efficient managers of our financial spending and saving.
