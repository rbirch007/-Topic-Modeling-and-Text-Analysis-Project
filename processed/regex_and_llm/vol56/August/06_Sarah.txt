Happy? M

Happiness is an abstract quality difficult to define because it has so many meanings; paradoxical in nature because it is present in the least expected places and absent where it appears most likely to be found; elusive because, like the butterfly, it alights wherever it so inclines, but escapes the grasp of those who seek to capture it. It is a quality much to be desired, the easiest, yet the most difficult to attain.

One of the expressions familiar to us and, therefore, often quoted: "Men are that they might have joy," contains both the secret and the key to the attainment of this most precious commodity. Just being here on this earth does not guarantee us happiness, but it offers us the possibility of attaining it. Its possession, as that of any other worthwhile goal, is dependent upon the laws which govern it, and wherever there is a law there is a responsibility. This is the secret. The key lies in the fulfillment of whatever that responsibility may be.

How shall we define this responsibility? Certainly not by listing set rules and forms or by quoting high-sounding platitudes, which, though well-meaning, bear little or no relation to reality.
