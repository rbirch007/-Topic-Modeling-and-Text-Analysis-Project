"You must hope that you've given her enough background that she'll stop herself from disobedience," Olga continued. Then she smiled warmly and reached for my hand. "She loves you. She—loves you, Maggie. You're all she's ever had and she loves you."

I hoped that was true. How I hoped that was true.

"That evening Kimberly came home late after the game. When she dashed upstairs to change her clothes for the dance, she did not say a word about the all-night canyon party. The moments passed slowly and awkwardly until John's knock finally shook the door, alternating with a few fisticuffs on the doorbell.

"Hello, Mrs. Grayson," he smiled, his white teeth striding his face like a picket fence. "Hello, John..." For a moment I had a wild thought that there might be an opportunity to talk to John—to tell him how I felt— to insist that he bring Kimberly in by midnight. But there was only a split second of hello before Kim flashed down the stairs looking like a princess.

Her hair was heaped in a shining crown set off by small curls around her face. Her eyes were sparkling. Her chiffon dress flew in a rhythmic cloud behind her. "Wow!" John grinned. She really was devastating. "We have to hurry, Mama. John is in charge of getting the band set up. Bye."

I had time only to smile and say goodbye. But I stopped them at the door. "I'll be waiting up for you, Kim... Midnight?" I smiled, trying to hide my pounding heart, my own fears.

She looked at John briefly; her eyes narrowed and dimmed. It was a cold moment. John didn't say a word. She gave me only one last look. I stood at the door while they moved out quietly, quickly. "Bye," was all she managed. "Bye, Mrs. Grayson," John finally said in a thin voice.

"Have a good time," I called after them as they swept down the walk and into the waiting chariot drawn by six cylinders and an infinitely noisy horsepower. The house was quiet and empty when I turned into it.

Behind the dining room one of the French screens was caught by a slight breeze and clapped the lawn chair out on the patio. I walked to the tall windows and shut them tightly. The clock chimed an noisy 8:30. I looked out onto the dimly lit lawn and felt a crushing feeling of loneliness and defeat. Olga's words alone seemed to buoy me up. "The one to fear is not Kimberly, but God. You have your own salvation to worry about even before you have Kimberly's." Twenty minutes before midnight I closed the covers of the novel in my lap and grew suddenly conscious of the loud clatter of the living room clock above the fireplace. Its usually quiet tick seemed deafening to me. I switched on the television set to fill up the emptiness. But I could not focus on the blur of black and white figures cavorting on the screen before me. I could think only of Kimberly. I remembered how she cried when we could not bring home one of the little beagle pups at the fair eight summers ago. I saw her turn into a flurry of arms and legs when she lost her four-year-old temper at bedtime. I saw her shaking with sobs when I left her with the baby sitter one important evening. There were many times we had come to grips with one another. And each time we had lost a little, yet gained so much more. We had learned that there were some things we must do, or must not do or have, no matter how much our wants or needs were in opposition. And we had learned that each crisis passes. The music from the television roared blatantly into the empty house. But would this crisis pass? How? What punishment would I mete for disobedience? The usual loss of privileges: no guests in the home, no use of the car? My nerves tingled when I tried to relax against the arms of my chair as the clock struck midnight. I strained to hear some commotion in the street, and my throat tightened when I suddenly sensed the crash of a car door shaking the silence. I started. Could it be...? But I stayed fastened to my chair, breathing hard. When the front door opened swiftly and noisily and I heard the brush of Kimberly's soft shoes against the tile in the foyer, I thought my whole head was going to burst with a sense of relief. But I stayed rooted numbly to my chair pretending to be engrossed in the midnight movie. There was not a word of greeting. I heard the shoes scrape across the hall into the kitchen. The refrigerator door opened; the cake pan clattered; a stream of milk gurgled from the pitcher into a plastic tumbler. I waited almost breathlessly while the opening scenes of the old cinema quietly flickered. But deep in my heart I was smiling. I finally heard the faucet rattle and screech, and I knew the dishes had been rinsed. So I turned off the light from the television and rose from my chair, straightening out my wrinkled housedress. I met Kimberly as she sauntered into the hallway. She stopped when she saw me. The chiffon hung limply from her shoulders. Her hair streaked in loose shreds from her forehead. I hesitated when I looked into her eyes. They were narrowed and stern. I could not utter a word. My throat seemed so stopped up. "Well, I got home," she said coolly. "I'm in by midnight." She was tired. I could tell by the shadow of darkness beneath her eyes. "I hope you're satisfied." My heart pounded. "Did you have a good time?" I asked cautiously. "Yes," she turned toward the stairway. "But I'm really tired." At the stairs she stopped and looked back at me as I stood half disheveled in the hallway. We made a grand pair, I with my housedress and she with her streaking makeup and flattened ringlets. I was surprised to hear her voice soften. "Mom, I'm almost glad you didn't let me go to the party." Her limp arm reached for the banister. "I guess I'm really too tired to go. It's been a big day." "Yes it has," I said softly. "Yes, it's been a big day for both of us." I was smiling.
