EDITORIAL / Leisure for Oneself

To some the words "relaxation" and "leisure" may contain derogatory undertones, but the meanings in the dictionary give one pause for thought. "Relaxation" is stated as "relief of bodily or mental effort." Leisure time is "the condition of having one's time free from the demands of work or duty." What a precious commodity these are for a mother with many young children, or a woman burdened with exacting household duties, including that of constant nursing.

One woman confided, "I have been pressuring myself too much. I am beginning to realize that some of my waking moments need to be spent in doing something I want to do. I need to let down and take or make the time to do something to relax myself."

Advertisements of modern appliances sometimes tell of the additional time released to a busy homemaker. Too often the claim reminds the homemaker of the holes she used to make as a child at the seashore which instantly filled up with water and the holes disappeared.

Two women were speaking of joys experienced in earlier days of going visiting. One explained, "Where could you ever find the time today to go visiting, and if you did, what kind of a welcome would you have in the home where every minute seems to be scheduled fully? I'd feel guilty expecting a friend to sit down and take up her time." Yet Samuel Johnson said, "All intellectual improvement arises from leisure."

If this is true, or even partly true, depending on one's interpretation of what he meant, should not every woman seek to capture some leisure when she may pursue her own desires? And how

She may wish to take a walk, to garden, do genealogical research, play or listen to music, study and admire reproductions of paintings, sculptures, study a foreign language, read for pleasure, do crewel work, knit, visit a sick friend, telephone a friend, bake a cake for a neighbor, or just sit still and dream. Others find release and rejuvenation in physical exercise, in tennis, golf, bowling, swimming, or hiking. Sometimes a trip to a lovely store is very satisfying. If one is not too weary, the time before one goes to sleep may be used for relaxing. Perhaps time can be found for reading the scriptures, and even with one's eyes shut, a happy or fond experience can be relived in memory. If no one else is disturbed it is pleasing to sing some songs or recite poetry or scriptures. It is a time to evaluate one's progress and review pleasant events so it will be a time of leisure and relaxation. It is not a proper time to lament one's shortcomings of the day, but rather do that when one is actively pursuing one's work. Sometimes a movie on television will be a source of relaxation. There is no rule that says what one should do with leisure time. It will vary with the individual's need, but every woman needs to enjoy some leisure time, and everyone will do well to rearrange her daily activities, if necessary, so that early or late she can devote some time to herself alone and to view her life as an individual for which life she is accountable.

Anne R. Gledhill
