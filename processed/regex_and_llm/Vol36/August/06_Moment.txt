Mr. Finley was a mild little man, a steady worker, a good provider, who had long since left the details of the family's education to his wife. "That was because Ruthie had to practice in the living room all the time." Mrs. Finley sighed, and went on clearing the table. "Yes, but our children had some talent for music, if I do say so. I remember the last time Ida played in Professor Ernesto's recital, every note in perfect tune, and such beautiful expression! Ruthie accompanied her at the piano! It was lovely, and you'd never have guessed how they'd fought while they were practicing together." Mrs. Finley wiped her eyes furtively on an apron corner. "But, as I've said before, you go through all that, and what happens? The girls get married, and Jerry goes away to medical school, and leaves his sax in the basement. So—my nerves the way they've been lately, I'm just as glad that Carl shows no talent whatever. All he's interested in is rabbits, or pigeons, and _ baseball."
