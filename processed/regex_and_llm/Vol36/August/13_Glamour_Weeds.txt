Technically speaking, a weed is any one of those herbaceous plants which are useless, especially those which are positively troublesome in a field, orchard, or garden. The application of this general definition is, however, somewhat relative. The exotics of cool countries are sometimes weeds in the tropics. Handsome, but pernicious plants, are weeds to the agriculturalist, flowers to the aesthetic. It all simmers down to what you see in a weed and what use you make of it. It is my pleasure to introduce to you this month Frances Aveson Wiscomb, a woman who has discovered for herself that the lowly weed can become a thing of exquisite beauty. From an interesting personal hobby, Mrs. Wiscomb's decorating of weeds has grown to such proportions that, within the last few months, she has opened her own florist shop. Mrs. Wiscomb likes best working in dainty pastel colors, although, at Christmas time, she adds festive reds and greens, both of which combine nicely with the more delicate colors which she enjoys for most of the year. Glam our Wreds Frances Aveson Wiscomb OST of us pay very little attention to the weeds we pass along the highway and in the fields, and yet "Fairy Flowers," "Snow Flowers," or "Glamour Weeds" are indeed descriptive and appropriate names for these seemingly uninteresting plants, once they have been dried, dipped, and brushed with cold water paint, and sprinkled with "artificial snow," "diamond dust," or the like. Plants to be painted should be gathered in the fall, after they have gone to seed, dried, and turned brown. It is hard to imagine the interesting forms and shapes found in dried plants. Many change after ' seeding beyond recognition and become even more interesting than when in bloom. Some weeds, which look as if they would paint up beautifully, prove very disappointing, while others, which at first look doubtful, turn out to be exquisite.
