"If his folks can stand it, that's their business, but as for me—well—!"' She switched into the kitchen, her round face glowing with more heat than had arisen from the cook stove. Mr. Finley sat by the kitchen table, finishing his good hot dinner preparatory to going on afternoon shift. Mrs. Finley addressed the newspaper that was propped up in front of him. "I'll declare to goodness, I've had enough of music to last me for a long, long time. And to cap the climax, the other day Professor Ernesto sent me a card, saying that I had been honored by receiving the appointment of head of the summer band committee." She opened the oven and, in spite of her agitation, gently removed a cake, leaving the oven door open. "Again! After three summers of it. Going from house to house, enlisting students, and then checking up on the ones who don't show up for practice, getting dates for programs, worrying about uniforms, worrying about funds. Well, I'm not doing it again. For once, I'll be firm with him. I'll say, Now that my older children are out of school, and I have no one taking lessons, I find that my interests are along other lines—something like that, Ill say.
