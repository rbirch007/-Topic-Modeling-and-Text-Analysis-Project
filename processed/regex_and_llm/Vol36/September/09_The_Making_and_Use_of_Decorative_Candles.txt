The Making and Use of Decorative Candles Mary Grant Judd Member, Relief Society General Board [This is the sixth in a series of short articles on table decorations, flower arrangements, and related subjects. In the coming months Sister Judd will present other suggestions suitable to particular seasons.—Ed.] Nite to June, September is the most popular month of the year for weddings. And weddings and candles just naturally go together. 'Though science has been able to give us far superior lighting from the standpoint of efficiency and brilliance, it has never been able to duplicate the soft glow of candlelight. For this reason, dinner tables, as well as background effeets for weddings (to mention only a few of many uses) turn to candles for just the nght touch. This month I would like to tell you of some interesting and beautiful decorations that can be made from new or partly burned candles. So, after the wedding dinner, don't throw the used candles away, but store them, to be brought out later, particularly at Christmas time. For individual candle favors that may serve, if desired, to support place cards, melt candles in a saucepan. Pour the melted wax into very small molds to the depth of about half an inch from the top. When the wax is cold and has hardened, place the mold in warm water until the candle can be released. In the meantime, slice a ready-made candle into one and a half-inch pieces and push one of them into the center of each mold, thus furnishing a wick. You may like to use contrasting Page 600 colors for the outside and the center. For the center of your table make the same sort of candles, half an inch only in depth. As you remove them from the mold, shape the edges to make them resemble an open flower like a wild rose. For the centerpiece use a low container with small pin frogs holding lilyshaped leaves. Float your wax flowers among the leaves. Just as your guests are to be seated, light the tapers at the individual places as well as those in the centerpiece. They will "oh" and "ah" at the lovely and unusual effect you have achieved. To make the lovely Christmas candles such as those illustrated, take tall cans in the forty-six ounce size, such as those used for grapefruit or tomato juice. Be sure to choose cans with no ndges. After cutting out the top of the can all the way around, make a circle of cardboard the size of the top. In the center of this cardboard make a small hole into which insert a ready-made candle. It is placed upside down and projects above the cardboard about six inches. This ready-made candle supplies the wick for the large candle and furnishes the base around which the conical top of the finished candle can be molded. When ready to pour your wax DECORATIVE CANDLES into the can, melt it in a saucepan, being careful to let it get warm but never hot, for hot wax discolors. The pouring process is done in three stages. First, fill the can to the top under the cardboard; allow it to harden thoroughly, when you will notice that there is a vacuum around the inserted candle. Your second step is to fill in this space with more warm wax. Remove the cardboard and, when the wax has hardened once more, take a spatula and build the candle to a conical shape by adding small amounts of soft wax and molding it with your fingers. (Note the candle at the extreme right in the illustration and you will see the smaller candle protruding from the top of the nearly finished one. ) Allow the wax to harden for the third time, then warm the can in a pan of water until the candle can be taken out. The sides will be soft, allowing you to score them with a fork for a pretty effect. Now comes the most fascinating part of all, when your own artistic ability is given free play and you decorate the candle according to the use you want to make of it. Notice the illustration and the Christmas bells, colorful balls, and ribbon that are about to be used. One woman takes sprigs of live 601 holly and attaches them to the candle with bits of wax, tying a final bunch at the top with a red bow. Artificial flowers, such a poinsettias, are effective and the silver leaves one finds at Christmas are always good. But keep the candles simple. Besides being so lovely, these candles may be used season after season. As they burn down, build them up again by adding more wax.
