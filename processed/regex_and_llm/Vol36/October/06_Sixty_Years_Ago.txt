'They are Chaucer, Spenser, Shakespeare, Milton, Dryden, Pope, Gray, Burns, Wordsworth, Coleridge, Byron, Keats, Shelley. It will be noticed that there are thirteen names here, and my reviewers have not failed to remind me that it is notoriously difficult to count the stars. The fact is that Gray, the real thirteenth, was an afterthought, and I will admit that, although Gray is the author of what is perhaps the most imposing single short poem in the language, and although he has charm, skill and distinction to a marvelous degree, his originality, his force of production, were so rigidly limited that he may scarcely be admitted to the first rank.— Exchange SEPTEMBER At eve cool shadows fall Across the garden wall, And on the clustered grapes in purple turning, And pearly vapors lie Along the eastern sky Where the broad harvest moon is redly burning. May often be noted that two children who have grown to maturity side by side, whose parents have earned comparable incomes, and who, to all appearances, have had equal opportunities, still may differ widely in the richness and variety of their backgrounds. These two children moreover may sit for years in the same classrooms exposed to the same training and yet, in some subjects, one may Te veal a broader understanding and keener perception of the implications of the written word than the other. A careful appraisal of the two homes would probably indicate the reason. While the one home might contain a bookcase full of beautifully bound sets of books, the only reading material to be found throughout the rest of the house might consist of current magazines and the latest books of only passing interest. 'The second home, on the other hand, might not possess beautifully bound books, but one would fnd bookcases in neatly every room in the house containing books suited to the age of the different family members, from picture books for the youngest child to great masterpieces for the older members of the family. A. Friend once asked another, Page 6/4 Martin Farquhar Tupper "How is it that your children know all about the Roman and Greek gods and goddesses and heroes? Mine are having a struggle to learn about them in school." "Why," the mother explained, "I used to read them myths when they were small and as they grew older I bought them Bullfinch's Age of Fables and the Iliad and the Odyssey. They love those stories." "Oh," her friend replied rather blankly, "I see. To tell you the truth, I have never read those books myself." When parents are lovers of books and great readers, they usually hand down the same interest to their children. When financial conditions did not allow for the purchase of a book every month for every child, she gave preference to keeping on building a library for the oldest one, since the younger ones had access to the books purchased in previous years. EDITORIAL Birthdays, Christmases and childish illnesses also provided opportunity for additional gift books. In this way the children had "measle" books, "tonsilectomy" books, etc. It was well that the mother would go to make her purchases armed with a list of desired books, for, too often, a clerk was most interested in selling a book just off the press when the mother preferred to buy a book which had stood the test of time with many children. The children's most treasured books were their own books of scripture with their names embossed in gold upon the leather covers. These they used all through seminary and Sunday School, proud in their ownership. Over the years each child of this family became a well-read individual. With the many amusements offered young people these days— 675 movies, radio, television, and the great number of school activities which they engage in—it is all too easy to find children neglecting to tread choice books. Parents who read to their children and unobtrusively guide their reading habits by generously providing appropriate books, introduce them into a wonderful world wherein they may always find "the best of friends." Building an individual library for each child is a worthwhile project for any family. Then, as each child marries, he will carry with him to his new home the treasured volumes, which reflect his own growth in reading experiences, to form the nucleus of his own family library through which, in future years, he and his family may find lasting enjoyment and companionship at their own hearthstone.
