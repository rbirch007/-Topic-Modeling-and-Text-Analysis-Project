In these words is found the keynote which characterized the administration of President Grant during neatly three decades—absolute obedience to the word and will of God. Heber J. Grant, who was the seventh President of the Church, was the first President to be born in Utah and to grow up in the pioneering age of the colonization of the saints in the valleys of the Rocky Mountains. He was bom in Salt Lake City on November 22, 1856, a son of Jedediah M. Grant, a member of the First Presidency, and Rachel Ridgway Ivins. His mother had accepted the restored gospel in New Jersey and, turning her back on the worldly wealth and a life of ease that her family offeied, cast her lot with the Latter-day Saints and moved to the West. Nine days following the birth of Heber, who was her first child, her husband died. She was a woman of strong character and independence of spirit. In order to be self-supporting, she became a seamstress and also maintained a boarding house, thus making a living for herself and child. The boyhood of President Grant was spent in Salt Lake City, Utah. In later life he often recalled his mother's struggles against poverty and adversity during his childhood, feeling that his character had been enriched and strengthened through these hardships. When he was about thirteen years of age, the youthful RELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1949 Heber became deeply impressed by the story of Nephi as recorded in the Book of Mormon. He often referred to the impression that the following declaration of Nephi made upon him: And it came to pass that I, Nephi, said unto my father: I will go and do the things which the Lord hath commanded, for I know that the Lord giveth no commandments unto the children of men, save he shall prepare a way for them that they may accomplish the thing which he commandeth them (I Nephi 3:7). The great accomplishments which this ancient prophet-leader achieved through obedience to the commandments of the Lord and the admonitions of his father, inspired the boy Heber to emulate him. He determined to make implicit obedience to the Lord's injunctions and his mother's teachings the guideposts of his life. He became a dutiful and obedient son and at an early age was actively engaged in Church service. But obedience meant more to him than merely doing what was advised or requested. He conceived obedience to be intimately connected with improvement or perfection. This led him to strive for improvement in whatever he was assigned to do, or in anything that he undertook to do. At an early stage in life he commenced to manifest the invincible determination that characterized his thought and conduct to the end of his life. His own narratives reveal the struggles through which he passed in learning to play baseball, to write legibly, to sing, to become a public speaker, and to overcome the handicap of a weak physique. At an early age Heber J. Grant embarked upon a career of busithat has placed him in the front ranks of state-builders in the Intermountain West. From a messenger for an insurance firm he rose to Positions of bookkeeper, then bank cashier, and later established his own insurance company, The training he gained in these ventures was later to be of inestimable worth to the Church. When the Church was _ staggering under a great burden of debt, intensitied by nation-wide depression, he was sent by the First Presidency on financial missions to the east and west coasts to borrow large sums of money for the benefit of the Church and its membership. Farlier, in October 1880, when not quite twenty-four years of age, he was called by President Taylor to take up his residence in Tooele and preside over that stake. Acceptance of this call meant a great financial sacrifice to the young business man but, true to the precept that guided him in his life, he devoted himself to the task with admirable success. Two years later he was called to the apostleship. He opened and presided over the mission of the Church in Japan from 1901 to 1903, and later presided over the European Mission from 1904 to 1906. These mission experiences occurred in days when the Church was maligned and persecuted on all sides. Throughout the years that he directed the destinies of the Church as the Prophet, Seer, and Revelator, he endeavored to make this admonition his guide. The policies of his predecessor were continued. He stressed the need for developing spiritual righteousness through proper living as a forerunner of the higher spirituality toward which mankind should be striving.
