In these words is found the keynote which characterized the administration of President Grant during neatly three decades—absolute obedience to the word and will of God. Heber J. Grant, who was the seventh President of the Church, was the first President to be born in Utah and to grow up in the pioneering age of the colonization of the saints in the valleys of the Rocky Mountains. He was bom in Salt Lake City on November 22, 1856, a son of Jedediah M. Grant, a member of the First Presidency, and Rachel Ridgway Ivins. His mother had accepted the restored gospel in New Jersey and, turning her back on the worldly wealth and a life of ease that her family offeied, cast her lot with the Latter-day Saints and moved to the West. Nine days following the birth of Heber, who was her first child, her husband died. She was a woman of strong character and independence of spirit. In order to be self-supporting, she became a seamstress and also maintained a boarding house, thus making a living for herself and child. The boyhood of President Grant was spent in Salt Lake City, Utah. In later life he often recalled his mother's struggles against poverty and adversity during his childhood, feeling that his character had been enriched and strengthened through these hardships. He often referred to the impression that the following declaration of Nephi made upon him: And it came to pass that I, Nephi, said unto my father: I will go and do the things which the Lord hath commanded, for I know that the Lord giveth no commandments unto the children of men, save he shall prepare a way for them that they may accomplish the thing which he commandeth them (I Nephi 3:7). The great accomplishments which this ancient prophet-leader achieved through obedience to the commandments of the Lord and the admonitions of his father, inspired the boy Heber to emulate him. He determined to make implicit obedience to the Lord's injunctions and his mother's teachings the guideposts of his life. He became a dutiful and obedient son and at an early age was actively engaged in Church service. But obedience meant more to him than merely doing what was advised or requested. He conceived obedience to be intimately connected with improvement or perfection. This led him to strive for improvement in whatever he was assigned to do, or in anything that he undertook to do. At an early stage in life he commenced to manifest the invincible determination that characterized his thought and conduct to the end of his life. His own narratives reveal the struggles through which he passed in learning to play baseball, to write legibly, to sing, to become a public speaker, and to overcome the handicap of a weak physique.
