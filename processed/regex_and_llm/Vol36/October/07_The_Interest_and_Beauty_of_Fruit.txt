In the coming months Sister Judd will present other suggestions suitable to particular seasons.—Ed.] HO was it said that "Variety is the spice of life?" No matter, for though the statement has been so often repeated as to become almost trite, it is still axiomatic. What a marvelous example nature sets in the interest she gives us through variety. As I write I am at my cabin at Brighton, in Big Cottonwood Canyon, Utah, sitting on the porch enjoying the early morning sunshine. And I'm thinking: how wonderful that there are never two of those tall pine trees just alike; never two slippery rocks, in the dashing creek down there at the foot of the hill, which duplicate each other; and never two of those great billowy cloud masses in exactly the same form. I'm thinking especially this morning of the limitless variety one finds in the colors, contours, and textures, as well as flavors, of fruits and vegetables. (The latter, with their decorative possibilities, will be discussed next month). It is as if Mother Nature tries to intrigue our imaginations as she tempts our appetites with her myriad products. Does a peach produce fuzz on the surface, I'm wondering, because a peach skin is thin? Then what about an apricot? Why is a watermelon smooth on the outside while its cousin, the cantaloupe, is rough? Parenthetically, I'd like to pass on a bit from Wilham Jennings Bryan, Page 676 showing that he used his imagination as he contemplated a watermelon. He said that while eating a piece of this thirst-quenching fruit, he was struck with the miracle of its beauty. He weighed some of the seeds and found that it would tequire several thousand to make a pound and yet he had been eating a melon weighing in the neighborhood of thirty pounds. One of these watermelon seeds put into the ground and surrounded by favorable conditions "takes off its coat and goes to work." From somewhere the seed gathers over a hundred thousand times its own weight and constructs a watermelon. "It ornaments the outside with a covering of green; inside the green it puts a layer of white, and within the white a core of red, and all through the red it scatters seeds, each one capable of continuing the work of reproduction." In conclusion, Mr. Byran asks: "Where does it get its tremendous strength? Where does it find its coloring matter? How does it collect its flavoring extract?" Considering the great amount of interest, as well as beauty, that fruit holds, it is no wonder that many of us love an arrangement of fruit, for a change, as a table decoration. Most people prefer a combination of several fruits, but the beautiful simplicity of one variety—such as gtapes—can be very striking. A happy home where parents and children live together in love and mutual helpfulness is most likely to be a safe one too. Safety for the family depends not only upon the conditions of the house and its equipment, but also upon the physical and emotional health of both parents and children. There must be a feeling of well-being and security. Physical handicaps and emotional conflicts are causes of accidents. 'The individual who is unhappy, frightened, or worried may unconsciously bring injuries to himself or others. If he has poor muscular co-ordination, defective vision, or impaired hearing, mishaps can easily happen. Special care must be taken to protect such a one. It is important for parents to provide an environment of safety as well as creating an atmosphere of love and sympathetic understanding. They should strive to instill a feeling of confidence in each child that he may step forward unafraid. 'The learning of the right safety attitudes and habits is fundamental in establishing this self-assurance. The burden of responsibility rests with parents, but safety at home cannot be attained without the co-operation of the whole family. Teamwork is the key to success. Parents and older members of the household must practice the rules of safety. Children will unconsciously imitate their elders. If father puts his tools in a safe place and, in case there is a gun, he unloads and dismantles it immediately and then locks it securely away, the children will be set a good ex: ample and many a life will be saved. Children will usually enter into a family project with enthusiasm especially if their interest is kindled. They will take pride in keeping their clothing hung up and their toys put away if the storage places are glamorized. Incorporate safety in every activity of a child's day, his health chores and his play. Teach him to climb only as high as his age warrants, not to run with a lollipop stick or a pencil in his mouth, to hold points of scissors down and cut with a knife away rather than toward his body. Parents should use judgment in choosing the appropriate time to teach a child the proper way to use such things as scissors, knives, skates, scooter, etc. [E is wise to appease a child's curiosity. Let him see what is cooking on the stove—he will be less tempted to pull the pot on to himself. Show him how the food mixPage 685 686 er works. Let him strike the matches, under close supervision, for his birthday candles. Explain the danger of doing things the wrong way. The right way is always the safe way. Encourage children to be alert, to detect and report the hazard they find that it may be either repaired or eliminated. Teach children to be courteous and thoughtful, especially to other children. The polite child is, in most cases, a safe child. Examples of courtesy set by the parents and regard for the well-being of others will largely determine the children's behavior in this regard. Living the golden rule in the home will pay dividends. Thoughtlessness is the virus that is the insidious breeder of many home casualties. Some parents are being indicted for their thoughtlessness and outright carelessness, for poor and inadequate supervision of their children. Half of the fatal accidents to children occur under their own roofs and in their own yards. 'These casualties usually happen in moments of inattention or careless lack of supervision by their parents or guardians. It is inhuman to leave little ones alone in any situation where their lives could be endangered—such as leaving baby in the bathtub while mother answers the telephone, or locking him in the house or in the family car while parents shop or go to a show. Let's Do Better Embroidery Doris Feil N the seventeen years that I have been a member of the Church, I have attended many bazaars and similar functions sponsored by the Relief Society and other auxiliaries of the Church and, because embroidery is my own hobby, I have handled and examined countless pieces of needlework: dish towels, pillowcases, napkins, luncheon cloths, children's clothes, aprons, and other items, large and small. Every needlewoman should realize that all needlework has two sides, the front or "on-display side," and the back, or "you're-looking-atthe-wrong-side," of the article. Wherever handwork is judged competitively, it is the neat back and not the impressive-looking front of an embroidered article that is always the criterion of its workmanship and the sign of finished skill in the needlewoman. There is no need for the back of our embroidery to be an untidy hodge-podge of knots, loose ends, long loops of thread going from one leaf or flower or stem to another. It is just as easy to do embroidery that is, to all intents and purposes, reversible. This takes very little longer, is amazingly easy to do, and is well worth any little extra time and effort. Let us suppose, for instance, that you are embroidering a simple spray of flowers on a napkin. You arc going to use satin stitch for the flowers, outline or stem stitch for the stalk, and a bunch of French knots for the center of the flower. Use a crewel needle for best results. These are fine and strong, yet they have large, easily threaded eyes. With two, or at the most three strands of embroidery thread in your needle, you start off on the flower stalk. On the right side of the material, about a third of an inch from the end, take four or five tiny running stitches back to the starting point. Pull until the end of your thread is barely above the surface of the material—and of course you have no knot in your thread. Then start embroidering, using regular outline stitch, directly over your little running stitches. Your embroidery will hide them completely and hold them firm. When you reach the end of the stalk, draw your thread through to the other side and on the wrong side take half a dozen more tiny running stitches back over the part you have just finished embroidering. Cut off the end. Both the beginning and the end of your thread are firmly held and completely invisible, without any unsightly knots and untidy ends. For the satin stitch petals of the flower, you do exactly the same thing. Take half a dozen little running stitches rght on the petal of the flower to the starting point, and then embroider over it until you reach the end of the petal. Bring your thread through to the wrong Page 687 688 side and then pass the thread under the embroidery on the wrong side for about a quarter to a half inch and cut flat with the cloth. Again, no knots, no ends, and your embroidery is as pretty on the wrong side as it is on the right. HE cluster of French knots in the center offers a little different problem, but the solution is just as easy. Because of the type of stitch you are using, it is not so easy to start with running stitches and work over them. But this problem may be solved by having a big, beautiful knot at the end of the thread and starting it on the right side. Why not? You are going to have a cluster of some dozen or so knots right there, so why not have your starting knot on the right side where it will be lost in the crowd, so to speak, rather than on the wrong side where it will be conspicuous and ugly. Finish off with another knot, also on the right side, and all you will have to show on the wrong side will be a little cluster of neat, tiny, seed stitches. Most of the common stitches used in embroidery can be adapted to this neat and effective way of commencing and finishing the work. With cross-stitch, I do the same thing and start without any knot in my thread but with a few running stitches across the center of the crosses, which the embroidery itself later covers. And in finishing, I take a few invisible stitches underneath the cross-stitching that has already been done. Much of the perfection of a finished piece lies in the careful seRELIEF SOCIETY MAGAZINE—OCTOBER 1949 lection of materials as well as in the embroidery itself. Every woman knows what wonderful dish towels can be made out of used, bleached flour sacks. Aprons, also, can be made out of these readily available materials, either plain or dyed in various shades. But where a lot of time and effort is going into the work, it pays in the long run to spend a little more and buy the best of materials. A lovely little luncheon cloth with a simple design can be made out of dyed flour sacking, and it will take a lot of hard wear for everyday use, but, if you are going to do an intricate design such as a cutwork cloth and napkins, only the best linen should be used and then you will have an exquisite piece that you can use all your life and your children can use and cherish after you are gone. Cheap rayons, thin cottons, any material that is not colorfast or guaranteed against shrinkage is not worth the buying, nor the time and effort spent in working it. It hardly seems necessary to say that it is important to make sure that the thread, also, is colorfast and able to stand up to hard wear and constant washing. Nowadays, most threads are thus guaranteed, whether they are silk, cotton, rayon or wool. By following these simple rules we can do beautiful embroidery that reflects exquisite neatness on both sides—embroidery work which will wash and wear and last long enough to become heirlooms in the family.
