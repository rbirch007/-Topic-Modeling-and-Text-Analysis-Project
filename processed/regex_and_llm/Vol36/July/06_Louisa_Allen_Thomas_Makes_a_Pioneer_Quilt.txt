We _ walked through the rooms, and Martin found the garbage and started to stir it with one of the spoons from the dining car on the train. It began to snow again and got dark very early. "It's nearly time for bed," said Papa, and he told us all the stories we asked for and turned the lights dim. "If you hadn't come, I would have come to you," said Papa, looking at Mama. Martin played with his silver string and Mama's hair, looked at the snow and said, "Lalalalala," and then went to sleep. And then we were all going to bed. Randy and I and Richard said goodnight. Papa kissed us and then took Mama and lifted up her chin. Mama began to blush, and a piece of silver string fell forward across the place where Mama's hair was the curliest and darkest against her temple. "Hugh," Mama said. "My word," said Randy. "Don't Mama and Papa look different!' "You do, too," said Papa. "Is it the hives again?" Randy sighed. "I thought you were going to be romantic. Hives! I didn't even get to dance. I wrote you all about it." Mama moved to go with us to comfort Randy, but Papa kept hold of her. "Good night," said Papa, pulling her back into the living room. "See you kids in the morning.
