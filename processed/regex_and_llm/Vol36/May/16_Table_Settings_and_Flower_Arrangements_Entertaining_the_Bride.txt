Table Settings and Flower Arrangements Entertaining the Bride Mary Grant Judd Member, Relief Society General Board [This is the second in a series of short articles on table decorations, flower arrangements, and related subjects. In the coming months Mrs. Judd will present other suggestions suitable to particular seasons.—Ed.] INCE June is the month that more brides choose for their weddings than any other in the year, I want to give you, a month ahead, some ideas for announcements, showers, wedding receptions and so on.. When I think of brides, there often comes to my mind a line from "Peg O' My Heart," a popular play on Broadway in New York City years ago. So long ago, in fact, that I myself was about to be a bride. "In all this world,' remarked one of the characters who represented an older, experienced person in the play, "there's nothing half so sweet as love's young dream." That is why it is such a pleasure to plan social affairs for brides. Some of the joy that they are experiencing seems to spill over, and all of the guests, provided they have perennially young hearts, get their share. Putting first things first, let's talk about announcements. Although the actual announcement is seldom a surprise, it can be told in a surprising and orginal way. At one announcement luncheon, _ balloons were tied to ribbons attached to the stems of the flowers in the center of the table. Other balloons were at each place, and in them anPage 318 nouncements had been put before the balloons were inflated. When the balloons were popped, the announcements were discovered. Sometimes the news is tied in with the bride's background, with the business of the future bridegroom, or with the hobbies of either one of the bridal pair. To announce the forthcoming marriage of her daughter, one bride's mother, the wife of a doctor, passed around a good-sized prescription bottle filled with vitamin capsules in the largest size obtainable. These contained the names of the happy pair and, on the outside of the bottle, was the date of the wedding, the doctor's name, and his prescription: "Take one only for complete happiness." There are many clever ways of highlighting the display of a trousseau. What would we do without flowers, satin bows, and tiny silvery bells that have lain hidden in our stored Christmas decorations until pressed into service? If you have an old-fashioned walnut wardrobe try painting the inside with "shocking pink" as Schiapereli's favorite color is called. GPEAKING of Christmas bells, the large ones are ideal to use for wedding decorations. One lovely garden wedding where a big bell had been hung (on a wide white satin ribbon) from the branch of a beautiful old tree. Under the bell was a round table holding the exquisite wedding cake and the charming picture was spotted with a flood light. Try pinning small white flowers to these Christmas bells as table decorations, OT, for a very striking effect, make a bell of chicken wire and completely cover it by wiring flowers to it. But, whatever you do, don't forget to sprinkle the flower arrangements with ice water and place them in the refrigerator until needed. In planning decorations for any large group, such as that included 319 in a wedding reception, try to visualize the crowd and have your decorations in large masses and rather high in the room so that they won't be lost when the guests crowd in. I have purposely left showers until the last because I'm afraid they sometimes work an imposition on those invited. Often the same group ls asked to several showers and then, as close friends of the bride, there is still a wedding pres ent to be given. If this is the case, and you still want to have this type of party, try having a spice shower. Send out invitations to the guest to bring "sugar OT spice Or anything nice," and she will not be emparrassed in this day of high prices.
