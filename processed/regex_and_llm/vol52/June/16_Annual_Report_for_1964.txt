Annual Report for 1964 'he Retief Society, established by the Prophet Joseph Smith 123 vears ago, providing for the women of the Church an organization ''according to the natures of women,'' continued to bless and strengthen its increasing number of members during 1964. The 274,703 members who made up its ranks in the past year 'included sisters of many nationalities and races, including more than 6,000 coeds on various college campuses. The mothers ranged in age from their late teens to grandmothers and great-grandmothers crowding the century mark. This represents an increase of 12,701 members. Of this total membership 2,412 were women wha were not baptized members of the Church, but who were edified by the lofty program and activities of the organization. The varied aspects of the Society were carried forward by a devoted corps of 182,966 leaders including 4,568 stake officers, and 1,273 district and mission officers. The work in 5,735 wards and branches throughout the Church was accomplished by 21,596 ward and branch executive officers, 11,567 other officers (including choristers, organists and Magazine representatives), 23,251 class leaders and 120,611 visiting teachers. Increased testimony, cultural development, and unbounded satisfaction came to these leaders as they unselfishly gave of themselves in service. The blessings of the organization were extended to sisters of the Church in 400 stakes and 74 missions located in each of the United States and in 53 other countries throughout the world from the tip of Tasmania, the Union of South Africa and Argentina on the south, to Alaska, northern Canada, the British Isles, Finland, and the Scandinavian countries on the north. Areas in which Relief Societies are located include, among others, Germany, Samoa, Japan, Korea, Libya, Lebanon, Brazil, Spain and Guatemala. Sisterly visits were made by visiting teachers visiting in pairs to 559,795 Latter-day Saint families throughout the Church and to 10,779 families who were not members of the Church, but who welcomed the visits of these sisters. These visits were made monthly to observe instances of need in the respective homes and to carry a spiritual message to the mother of the home, with a total of 4,466,668 visits being made. Inspiration and musical development came into the lives of 43,711 sisters who united with heart and voice in singing the songs of Zion as they participated in 3,113 Singing Mothers choruses. Their messages of faith, testimony, peace, and happiness warmed the hearts of saints and friends assembled in conferences, in special concerts throughout the Church, at The American Mothers Annual Awards Luncheon and at the New York World's Fair, and in social functions and programs conducted by Relief Societies. A total of 231,933 s carried into Latter-day Saint and non-Latter-day Saint homes the spirit of this great women's organization. They contained articles of general reading interest. Inspirational messages by Genera! Authorities and the Relief Society General Presidency, suggestions on good homemaking, the courses studied by Relief Societies throughout the world, accounts of important events of historical interest, instructions to Relief Society leaders, and stimulating creative writings in fiction and poetry. Sympathetic, kindly, merciful services to the sick, bereaved, and those in distress, which is the fundamental work of Relief Society, claimed many hours of service by sisters upon assignment of their Relief Society presidents. This included 29,896 eight-hour days care of the sick, 407,621 visits to the sick and homebound, assistance at 10.203 funerals, the dressing of 855 bodies for burial and the giving of 684.805 hours to other compassionate services. Many anxieties were lessened, hearts comforted, and suffering assuaged through this untiring service. Relief Society, in 1964, also aided in the care of the needy through service considered a part of the Church Welfare Program. Under the direction of bishops, 108,948 visits were made by Relief Society presidents to families in need, 73,580 additional contacts were made, and 809,524 hours contributed by females on welfare projects. Through attendance at the weekly Relief Society meetings, the sisters consistently studied and saw the application in their lives of truths contained in The Doctrine and Covenants and in good literature. They came to understand more fully divine law in relation to Church government. They increased their homemaking skills. As a result of their conscientious efforts stimulated by bazaars and the teaching of homemaking skills, the sisters completed a total of 1,164,990 articles under the supervision or at the direction of Relief Society. This included 747,236 quilts, articles of men's, women's and children's clothing and household furnishings, and 417,754 useful and artistic articles which did not require sewing. All of these studies, services, and activities engaged in during the past year helped to equip the members of Relief Society to be better wives, mothers, homemakers, members of the Church, and citizens of their respective communities. Thus Relief Society continues to accomplish the purposes for which it was divinely established.
